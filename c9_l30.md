The rooms toured, the unit must now do the sum — and do it more honestly than either the scoreboard-wavers or the minimizers, because the summing is where this subject's credibility is won or lost. The reference point is the book whose title became shorthand for the enterprise: The Black Book of Communism (1997), the volume in which a team of French scholars — most of them, pointedly, former communists or fellow-travelers working from the opened archives — assembled the regime-by-regime record and whose editor, Stéphane Courtois, offered the figure that has framed every argument since: on the order of 94 million, rounded in public debate to "a hundred million dead." This lesson does four things with that number: explains where it comes from, component by component; airs the genuine methodological debates — including the famous fact that the Black Book's own principal contributors publicly objected to their editor's arithmetic and framing, a family quarrel this course treats as a model of scholarly honesty rather than a scandal; states the course's own accounting standard, with the ranges the evidence supports and the uses of the number it will and will not endorse; and confronts the two comparisons that always arrive — with Nazism, and with "capitalism's body count" — each of which deserves a serious answer rather than a slogan. The lesson's thesis, stated up front: the precision debates are real and the order of magnitude is not — every defensible accounting, including the most conservative, yields a toll in the many tens of millions, without parallel among secular ideologies; and the reader should distrust anyone, on any side, who needs the numbers to be other than what the archives say.

## The Number and Its Components

Courtois's tally, as the book presents it: USSR, some 20 million; China, 65 million; Vietnam, 1 million; North Korea, 2 million; Cambodia, 2 million; Eastern Europe, 1 million; Latin America, 150,000; Africa, 1.7 million; Afghanistan, 1.5 million; plus the international movement's victims — summing to roughly 94 million.[^1] The components' quality varies exactly as this unit has shown: the Soviet figures rest on the opened archives (the execution registers of L22, the Gulag series of L23, the famine demographics of L21) and have been refined since — the archival era generally lowering execution-and-camp components from Cold War guesses while confirming and firming the famine components; the China figure turns overwhelmingly on the Great Leap famine (L24), whose 23–45 million range spans nearly the whole debate by itself; Cambodia's is now tribunal-grade (L26); the African and Afghan entries carry the attribution flags L29 planted. Assemble the whole with each component at its scholarly range rather than its point estimate, and the honest spread runs from a conservative floor around 60–70 million (taking low famine bounds and strict attribution everywhere) through the Black Book's mid-90s, toward higher totals (Rummel's "democide" reconstructions, built by different and looser methods, ran higher still) — with the famine-inclusion question (below) the largest single lever. The course adopts the range, not the point: between roughly sixty and a hundred million human beings, killed by execution, camp, deportation, engineered famine, and terror, by regimes governing in Marx's name, within one century — and it pauses on what the debate's very shape concedes: the argument among serious scholars is over which tens of millions, a sentence that has no analogue for any other secular political tradition in history.

## The Debates, Honestly

Three methodological quarrels deserve the reader's command. First, famine inclusion — the largest component and the sharpest question: should famine dead be "counted" against a regime like executions? The course's answer was built in the case lessons and only needs assembly: it distinguishes natural famine (weather's dead, any regime's tragedy), negligent famine (policy-caused, unintended, unaddressed), and policy famine — where extraction continued against known starvation, escape was blocked, food was exported, and truth was criminalized (the Holodomor's border directive, L21; the Leap's Lushan lock-in, L24; Ethiopia's counterinsurgency famine, L29) — and it books the third category, at the demographers' ranges, as killing: not metaphorically, but on the legal logic every tradition recognizes, that starving a captive is homicide regardless of the weapon's slowness. Second, attribution boundaries: civil-war dead, proxy-war dead, invasion tolls — the course's discipline, applied throughout, books regime policy (terror, camps, requisition) fully, books war ledgers as shared with all arsonists, and flags each case (Afghanistan, Angola) where the boundary is genuinely contested; run strictly, this discipline is what produces the conservative floor, and the floor is the number the course leads with in argument precisely because it cannot be haggled. Third, the family quarrel: on publication, the Black Book's two most substantial contributors — Nicolas Werth (the Soviet chapters this unit has cited throughout) and Jean-Louis Margolin (the Asia chapters) — publicly dissociated themselves from Courtois's introduction, objecting that he had inflated toward the round hundred million, flattened distinctions among regimes and periods, and framed the total for a polemical comparison with Nazism they judged unearned by the book's own evidence. The course reads the episode as this unit's method vindicated: the scholars kept their ranges against their own editor and their own side's rhetorical convenience — and the reader should note that the corrected, contributor-approved arithmetic still sums to many tens of millions; the quarrel was over the last decimal place of an abyss.

## The Two Comparisons

The Nazi comparison first, because Courtois's framing forced it and the course must state its own position. The position: unique evils do not compete, and this curriculum — whose other volumes teach the Holocaust's singularity without qualification — refuses the scoreboard use of these numbers in both directions: "communism killed more" is not an argument that could comfort a single victim of either system, and treating the totals as trumps degrades the dead into ammunition. What the comparison legitimately yields is structural, and this unit has already built it: the kinship of category killing — Latsis's class question (L19) and the race question differ in the category's content, not the operation's form (the file consulted for what you are, not what you did; the family booked with the individual; the extermination framed as hygiene of history) — a kinship the Nuremberg-era jurists saw and the Genocide Convention's drafting politics (the Soviet delegation's successful exclusion of political and class groups from the definition) deliberately obscured. And it yields the double-standard question that Unit 5 owns: why the one ideology's symbols end careers while the other's decorate dormitories; why there was no Nuremberg for communism — no successor regime save tiny Cambodia's tribunal ever tried the system's crimes as a system; why Memorial's liquidation (L23) drew a fraction of the attention its work deserved — the asymmetry of memory being, by this point in the unit, a documented phenomenon in search of the psychological explanation the next unit supplies. The second comparison arrives as the tu quoque: "and capitalism's body count?" — colonial famines (Bengal 1943, the Victorian El Niño famines), the Atlantic slave trade, the Congo, plus the modern move that books global poverty's "excess deaths" to the market system as such. The course's answer has two blades. It concedes and counts the real crimes: this curriculum's other volumes prosecute the slave trade, the Congo, and colonial famine at full length, by the same method — documented policy, named perpetrators — and a Bengal 1943 (imperial priorities, requisition, denial) genuinely belongs in the same moral genus as the policy famines above; the course keeps one standard, and empires flunk it too. And it rejects the accounting sleight in the modern move: attributing systemic poverty's mortality to "capitalism" requires a counterfactual baseline (poverty is humanity's default; the market era is history's only sustained mass escape from it — the sister course's hockey-stick lessons), whereas the tolls in this unit require no counterfactual at all — they are departures from the victims' own prior condition, executed by documented decision; if the loose method were applied symmetrically, it would indict every system that ever presided over scarcity, which is to say the human past as such, and dissolve the concept of responsibility the tight method preserves. The tight method — intent, policy, documents — is the one this unit has used for thirteen lessons, and under it the asymmetry stands as the archives left it.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The remaining defenses, weighed. First: "the numbers are contested, therefore the indictment is." The lesson's whole architecture answers: the contest is real and bounded — ranges, not doubt; and the course's floor is built from the defenders' own preferred figures, so the haggling, granted in full, leaves sixty million on the table. "We only killed sixty million" — the reader should hear the sentence spoken — is not a defense in any court that has ever sat. Second: "the deaths were the price of development — literacy, industry, beaten fascism." Audited already in the cases (L21's collectivization economics; L27's Korean control) and answered in principle: the comparative record shows every celebrated gain achieved elsewhere, faster, without the graves — the counterfactual price of the achievements was not the famines; the famines were the price of the doctrine, and the achievements happened despite it where they happened at all. Third: "this accounting serves reaction — it is deployed to smear all egalitarian politics." The course accepts the warning as a discipline, not a veto: the numbers are abused by scoreboard polemics (the lesson has forbidden itself the use), the distinction between democratic socialism and Leninism will be drawn precisely where Unit 6 draws it, and none of that lowers a single count in the indictment of the systems that actually stand charged — the reader who suspects this course of stretching the ledger for politics is invited to reread the attribution flags, the conservative floor, and the both-ledgers section above, which exist because the course wants the verdict to survive hostile audit. One defense remains — the greatest of them, the one that concedes every number and saves the doctrine anyway: that none of this was real communism, that the idea remains untried and innocent. It has a lesson of its own, the unit's summation, and it is next.
