If the inheritance grammar has a founding document, it was written at white heat in 1790 by an Irish member of the British Parliament watching, from across the Channel, the most intoxicating political spectacle of his age — and predicting, while Europe's intellectuals toasted the dawn, that it would end in massacre, military dictatorship, and a generation of war. Edmund Burke's Reflections on the Revolution in France is the inheritance grammar's summa: the book that gave the tradition its central image (society as a contract between the dead, the living, and the unborn), its central warning (that abstract rights-reasoning, unmoored from inherited practice, destroys the very goods it promises), and its central credential — because Burke wrote before the Terror, before the guillotine turned on the revolutionaries themselves, before Napoleon, and called every act. This lesson does three things: it establishes who Burke was, which matters enormously because he was no throne-worshipping reactionary but the eighteenth century's great reformer — the man who spent his career defending Americans, Irish Catholics, and Indians against British power, which is precisely what makes his verdict on France unanswerable by the usual dismissals; it walks the argument of the Reflections at full strength, the famous passages quoted whole; and it runs the course's method on him — the prophecy graded against the record, and the Honest Counterpoint giving Paine's reply and Burke's real blind spots their full weight.

## The Reformer Who Wrote the Conservative Bible

Begin with the biography, because the lazy reading of Burke dies on it.[^1] Edmund Burke (1729–1797) was a Dubliner of modest origins and mixed religious heritage (Protestant father, Catholic mother and sisters — in penal-law Ireland, a formation in what legal disability does to a people), who rose in London by pen and voice to become the Whig party's intellectual engine and, by common consent then and since, the greatest parliamentary orator of his century. Mark what he spent that career doing, because it is the opposite of reflexive establishment-defense. He championed the American colonists for a decade — his speeches on Conciliation with America (1775) argued that Britain's abstract right to tax was worthless against the concrete reality of American character and circumstance ("the question with me is, not whether you have a right to render your people miserable; but whether it is not your interest to make them happy") — and when war came he blamed British arrogance. He fought the penal laws grinding his mother's people in Ireland. He spent fourteen years — the great crusade of his life — prosecuting Warren Hastings and the East India Company for the plunder of Bengal, thundering before the Lords that power held in trust, abused, must answer ("I impeach him in the name of human nature itself, which he has cruelly outraged"). He supported freer trade, opposed the slave trade, and defended the 1688 constitutional settlement precisely as a system of limited, accountable power. This is the man — the empire's own auditor, the friend of every oppressed party in the British world — who read the news from Paris in 1789, while his own party's leaders celebrated and young Wordsworth wrote that bliss was it in that dawn to be alive, and concluded that something categorically different from 1688 or 1776 was underway: not the correction of abuses within an inheritance, but the deliberate demolition of an inheritance in the name of abstractions — and that the demolition would not stop where its authors intended, because nothing would remain standing to stop it.

## The Argument: Rights, Inheritance, and the Contract

The Reflections' case runs on three linked claims, and each is a pillar of the grammar this unit maps. First, against abstract rights as a foundation: Burke does not deny that men have real rights — he affirms them, and lists them ("whatever each man can separately do... he has a right to do for himself; and he has a right to a fair portion of all which society... can do in his favour") — his target is the metaphysical method: rights deduced from theory, in disregard of circumstance, history, and consequence, wielded as solvents. "The pretended rights of these theorists are all extremes; and in proportion as they are metaphysically true, they are morally and politically false." Real liberty is not a chemical abstraction but an inheritance with a pedigree: the Englishman's freedoms, he argues, were claimed at every juncture — Magna Carta to the Bill of Rights — "as an entailed inheritance derived to us from our forefathers, and to be transmitted to our posterity," an estate to which each generation holds life-tenancy, not freehold; and this inheritance-frame is not mere sentiment but engineering — rights embedded in institutions, precedents, and habits survive storms that parchment declarations do not, because they are held by the whole social organism rather than by a theory's fragile thread. Second, the epistemology of tradition, the argument Hayek would later translate into information theory (readers of the Marxism course will recognize L33's ancestry): the individual's private stock of reason is small, and men do better drawing on "the general bank and capital of nations and of ages"; institutions and even prejudices (Burke deliberately rehabilitates the word — prejudice as pre-judgment, the compressed experience of generations available at the moment of action) encode trials and errors no living mind witnessed, so that the reformer who cannot see an institution's use should suspect his sight before the institution — Chesterton's fence (next lesson's subject) is this passage with a gate in it. Hence Burke's actual doctrine of change, which the caricature omits: "A state without the means of some change is without the means of its conservation" — he demands reform, constant and careful; what he forbids is demolition-and-redesign, the treating of a living society as a draftsman's blank sheet. Third, the summit: the contract of the generations, and the passage deserves its full quotation because it is the inheritance grammar's creed — "Society is indeed a contract... but the state ought not to be considered as nothing better than a partnership agreement in a trade of pepper and coffee... to be taken up for a little temporary interest, and to be dissolved by the fancy of the parties. It is to be looked on with other reverence... It is a partnership in all science; a partnership in all art; a partnership in every virtue, and in all perfection. As the ends of such a partnership cannot be obtained in many generations, it becomes a partnership not only between those who are living, but between those who are living, those who are dead, and those who are to be born." The living hold the estate in trust; the dead have standing; the unborn have claims; and the generation that treats its inheritance as disposable — that will "hazard to leave to those who come after them a ruin instead of an habitation" — has committed the primal political sin. From these premises the predictions followed, published in November 1790, while the Revolution still wore its liberal face: that the abstractions would devour their authors; that confiscation (the church lands) would teach the revolution that property is conditional and appetite constitutional; that paper assignats would rot into inflation; that the levelling of all authorities would end in the one authority that needs no tradition — the army — until "some popular general... shall draw the eyes of all men upon himself" and become "the master of your whole republic." The Terror arrived in 1793, the guillotine ate Girondins and then Jacobins on schedule, the assignats collapsed, and the popular general crowned himself in 1804, seven years after Burke died — the prophecy graded, by this platform's Lakatos standard (Marxism course, L16), among the most exactly cashed in political literature.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Now the audit, at the strength the course owes. First, Paine's reply must be heard, because The Rights of Man (1791) lands real blows: Burke's reverence, Paine charged, "pities the plumage, but forgets the dying bird" — the peasant ground under the ancien régime's actual weight is nearly invisible in the Reflections, whose most famous purple passage mourns Marie Antoinette's lost chivalry while the tax rolls and lettres de cachet go unexamined; and Paine's structural point stands as the rights grammar's permanent rejoinder: the dead have no right to bind the living forever — "the vanity and presumption of governing beyond the grave is the most ridiculous and insolent of all tyrannies" — every generation must be free to correct what it inherits, or inheritance-talk becomes the eternal alibi of every entrenched abuse. The course's synthesis: Burke actually agrees more than the duel suggests (his life-tenancy image grants each generation real agency; his reform doctrine demands correction), and the genuine disagreement is about defaults and burdens — presumed-precious versus presumed-guilty, the exact axis Lesson 1 mapped — with history's verdict split by case: on France's Revolution, Burke; on France's grievances, Paine; on the method question that is this unit's real subject, the twentieth century's demolition projects (the Marxism course's Unit 3 is one long grading of blank-sheet politics) have paid Burke's account compound interest. Second, Burke's own drift must be named: the late Burke hardened — the Reflections' rhetorical extravagance ("the age of chivalry is gone... that of sophisters, economists, and calculators has succeeded") shades toward sanctifying hierarchy as such; his horror of the "swinish multitude" phrase-making handed ammunition to every later reactionary; and the inheritance grammar's rap sheet (Lesson 1) includes men quoting Burke against every reform he would have championed — the fence-defenders of slavery and empire claimed his mantle, and the course, which spent fourteen lessons on the American argument, notes that Burke's own anti-slavery, anti-Hastings record is the internal refutation of his worst heirs: the trustee who defends the estate's crimes has misread the trust, whose beneficiaries include the wronged. Third, the falsifiability check the platform applies to everyone: Burkeanism can degenerate into an unfalsifiable reverence — every failure of an old institution explained by insufficient piety, every reform's success absorbed as "true conservatism" — and the guard is Burke's own test made explicit: the presumption favors the inherited, and the presumption is rebuttable by evidence of concrete harm and workable alternative, argued, not assumed. Held so, the contract of the generations stands as the inheritance grammar's charter and this course's first pillar: we are trustees, not owners; gratitude is the beginning of political wisdom; and the man who taught it proved his sight by calling the century that ignored him. His twentieth-century heir — the philosopher who rebuilt the same case from love rather than fear, and gave this unit its most quotable axiom about good things being easy to destroy — is next: Scruton.
