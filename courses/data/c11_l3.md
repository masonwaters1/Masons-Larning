Burke gave the inheritance grammar its charter from inside a crisis; its twentieth-century refounding came from a philosopher who watched the demolition projects Burke predicted run their full course — and who rebuilt the tradition not on fear of the mob but on something almost embarrassingly unfashionable: love. Roger Scruton (1944–2020) was the Anglosphere's foremost conservative philosopher for four decades, and his signature move reframed everything this unit has built so far: conservatism, he argued, is not primarily a doctrine about markets, hierarchy, or nostalgia — it is the political expression of gratitude; it "starts from a sentiment that all mature people can readily share: the sentiment that good things are easily destroyed, but not easily created." That sentence — this course's most quotable axiom, and the one your commissioning notes circled — carries an entire politics inside it: an asymmetry claim (destruction is cheap, creation is dear), an epistemology (you rarely understand what sustains a good thing until it's gone), and an ethic (the burden of proof belongs on the wrecking ball). This lesson gives Scruton whole: the biography that authenticates the philosophy — the 1968 Paris conversion, the decade smuggling philosophy seminars past the secret police of communist Czechoslovakia, the professional exile in his own country; the ideas — gratitude, oikophilia and its shadow-concept oikophobia, the first-person plural, and beauty as a moral resource; and, per the platform's iron rule, the audit — because Scruton had real failures, and a course built on honest counterpoints does not canonize its own saints.

## Paris 1968 and the Making of a Heretic

The conversion story matters because Scruton's conservatism was chosen against his class, his profession, and his interest — the same authentication test this platform applied to Sowell.[^1] The son of a Labour-voting schoolteacher who despised the gentry, Scruton was a scholarship boy at Cambridge when, in May 1968, he found himself in Paris's Latin Quarter watching students overturn cars, smash shop windows, and pry up the cobblestones of the Boulevard Saint-Germain to hurl at police. His friends were exhilarated; he asked them what they proposed to put in place of what they were destroying and received, he recalled, "that ludicrous Marxist gobbledegook" — and the question that formed in him that week became his life: "I suddenly realized I was on the other side. What I saw was an unruly mob of self-indulgent middle-class hooligans... and I was disgusted by it, and thought there must be a way back to the defence of western civilization against these things. That's when I became a conservative. I knew I wanted to conserve things rather than pull them down." Mark the structure of the conversion, because it is this unit's Lesson 1 lived in a single scene: confronted with the rights grammar at its most intoxicated — everything owed, nothing owned, the inherited world as mere obstacle — Scruton's response was not a counter-theory but the inheritance grammar's primal question: do you know what this cost, and what will you put in its place? The answer he spent fifty years elaborating cost him his career's easy path: in the British academy of the 1970s–90s, an open conservative philosopher was a scandal — his 1980 book The Meaning of Conservatism, he wrote later, "blighted what remained of my academic career" — reviews turned to vendettas, appointments closed, and he eventually left the university world to write from a Wiltshire farm. And the credential that answers every sneer about armchair traditionalism: from 1979 through 1989, Scruton and his colleagues in the Jan Hus Educational Foundation ran an underground university behind the Iron Curtain — smuggling books, delivering secret seminars in Prague and Brno apartments to students banned from education for their parents' politics, building clandestine examination and degree channels with Cambridge — until the Czechoslovak secret police detained and expelled him (1985) and put him on the index of undesirables. The men and women he taught in those apartments became ministers and rectors of the free Czechoslovakia after 1989; the state he defied gave him its Medal of Merit (First Class), presented by Václav Havel. When this course's later units examine regimes that wore compassion's vocabulary while criminalizing thought (Unit 6), remember that its foundational philosopher tested his ideas where the stakes were arrest — the inheritance grammar's claim that civilization is fragile and defended at cost was, for Scruton, fieldwork.

## Gratitude, Oikophilia, and the First-Person Plural

Now the architecture, in three load-bearing concepts. First, the founding asymmetry, given its full statement: "Conservatism starts from a sentiment that all mature people can readily share: the sentiment that good things are easily destroyed, but not easily created. This is especially true of the good things that come to us as collective assets: peace, freedom, law, civility, public spirit, the security of property and family life, in all of which we depend on the cooperation of others while having no means singlehandedly to obtain it. In respect of such things, the work of destruction is quick, easy and exhilarating; the work of creation slow, laborious and dull." Read it as analysis, not mood: collective goods are cooperation-dependent (no individual can produce peace or trust alone), which makes them inherently vulnerable to individual defection and generational neglect; their maintenance is invisible when it succeeds (nobody notices the trust that lets strangers transact until it's gone — the reader of the Spend Game course will recognize an economy built on exactly such invisible trust); and destruction is exhilarating while maintenance is dull — which loads the cultural dice against the maintainers in every age of spectacle. Gratitude, on this account, is not sentimentality but accuracy: the correct emotional registration of the fact that you are a net debtor to arrangements you did not create — and its political expression is the trusteeship Burke named. Second, oikophilia — Scruton's coinage from the Greek oikos, home: the love of home, in widening rings (household, neighborhood, landscape, nation), which he argued is the actual motive-power of every successful conservation, environmental ones included (his green conservatism book made the case that people protect what they love as theirs — settled, local, accountable love outperforming abstract global management). Its shadow is his most controversial diagnostic: oikophobia — the repudiation of home; the educated-class reflex that treats one's own inheritance as uniquely contemptible, "the felt need to denigrate the customs, culture and institutions that are identifiably 'ours'" — a real phenomenon this course will meet again (the Zinn unit planned for the American Story course is its case study), and a term to be handled with the care the counterpoint below enforces, since diagnosis-words can become the very heart-verdict weapons Unit 3 anatomizes. Third, the first-person plural: politics, Scruton argued, presupposes a "we" — a pre-political membership (territory, language, shared institutions, mutual obligation among strangers who recognize each other as home-sharers) that makes democratic disagreement survivable, because opponents who share a "we" can lose elections without fleeing the country; dissolve the membership, and politics reverts to the war of tribes or the management of clients. The nation, on this account, is not idolatry but the largest workable circle of accountability yet discovered — and gratitude's proper political object.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The audit, at full strength, because Scruton's late-life vindication tempts hagiography and this platform forbids it. First, the real failures: his 1980s Salisbury Review published, under his editorship, material on race and immigration (the Honeyford affair's aftermath among it) that even sympathetic assessors judge crossed from cultural argument into wound; his commentary on homosexuality in the 1980s–2000s included claims he later substantially retracted; and his paid consulting for tobacco interests while writing on related policy (exposed 1999) was a genuine ethical breach — conflicts of interest are not decontaminated by elegant prose, and the course applies to Scruton the standard it applied to Mises's 1927 passage and Hayek's Chile: name it, no laundering. Second, the concept-audit: oikophobia is a powerful diagnostic and a loaded gun — in careless hands it converts every internal critic into a self-hater, which is Bulverism (Unit 3, Lesson 5) flying right-wing colors; the course's rule for using it: the term describes a repudiation-reflex (contempt prior to and immune from evidence), never mere criticism — the auditor who documents his country's real crimes (this platform's American course does it for fourteen lessons) is practicing stewardship, not oikophobia, and anyone deploying Scruton's word against honest audit has joined the enemy this course is mapping. Third, the 2019 affair as the course's own case file in miniature: a New Statesman interviewer published doctored quotations that cost Scruton his (unpaid) government architecture post within hours — a tribal pile-on complete with heart-verdicts — until the full tape surfaced, the magazine apologized, and he was reinstated; he died months later, having written that the episode taught him gratitude even for enemies ("coming close to death you begin to know what life means, and what it means is gratitude"). The reader should hold the affair as preview: Units 3 and 9 will dissect exactly the machinery that ran him over, and the vindication-by-tape is the precision-of-speech lesson (L—— in Unit 9) in documentary form. The audit entered, the contribution stands: Scruton completed what Burke began — an inheritance politics grounded not in fear of change but in love of the given, tested against actual totalitarianism, and armed with the axiom this whole course will wield: good things are easily destroyed, and not easily created. The unit's next lesson takes the axiom to its most practical form — the fence in the road, and the reformer's test for when it may come down.
