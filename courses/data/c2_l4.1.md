The parent lesson mapped the Scottish Enlightenment as the intellectual soil Adam Smith grew from; this sub-lesson examines the most substantial recent argument about what, specifically, was in that soil — Benjamin M. Friedman's Religion and the Rise of Capitalism (2021), a book whose title deliberately echoes Tawney's 1926 classic and whose thesis completes this cluster's arc. Where the work-ethic lessons traced Protestantism's effect on economic conduct (and found the literacy engine underneath), Friedman — a Harvard economist of unimpeachable mainstream standing, which is part of the story's interest — argues for something different and in a way deeper: that economics itself, as a system of ideas, was born inside a theological transformation. The claim, in one sentence: the softening of orthodox Calvinism in the English-speaking world of the early eighteenth century — the retreat from utter depravity and inscrutable predestination toward a more benign view of human nature, human agency, and a providentially harmonious creation — is what made Adam Smith's central insight thinkable: that individuals freely pursuing their own interests could produce, without anyone intending it, the good of all. The invisible hand, on this reading, is a secularized providence — and the discipline this entire course studies carries, in its founding assumptions, the fingerprints of a Scottish theological argument. This lesson states the thesis at strength, follows it into its striking American sequel, and then audits it with the care a bold intellectual-history claim demands.

## The Theological Sea Smith Swam In

Friedman's reconstruction begins where this course's sister volumes live: in the Calvinist world whose doctrines — total depravity, unconditional election, salvation by God's inscrutable decree with human effort availing nothing — dominated Scottish and much of English religion into the eighteenth century.[^1] A thought experiment sharpens the stakes: in a strict predestinarian universe, the Smithian vision is close to unthinkable — human nature is corrupt, so self-interest untransformed by grace tends to sin, not harmony; human agency is soteriologically null, so schemes of improvement carry a whiff of works-righteousness; and history is not a progress but a sifting. For the invisible hand to make sense, three prior convictions must be in place: that ordinary human motives are not simply depraved but workable material; that human choices genuinely matter and can better man's estate; and that the world is so ordered — designed — that uncoordinated actions can sum to good. Friedman's historical argument is that exactly these convictions were winning, noisily and against resistance, in the very decades and places that formed Smith. In England, the latitudinarians and the moralists; in Scotland — the crucial theater — the rise of the "Moderate" party within the Kirk (the same Moderates this curriculum's Scotland lessons meet from the other side): clergy who soft-pedaled the Westminster Confession's hardest edges, preached virtue, benevolence, and an amiable Deity, fought the heresy-hunters, and protected precisely the intellectual culture the Enlightenment needed. The emblematic battles make the case concrete: the prosecution of professor John Simson for softening predestinarian orthodoxy; the Kirk's failed attempts to censure Francis Hutcheson — Smith's beloved teacher, "the never to be forgotten Dr. Hutcheson" — whose chair-room heresy was teaching that humans possess a natural moral sense, that God wills human happiness, and that the standard of action is "the greatest happiness for the greatest numbers" (the phrase is Hutcheson's before it was Bentham's); and, a generation on, the Moderate establishment shielding David Hume himself from excommunication proceedings. Smith absorbed this world at Glasgow under Hutcheson, taught inside it, and wrote The Theory of Moral Sentiments (the parent unit's Smith Part I) saturated in its assumptions: a benevolent Author of nature; a human constitution wired — designed — with sentiments whose free operation conduces to social order; nature "intending" the happiness of mankind and arranging means accordingly. When The Wealth of Nations then argues that the individual pursuing his own gain is "led by an invisible hand to promote an end which was no part of his intention," Friedman's point is that the argument's plausibility structure — self-interest as workable, agency as effective, unintended coordination as benign because the system is providentially tuned — is the Moderates' theology doing economic work. Smith did not need to be personally devout (his own guarded religion, closer to Stoic-flavored theism than to the Kirk, is examined below); he needed, and had, a mental world in which the optimistic premises were ambient. Economics, on this account, is a child of the eighteenth-century argument about predestination — born, as Friedman likes to put it, not against religion but out of it.

## The American Sequel

The thesis's second half explains something every observer of American life notices and few can account for: the peculiar, persistent entanglement of American economic opinion with religion. Friedman traces the lineage forward: the young republic's economics was literally taught by clergymen — the standard antebellum textbook writers (Francis Wayland's Elements of Political Economy above all, Brown's Baptist president outselling everyone) were ministers who presented free exchange, property, and industry as the moral order of a providential universe, Smith's system with the theology restored to the surface; the Gilded Age split the inheritance into rival theologies of wealth — the Gospel of Wealth's baptized laissez-faire against the Social Gospel's kingdom-building reformism, both arguing economics in explicitly Christian categories; the twentieth century re-ran the quarrel through the fundamentalist-modernist divide into the familiar present, where premillennial pessimism about human schemes, prosperity-gospel individualism, and Social-Gospel-descended progressivism still map, with statistical regularity Friedman cites, onto Americans' economic politics — the correlation of theological with economic conviction that survives controls for income and interest, and that his thesis renders intelligible: Americans argue about markets in religious grammar because the subject was born speaking it. For this curriculum the sequel pays double: it links this course's economics to the American course's religious history (the civic theology, the awakenings, the Social Gospel lessons) with one causal thread, and it arms the reader against the shallow secular telling in which economics descended from pure Enlightenment reason and religion is a contaminant — the discipline's own genealogy, on the evidence marshaled here, runs through the manse.

## The Honest Counterpoint

A thesis this elegant demands a rigorous audit, and the scholarly reception supplies one; the course adopts its four main cautions. First, causation versus climate: intellectual history's hardest problem is that "made thinkable" is not "caused" — the optimistic anthropology Friedman tracks was itself entangled with commerce's visible successes (Tawney's old arrow, running the other way: prospering societies breed benign theologies), with Newtonian natural philosophy's ordered cosmos, and with the classical inheritance; critics note that Friedman shows correlation of climates with more confidence than mechanism, and that Smith's optimism has non-theological parents in equal standing. Second, the Stoic problem: the deepest Smith scholarship (Rothschild, Phillipson, the TMS editors) finds Smith's providential language substantially Stoic — the "great Director of nature," the harmony of the cosmos — a classical idiom that a skeptical philosopher could use without Christian commitments; Smith's carefully managed distance from the Kirk (his praise of Hume's character scandalized the devout; his revisions late in life trimmed some theological passages) means the thesis must run through ambient culture rather than personal creed, which weakens its bite exactly where it is most quotable. Third, the priority question: much of the ground was surveyed before — Jacob Viner's studies of providence and commerce, the "doux commerce" literature, Waterman's work on political economy and Christian theology — and specialists grumble that Friedman's synthesis under-credits the guild; a fair note that does not touch the synthesis's value for general readers, which is real and this lesson's reason for existing. Fourth, the selection risk: the story can be told as triumphal Whig history — enlightened Moderates defeating dour Calvinists and thereby gifting us economics — and this curriculum, whose Sovereign Grace course treats the orthodox side with full seriousness, marks the distortion: the "hard" Calvinism here cast as the obstacle also built (the previous lessons of this very cluster) the literacy, the discipline, and — the American course's anthropology lesson — the realism about power that other parts of the modern achievement rest on; the softening that enabled Smith's optimism also, on the orthodox account, drifted toward the Unitarianism and thin moralism those volumes chronicle, and a full ledger records both columns. With the audits entered, the course's verdict: Friedman's thesis, held with appropriate tentativeness about mechanism, is a valuable and probably correct corrective at the level that matters most — economics did not spring from secular reason ex nihilo; its founding optimism about self-interest, agency, and unintended order was formed inside an eighteenth-century theological transformation, and the discipline's American career has never left the church basement where it was born. The reader now holds this cluster complete: the Book that dignified work, the champions who forged the ethic, the literacy engine the data revealed, and the theological door through which economics itself walked into the world. The parent unit's story — Smith, Hume, and the system they built — continues with that genealogy in hand.
