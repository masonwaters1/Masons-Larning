The parent lesson surveys the Reformation's reach into the modern world; this sub-lesson completes the cluster that began with Wulfila's Gothic letters by following the same engine — the Word demanding readers, and readers demanding writing — out of Europe and across the entire planet, where it is still running at this hour. The modern missionary movement, whatever else it carried (and this course's sister volumes weigh the imperial entanglements honestly), was history's largest literacy and language-documentation enterprise: its agents reduced hundreds of unwritten languages to writing, compiled the first grammars and dictionaries of a substantial fraction of humanity's tongues, invented scripts that gave whole peoples near-universal literacy within a generation, rescued at least one great alphabet from centuries of contempt, and created, in the process, the strange modern fact this lesson ends on — that the most translated book in human history is also the reason a large share of the world's languages can be written at all. Four cases carry the story: a Baptist cobbler on the Hooghly, a freed slave who became a bishop and gave his people their written language, a Methodist who invented a writing system so successful his own society disowned him, and the missionaries in Korea whose choice of script helped turn a despised alphabet into the pride of a nation.

## Serampore: The Translation Factory

The modern movement's founding scene is William Carey — the self-taught Northamptonshire shoemaker-pastor whose 1792 pamphlet and sermon ("Expect great things from God; attempt great things for God") launched the Baptist Missionary Society and, with it, the Protestant missionary century.[^1] Barred from British-controlled Calcutta by an East India Company that (the counterpoint will return to this) wanted no missionaries disturbing its revenues, Carey settled in Danish Serampore, and there, with Joshua Marshman and the printer William Ward, built what can only be called a translation factory: by Carey's death in 1834 the Serampore press had issued Scriptures — whole Bibles, Testaments, or portions — in over forty languages and dialects, from Bengali (Carey's own great labor: the complete Bible, plus the grammar and the massive Bengali-English dictionary that helped found the modern study of the language) through Sanskrit, Hindi, Marathi, Assamese, Punjabi, and beyond, cutting type for scripts that had rarely or never seen a press. The linguistic by-products were foundational: Carey produced grammars of a half-dozen Indian languages, occupied the first professorship of Bengali at Fort William College (the shoemaker teaching the Company's own civil servants the language of the province they taxed), and printed the first Bengali newspaper and prose works — so that historians of Bengali literature, tracing the nineteenth-century Bengal Renaissance's instrument, the modern Bengali prose sentence, begin their story, without embarrassment about the irony, at a Baptist mission press. Serampore set the template the whole century followed: wherever the mission went, the sequence went — learn the language, reduce it to writing if unwritten, produce grammar, dictionary, and Scripture, open the school, print. The Bible was the deliverable; the language infrastructure was the road built to deliver it; and the road remained for every later traveler, whatever their destination.

## Crowther's Yoruba and Evans's Cree: The New Alphabet-Makers

Two mid-century cases show the ancient pattern — Mesrop's pattern — reborn. Samuel Ajayi Crowther was a Yoruba boy seized by slavers in 1821, freed at sea by the Royal Navy's anti-slavery squadron (the abolition story this curriculum tells elsewhere), landed at Sierra Leone, educated in mission schools, and ordained; in 1864 he became the first African Anglican bishop. His life's linguistic work was to give his mother tongue its written form: from the 1840s Crowther led the reduction of Yoruba to writing — settling the orthography (the diacritic system for Yoruba's tones that, in refined form, is still in use), publishing the first substantial Yoruba grammar and vocabulary, and directing the translation of the Bible into Yoruba (completed by the 1880s), the first complete Bible in a West African language rendered under a native speaker's authority. The result is exactly the small-nations pattern of the previous sub-lesson transposed to Africa: written Yoruba — today the literary vehicle of tens of millions — descends from the mission orthography and the Crowther Bible, and the first generations of Yoruba literacy, journalism, and literature grew directly from the mission press and school. The Canadian case is stranger and even more complete. James Evans, a Methodist missionary among the Cree at Norway House, concluded in 1840 that the Roman alphabet fit Cree badly and did what this cluster's heroes do: invented a writing system — a syllabary of spare geometric characters whose rotation encodes the vowel, learnable, famously, in days rather than years. Lacking a press, he cut type from the lead lining of tea chests, used soot-based ink and birchbark for paper, and printed hymns and Scripture portions on a converted fur press. The syllabary spread along the trade routes faster than the missionaries themselves — Cree travelers taught it to each other — and within a generation observers were reporting what remains one of the most startling facts in the history of literacy: the Cree of the syllabic zone had achieved something close to universal adult literacy, plausibly exceeding contemporary England's rate, in a script weeks old by historical standards. Adapted for Ojibwe and, above all, for Inuktitut (whose modern standard syllabics descend from Evans's system), the invention remains in daily use across the Canadian north — street signs, legislatures, Bibles — a living alphabet invented for Scripture within living memory's reach. (Evans's own end was tragic — conflict with the Hudson's Bay Company, accusations, an early death — and the syllabics' later career includes the residential-school shadow the counterpoint takes up; the invention's brilliance and its people's embrace of it are, meanwhile, matters of record.)

## Korea: The Bible and the Rehabilitated Alphabet

The Korean case inverts the pattern beautifully: here the alphabet already existed — and the Bible saved it. Hangul, the featural alphabet promulgated by King Sejong in 1446, is by common linguistic judgment among the most scientifically designed scripts ever created; for four centuries it was also among the most despised, dismissed by Korea's Confucian literati as ŏnmun — the "vulgar script" fit for women and children — while true literacy meant classical Chinese, the possession of a small male elite. The Protestant missionaries and their Korean collaborators, arriving in the 1880s, faced the standard choice — the prestige script or the people's — and chose the people's without hesitation: the Ross New Testament (1887), translated in Manchuria by John Ross with Korean colleagues, was printed in pure Hangul, and the entire subsequent Bible, tract, hymnal, and school literature of Korean Protestantism followed. The consequences ran far beyond the churches: the mission presses and schools created the first mass Hangul readership; Bible women — the colporteur-teachers who carried Scripture village to village — taught thousands of women to read a script they could master in weeks; and Korean nationalism, awakening under the approaching Japanese shadow, embraced Hangul (the name itself, "great script," is a nationalist coinage of this era) as the emblem of Korean identity — the vulgar script, standardized and dignified in large part through the Bible literature that first took it seriously, becoming the alphabet whose day Koreans now celebrate as a national holiday, in the world's most literate society. Korean church historians and linguists alike credit the Hangul Bible as a hinge of the script's rehabilitation; and the case completes the cluster's typology: the Bible has made alphabets (Gothic, Armenian, Cyrillic, Cree), made standards (German, English, Dutch, Finnish), saved a language (Welsh) — and here, restored a script to its people.

## The Enterprise Today, and the Honest Counterpoint

Scale the story to the present, because the engine never stopped: the successor organizations — the Bible societies, and above all Wycliffe Bible Translators with its academic arm SIL International — constitute the largest language-documentation enterprise in existence: SIL's Ethnologue is the standard global census of the world's languages precisely because Bible translators needed one; complete Bibles now exist in more than 700 languages, New Testaments in over 1,600 more, and portions in thousands beyond that — comfortably the most translated text in human history, with a substantial share of the world's written languages owing their first orthography, first grammar, or first book to the translation movement this lesson has traced from Wulfila to the present. Now the counterpoint, at the full strength this subject demands. First, the imperial entanglement is real: missions traveled empire's roads, sometimes blessed its flags, and the same literacy that empowered could domesticate — colonial governments valued mission schools as instruments of order, and critics from that day to this (including African and Asian Christian scholars, whose critiques are the sharpest and best) have documented the compound of gospel and cultural imperialism honestly; this course's method is to weigh, not to launder, and the weighing must include the countervailing record — Carey's press campaigning against sati, Crowther freed by the abolition squadron the missionaries' constituency drove into existence, the mission-schooled generations who led the independence movements with the mission's own book in hand. Second, the language ledger has a debit column: standardizing elevates one dialect over others; mission orthographies sometimes fit badly and stuck anyway; and in settler-colonial contexts the same syllabics that empowered Cree literacy were later entangled in residential-school systems whose purpose was the opposite of empowerment — the tool, as the Luther lesson said, powers whatever it is attached to. Third, the secular objection: literacy was coming anyway — states, markets, and print would eventually have written these languages. Perhaps some; but the historical record answers with its pattern — for hundreds of languages the missionaries got there decades or centuries before any state or market found it worth the cost, because their motive structure was unique: no commercial or administrative calculus makes it rational to spend thirty years writing a grammar for four thousand speakers, and the belief that those four thousand must hear God in their own tongue is, on the evidence of this entire cluster, the single most productive linguistic conviction in human history. From the Gothic Danube to the Cree syllabics on a Nunavut street sign, the finding stands: the Book that demanded to be read taught the world, language by language, to write.
