Before judging the fourteenth century's religious wars, the reader should live in it for a lesson. This sub-lesson reconstructs the world Wycliffe and the Lollards shared with their persecutors: the England of roughly 1380 — a nation of perhaps two and a half million souls, nine in ten of them on the land, organized into some nine thousand parishes whose churches were, without rival, the center of every community's worship, art, theater, calendar, welfare, and news. The reconstruction has three purposes. It answers, honestly, the questions modern readers always ask and are usually misinformed about — how long did people actually live (the "everyone died at thirty" legend needs correcting), what did they eat, fear, and hope. It recovers what ordinary faith actually looked like on the ground — not the theologians' religion but the parish's: the Latin mass experienced from the nave, the cult of the saints, purgatory and the vast machinery built on it — so that the Lollard protest (previous sub-lesson) and the Reformation to come can be seen as protests against something concrete and, crucially, something most English people loved. And it sets the demographic stage without which nothing in the period makes sense: this is post-plague England — a society that, thirty years before, had watched close to half of itself die in eighteen months, and was living in the long shadow of that fact.

## The Shadow of the Plague, and How Long People Lived

Begin with death, because the fourteenth century did.[^1] The Black Death reached England in the summer of 1348 and, by the end of 1349, had killed — on the convergent estimates of the manorial and clerical records — somewhere near forty to fifty percent of the population; further national outbreaks (1361's "children's plague," 1369, 1375) kept mortality's hand on the scale for the rest of the century, and England in 1380 held perhaps half the people it had held in 1300. The survivors' world was measurably transformed: labor scarce and suddenly valuable (wages rising despite the Statute of Labourers' attempt to freeze them — the resentment that fed the Peasants' Revolt of 1381, through which Wycliffe's enemies tried to hang him, as the parent lesson noted), land cheap, villages emptied, and a religious culture pushed hard toward the themes the visitor to any surviving medieval church can still read on the walls: the Doom paintings over the chancel arch, the Three Living and the Three Dead, the cadaver tombs — memento mori as public decoration. Now the lifespan question, answered with the precision the legend lacks. Life expectancy at birth in this England was indeed brutal — roughly 25 to 30 years — but the figure is an average devastated by child mortality, not a description of adult lives: something like a quarter to a third of children died before their fifth birthday, and it is that slaughter of the innocents which drags the average down. The conditional numbers tell the real story: an English person who survived to twenty could expect, plague years aside, to live into their late forties or fifties; the tenant-in-chief records show men at twenty-five expecting twenty-five to thirty more years; village elders in their sixties and seventies were normal presences (the manorial courts are full of them), and the era's own institutions assumed mature spans — canonical office, guild mastery, the twelve "good men and true." So the honest picture: not a world of dead-at-thirty adults, but a world where death was concentrated at the doors of life — the childbed (maternal mortality claiming perhaps one mother per hundred births, accumulating across a childbearing life to real and feared risk) and the cradle — and where, past those doors, lives of biblical threescore were common enough that no one marveled at them. Every family had buried children; every adult had survived what half their generation had not; and the religion this lesson now turns to was, before it was anything else, the technology by which such a people faced such facts.

## The Parish Year

The English Christian of 1380 did not "go to church"; he or she lived inside it. The year itself was the church's: time was kept by the liturgical calendar — Advent's fast to Christmas's twelve days, Candlemas with its procession of lights, Lent's forty days of genuine austerity (no meat, no eggs; the fish trade was a Lenten economy), Palm Sunday's procession, the Easter duty (the one obligatory annual confession and communion, required of all adults since the Fourth Lateran Council of 1215), Rogationtide's beating of the parish bounds, Corpus Christi's midsummer triumph — the consecrated host carried under canopy through decorated streets, and, in the great towns, the guilds performing the cycle plays (York's, Chester's, Coventry's) that staged the whole of salvation history from Creation to Doom on pageant wagons: the Bible in English, note, performed publicly and legally, at the very moment reading it in English was becoming a crime — one of the period's telling paradoxes. Holy days meant holidays — some forty to fifty workless feast days beyond Sundays, the medieval laborer's calendar containing more mandated rest than the Victorian's. Sunday worship itself was the Latin mass, and the modern reader must reconstruct it without anachronism: the parishioner did not "follow the service" — he attended a sacrifice performed on his behalf beyond the rood screen (the great carved barrier, topped by the crucifix flanked by Mary and John, that separated nave from chancel), hearing Latin he did not construe, kneeling at the sacring bell for the elevation of the host — seeing God, the moment the whole rite built toward, popular devotion holding that on any day one had seen the elevated host one would not die unshriven. Around the mass clustered everything else: baptism within days of birth (given infant mortality, urgently — midwives were licensed to baptize in emergency, as L22.1 noted); churching of mothers; marriage at the church door; the last rites and the passing bell; burial in the churchyard where the community's dead outnumbered its living. And the building itself was the community's art gallery, theater, and news exchange: wall paintings (St Christopher facing the door — sight of him protected the traveler that day), the images and lights of the saints maintained by guilds and bequests, the parish's treasures inventoried like a family's plate.

## Purgatory, the Saints, and the Economy of Salvation

What the Reformation would attack, this section inventories at its living height. The map of the afterlife had three regions, and the middle one organized more of English religion than heaven and hell combined: purgatory — where the saved-but-imperfect burned toward fitness for glory, for spans prayer could shorten — generated the era's most characteristic institutions. The chantry: an endowment paying a priest to sing masses for the founder's soul, from the perpetual chantries of the rich (whole colleges founded to pray for one family) down to the poor man's "trental" of thirty masses; by the system's end England held thousands of them. The bede-roll, read from the pulpit, naming the parish's benefactors for the congregation's prayers; the religious guilds — part burial club, part mutual-aid society, part devotional confraternity — which for a small subscription guaranteed the member candles, a funeral, and a company of brethren praying for his soul: the ordinary Englishman's purchase on eternity, and (the records show) enormously popular. Above, the saints: not rivals to God in the theology, but the court through which petitions traveled — Mary supreme (Walsingham's shrine of the Holy House among Europe's great pilgrimage destinations), the parish's patron, the specialist intercessors (Apollonia for toothache, Margaret for childbirth), their relics and images the points where heaven touched geography; and pilgrimage — Canterbury above all, where Becket's shrine blazed with gold and the roads carried exactly the company Chaucer, in precisely these years, was loading onto the road to Canterbury: the knight, the miller, the wife, the pardoner. The pardoner points to the system's sore: indulgences — remissions of purgatorial penalty, grounded in the church's treasury of merit, retailed by the pardoners whose frauds Chaucer and Langland flayed in verse decades before any Lollard needed to. This was the "economy of salvation" — and the phrase should be heard both ways: a genuine spiritual economy of exchanged prayer binding living and dead into one community across the grave, and an economy, with prices, professionals, and profits, in which the fear of purgatory was, among other things, the largest funding mechanism of the medieval church. The Lollard of the previous sub-lesson looked at all this and saw idolatry and commerce; the reader now knows exactly what he was looking at.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The modern reflex — and the Protestant historiography that trained it — reads this world as darkness awaiting dawn: superstition, priestcraft, and fear, exploited by a corrupt machine until Wycliffe's morning star rose. The course's honesty requires the great revisionist correction: Eamon Duffy's The Stripping of the Altars and the scholarship it led demonstrated, from the parishes' own records — churchwardens' accounts, wills, bequests, the fabric of the buildings — that traditional religion in late medieval England was not a decaying imposition but a vigorous, popular, deeply loved system: laypeople poured money into their churches (the great rebuilding of English parish churches in the century before the Reformation was overwhelmingly lay-funded), joined guilds by choice, endowed lights and masses to the limit of their means, and, when the Reformation came, surrendered the old ways in most places slowly and under compulsion, hiding the images and restoring them under Mary. On this evidence, the Reformation was not the collapse of a hollow system but the destruction of a beloved one — and this course, whose sympathies the reader knows, states the finding at full strength because its method requires it: whatever was wrong with the old religion, "nobody believed it anymore" is not among the true statements. What remains true, and keeps the Lollard and Protestant critiques serious rather than merely rude: the system's abuses were real and contemporaneously documented by its own faithful (Langland and Chaucer were orthodox Catholics; their pardoners and friars are inside evidence); the theology at the parish's popular edge did shade into mechanism and commerce in ways the church's own reformers repeatedly condemned; and the whole structure rested on the laity's exclusion from the Book — a Latin Bible, a Latin mass, and a statute making the English scriptures contraband — so that the choice Duffy's evidence forces is not between a loved system and a corrupt one (it was both) but between two accounts of what the laity's devotion was owed: the old religion gave the people everything except the text, and the movements this unit follows staked everything on the proposition that the text was the one thing that could not be withheld. Whether withholding it justified fire — how the church of this warm parish world reasoned its way to burning the wrights' wives of the previous lesson — is the question the next sub-lesson answers from the sources.
