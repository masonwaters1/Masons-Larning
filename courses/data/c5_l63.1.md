If you have ever said "the powers that be," "my brother's keeper," "the salt of the earth," "a law unto themselves," "filthy lucre," "fight the good fight," "the spirit is willing but the flesh is weak," "eat, drink and be merry," or "let there be light" — you have quoted a man who was strangled and burned at a stake near Brussels in 1536, with the King of England's approval, for the crime of translating the sentences you just quoted. William Tyndale is the most influential writer in the history of the English language and among the least known to the people who speak in his phrases daily; the King James Bible, which fixed his cadences into four centuries of English speech, carries his work on nearly every page without carrying his name on any. This sub-lesson tells the story the parent lesson's political drama leaves in the margins: how the English Bible was made in exile and smuggled in wool bales, why its maker died for it a year before his king authorized the very thing he died for, and how one fugitive's ear — his instinct for the punchy, monosyllabic, rhythmic English of the plough and the market — became, through the 1611 Bible built on his bones, the sound of the English language itself.

## The Fugitive and the Wool Bales

Tyndale was an Oxford-trained Gloucestershire scholar with a gift the age had no safe use for: by the accounts of contemporaries he commanded seven or eight languages, and he had absorbed from Erasmus's Greek New Testament (the recovered Word of this unit's earlier lesson) the conviction that England must have the Scriptures in English.[^1] England was, on this point, the most repressive country in Europe: the Constitutions of Oxford (1409), panicked response to Wycliffe's movement, had made unlicensed English Scripture heresy — uniquely among major European nations, England criminalized the vernacular Bible as such — and when Tyndale sought the Bishop of London's patronage for a new translation, he was frozen out. His response became the era's most quoted vow, flung (his biographer records) at a learned cleric who had said England were better without God's law than the pope's: "If God spare my life, ere many years I will cause a boy that driveth the plough shall know more of the Scripture than thou dost." He left England in 1524 and never saw it again. The rest is a thriller conducted in printshops: the Cologne printing of his New Testament raided mid-run in 1525 (a fragment survives); the complete edition finished at Worms in 1526 — pocket-sized by design, made for hiding; the copies smuggled into England in bales of cloth and sacks of flour through the wool trade's arteries; the Bishop of London burning them at St Paul's Cross and then, in the period's great comedy, buying up whole print runs to destroy — financing, as Tyndale's circle gleefully noted, the corrected editions with the church's own gold. Tyndale meanwhile pressed into Hebrew — mastering it essentially from scratch, in exile, when perhaps no one in England could read it — and published the Pentateuch (1530) and Jonah, revising his Testament (1534) toward the rhythms that would conquer the language. Betrayed at Antwerp in 1535 by a smiling English spy named Henry Phillips, he was imprisoned sixteen months in Vilvoorde castle (his one surviving prison letter asks his keepers, in words that undo the reader, for a warmer cap, a candle, and above all his Hebrew Bible, grammar, and dictionary, "that I may pass the time in that study"), condemned as a heretic, strangled at the stake, and burned, in October 1536. His last words, reported by Foxe: "Lord! open the King of England's eyes."

## The Ear That Made the Language

Within a year, the prayer was answered with an irony history rarely improves on: Henry VIII, having broken with Rome for his own reasons (the parent lesson's story), licensed an English Bible — the "Matthew Bible" of 1537, which was substantially Tyndale's work smuggled under a pseudonym, with his colleague Coverdale supplying what the martyr hadn't lived to translate; by 1539 the Great Bible, ordered set up in every parish church in England, was carrying the outlaw's sentences to the whole nation, king's license on the title page. But the deeper conquest was stylistic, and this is the section where the lesson earns its title. Tyndale made deliberate choices that defined English religious prose forever: plain words over Latinate ("love" where rivals wrote "charity"; "congregation" for "church" and "elder" for "priest" — choices that enraged Thomas More precisely because vocabulary is theology); short declarative rhythms built on the native Anglo-Saxon word-stock; and an ear for the memorable that amounts to genius — "In the beginning God created heaven and earth... and God said: let there be light, and there was light"; "Ask and it shall be given you; seek and ye shall find; knock and it shall be opened unto you"; "with God all things are possible"; "the signs of the times"; "where two or three are gathered together." Where English lacked a word he made one: scapegoat, Passover, atonement (at-one-ment — a theological coinage of the first rank), mercy seat, long-suffering, peacemaker, stumbling-block. The linguists' arithmetic, run in the computer age, confirms what readers always felt: on the order of 84 percent of the King James New Testament and three-quarters of its Old Testament (in the books he reached) is Tyndale's wording, taken over verbatim or near-verbatim by the 1611 committees — which means the most influential book in the history of English prose is, in the main, the work of a man executed 75 years before its publication, and the six companies of Jacobean scholars who produced the KJV are best understood, on the stylistic level, as Tyndale's most careful editors.

## 1611: The Fixing of English

The King James Bible's own story belongs partly to the parent unit's politics — Hampton Court, a new king buying religious peace, the one lasting product of a failed conference — but its linguistic career belongs here. The translators worked as no committee before or since: some four dozen of the age's best Hebraists and Grecians in six companies, every draft reviewed by the others, the final text read aloud in committee — tuned, deliberately, for the ear, because this Bible was "appointed to be read in churches," and English speech for three centuries would be marinated in the result. What followed the slow-burning triumph (the Geneva Bible, the Puritans' beloved annotated rival, took decades to die) was an immersion without parallel: the KJV became the one text heard weekly by every English speaker of every class on both sides of the Atlantic, the book children learned letters from, the cadence sermon and statesman alike reached for — and its idioms dissolved so completely into the language that speakers cannot feel the seams: labour of love, thorn in the flesh, root of the matter, sour grapes, the skin of my teeth, no rest for the wicked, a fly in the ointment (near-verbatim), how the mighty are fallen, out of the mouths of babes, a man after his own heart, set thine house in order, turned the world upside down. Crystal's census counts some 257 idioms contributed or fixed by the KJV — more than any other single source in English, Shakespeare included — with the majority, per the previous section's arithmetic, Tyndale's coin passed through the 1611 mint. Add the literacy engine: in England and above all in Protestant America (the sister course's colonial lessons document it), this Bible was the reading curriculum — the New England Primer's scripture drills, the family Bible as the household's book and register — and the case is complete: modern English was not merely enriched by its Bible; it was tuned, stocked, and taught by it, and Lincoln at Gettysburg ("four score"), the spirituals, and the King James rhythms of the civil-rights pulpit are the long echo.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Calibrations, as the course requires. First, English was not made from nothing at Worms and Westminster: Chaucer and the Wycliffite Bibles preceded Tyndale (he knew the latter; his revolution was translating from Greek and Hebrew rather than Latin, with an incomparably better ear), Shakespeare's contribution to the word-stock rivals the Bible's to the idiom-stock, and the standard written English of the period was consolidating through print and chancery exactly as the German lesson described — the Bible is the greatest single force in the story, not the story entire. Second, the KJV's linguistic triumph was partly a function of monopoly and time: appointed use, printing patents, and three centuries of compulsory familiarity will fix any decent text into a language, and counterfactual English would still have been Bible-soaked through the Geneva version — though it would sound measurably different, and the reader has only to compare the versions' Psalm 23 to feel how much hung on the committees' ears. Third, the martyr-to-monopoly arc has its shadows: the same authorized Bible that made ploughboys biblical also served, in other hands, as the proof-text armory this curriculum's other volumes document (the slavery arguments; the KJV-only fundamentalism that would later freeze the very translation impulse Tyndale died for into a new Latin). And the More-Tyndale war deserves its honest note: Thomas More, saint and humanist, prosecuted this cause with venom and relished the burnings — a reminder the course keeps posting that the vernacular Bible was won, not granted, and that its enemies included the era's best men. None of the calibrations dents the finding: the English language's greatest single maker was a Bible translator the English state killed, and every speaker of the tongue — believer, atheist, and everything between — talks in his sentences. The same decades were performing the same miracle in smaller languages, some of which owe Scripture not their style but their survival — the Bibles of the small nations, next in this cluster.
