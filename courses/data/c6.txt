SUBTITLE|Origins, Texts, Law, and the Problem of Authority

DESC|A graduate seminar presenting, in its strongest form, a critical thesis about Islam: that the tradition makes unusually strong historical, textual, and legal claims about itself, that each is more fragile than the tradition allows, and that some carry consequences a constitutional-liberal order cannot fully accommodate. Three commitments keep it a seminar rather than a polemic. First, everything is anchored to a citable source — a Qur'anic verse, a line of classical exegesis (tafsir), a numbered hadith, a fiqh manual, a primary historical record, or a current statute — never to an assertion about Muslims as people. Second, the doctrine/people distinction is absolute: the course critiques texts, laws, doctrines, and the states that enforce them, not the inner character of 1.8 billion individuals. Third, the strongest academic counter-scholarship is built into every part and must be answered, not avoided. It deliberately excludes the popular claims that do not survive scrutiny and discredit the rest — the "subhuman" charge, the expansive "taqiyya" thesis, and demographic "replacement" theory.
