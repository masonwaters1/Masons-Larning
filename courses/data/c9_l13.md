The economic demolitions are complete: the value theory fell (L11), and the calculation argument proved a planned economy cannot know what anything costs (L12). This lesson and its two companions turn to the deeper stratum — the one the course's founding brief named first: Marxism runs contrary to human nature. The claim needs careful statement, because it is routinely made lazily and dismissed easily. It is not the claim that people are merely selfish (they are not, and the counterpoint will honor the evidence), nor that greed is good, nor that human behavior never varies across cultures (it varies enormously). It is the claim that certain regularities of human motivation — the responsiveness of effort to reward, the care that follows ownership, the priority of family and the near over the abstract and the far, the pursuit of status through whatever channels exist — are stable enough, across every tested culture and century, that a system whose functioning requires their absence will fail predictably; and that Marxism is exactly such a system. This lesson assembles the evidence in its most striking form: not laboratory findings but natural experiments — cases where communal and private arrangements were tried on the same people, on the same land, sometimes in the same decade, with the differences recorded by the participants themselves. The witnesses include a Pilgrim governor, a Soviet statistical yearbook, and eighteen Chinese farmers who signed a secret contract they expected to die for.

## The Oldest Argument in Political Philosophy

Begin by giving the argument its true age, because "incentives matter" is not a discovery of modern economics but the opening move of Western political thought's first great debate.[^1] Plato's Republic proposed communism of property (and wives and children) for its guardian class, on reasoning Marx would recognize: private attachment divides the city; abolish "mine" and all will say "ours." Aristotle's answer, in Book II of the Politics, laid down both pillars this lesson stands on, twenty-three centuries early. On care: "that which is common to the greatest number has the least care bestowed upon it. Every one thinks chiefly of his own, hardly at all of the common interest" — the tragedy of the commons, stated complete. On motivation and even on generosity: property "should be as a general rule private," for when everyone has a distinct interest, "men will not complain of one another, and they will make more progress, because every one will be attending to his own business" — and liberality itself requires property, since no one can share what is not his. Aristotle added the diagnosis that governs this whole unit: Plato's error was to legislate for men as they might be wished rather than as they are — the evil in existing systems arises "not from the absence of communism" but "from the wickedness of human nature," which communal property does not cure but merely deprives of honest channels. Every subsequent round of the argument — monastic property debates, More's Utopia, the socialist calculation of the last lesson — replays this exchange; Marxism's distinctive move was to break the deadlock by historicizing human nature itself: man as he is, the doctrine runs, is not man as such but capitalist man — selfishness is not nature but the artifact of property relations, and abolishing property abolishes the objection. That move (whose full engineering project is L15's subject) converts the philosophical dispute into an empirical bet: try the abolition, and see whether the motivational regularities persist. The bet has now been run many times, and the results follow.

## Plymouth: The Experiment With a Governor's Memoir

The most vivid trial in the Anglophone record was conducted, involuntarily, by saints. The Plymouth colony's merchant investors imposed on the Pilgrims a "common course and condition": for seven years, all production to the common store, all provision from it by need — communal farming, by contract, among a small, homogeneous, intensely religious community bound by covenant and facing starvation if it failed: as favorable a laboratory for property-less production as history offers, and the governor, William Bradford, kept the minutes. The result, in his own words, after the hungry years: the common course "was found to breed much confusion and discontent and retard much employment that would have been to their benefit and comfort. For the young men, that were most able and fit for labour and service, did repine that they should spend their time and strength to work for other men's wives and children without any recompense... And for men's wives to be commanded to do service for other men, as dressing their meat, washing their clothes, etc., they deemed it a kind of slavery." In 1623 the leadership assigned every family its own parcel to work for itself — and Bradford records the transformation with a farmer's precision: "This had very good success, for it made all hands very industrious, so as much more corn was planted than otherwise would have been by any means... The women now went willingly into the field, and took their little ones with them to set corn; which before would allege weakness and inability; whom to have compelled would have been thought great tyranny and oppression." And then the governor of a Calvinist commonwealth drew the theoretical conclusion, naming the ancient debate directly: "The experience that was had in this common course and condition, tried sundry years and that amongst godly and sober men, may well evince the vanity of that conceit of Plato's and other ancients applauded by some of later times; that the taking away of property and bringing in community into a commonwealth would make them happy and flourishing; as if they were wiser than God." Godly and sober men — Bradford's italics of circumstance: the failure could not be blamed on capitalist corruption, for these were saints; the free-riding, the resentment of the able, the withheld effort of the uncompensated arose among the covenanted elect within three seasons. The identical script had played at Jamestown before ("he that will not work, neither shall he eat," Captain Smith's Pauline decree against the death-spiral of the common store), and it would play again on collective farms across the twentieth century — same mechanism, same testimony, rising body count.

## The Private Plots and the Blood Contract

The twentieth century ran the experiment at civilizational scale and, crucially, ran the control condition alongside — which is why two statistics and one document close the case. The Soviet private plots: after collectivization (whose famine belongs to Unit 3), the regime grudgingly permitted rural households tiny personal plots and a few animals; for the next half-century the official statistics recorded, year upon year, the same impossible ratio — the private plots, on the order of 3 percent of cultivated land, produced roughly a quarter to a third of the Soviet Union's gross agricultural output, including the majority of its potatoes, eggs, and a huge share of vegetables, milk, and meat. The same peasants, the same soil, the same climate, the same year: on the collective's land, minimal, grudging, theft-prone effort; on their own thirtieth of a hectare, before dawn and after dusk, miracles. No propaganda survived that ratio inside the USSR — every citizen shopping the kolkhoz markets knew it — and no honest reader survives it outside. The Chinese document is the ratio's narrative form. In December 1978, in Xiaogang village, Anhui — a village that had starved in the Great Leap and was starving again on the collective's work-point system — eighteen farmers met at night and signed, with thumbprints, a secret contract: the collective's land divided among households; each to deliver the state quota and keep the rest; and — the clause that measures what they thought they were doing — if any signer was imprisoned or executed, the others swore to raise his children. Treason against the collective economy, capital in living memory; they signed anyway, because the alternative was watching their children starve on communal incentives. The first harvest under the secret household system exceeded, by the standard accounts, the village's previous several years combined; the neighbors noticed; the practice spread faster than the party could suppress it; and Deng Xiaoping's regime, reading the harvests, capitulated to the peasants and generalized the "household responsibility system" nationwide — the single policy change most responsible for the largest and fastest escape from mass poverty in human history, several hundred million people, initiated not by economists but by eighteen signatures under a kerosene lamp. The Xiaogang contract now sits in a museum, the regime celebrating the farmers who defied it; the reader may draw the lesson the museum's curators cannot print: when reward was severed from effort, the most collectivist peasantry on earth starved; when rejoined, it fed a nation — human nature, casting its vote by thumbprint.

## Ownership, Care, and the Markets That Would Not Die

Two further regularities complete the mechanism. The care that follows ownership: nobody in history has washed a rental car — the quip carries a literature: common property is systematically undermaintained (the Soviet apartment block's ruined stairwells beside its tended interiors; the kolkhoz machinery cannibalized for parts; Hardin's overgrazed commons), because stewardship tracks residual claims — he who bears the loss of neglect economizes against it, and where no one bears it, everyone neglects. (The honest refinement, banked for the counterpoint: Elinor Ostrom's Nobel-winning fieldwork showed local commons can be governed sustainably — by small communities with boundaries, monitoring, and sanction, which is to say by quasi-property institutions evolved from below; her findings demolish the crude "privatize everything" reading and demolish central planning harder, since her success conditions are precisely what state collectivization destroys.) And the ineradicable market: wherever exchange was abolished, it regrew instantly, everywhere, among everyone — the POW camp economies where cigarettes became currency with exchange rates and arbitrage (Radford's classic memoir-analysis from his own Stalag); the Gulag's internal markets; the Soviet universe of blat and na levo — the connections economy and the "on the left" second economy without which the official one could not function, tolerated because its suppression would have collapsed production; the black markets that fed every socialist city. Seventy years of terror, propaganda, and schooling could not stop human beings from trucking and bartering for one afternoon: Smith's "propensity to truck, barter, and exchange" proved, on the harshest test ever devised, not a bourgeois habit but a feature of the species.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Now the other half of human nature, stated at full strength, because the incentive argument is often deployed as a cruder anthropology than the evidence supports. Humans are not homo economicus: we are also, robustly and cross-culturally, cooperators — we share within families without accounting, give blood to strangers (Titmuss's famous study found voluntary systems outperforming paid ones on quality), build open-source software and Wikipedia without wages, tithe, volunteer, and die for comrades; experimental economics finds reciprocity and fairness norms everywhere it looks. Does this not refute the incentive case and vindicate "from each according to his ability"? The course's answer is the distinction on which this whole unit turns, and it is Hayek's: human beings run two moral operating systems, evolved for two different worlds — the ethics of the small band (solidarity, sharing by need, personal knowledge of every member's effort and circumstance) and the ethics of the extended order (property, contract, exchange among strangers) — and each is functional in its domain and catastrophic in the other. The family is a successful communism of five; the kibbutz (next lesson) a strained communism of four hundred; the attempt to run a nation of millions as a family — to organize anonymous society by the band's rules — destroys both systems at once: it demands band-level solidarity toward strangers (which cannot be monitored, so free-riding devours it — Bradford's young men repining, multiplied by a nation) while criminalizing the exchange institutions that actually coordinate strangers. Cooperation is real, precious, and scale-bound: it runs on faces, reputation, reciprocity, and choice, all of which anonymity and compulsion dissolve. The socialist error was never the belief that man can be generous; it was the belief that generosity can be nationalized. Whether it can at least be communed — whether small, voluntary, ideologically fervent communities can beat the mechanism this lesson has documented — is precisely the next lesson's question, and history has run that experiment too, hundreds of times, with results that are more interesting than either side predicts.
