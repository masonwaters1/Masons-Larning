Unit 2 closes with the lesson this course promised in its first hour: the full, ungrudging ledger of what Marx got right. The promise was not a courtesy. A prosecution that suppresses exculpatory evidence loses more than fairness — it loses the verdict, because the defendant's advocates will produce the suppressed material forever after and the jury will wonder what else was hidden; and in the specific case of Marxism, the "he was right about some things" move is the standing gateway through which each new generation is recruited — a gateway that stays open precisely as long as critics pretend there is nothing behind it. So this lesson walks through the gate deliberately: it states the genuine hits at their full strength, including several that should discomfit this course's own tradition; then it performs the audit that matters — showing, hit by hit, that each is derivable without the system, that the system consists precisely of what must be added to the hits (the value theory, the prophecy, the program), and that the additions are what this unit has spent eight lessons burying. The lesson ends on the ledger's last and most important entry: the grievances Marx channeled were real, remain real, and constitute the standing account that the free society must answer on its own terms — the assignment this course's final unit exists to complete.

## The Genuine Hits

State them without flinching.[^1] First, capitalism's revolutionary dynamism: the Manifesto's Part I (L6) grasped, earlier and more vividly than the system's own defenders, that capitalism is permanent revolution — incessant technical transformation, the dissolution of every settled arrangement, "all that is solid melts into air" — and Schumpeter, no Marxist, took the concept of creative destruction from these pages with acknowledgment; as description of the system's metabolism, Marx has few nineteenth-century rivals. Second, globalization: the 1848 forecast of a single world market — production and consumption cosmopolitan, national industries dislodged, "universal inter-dependence of nations" extending into intellectual life — reads, allowing for period furniture, like a briefing from the 1990s. Third, instability: against the era's complacent equilibrium economics, Marx insisted that crises are endogenous — generated by the system's own dynamics, recurrent, and worsening in scale — and the panics of 1873, 1929, and 2008 have kept the insistence honorable; the business cycle is real, and the smug versions of laissez-faire that denied it were wrong in a way Marx was not. Fourth, the social question of his century: the Working Day chapters (L5) documented, from the state's own inspectors, horrors that were exactly as described — the seven-year-olds, the phosphorus jaw, Mary Anne Walkley — and this course has already ruled that no honest account of capitalism's history may skip its early ledger. Fifth, ideology: the observation that ideas tend to serve interests — that a class's moral certainties correlate suspiciously with its advantages — is a permanent addition to critical equipment; this course wields it constantly, including against Marxists, which is the tool working as designed. Sixth, class as an occasional lens: elites do act on shared interests; laws are captured; and the public-choice economics of this course's own tradition — regulation as industry's tool, concentrated interests defeating diffuse ones — converges, from opposite premises, with more of the Marxian suspicion of official pieties than either camp likes to admit. Seventh — and here the discomfort is deliberate — the solvent problem: Marx praised capitalism for dissolving tradition, family authority, religious awe, and settled community ("all fixed, fast-frozen relations... swept away"); conservatives from Burke's heirs to Röpke and the agrarians have mourned the very dissolution Marx celebrated — which means the course's own coalition contains, in its traditionalist wing, people who accept Marx's description of the market's cultural corrosiveness while reversing his valuation; the tension inside fusionism is real, this curriculum does not hide it, and Marx saw the phenomenon clearly. Eighth, alienation as description: the drudgery, meaninglessness, and clock-servitude of much modern work are real experiences (L5's concession stands), and the literature from the factory memoirs to the modern burnout shelf keeps confirming the portrait, whatever becomes of the metaphysics beneath it.

## The Audit: What the Hits Do and Do Not Purchase

Now the grading discipline, because the ledger's meaning is decided here. Three findings govern. First, every hit is separable: dynamism and creative destruction were derived by Schumpeter from entrepreneurship, with no labor theory and no terminus; crisis theory exists in Austrian, monetarist, and Keynesian forms, each with mechanisms that survived testing better than Marx's (whose specific engine — the falling profit rate — failed with its value theory, L11); the social question's cures came from reform, growth, and the labor movement inside capitalism (L5); ideology-critique was generalized by Mannheim and turned on all comers; elite capture is public choice's home turf; the solvent-worry is Burke's older property. A reader can hold every genuine Marxian insight while owning not one distinctively Marxist doctrine — the hits are common intellectual property, and were mostly either inherited by Marx (the classical economists' machinery, the Romantics' alienation, the era's visible world market) or independently re-derived after him. Second, the system is the additions: what makes Marxism Marxism is not the observations but the apparatus welded to them — value-as-labor and profit-as-theft (fallen, L11); history as law with a known terminus (falsified and immunized, L4, L16); the class enemy and the licensed dictatorship (L8); the abolition of property, market, family-as-institution (L6) — and the additions are precisely what this unit convicted; crediting Marxism for the hits is crediting the tumor for the healthy tissue it grew in. Third, name the rhetorical move the ledger disarms, because the reader will meet it weekly: the motte-and-bailey — the expansive claim ("Marxism is the indispensable framework; the analysis was right") retreating, under fire, to the defensible motte ("well, he was right that capitalism is unstable and workers were mistreated"), then reoccupying the bailey when pressure lifts. The honest response, which this lesson equips: grant the motte instantly and entirely — instability, early horrors, elite interest, cultural corrosion, granted — and then ask for the bailey's separate deed: which distinctively Marxist claim — the value theory, the immiseration law, the terminal crisis, the withering state, the classless abundance — survives inspection? Unit 2's answer is on the record. A last calibration keeps the graders honest in the other direction: some celebrated "hits" are retrofits — "Marx predicted globalization" credits a forecast of processes already visibly underway in 1848; "Marx predicted 2008" converts a prophecy of terminal collapse into credit for a recession the system absorbed in eighteen months; the course's grading language from L6 stands — he got the wind right and the storm's location, victims, and date wrong, and a forecaster is not vindicated by the wind.

## The Genre Test

Because the reader will meet the ledger mostly in journalistic form, close the audit with a field exercise. Every crisis since 1989 has produced its "Marx was right" essay-wave — the genre surged after 2008 and returns on schedule — and the pieces share a template this lesson now makes transparent: open with a genuine hit (inequality data, a crisis, gig-economy precarity), attach Marx's name to the observation, gesture at Capital's renewed sales, and conclude that "we need Marx now more than ever" — without ever specifying which Marxist doctrine, as opposed to which common observation, is being endorsed. Run the audit on the strongest recent specimen class: the Piketty-era inequality literature is frequently drafted into the genre, yet Piketty's own framework — whatever its merits, which the sister course weighs — is built on neoclassical capital theory, explicitly rejects the labor theory of value, proposes taxes rather than expropriation, and forecasts no terminal crisis: it is, in this lesson's terms, motte all the way down, and its conscription as "Marx vindicated" is the bailey move performed in broad daylight. The same test dispatches the crisis pieces ("Marx predicted 2008": graded above), the gig-economy pieces (precarity is real; the reserve-army mechanism attached to it predicts wage trajectories the data then embarrass), and the perennial "young people are discovering Marx" features (discovering, on inspection, the motte's genuine grievances — which is why Unit 6's answer matters more than another round of refutation). The exercise's yield is a habit, not a gotcha: whenever the name appears, ask the deed question — which distinctively Marxist claim is being asserted, and would its author accept the whole apparatus the claim is packaged in? Ninety times in a hundred the answer dissolves the piece into observations this lesson has already granted and doctrines it has already buried; the hundredth time, the reader is equipped for the real argument.

## The Grievances Remain

The ledger's final entry is the one this course weighs heaviest, because it is not about Marx's mind but about his market. Strip every doctrine, and what remains is what gave the doctrines their armies: real grievances, recurring in every generation of every market society — inequality that offends the moral sense even when absolute conditions improve; precarity, the worker's exposure to forces he cannot see or influence; work that consumes lives without meaning; elites that fail, self-deal, and moralize their advantages; communities dissolved by the very dynamism the system rightly boasts of; and the hunger, sharpened wherever older faiths recede, for significance and solidarity that consumption does not feed. Marx did not invent one of these; he built the most powerful machine in history for collecting them — and the machine's blueprints are public domain: any generation's grievances can be loaded into the victim-oppressor engine (Unit 5 will watch it happen), and they will be, perpetually, wherever the grievances go unanswered by better hands. This is the course's standing thesis, planted in L1 and now due: refutation is necessary and insufficient. The labor theory's collapse does not lower anyone's rent; Popper's criterion comforts no one whose town lost its factory and its church in the same decade; and a critique of Marxism that ends at "false and murderous" — however true — leaves the field's actual demand unserved and merely waits for the next supplier. The free society's case must therefore be made positively — prosperity with dignity, dynamism with community, liberty with meaning — and made to the very people the grievance-machine recruits; that constructive brief is Unit 6's assignment and the course's destination.

## The Honest Counterpoint

This lesson's counterpoint runs, for once, in the prosecution's favor — the audit of over-crediting, since a ledger lesson's characteristic failure is generosity theater. Three cautions. First, the concession trap: granting hits can slide into granting frameworks — "Marx was right about instability" is true; "Marx's theory of instability was right" is false, and the two are exchanged in careless prose constantly; the reader should police the difference in this course as elsewhere. Second, the company of the hits: several are truisms wearing genius's laurels — that elites self-deal and ideas serve interests was known to Thucydides, the Hebrew prophets, and every peasant; Marx systematized suspicions mankind already possessed, and the systematization (interest-analysis as universal solvent, exempting itself) is where the trouble entered; credit the observation to humanity and the weaponization to Marx. Third, the asymmetry of stakes: this lesson's fairness must not be mistaken for symmetry — the question "what did Marx get right?" is worth one lesson; the question "what did Marxism do?" is worth thirteen, and they are next. The ledger closed, the trial's evidentiary phase ends where such trials must: with the record of the regimes — the decrees, the famines, the quotas, the camps, told with names, dates, and numbers, beginning where the system first touched state power and terror began not as degeneration but as policy.
