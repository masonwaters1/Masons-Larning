Every course on Marxism's record arrives at Cambodia, because Cambodia is the limit case: the regime that pursued the doctrine's totality fastest — money, markets, cities, family meals, religion, and the past abolished not over decades but in days — and achieved, in forty-four months, the highest kill-rate relative to population of any regime in modern history: between a fifth and a quarter of the Cambodian nation, roughly 1.5 to 2 million of some 7.9 million people, dead by execution, starvation, and worked exhaustion between April 1975 and January 1979. The Khmer Rouge matter to this course's argument with a precision beyond horror: they are the dose-response experiment — the demonstration that lethality scaled with purity, that the closer a regime ran to the blueprint's full program (Manifesto's spine, L6; the New Man, L15; the category system, L19), the faster the dying; and they are the case that forecloses the "peasant aberration" escape, because the men who designed Year Zero were Paris-educated Marxist intellectuals with doctorates, applying, by their own testimony and texts, the most advanced revolutionary theory of their generation. This lesson gives the sequence — the emptying, the machinery, the categories, the count — the tribunal's findings, the Western intellectuals' disgrace, and the counterpoint (the American bombing's real role included) at the full strength the course requires.

## April 17, 1975: The Emptying

The Khmer Rouge took Phnom Penh on the morning of April 17, 1975, and began, within hours, the act that announced everything: the evacuation of the capital — some two million people, swollen with war refugees, marched out at gunpoint in days, on the pretext of American bombing to come; hospitals emptied with the patients pushed in their beds, the dying and the newborn on the roads in April heat, the old who could not walk left where they fell.[^1] Every city and town followed; Cambodia became, by design, a nation of villages and labor camps, its urban civilization not conquered but deleted. The symbols were made literal with a thoroughness historians still pause over: money was abolished — not suspended: the National Bank of Cambodia was dynamited, riel notes drifting in the streets as trash, and for nearly four years Cambodia ran, alone among modern states, with no currency, no markets, no wages — barter itself criminalized, the Manifesto's centralization-of-credit plank resolved by removing credit from existence; private property vanished to the level of the cooking pot (communal mess halls compulsory — the Chinese model, L24, without the retreat); the calendar itself was reset — Year Zero, history abolished as policy; religion was eradicated, not restricted: the Buddhist sangha, some sixty-five thousand monks, was defrocked into labor gangs or killed until organized Buddhism ceased to exist in the country of Angkor, with the Cham Muslims and the small Christian communities receiving the treatment the tribunal would later name (below); the family was dissolved operationally — children separated into mobile work brigades, marriages assigned by the Organization, mealtimes communal, and children trained, systematically, as watchers and informers against their parents (the regime's most quoted proverbs taught that the Angkar — "the Organization," the party's faceless name — had "the eyes of a pineapple," and its youngest eyes were in every household). The population was sorted into the category system this course has traced from Latsis (L19) to songbun (next lesson): "base people" (the old-liberated peasantry) above "new people" (the April 17 people — the evacuated urbanites), the latter marked for extraction and expendability, rationed last, worked hardest, killed on suspicion; and suspicion's evidentiary standard became the era's grim shorthand — eyeglasses, soft hands, a foreign language, a secondary education: intellectual, therefore enemy, therefore, in the Angkar's arithmetic, disposable, per the slogan survivors report from every province: "To keep you is no benefit; to destroy you is no loss."

## The Machinery: S-21 and the Fields

The killing had a bureaucracy, and its capital was a former high school in Phnom Penh: S-21, Tuol Sleng — the regime's central interrogation facility, where between fourteen and twenty thousand men, women, and children were processed between 1975 and 1979, photographed on arrival (the archive of faces that now lines the museum's walls), tortured into confessions of espionage for the CIA, the KGB, and Vietnam — often all three, the Moscow-trial genre (L22) reproduced in a Khmer key, complete with the recantation-proof absurdities — and then trucked to the killing field at Choeung Ek, where they were clubbed at the pit's edge to save bullets, the children swung against the tree the visitor is still shown. Documented survivors of S-21 number in the single digits to low teens. Its commandant, Comrade Duch — a former mathematics teacher — kept the records that convicted him: the annotated lists ("smash," in the margin), the interrogation manuals, the daily execution logs; and S-21 was one node of a national grid of security centers and burial fields — the mapping projects have located killing sites and mass graves by the thousand across the country. Above the grid stood the leadership this lesson's argument requires the reader to see exactly: Saloth Sar, "Pol Pot," educated in Paris on a government scholarship, member of the French Communist Party's Cambodian circle; Ieng Sary, Khieu Samphan (doctorate in economics, Paris — his dissertation on Cambodia's economy prefiguring the autarkic program), Son Sen, and the rest of the Paris study group — the revolution designed in the Latin Quarter's seminars, its cadres drilled in the classics of the tradition this course has spent two units examining, its documents (the Party's own Four-Year Plan, its confessions of "super great leap" ambition) speaking fluent Maoist Marxism-Leninism radicalized one turn further. The toll, by the careful reconstructions: the Cambodian Genocide Program's and allied demographic studies converge on roughly 1.5 to 2.2 million excess deaths — the standard central estimates around 1.7 to 2 million — of a 1975 population near 7.9 million: between one person in five and one in four, in under four years, with the Cham Muslims suffering proportionally worst (on the order of a third of the community dead — the basis of the tribunal's genocide conviction), the Vietnamese minority effectively annihilated within the borders, and the educated classes — doctors, teachers, engineers, monks — destroyed so thoroughly that the country restarted in 1979 with a professional class countable in dozens.

## The Trial, the Deniers, and the Dose-Response Finding

The reckoning came late and partial: the UN-backed tribunal (ECCC) convicted Duch in 2010 (crimes against humanity; the mathematics teacher acknowledging the archive he kept), and in 2014 and 2018 convicted the surviving seniors Nuon Chea and Khieu Samphan — the 2018 judgment entering formal genocide convictions for the destruction of the Cham and the Vietnamese; Pol Pot himself died in 1998, untried, in a jungle cot, telling his last interviewer "my conscience is clear." The Western intellectual record around the catastrophe belongs in this course as a named exhibit, because it is the fellow-traveling pattern (Duranty, L21) in its post-1968 edition: as the refugee testimony accumulated from 1975, a portion of the Western left dismissed it as propaganda — the emblematic episode being Noam Chomsky and Edward Herman's 1977 "Distortions at Fourth Hand" and their 1979 After the Cataclysm, which cast systematic doubt on the refugee accounts, favorably compared apologetic sources against them, and framed the emerging record as manufactured atrocity literature; the defense (offered then and since — that the target was media hypocrisy and evidentiary standards, not the regime) is noted per the course's method, and the verdict is the record's: the refugees were right, in detail, about everything, and the sophisticated skepticism was aimed, with instructive selectivity, at the victims' testimony rather than the perpetrators' claims — a case study Unit 5 will file under the psychology it belongs to. For this unit, Cambodia's analytic yield is the dose-response finding, stated carefully: across this unit's cases, lethality has tracked the program's totality and tempo — NEP's partial market retreat versus war communism (L20), the Soviet private plots versus their Chinese abolition (L24), and now the fastest, most total implementation ever attempted producing the highest proportional death rate ever recorded; the correlation runs within the tradition, across cultures, climates, and decades, and its direction is not what the "betrayed ideal" defense predicts — the purer the application, the higher the pile. Cambodia is what the blueprint looks like executed by believers in a hurry, and its authors' diplomas answer, in advance, the aberration defense the counterpoint now weighs.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Three defenses, at strength. First — and this one carries real weight — the context: the Khmer Rouge rose from a country already wrecked; the American bombing of Cambodia (1969–73, hundreds of thousands of tons on the countryside), the 1970 coup and civil war, and the region's great-power politics devastated rural society and, as Kiernan's scholarship argues, served the insurgency as recruiting material — peasants bombed into the arms of the revolution. The course concedes the contribution genuinely: Western policy helped create the conditions of the Khmer Rouge's victory, and that ledger entry stands in this curriculum's accounting of the era. But the concession has a boundary the record enforces: the bombing explains recruitment and victory; it does not touch design — the evacuation, the money's abolition, the category system, S-21's procedures, and the Four-Year Plan were doctrinal choices, drafted by the Paris circle, paralleled nowhere among the war's other traumatized populations, and prefigured in the leadership's texts before the first B-52; grievance explains armies, not blueprints. Second, the disavowal defense: "this was not real Marxism — a millenarian peasant cult, an Asian aberration"; even fraternal regimes said so afterward. The answer assembles what the lesson has laid out: the leadership's formation, the explicit Marxist-Leninist-Maoist self-understanding in the party's internal documents, the recognizably Leninist architecture (vanguard secrecy, category enemies, confession-extraction, plan autarky), and the family resemblance of every mechanism to cases already documented in this unit — and flags the move itself for L31's full treatment: a tradition whose worst outcomes are always, retroactively, "not really it" has adopted the immunizing structure of L16, and the disavowal's timing (after defeat, by the Vietnamese Communists who installed the successor regime and by Western sympathizers in retreat) is part of the evidence. Third, the numbers debate: early figures were propaganda-inflated in both directions (the Vietnamese-installed regime's 3.3 million; early minimizers' hundreds of thousands); the course uses the mature demographic range and notes that the debate's residue — a factor well under two — brackets a fifth of a nation either way. The unit's tour now reaches the one regime from this catalogue still operating unreformed — the living end-state, where the doctrine, never revised, completed its mutation into the thing L10 said it structurally was: North Korea, next.
