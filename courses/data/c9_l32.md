Unit 4 changes the camera angle. Three units have tried the doctrine and the regimes; this one profiles the prosecution's witnesses — the thinkers who saw through Marxism earliest, deepest, or from furthest inside, each given a full lesson, because this course's patron conviction (L1) is that refutation must be learned from its masters, in their own arguments, at their best. The gallery opens with the founder of the modern case: Ludwig von Mises, the Austrian economist who, in 1920 — with the Bolsheviks not yet three years in power and half of Europe's intellectuals saluting the future — published the argument that socialism was not wicked, not premature, not badly led, but impossible: incapable in principle of the economic calculation any complex society requires; who spent the next two decades elaborating the case into the fullest critique of socialism ever written while the profession drifted the other way; who lost his country, his library, and his standing to the age he diagnosed, and taught for a quarter-century in New York as an unsalaried exile; and who was vindicated, sixteen years after his death, in 1989, with a completeness no social scientist has ever matched — certified by his opponents' own obituaries for their cause. L12 presented his argument as economics; this lesson presents the man, the argument's career, and the century-long arc from ridicule to the sentence the profession's leading socialist finally wrote: "Mises was right."

## Vienna: The Seminar and the Stand

The setting formed the mind.[^1] Mises was born in 1881 in Lemberg (today's Lviv), in the Austro-Hungarian empire whose liberal Indian summer — the Vienna of Menger's marginalism (the sister course's marginal-revolution lessons), of coffee-house seminars and a genuinely cosmopolitan intelligentsia — gave him both his school and his cause: the classical liberalism the empire's collapse would bury. He absorbed Menger's Principles as a student ("it made an economist of me," he recalled), took Böhm-Bawerk's seminar (the same Böhm-Bawerk whose demolition of Marx's value theory the reader met at L11 — Mises inherited the front line directly), and built, from 1920, the institution through which his influence ran for a generation: the Privatseminar, the fortnightly evening circle in his Chamber of Commerce office whose alumni — Hayek, Machlup, Morgenstern, Haberler, Schütz, Voegelin — read like a roster of the century's emigre social science. His day job matters to the story: never a paid professor in Vienna (the university's politics and his uncompromising liberalism saw to it), he worked as the Chamber's chief economist — a practitioner watching the Austrian hyperinflation, the planning experiments, and socialist Red Vienna from inside the data — and his first great book, The Theory of Money and Credit (1912), had already made him Europe's leading monetary economist when the revolution handed him his subject. The personal seal belongs early in the portrait because it governed everything: his motto, taken from Virgil — Tu ne cede malis, sed contra audentior ito: do not yield to evil, but proceed ever more boldly against it — was, by every witness's account, a description of practice. Mises would not trim: not for tenure, not for fashion, not when the profession's entire mainstream concluded he had lost; and the stubbornness that cost him every comfort is inseparable from the fact that he turned out to be right.

## 1920: The Impossibility Argument, and the Book That Followed

The essay came in 1920 — "Economic Calculation in the Socialist Commonwealth" — while Lenin's war communism (L20) was performing the experiment in real time, and its argument the reader holds from L12: without private ownership of the means of production there is no genuine exchange of producer goods; without exchange, no market prices for them; without prices, no common denominator reducing heterogeneous alternatives — the tractor factory's steel against the bridge's, this railway against that power plant — to comparable costs; and therefore no economic calculation: the planner, however brilliant and benevolent, stands before the boundless sea of possible combinations without a compass — production, in Mises's phrase, becomes "groping in the dark." Socialism's problem was thus not incentives alone (though L13 stands) and not knowledge alone (Hayek's deepening, next lesson) but arithmetic: abolishing the price system abolishes the possibility of knowing what anything costs, and an economy that cannot cost cannot choose rationally among the infinite uses of scarce means — it can only copy (world prices, yesterday's ratios), guess, and waste, which is precisely the crutch-and-shortage economy every planned state then ran (L12's Soviet documentation). Two years later Mises widened the front: Socialism (1922) is the tradition's most comprehensive critique — six hundred pages taking the doctrine seriously enough to examine everything: the calculation core, but also socialist positions on marriage and family, art, religion, and the dynamics of "destructionism"; its analysis of socialism as secularized chiliasm anticipates this course's L10 by a generation. The book's documented effect is part of the historical record because its most famous reader said so: Hayek — then, like most of his generation, a mild Fabian-leaning socialist — testified that Socialism "gradually but fundamentally altered the outlook" of the young idealists who read it; the book converted, among others, the man who would carry the argument to Stockholm. The profession's mid-century response completed the stage-set for the vindication arc: the "market socialists" of the 1930s (Lange, Lerner) claimed to solve calculation with trial-and-error pricing by planning boards — Lange jesting that a statue of Mises should stand in the socialist ministry's hall of honor for forcing the problem's clarification — the textbooks scored the debate for Lange, and by mid-century Mises stood, in the mainstream's settled view, as a brilliant reactionary who had lost: Samuelson's dominant textbook was still teaching, in editions into the 1980s, that the Soviet command economy could not merely function but might outgrow the West.

## Exile and Vindication

The age then demonstrated its regard for the diagnosis by destroying the diagnostician's world. Mises — a Jew, a liberal, and the Nazis' named intellectual enemy — left Vienna for Geneva in 1934; when Hitler took Austria, the Gestapo raided his apartment and seized his library and papers (which surfaced, in one of the century's ironies, in a secret Moscow archive half a century later — the anti-socialist's books held captive by the Soviet state, catalogued and preserved by the system he had refuted); in 1940 he crossed falling France by bus, rerouting around Wehrmacht columns, and landed in New York, nearly sixty, with neither position nor funds. The American years complete the portrait's moral: a visiting professorship at NYU, famously unsalaried for a quarter-century (patrons and foundations paid what the university would not), from which he rebuilt the seminar, wrote Human Action (1949) — the thousand-page treatise deriving the market order from the logic of purposeful conduct, praxeology, and re-deriving the impossibility of the order's abolition — and trained the American Austrian school (Rothbard, Kirzner — the sister course's lessons) while the profession treated him as a museum piece. He died in October 1973, sixteen years before the verdict. The verdict, when it came, was total and signed by the opposition: as the socialist economies opened their books in 1989–91, the calculation argument's predictions — systematic mispricing, innovation sterility, the copying, the shortage economy, the reform attempts that failed until they became abolitions of planning itself — stood in the wreckage exactly as drafted in 1920; and Robert Heilbroner, the profession's most literate socialist and author of the best-selling history of economics ever written, published the sentence this course has quoted before and repeats because nothing in the literature of intellectual vindication matches it: "It turns out, of course, that Mises was right." Under Lakatos's standard (L16) — the stunning novel prediction, staked in advance against the predictor's whole standing, cashed decades later in detail — the 1920 essay is the cleanest specimen the social sciences possess, and it belongs to this side of the ledger: when the course flagged the symmetry obligation at L16, this is the exhibit it promised.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The audit, per the course's own rule. First, praxeology: Mises grounded economics in a priori deduction from the axiom of action, holding the core theorems immune to empirical refutation — and the Popperian flag planted at L16 flies here: critics from Blaug onward have judged the method unfalsifiable in structure, and this course, having convicted Marxism partly on that charge, exempts no one; its resolution is to rest the Misesian case, for the course's purposes, on the empirical record the predictions compiled — the argument stands on 1989's evidence whatever one concludes about its author's epistemology, which the reader may weigh as philosophy on separate merits. Second, the lost decades were partly earned: the intransigence history vindicated also narrowed his hearing — Mises read opponents' motives harshly, conceded little that later Austrians conceded safely (Hayek's reformulation rescued the argument for the profession precisely by recasting impossibility as an information problem), and his political judgment left real blemishes the course names rather than launders: above all the notorious passage of Liberalism (1927) crediting Italian fascism's anti-Bolshevik "emergency" with a merit history would remember — a sentence surrounded in the same pages by the warning that fascism was a catastrophic long-run error and no civilization's principle, but written, and fairly hung around the tradition's neck by critics since; the course's standard is one standard, and the passage fails it. Third, the theorem's boundary: the calculation argument demolishes comprehensive planning; it does not by itself adjudicate the mixed economy, the welfare state, or regulation's margins — Mises's separate thesis that intervention slides inevitably toward socialism is a weaker, contestable claim (the sister course audits it against the postwar record), and this course keeps the strong theorem and the extension distinct, as Mises himself did not always. The audit entered, the standing remains: one man, in 1920, against his century's learned consensus, specified the mechanism of a world system's failure seventy years in advance, paid full price for the position, and collected posthumously in the currency of his enemies' concessions. His greatest student softened the claim, deepened it, and added the political theorem the century tested just as thoroughly — why planning's road runs where it runs, and why the worst get on top. Hayek, next.
