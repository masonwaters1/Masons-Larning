The third witness brought the case out of the seminar and into the living room. Milton Friedman — Brooklyn-born son of immigrant sweatshop workers, five feet three of relentless cheerful argument — was the free society's greatest twentieth-century salesman and, before that, its most consequential empirical economist: the man who took capitalism's single worst exhibit, the Great Depression, and reversed its meaning with a thousand pages of data; who predicted the 1970s' defining economic catastrophe years in advance, on stage, against the profession's consensus; whose "radical" proposals of 1962 (the volunteer army, floating exchange rates, school choice, a negative income tax) became, one by one, furniture of the world he left; and who, in 1980, did what no economist had done — argued the whole case for economic freedom on prime-time television, to tens of millions, with a pencil in his hand and a grin that his opponents found harder to answer than his regressions. Friedman matters to this course's brief on two fronts this lesson develops: his empirical method was itself an anti-ideology — claims tested against evidence, the direct antithesis of the immunized system Unit 2 dissected; and his central thesis, the connection between economic and political freedom, is the precise inversion of Marx's — where the doctrine taught that the market is the mask of unfreedom, Friedman argued, with history's full sample behind him, that the market is freedom's precondition: no society, ever, has sustained political liberty without a substantially free economy. The lesson closes, per the course's rule, with the honest audit — monetarism's operational failures and the Chile controversy included.

## The Empirical Turn: The Depression Reversed and Stagflation Called

Friedman's scientific standing rests on two feats, and both bear directly on this course.[^1] The first rewrote the twentieth century's founding economic story. The Great Depression was capitalism's Exhibit A — the collapse that "proved" market instability, licensed planning worldwide, and gave Marxist crisis theory (L18) its best decade; the postwar consensus held that markets had self-destructed and government had rescued them. A Monetary History of the United States (1963), Friedman's monument with Anna Schwartz — nearly a century of monetary data assembled with archival patience — inverted the exhibit: the Depression's engine was not market failure but the Federal Reserve's failure — the central bank, created expressly to prevent banking collapses, presided over the destruction of one-third of the money supply between 1929 and 1933, standing by through waves of bank failures it possessed the tools to stop, converting a recession into catastrophe. "The Great Contraction," they showed, was government failure at the economy's monetary core — and the reinterpretation, contested at first, became the profession's working consensus, sealed in the most gracious concession in modern economics: Ben Bernanke, then a Federal Reserve governor and the Depression's leading academic historian, at Friedman's ninetieth birthday in 2002 — "Regarding the Great Depression: you're right, we did it. We're very sorry." For this course, the stakes of that sentence are the stakes of L18's audit: the strongest single datum for "capitalism self-destructs" turns out, on the archival evidence, to indict the state's mismanagement of money — the crisis theory's crown exhibit reassigned. The second feat was prophecy under Lakatos's rules (L16). In his 1967 presidential address, Friedman argued from the natural-rate hypothesis that the reigning Phillips-curve consensus — the stable menu trading a little more inflation for a little less unemployment — was an illusion that would collapse: sustained inflation would come to be expected, and the economy would deliver rising inflation and rising unemployment together. The consensus said the combination was impossible; the 1970s delivered it by name — stagflation — and the prediction, staked in advance against the field, cashed exactly (the Nobel followed in 1976): the free-market tradition's second stunning-novel-fact exhibit, alongside Mises's 1920 (L32), entered on the ledger the course promised to keep symmetrically.

## Capitalism and Freedom: The Inversion of Marx

The political argument came in Capitalism and Freedom (1962) and matured on television. Its core thesis inverts the doctrine this course has spent three units examining. Marx taught that market freedom is formal and fraudulent — the worker "free" only to sell himself (L5) — and that real freedom waits beyond the market's abolition; Unit 3 documented what waited. Friedman's counter-thesis: economic freedom is both a component of freedom (the liberty to earn, spend, associate, and build is not beneath dignity but part of it — a point the century's refugees, fleeing toward it in rafts and over walls, ratified with their feet) and the precondition of political freedom, because dispersed economic power is what makes dissent survivable: where the state is the sole employer, publisher, landlord, and grocer, opposition is starved by administrative act (the reader has watched the mechanism: the blacklisted villages of L21, the songbun rations of L27 — Trotsky himself had stated the theorem from the other side: where the state is the only employer, "who does not obey shall not eat"); whereas the dissident in a market society can be fired by the government and hired by its critics, print on presses the state does not own, and fund heresy from private fortunes. The historical claim attached is Friedman's boldest and has survived four decades of hostile search: political freedom has appeared only in societies with substantially free-market economies — capitalism is not sufficient for liberty (unfree market societies abound, and the counterpoint weighs them) but it has proven necessary: the set of politically free, economically collectivized societies is, on history's full sample, empty. Around the thesis the book arrayed the proposals that measured the century's movement by becoming ordinary: the all-volunteer army (Friedman's Gates Commission service ended conscription in 1973 — the achievement he called his proudest; his exchange with General Westmoreland — "I don't want to command an army of mercenaries"; "Would you rather command an army of slaves?" — belongs to the era's folklore), floating exchange rates (reality since 1973), the negative income tax (living on as the EITC), school choice, and the deregulatory and licensing critiques that framed two decades of policy. And in 1980 the argument went to air: Free to Choose, ten hours of prime-time political economy plus the year's best-selling nonfiction book, carried the case to tens of millions — its emblem the pencil (Leonard Read's parable, delivered by Friedman with collector's delight): no person on earth knows how to make one; its wood, graphite, rubber, and brass assemble through the price-coordinated cooperation of thousands of strangers of every nation and creed — Hayek's knowledge argument (L33) as a thirty-second television moment; and its combative registers preserved in the Donahue exchange the internet still circulates: asked whether capitalism's engine of greed ever gave him doubt, Friedman: "Is there some society you know that doesn't run on greed?... The world runs on individuals pursuing their separate interests... In the only cases in which the masses have escaped from grinding poverty... they have had capitalism and largely free trade. If you want to know where the masses are worst off, it's exactly in the kinds of societies that depart from that."

## The Honest Counterpoint

Four entries, weighed by the course's one standard. First, monetarism's operational record: the policy doctrine (steady money-growth targets) had its trial in the early 1980s and partly failed on its own terms — velocity destabilized, the money-demand relationships broke down, and central banks worldwide abandoned monetary targeting within the decade; Friedman himself conceded late that the money-supply rule had disappointed him. The course grades precisely: the Monetary History's Depression verdict stands as consensus scholarship; the natural-rate prediction stands cashed; the operational k-percent rule is scored a failure — the empiricist's own method applied to the empiricist, as he would have insisted. Second, Chile: Friedman's 1975 Santiago visit, his letter to Pinochet urging "shock treatment" against inflation, and the "Chicago Boys" ministers trained in his department attached his name to a torturing regime, and the Nobel protests followed him for years. His defense is on the record and carries real weight — six days, no fee, the identical advice delivered in communist capitals without protest ("I gave the same lectures in China... nobody objected"), advice on inflation being neither endorsement nor employment, and his lifelong argument that the free-market reforms would corrode the dictatorship (Chile's eventual path — the 1988 plebiscite ending Pinochet by ballot, and the region's most durable democracy-plus-prosperity since — is cited by his defenders as the argument vindicated); the course's finding, one standard applied: the economic counsel was defensible and the moral register was deficient — the man who taught that concentrated power is the enemy owed the junta's victims a louder naming than he gave, and the episode stands, with Hayek's (L33), as the tradition's caution about anti-socialist charity toward power. Third, the freedom thesis's stress test: Singapore and, above all, post-1978 China — market economics under Leninist politics (L27's formula) — are pressed as counterexamples; the course states the honest score: they refute any claim that markets produce democracy on a schedule (Friedman's necessary-not-sufficient formulation never made that claim, and his own late reflections on China were sober), while leaving the necessity claim untouched — the free-and-collectivized quadrant remains empty, and China's own liberties, such as they are, cluster exactly where its markets do. Fourth, 2008 and "market fundamentalism": the crisis is regularly billed to his deregulatory legacy; the course notes the debate's actual complexity (the monetary-policy and housing-policy components of 2008 fit his framework as much as indict it) and files the full argument where it belongs — the sister course's crisis lessons — while conceding the reputational fact: the episode ended the Friedman era's policy hegemony as surely as stagflation ended Keynes's, a symmetry he, of all people, would have appreciated. The audit entered, the witness stands: the century's most effective answer to the claim that freedom and bread are rivals — demonstrated in data, predicted in advance, and argued, pencil in hand, to the largest jury any economist ever addressed. The next witness needed no television: a Marxist for a decade, trained under Friedman himself and unconverted by him, he was argued out of the doctrine by a summer's worth of government data — and became its most surgical living critic. Sowell, next, and the arc this course was built around.
