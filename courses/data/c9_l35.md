The course now reaches the witness it was built around, and it gives him three lessons because his testimony has three distinct parts: the life, the anatomy, and the vision. Thomas Sowell is the rare critic of Marxism who was a Marxist — not a flirtation but a decade, from roughly age twenty to thirty, held through poverty, through the Marines, through Harvard, and, most instructively, straight through the University of Chicago itself, where a year in Milton Friedman's own price-theory course left the conviction intact; and he is the rarest kind of ex-believer: one argued out not by a rival ideology, a conversion experience, or a disillusioning atrocity, but by a summer's worth of government data and one institutional observation — an exit so undramatic, so purely evidential, that it constitutes a small case study in everything this course teaches about how beliefs should change and usually don't. This first lesson of the arc is the biography as argument: the Harlem childhood that gave Marxism its plausibility (Sowell's account of why the doctrine attracts is the fairest in this course's sources, because he is describing himself); the Chicago year that proves proximity to refutation refutes nothing (L17's visions, exhibited in one mind); and the Labor Department summer of 1960 — the minimum-wage study, the question nobody wanted answered, and the incentive insight that ended a decade's faith in an afternoon's realization: the reformers' institutions have interests of their own.

## Gastonia to Harlem: Where the Vision Made Sense

The life must be told first because Sowell insists the doctrine's appeal was never a mystery to him.[^1] Born in 1930 in Gastonia, North Carolina, in the segregated South, to a father who died before his birth and a mother, a domestic worker, who died when he was small, he was raised by a great-aunt and carried, at eight, to Harlem in the tail of the Great Migration — a childhood of genuine poverty (the family's Harlem apartments, he has noted dryly, would later qualify as historic sites of deprivation; his memoir's texture is cold-water flats, public libraries, and fistfights), a home with no books until he found the public library, an IQ-track admission to the elite Stuyvesant High School — and then the drop: family crisis and poverty forced him out at sixteen, into years of Western Union delivery runs and machine-shop work, a stint of homelessness with a shelter's knife fights for education, and, at twenty, the draft into the Marine Corps (Korea-era; he became a photographer — the lifelong avocation dates there). The Marxism arrived in these years, and his account of it, in the memoir A Personal Odyssey and the essays, is this course's L18 written from inside: the vision seemed to explain what he could see from a Harlem window — poverty amid plenty, work that never accumulated into security, a hierarchy whose beneficiaries explained it with pieties — and it offered what L10 named: a total account, a side, and a future. He read Marx seriously (the later anatomy, next lesson, rests on command of the texts acquired as a believer), and the belief survived contact with everything that was supposed to cure it: night classes at Howard, then Harvard (admitted at twenty-eight on the GI Bill and night-school record; graduated magna cum laude in economics, 1958), a Columbia master's under George Stigler's influence — and then Chicago, where he followed Stigler, sat under Friedman himself, absorbed the price theory that made him an economist — and remained a Marxist. Sowell has retold the fact all his life because of what it establishes: "I was a Marxist when I went to the University of Chicago, and I was a Marxist when I left" — ideas do not yield to proximity, eminence, or even mastery of the counter-arguments; visions (L17) filter what arguments can reach, and the man who would write the book on that filtering is its first exhibit. What no professor accomplished, a bureaucracy would.

## The Summer of 1960: The Study Nobody Wanted

The conversion came on the clock of the United States government. In the summer of 1960, Sowell — a doctoral student and still a man of the left — took an internship at the U.S. Department of Labor, assigned to the division administering the minimum wage, and was set to work on Puerto Rico's sugar industry, where the federal minimum (extended to the island at mainland levels wildly above local productivity) coincided with collapsing employment. The question was the classic one his Chicago training framed instantly: was employment falling because the minimum wage priced workers out (the price-theory prediction), or because hurricanes had damaged the crop (the department's preferred story)? Sowell, believing the data should settle it, devised the test — comparative sugar-crop and employment figures that would separate the storm's effect from the wage floor's — and took it to his superiors with a young researcher's confidence that everyone wanted the answer. What he met instead became the hinge of his intellectual life: the department, he discovered, had no interest in the question — the minimum-wage program employed the officials, justified the budgets, and constituted the mission; evidence that it destroyed jobs among the poorest workers threatened the institution, and the institution's response to the threat was not refutation but bureaucratic evasion (the data request, he recounts, was deflected with the observation that obtaining it would be difficult, and the study rerouted into harmlessness). Walking out of that building, Sowell has written, he underwent the only conversion the record contains: not from Marxism to conservatism — that came slowly, over years of evidence — but from the assumption that had underwritten his Marxism: that government was the instrument that would do what the vision required, staffed by people whose incentive was the poor's welfare. "It forced me to realize that government agencies have their own self-interest to look after, regardless of those for whom a program has been set up," he wrote; the reformer's state, examined with the same materialist eye Marx turned on the bourgeoisie, turned out to be a set of institutions with interests — and a doctrine whose entire remedy is the state cannot survive that observation. The reader should mark the structure of the exit, because it is the course's method in miniature: Sowell did not meet a better sermon; he ran a test, watched the institution flinch from it, and followed the incentives — public choice economics (L18's convergence) lived as autobiography, a decade of vision dissolved by one honest look at who benefits.

## The Scholar the Exit Produced

What Marxism's loss produced was not an anti-Marxist pamphleteer but the most empirically omnivorous social scientist of his generation, and the arc's remaining lessons need the catalogue's shape now. The dissertation (Chicago, 1968) was, fittingly, on Say's Law — classical economics mastered at the source; the early academic career (Cornell during the 1969 armed takeover — his account of that episode seeded the education writings) gave way, from his Hoover Institution perch after 1980, to the output: the economics of knowledge (Knowledge and Decisions, 1980 — Hayek's L33 argument operationalized into a general theory of institutions, which Hayek himself saluted); the international trilogies on race, migration, and culture (Ethnic America; the Race and Culture trilogy; Black Rednecks and White Liberals) that made him the great empiricist of group differences — always with the same method, the world as the sample, the same groups compared across countries and centuries, the fashionable explanation tested against the data and usually buried; the education and policy books; Basic Economics for the general reader; and — the spine of this arc — the direct engagements: Marxism: Philosophy and Economics (1985), the fairest reconstruction and most surgical demolition in the critical literature (next lesson), and the visions books (A Conflict of Visions, already this course's L17; The Vision of the Anointed; Intellectuals and Society) that explain the believers (L37). The biography's last fact belongs to the record because it answers a slur in advance: the standard dismissal of black conservatives — that they carry water for the comfortable — collides, in Sowell's case, with a man who was poorer than his critics, longer, than nearly anyone in the American academy; who held the doctrine they romanticize for a decade of adult life; and who abandoned it over evidence about the poorest workers in the American jurisdiction — the Puerto Rican cane cutters whose jobs the enlightened had priced away and whose fate the enlightened's department declined to measure.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Three entries. First, the sample-of-one caution: a conversion story, however clean, is an anecdote — bureaucratic self-interest in one Labor Department summer does not by itself refute Marxism or minimum wages; Sowell would say so first, and his own career is the answer's second half: the insight was a hypothesis he then spent sixty years testing against the widest evidentiary base any social scientist has assembled — the arc's next lessons carry the results, and the course rests on those, not on the epiphany. Second, the minimum-wage literature moved: the modern empirical debate (Card-Krueger and after) is genuinely contested in ways the 1960 price-theory confidence was not, and the course flags it honestly — while noting the precise claim the biography requires is narrower and untouched: that the department had no interest in finding out, which is an incentive fact, not an elasticity estimate, and it is the incentive fact that ended the Marxism. Third, the hero-narrative discipline: this course, having spent Unit 3 on cults of personality, declines to build one — Sowell's late-career polemics are sharper and looser than his scholarship (his critics land hits on the columns that they cannot land on the trilogies), his dismissals of opponents' motives sometimes practice what L17 forbids, and the arc ahead will audit the books, not venerate the man. The audit stands ready; the next lesson opens the anatomy — the ex-believer's Marxism, the fairest and most lethal book ever written on the system, next.
