The arc's second lesson is about a book — the one this course has been silently leaning on since Unit 1. Marxism: Philosophy and Economics (1985) is Thomas Sowell's full-dress engagement with the system he believed for a decade, and it holds a unique position in the critical literature: reviewers across the spectrum, including Marxists, conceded that its reconstruction of Marx is among the most accurate ever written by anyone — friend or enemy — because Sowell performs, at book length, the discipline this course adopted as its charter (L1): state the system as its author understood it, from the texts, at full strength, before laying a finger on it. The book's power is the sequence: two hundred pages of scrupulous exposition — the dialectic actually explained, the value theory in its own terms, the base-superstructure architecture with its qualifications intact, the things Marx never said carefully separated from the things he did — and then the demolition, which lands with surgical finality precisely because the patient was accurately drawn: the critique cannot be deflected as strawmanning, since the strawman-detector has been satisfied by the prosecution itself. This lesson walks the anatomy: the reconstruction's key corrections (where Sowell shows that common attacks on Marx miss, and common defenses misread him); the demolition's central findings; the closing biographical verdict — Sowell's portrait of Marx the man, the harshest passage in the book, earned by the two hundred fair pages before it; and the model the whole performance offers for how intellectual enemies should be fought.

## The Reconstruction: Getting Marx Right First

Begin with what the book corrects, because Sowell's fair Marx embarrasses both camps.[^1] Against the casual critics, he establishes: Marx's theory of history is not crude economic determinism — the base "determines" the superstructure only in the sense of setting limits and pressures, with politics, law, and ideas reacting back (Engels's late letters, which Sowell deploys, said so explicitly: the economic element is determining only "in the last instance," and anyone who converts that into the-economy-decides-everything has, in Engels's own words, produced "a meaningless, abstract, senseless phrase"); the labor theory of value is not the claim that workers deserve the whole product (Marx mocked that reading in the Gotha critique — L8), nor a theory of what prices should be, but an analytical account of what exchange-value is — which is why (L11) it dies by analysis rather than by indignation; "immiseration" in the mature texts is more nearly relational than absolute; and Marx was not a utopian blueprint-drawer — his refusal to write recipes for the cookshops of the future (L8) was principled, which is precisely (Sowell will argue) where the catastrophe enters. Against the casual defenders, the same fidelity cuts deeper: Sowell documents that the dialectic is not a magic method adding content to any argument (his chapter patiently shows it functioning as a way of talking about change and internal tension — and shows Marx himself deploying it as presentation, not proof); that the "young humanist Marx vs. old scientific Marx" rescue fails on textual continuity; that Marx's system is genuinely committed to the claims that failed — the value substructure, the polarization dynamics, the revolutionary terminus — so that the modern academic's "Marxism as method, not predictions" keeps the brand by discarding the cargo (L18's motte-and-bailey, given its scholarly form). The reconstruction's cumulative effect is the book's first service to this course: it fixes the target. What Marx actually built was a majestic, internally disciplined system of historical explanation with a revolutionary conclusion — not a charity, not a sociology of compassion, not a blueprint, and not a fraud: a system, whose claims can therefore be tested. Then Sowell tests them.

## The Demolition: The System Against the World

The critique's findings the course has largely met in Unit 2 — the book is a primary source behind those lessons — so this lesson states them in Sowell's own arrangement, which adds two emphases the earlier treatments deferred. The empirical ledger first: the predictions failed as a system — not at the edges but at the load-bearing points (the advanced-country revolutions, the polarization, the crisis terminus — L4's grading, which follows Sowell's), and the value theory beneath them had already failed analytically (L11) — Sowell's exposition of the Volume III transformation retreat is among the clearest in print, and his verdict is the one this course adopted: a scientific research program that answered its refutations by immunization (L16; Sowell's chapters track the same devices). His first added emphasis is the vacuum where the economics of socialism should be: having mastered Marx's texts as a believer, Sowell documents what the reader of L8 was told — that the corpus contains no theory whatever of how a socialist economy would function: no account of coordination, incentives, calculation, or administration, only the assurance that the associated producers would manage; and he draws the connecting line this unit's architecture rests on — into that theoretical vacuum poured, of necessity, the improvisations of men with absolute power (L19–L20's war communism was Marxism's economics being written for the first time, by the Cheka), so that the gap in the books and the terror in the record are cause and effect: "the crucial question of how a socialist economy would function was never seriously addressed," and a doctrine that seizes total power on the strength of an unwritten chapter has guaranteed that the chapter will be written in blood. The second emphasis is moral bookkeeping: Sowell's chapters on Marx the man assemble, from the correspondence and the biographical record, the portrait this course sampled at L9 — the anathemas, the ethnic venom in the letters, the exploitation of Engels, the family's ruin, the lifelong pattern of treating persons as instruments and disagreement as treason — and then draws the inference with a precision that keeps it evidence rather than ad hominem: the point is not that bad character refutes theorems (it does not, and Sowell says so), but that the man's practiced relationship to opponents — excommunication, not persuasion — was inherited by the movement as method (L9's thesis; Sowell's documentation), and that the vision of the anointed (next lesson) begins in the founder's own certainty that those who differed were not mistaken but corrupt. His closing verdict on the corpus deserves quotation as the book's summary sentence and this lesson's: Marx's Capital was "a detour into a blind alley" — an enormous intellectual feat "in the service of a vision that could not survive empirical scrutiny," whose enduring achievement was not economics ("as an economist, Marx is unimportant" — the theory contributed nothing that survived) but the creation of "a whole climate of opinion": the framework of oppressor and oppressed, of society as a zero-sum crime scene, which outlived every refuted proposition and became — the arc's final lesson takes it from here — the intellectuals' default lens.

## The Model: How to Fight an Idea

Before the audit, the course extracts the book's second-order lesson, because it is the reason this arc sits at the curriculum's center. Marxism: Philosophy and Economics demonstrates, at full length, the only honorable and the only effective way to fight a great idea: total fidelity first, then total war — the sequence being not etiquette but strategy, since (L16, L17) a belief system's holders are armored precisely against caricature, and only the critic who can state their creed better than they can bypasses the armor (the book's Marxist reviewers, granting the exposition's accuracy, were maneuvered by that grant into engaging the critique). The reader should register what the performance cost and presupposed: a decade inside the system (L35), command of the full corpus in its own terms, and the willingness — rarest of all — to let the enemy's texts correct one's own side's favorite attacks. This is L1's trial method with its proof of concept, and the course states the standing instruction it derives: the reader who cannot pass Sowell's exposition chapters — who cannot explain the transformation problem, the Engels letters, and what Marx did not say — has not yet earned the demolition chapters, and the same rule binds in every direction, including toward the doctrines this course favors.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Three entries. First, the book's reception includes substantive pushback, not just tribute: Marxist economists disputed the finality of the value-theory verdict (the L11 counterpoint's Sraffian and probabilistic rescue attempts postdate and contest Sowell's closure — the course has already weighed and priced them), and historians of thought have quarreled with details of his Ricardo-to-Marx lineage; the demolition's core has, in the course's judgment, held, but "surgical" is this course's grading, not an uncontested consensus, and the reader should know the difference. Second, the fairness has limits its admirers should name: the book's Marx-the-man chapters, though documented, select for the prosecution (the warmth in the family letters, the Darwin-era intellectual generosity, the qualifications a sympathetic biographer assembles are present in the record too — L2 and L9 balanced them; Sowell's portrait is true and partial), and the closing "unimportant as an economist" verdict, while defensible on the criterion of surviving contributions, undersells the analytical stimulus Marx's questions gave the discipline (Schumpeter's tribute, L18, is the counterweight the course keeps in view). Third, the vacuum argument's edge: "Marx wrote no socialist economics, therefore the terror filled the gap" is powerful but not exhaustive — Lenin's terror had doctrinal licenses beyond the silence (L8, L19), and some heirs filled the same silence with ballots instead of Chekas (the democratic-socialist line, judged separately at Unit 6); Sowell's causal line is one strand of this course's braid, not its whole. The audit entered, the anatomy stands as the arc's centerpiece: the fairest reconstruction, the cleanest kill, and the method's demonstration. What remains is the question the book's last pages open — why the climate of opinion outlived the corpse; why the intellectuals, of all people, keep the faith — Sowell's answer, and the arc's finale: the anointed, next.
