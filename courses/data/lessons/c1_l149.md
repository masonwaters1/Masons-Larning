Begin with something you have almost certainly experienced. Imagine a friend who has been through something terrible — a loss, a failure, a stretch of life that nearly broke him — and who is now, quietly and against the odds, putting himself back together. Imagine you say to him, simply and sincerely, that you see what he is doing, that you have watched him struggle, and that he is doing a remarkable job. And imagine his eyes fill with tears. Nothing in his external situation has changed. No money has appeared, no problem has been solved, no fact about the world has been altered. All that has happened is that certain sounds left your mouth and entered his ears. And yet something real has happened to him — something has moved, shifted, been given, that was not there a moment before. Your words did not merely describe his situation; they did something to it. This unit is about that mysterious power, and it begins with the recognition, developed by twentieth-century philosophers of language, that a vast amount of what we do with words is not describing the world at all. It is acting upon it.

## Saying Is Doing

For a long time, philosophers tended to treat language chiefly as a tool for describing reality — for making statements that are true or false, that picture how things stand.[^1] The Oxford philosopher J.L. Austin noticed that this picture, however natural, misses something enormous, and he laid out the discovery in a series of lectures published as How to Do Things With Words. Consider, Austin said, a whole class of utterances that do not describe anything at all, and that therefore cannot be either true or false. When a person says "I promise," they are not reporting the existence of a promise; they are making one. When the officiant says "I now pronounce you husband and wife," those words do not describe a marriage that already exists; they bring the marriage into being. When a boss says "you're fired," a judge says "I sentence you," a person says "I forgive you," an umpire says "you're out" — in every case, the words do not report a pre-existing state of affairs. They create one. Austin called these performative utterances, and his point was revolutionary in its simplicity: with such words, saying is doing. The utterance is not a description of an act; the utterance is the act.

Once you notice this, you begin to see it everywhere, and Austin came to argue that in fact nearly all speech does things, not just the obvious performatives. Every time we speak, we are performing an act of some kind — asserting, warning, promising, requesting, ordering, thanking, apologizing, naming, blessing, cursing. Philosophers came to call this the speech act, and to distinguish its layers: there is what is literally said, there is what is done in the saying — the promising or warning or ordering — and there is the effect produced in the hearer, the persuading or frightening or consoling or, in our opening example, the moving to tears. Language turns out to be, through and through, a mode of action. We do not merely picture the world with words; we reach into it and change it.

## The Word That Makes It So

The philosopher John Searle, Austin's most important successor, organized this insight into a systematic account of the kinds of things we do with words, and one category in his scheme is especially striking for the purposes of this unit. Searle noticed a class of speech acts he called declarations — utterances that make something the case simply by declaring it to be the case. When the chairman says "the meeting is adjourned," the meeting is thereby adjourned. When the appropriate authority says "war is declared," war is declared. When a person in the right position says "I resign," "I name this ship," "you are hereby appointed" — the saying accomplishes the very thing it names. These are the purest instances of language as creative power: words that bring a new reality into existence by the sheer act of pronouncing it. There is something almost uncanny about them, a faint echo, as later lessons will explore, of a more ancient and cosmic declaration — the "let there be" by which, in the oldest of stories, a world was spoken into being.

We live, once we see this, inside a dense web of such creative and active speech, and it constitutes the very texture of our lives. Our relationships are held together by speech acts: the "I love you," the "I'm sorry," the "I forgive you," the "I do," the "I promise." Our obligations are created by them: the vow, the oath, the contract, the given word. Our identities and commitments are spoken into being and sustained by continual saying. Strip away the performative dimension of language — imagine a world in which words could only describe and never do — and you would strip away marriage, promise, forgiveness, apology, appointment, and most of what makes human life human. The friend brought to tears by a word of affirmation is not an anomaly; he is a small, vivid instance of the fundamental truth that words act, that speech reaches into reality and moves it, that to say certain things is to do certain things.

## The Ground Floor of the Unit

This is the ground floor of everything that follows in this unit. We are going to ascend from here to the highest and most cosmic claims — that the universe itself is, at its foundations, a kind of language, and that a divine Word spoke reality into being — and we are going to descend to the most intimate and practical realities, the inner voice that shapes the self and the daily words that build or ruin a child. But all of it rests on the recognition established here, by sober secular philosophy, that language is not a passive mirror held up to a finished reality. It is an active, formative power. Words do things. Saying is doing. And if that is true of the small performatives of ordinary life — the promise, the blessing, the word of forgiveness — then the question this unit pursues is how far the power reaches, and what exactly it is capable of making and unmaking, at every scale from the cosmos to the soul.

We should be careful, at the outset, not to mystify this power or to imagine that words work by magic. The performative utterance does its work not by some incantatory force resident in the syllables themselves, but within a framework of shared conventions, practices, and authority. "I now pronounce you married" transforms two lives only when spoken by someone with the standing to say it, within a community that recognizes the practice; a promise binds only because there exists a shared institution of promising; "you're out" settles the matter only on a field where the umpire's authority is accepted. The creative power of speech, in other words, is real but not free-floating; it operates within human conventions and institutions that give it its force — a point that a later lesson, on how we build whole social worlds with words, will develop into one of the most remarkable claims in modern philosophy. The word does things; but it does them, for the most part, within worlds that words themselves have already built. To the largest of those worlds — the cosmos itself, and the strange discovery that it looks, at bottom, like language — we turn next.
