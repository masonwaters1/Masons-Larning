Begin with something you have almost certainly experienced. Imagine a friend who has been through something terrible — a loss, a failure, a stretch of life that nearly broke him — and who is now, quietly and against the odds, putting himself back together. Imagine you say to him, simply and sincerely, that you have watched him struggle, that you see what he is doing, and that he is doing a remarkable job. And imagine his eyes fill with tears. Nothing in his external situation has changed. No money has appeared, no problem has been solved, no fact about the world has been altered. All that has happened is that certain sounds left your mouth and entered his ears. And yet something real has happened to him — something has moved, shifted, been given, that was not there a moment before. Your words did not merely describe his situation; they did something to it. They reached into his interior life and rearranged it. They conferred a recognition he did not have, and in receiving it he was changed.

This unit is about that mysterious power — the power of speech to make and unmake, to build and destroy, to create worlds at every scale from the cosmos to the human soul. It is a power that Jordan Peterson returns to again and again, and that the oldest religious traditions place at the very origin of things. But we are going to begin not with the cosmos or with God but with something small and near and verifiable: the ordinary discovery, made by twentieth-century philosophers of language, that a vast amount of what we do with words is not describing the world at all. It is acting upon it. Before we can understand how a divine Word might speak a universe into being, we need to see clearly that our own words are already, constantly, doing things. That is the ground floor, and everything else in this unit is built upon it.

## Saying Is Doing

For a long time philosophers tended to treat language chiefly as a tool for describing reality — for making statements that are true or false, that picture how things stand in the world.[^1] On this older view, the paradigm of a meaningful sentence is something like "the cat is on the mat" or "water boils at one hundred degrees": a description that can be checked against the facts and found to match or not match. The Oxford philosopher J.L. Austin, lecturing in the middle of the last century, noticed that this picture, however natural, misses something enormous — that a huge proportion of ordinary speech does not fit it at all, and that forcing it to fit distorts everything.

Consider, Austin said, a whole class of utterances that do not describe anything, and that therefore cannot be either true or false. When a person says "I promise," they are not reporting the pre-existing fact of a promise; they are making one. The promise did not exist until the words were spoken; the speaking is what brings it into being. When the officiant at a wedding says "I now pronounce you husband and wife," those words do not describe a marriage that already exists — they create the marriage, changing the legal, social, and personal reality of two lives in the instant of their utterance. When a boss says "you're fired," a judge says "I sentence you to five years," a christener says "I name this ship the Queen Elizabeth," a person says "I forgive you," an umpire says "you're out" — in every case, the words do not report a state of affairs that was already there. They bring a new state of affairs into existence. Austin called these performative utterances, to mark that in speaking them we perform an act rather than describe one, and his central insight was as simple as it was revolutionary: with such words, saying is doing. The utterance is not the description of an act; the utterance is the act.

Once you have seen this, you begin to notice it everywhere, and the ordinary landscape of speech looks different. A promise, a bet, a vow, a bequest, a warning, an apology, a christening, a verdict, a declaration of war, a pronouncement of marriage, an act of naming, a word of forgiveness — none of these describes a world that stands there independently. Each of them reaches into the world and alters it, creating obligations that did not exist, dissolving bonds that did, conferring names and statuses and sentences. Language, Austin came to argue, is not fundamentally a mirror. It is fundamentally a set of tools for doing things, and describing is only one of the many things it does.

## When Words Misfire

Austin pressed the point further in a way that turns out to matter a great deal for this whole unit, because it reveals both the reach and the limits of the word's power. If performative utterances cannot be true or false, he asked, how can they go wrong — for surely they can go wrong? The answer is that they succeed or fail in a different way. A performative does not fail by being false; it fails by being, in Austin's word, unhappy — by misfiring. And it misfires when the conditions that give it its force are not met.

Consider what those conditions are. If a random passerby announces "I now pronounce you husband and wife" to two strangers on the street, no marriage occurs, because the speaker lacks the authority and the setting that give the words their power. If I say "I bet you fifty pounds it rains" and you do not take the bet, no bet exists, because a bet requires uptake — the other party must receive and accept it. If I promise something I have no intention of doing, the promise is in a sense hollow, an abuse of the form. Austin catalogued these conditions with great care: there must be an accepted conventional procedure, the right persons must invoke it in the right circumstances, they must execute it correctly and completely, and, where feelings or intentions are involved, they must actually have them. Get these right and the word does its work; get them wrong and the word misfires, producing not a falsehood but a failed act — a marriage that is null, a promise that is void, a christening that does not take.

This is crucial, because it tells us something the rest of the unit will develop: the creative power of speech is real, but it is not magic. The performative does not do its work by some occult force resident in the syllables themselves, as though the sounds "I forgive you" carried a physical charge. It does its work within a framework of shared conventions, practices, institutions, and authority — a framework that gives the words their standing and their force. We will see this theme return at the largest scale when we come to the philosopher John Searle and his account of how whole societies are spoken into being. For now, hold onto the double truth: words genuinely act, and they act within worlds of convention that words themselves have built.

## The Whole of Speech Is Action

Having discovered the performative, Austin made a further and even more radical move: he came to suspect that the neat division between describing sentences and doing sentences could not be sustained, because in the end all speech does things. Even "the cat is on the mat" is an act — the act of asserting, of stating, of committing oneself to a claim and inviting the hearer to believe it. Describing is not the opposite of doing; describing is one of the things we do with words. And so Austin generalized his discovery into a general theory of what he called the speech act, and taught us to distinguish its layers.

There is, first, the sheer saying of something meaningful — the locution, the words with their sense and reference. There is, second and more importantly, what we do in saying them — the illocution: whether we are promising or warning, asserting or asking, ordering or thanking, blessing or cursing. And there is, third, the effect our saying produces in the hearer — the perlocution: the persuading, the frightening, the consoling, the convincing, the moving to tears. Return to our opening example and the three layers are all present. The locution is the plain sentence, "you are doing a remarkable job." The illocution is the act performed in saying it — an act of recognition, of affirmation, of blessing. And the perlocution is the effect — the tears, the shift in the friend's whole sense of himself. Language operates on all three levels at once, and it is a mode of action through and through. We do not merely picture reality with words; we reach into it and change it, and we change one another.

## The Kinds of Things We Do

It was Austin's most important successor, John Searle, who took this insight and organized it into a systematic account of the kinds of acts we perform in speaking — a taxonomy that has become part of the permanent furniture of the philosophy of language. There are, Searle argued, a limited number of fundamental things we do with words, and nearly all speech falls into one of a few families.

We make assertives, committing ourselves to the truth of something — describing, claiming, reporting, concluding. We issue directives, trying to get the hearer to do something — requesting, commanding, advising, begging, questioning. We undertake commissives, committing ourselves to some future course — promising, vowing, offering, guaranteeing. We perform expressives, giving voice to an inner state — thanking, apologizing, congratulating, welcoming, condoling. And, most strikingly for our purposes, we make declarations — utterances that bring about the very state of affairs they represent, simply by being successfully performed. When the chairman says "the meeting is adjourned," the meeting is thereby adjourned. When the authorized person says "war is declared," war is declared. When someone in the right position says "I resign," "you're hired," "this court is now in session," "I excommunicate you" — the saying accomplishes the very thing it names. Declarations are the purest instances of language as creative power: words that call a new reality into existence by the sheer act of pronouncing it. There is something almost uncanny about them, and a later lesson will suggest that they are a faint and distant echo of a more primordial declaration — the "let there be" by which, in the oldest of all stories, a world itself was spoken into being.

## The Web of Speech We Live In

Step back from the philosophy and look at a human life, and you will see that it is woven, through and through, of speech acts — that they are not a curious corner of language but the very fabric of our shared existence. Our relationships are constituted and sustained by them. The bond between two people is created and recreated by the "I love you," the "I'm sorry," the "I forgive you," the "I do," the "I promise," the "I'm here." Strip these performatives away — imagine a relationship in which words could only report and never do, in which no one could promise or forgive or vow — and the relationship would have no skeleton, nothing to hold it upright.

Our obligations, likewise, are spoken into being. The vow, the oath, the contract, the given word, the signature, the sworn testimony — these are the speech acts by which we bind ourselves and create the dense network of duties and expectations that make cooperative life possible. Trust itself rests on the assumption that a person's word does something, that "I will be there" creates a commitment they are now bound to honor. And our very identities are, in part, spoken into place: the name conferred at birth, the vows that make one a spouse, the oath that makes one a citizen or an officer, the "I do solemnly swear" that transforms a private person into a president. We are named, claimed, pledged, and pronounced into the roles and relationships that constitute who we are. The friend brought to tears by a word of affirmation is not an anomaly to be explained away; he is a vivid, concentrated instance of the most ordinary and most fundamental truth about human speech — that words act, that saying is doing, that to speak certain things is to make certain things so.

## The Ground Floor of the Unit

This is the foundation on which the whole unit is built, and it is worth being clear about its status. Everything established so far has been established by sober, secular, analytic philosophy — by careful attention to how ordinary language actually works, with no appeal to theology or mysticism. And what it has shown is that language is not a passive mirror held up to a finished reality but an active, formative power that reaches into the world and remakes it. Words do things. Saying is doing.

From this solid ground the unit will ascend and descend in both directions. It will ascend to the highest and most vertiginous claims — that the universe itself is, at its foundations, a kind of language; that a divine Word spoke reality into being and holds it in existence; that human beings, made in the image of that speaking God, are themselves small makers of worlds. And it will descend to the most intimate and practical realities — the inner voice that shapes the self, the daily words of a parent that build or ruin a child, the disciplined speech that can order a chaotic life. But every one of those claims rests on the modest, verifiable insight reached here: that in the smallest performatives of ordinary life — the promise, the blessing, the word of forgiveness that brings a struggling friend to tears — speech is already doing what the grand claims will attribute to it at the largest scale. It is already making worlds.

We should carry forward, too, the caution Austin taught us, because it will keep the whole unit honest. The word does things, but it does them within worlds of convention, authority, and shared acceptance — worlds that words themselves have already built and continually sustain. The power is real but not free-floating; it lives inside human practices that give it force. That very fact — that we speak into being not only promises and marriages but the entire social reality we inhabit — turns out to be one of the most remarkable claims in modern philosophy, and we will reach it near the end of the unit's descent into the human world. First, though, we must follow the creative word upward, to its largest possible scale, and confront a strange and serious suggestion: that the cosmos itself, examined at its foundations, looks less like brute and mindless stuff and more like language. To the word-based universe we now turn.
