The previous lesson established, on sober philosophical grounds, that words do things — that language is an active power reaching into human reality and reshaping it. But the ambition of this unit is larger than the human. It wants to ask whether the power of the word goes all the way down, whether there is something about the fundamental structure of reality itself that is word-like, language-like, information-shaped. This sounds, at first, like mysticism. But it is an idea pressed with unusual force by a very unmystical figure — John Lennox, an Oxford mathematician and philosopher of science — who argues that the deeper we look into the foundations of the physical world, the more it comes to resemble not brute, mindless stuff but something startlingly like language. He calls it, provocatively, a word-based universe, and his argument rests on three observations that any honest inquirer must reckon with, whatever conclusion they finally draw.

## The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics

The first observation concerns mathematics.[^1] The physicist Eugene Wigner, in a famous essay, spoke of the "unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics" in describing the physical world, and the phrase captured a genuine and deep puzzle. Mathematics is, in a sense, a language — a system of abstract symbols and structures, developed by the human mind, seemingly the purest product of thought. And yet this abstract language turns out to describe the physical universe with uncanny, almost eerie precision. The orbits of planets, the behavior of light, the structure of the atom, the curvature of spacetime — all of it yields to mathematical description, often to many decimal places, and sometimes the mathematics runs ahead of the physics, predicting particles and phenomena that are only later discovered to exist. Why should this be so? Why should the universe be written, as Galileo put it, in the language of mathematics? Why should abstract symbols map physical reality at all, let alone so exactly? The intelligibility of the cosmos — its deep, pervasive, mathematical orderliness — is the precondition of all science, and yet science itself cannot explain it. That the universe is the kind of thing that can be captured in a language is, when you stop to consider it, astonishing.

## DNA Is a Code

The second observation concerns life, and it is even more striking. When scientists cracked the structure of DNA, they discovered something that is not a metaphor: the molecule of heredity is a code. It uses a four-letter chemical alphabet, arranged in sequences that spell out precise, executable instructions — genes — that direct the construction of the proteins from which living organisms are built. This is not a loose analogy; the language of information science applies to DNA literally and productively. There is an alphabet, there is syntax, there is meaning in the sense of function, there is the storage and transmission and translation of information. The living cell is, among other things, an information-processing system of staggering sophistication, running on a digital code written in a chemical script. And this raises a question that Lennox presses hard: where does such information come from? We know, from every other instance in our experience, that codes, languages, and information trace back to minds — that alphabets have authors and messages have senders. At the very root of life, we find language and code. What does that imply about the root of life?

## Information at the Bottom

The third observation generalizes the first two. Across both physics and biology, a growing body of thought holds that information is not a secondary or derivative feature of reality but a fundamental one — as basic, in its way, as matter and energy. The physicist John Wheeler coined the slogan "it from bit," suggesting that physical existence itself might be grounded, at the deepest level, in information. Whether or not one accepts so radical a formulation, the trend of discovery has been to find, again and again, that reality at its foundations is structured, encoded, information-rich — that the universe looks less like a heap of mindless stuff and more like a vast, intricate, meaningful order. Matter, on this view, is not the bottom; information is closer to the bottom, and information is the sort of thing that pertains to language, to meaning, to mind.

## The Inference and Its Frame

From these three observations Lennox draws an inference, and it is here that the argument becomes philosophical and contested. A universe whose foundations look like this — mathematically ordered, encoded, information-rich, intelligible, language-like — fits far more naturally, he argues, with the claim that in the beginning was the Word than with the claim that in the beginning were mindless particles. Codes point to coders; languages imply authors; information suggests a source in mind. If the cosmos is, at its root, word-based, then perhaps it is so because it is the utterance of a Word — the expression of a rational Mind whose speech the fabric of reality reflects. This is the cosmic frame of the entire unit: the suggestion that the human power of speech, which the coming lessons will trace through the psyche and the society and the soul, is a small and derivative echo of a Word that structures reality itself. It connects directly to the vision of Athanasius, whom we will meet next, and his claim that a divine Logos holds the ordered cosmos in being.

## The Honest Reckoning

Here we must be scrupulously honest about what has and has not been established, for the line between the science and the inference is exactly where the real dispute lies. The three observations themselves are essentially uncontested: mathematics is indeed uncannily effective, DNA is indeed a genuine code, and information is indeed a deep and possibly fundamental feature of reality. These are not the special pleadings of apologists; they are mainstream. But the inference from these facts to a divine Word, a cosmic Mind, is a philosophical interpretation of the facts, not a scientific deduction from them, and it is resisted by many thoughtful people on serious grounds. To the effectiveness of mathematics, a naturalist may reply that we notice and retain the mathematics that works and forget the rest, or that a structured universe will of course be describable by the language of structure, so that the "unreasonable" effectiveness is less mysterious than it first appears. To the claim that DNA is a code implying a coder, the reply is that the code-and-coder inference is precisely what is in dispute: DNA is a chemical system that functions like a code, and a vast research program in the origin of life seeks to explain how such information-bearing systems could arise through unguided chemistry, without a mind — so to assume that a code requires a conscious author is, critics say, to beg the very question at issue. And the claim that information is fundamental, while a live and fascinating hypothesis, is not settled physics.

Lennox's argument, in short, belongs to a family of arguments — from the intelligibility, fine-tuning, and information-content of the universe toward a rational Author — that are serious, respectable, and pressed by capable thinkers, but that are not knockdown proofs, and that leave room for the naturalist to hold the order of the cosmos to be a brute fact requiring no further explanation. What no one can dismiss is the underlying strangeness that the argument fastens upon: that the universe is intelligible at all, that it can be read like a text, that its foundations wear the look of language and information rather than mere blind stuff. Why reality should be the kind of thing that can be spoken, encoded, and understood is a question that measurement cannot answer, and it remains genuinely, humblingly open. A word-based universe is at least a universe that invites the question of a Word. Whether it compels the answer is for each inquirer to weigh. With the cosmic frame in place, we turn to the ancient source of the idea — the oldest story of a world spoken into being.
