Athanasius, whom we met in the previous lesson, developed his vision of the cosmos-holding Logos out of a text — indeed out of two texts, standing at the openings of the two testaments of the Christian Bible, that between them form the deepest wellspring of the idea running through this entire unit. The first is the opening chapter of Genesis, where a world is spoken into being. The second is the opening of John's Gospel, which reaches back to that first creation and reveals, in the Christian reading, who the Speaker was. Between them lies a peculiar and profound feature of the Hebrew language itself, in which the very word for "word" refuses to stay safely on the page and insists on being also a thing, a deed, an event. To understand why so many thinkers have found in Scripture the charter for the creative power of speech, we must read these openings closely.

## And God Said

Open the Bible and the very first thing God does is speak.[^1] The account of creation in Genesis is structured, with deliberate and hammering repetition, around a single refrain: and God said. God said, let there be light — and there was light. God said, let there be a firmament; let the waters be gathered; let the earth bring forth; let there be lights in the heavens. Ten times, the text tells us, God speaks, and each time the speaking accomplishes what it says. This is a striking way to portray creation, and its strangeness is easy to miss through familiarity. God does not, in this account, fashion the world with his hands like a potter at the wheel or a smith at the forge; he does not struggle with pre-existing matter or wrestle rival powers into submission, as the gods do in the creation myths of the surrounding cultures. He simply speaks, and reality answers. The word is sufficient. What God says, is. The Psalmist would later distill the whole vision into a single line: by the word of the Lord the heavens were made; he spoke, and it came to be. Creation, in this vision, is the supreme performative utterance — the ultimate instance of the truth established in an earlier lesson, that saying can be doing. Here, saying is making. The world is a spoken world.

## The Word That Does Not Return Empty

To feel the full weight of this, we must understand something about the Hebrew word that stands behind the English "word" — the term dabar. In Hebrew, dabar carries a range of meaning that English separates into distinct concepts: it means word, but it also means thing, matter, affair, deed, event. The same term can denote what is said and what is done and what happens. This is not sloppiness; it reflects a whole way of understanding the relation between speech and reality. In the Hebrew imagination, a word is not a mere label pasted onto a reality that exists independently of it; a word is itself a kind of event, a dynamic and effective thing that goes forth and accomplishes. When the prophet Isaiah has God declare that his word will not return to him empty but will accomplish that which he purposes, he is expressing this deep conviction: the word is not inert information but active power, sent out to do work in the world and returning only when the work is done. The divine dabar is an agent, not a description. It is spoken, and things happen.

This Hebrew sense of the word as effective deed is the linguistic and conceptual soil from which the whole idea of "words creating worlds" grows. In a culture that instinctively felt the word to be a kind of act, a spoken reality with the power to accomplish, it was natural to portray creation itself as an act of speech, and to regard human speech — blessing and cursing, promising and prophesying, naming and swearing — as genuinely potent, genuinely capable of shaping reality for good or ill. The later lessons of this unit, on the words that raise a child and the tongue that sets a life on fire, are working out implications that were already present in this ancient conviction that a word is a deed.

## And the Word Became Flesh

Centuries after Genesis, the author of the Gospel of John reached back deliberately to that opening and wrote a new one that has echoed ever since. In the beginning, John wrote — using the very first words of Genesis — was the Word; and the Word was with God; and the Word was God. Through this Word, he continued, all things were made, and without it nothing was made that was made. John took the creative speech of Genesis, the effective divine dabar by which the heavens were made, and identified it with a person — the Logos, the Word who was with God and was God, the one through whom the whole creation came to be. And then he made the claim that would define Christianity: that this Word, the creative speech of God, the agent of all creation, became flesh and dwelt among us. The utterance by which the world was spoken took on a human face and a human voice.

This is the vision that Athanasius inherited and that runs beneath the entire unit: a universe spoken into being by a Word who is God, a Word that is not a mere sound or symbol but a living, personal, creative reality. It is the theological form of the intuition the previous lesson approached from the side of science — that reality is, at its root, word-shaped, the expression of a speaking Mind. And it sets up the anthropological claim that the coming lessons will develop: that human beings, made in the image of this speaking God, are themselves speakers who create, and that our own small words carry a faint but real echo of the Word by which all things were made. This is the source, too, of Jordan Peterson's sustained fascination with these texts — with the Logos, with the creative and ordering power of the word, with Genesis as a meditation on how speech confronts formlessness and calls forth a habitable world.

That last theme — how the creative word brings order out of chaos — is where we turn next, and it is where Peterson's distinctive reading of Genesis comes into focus. Before we do, a word of honesty is in order. The reading of Genesis offered here — creation as divine speech act, the world as spoken order — is the plain surface of the text and a reading with deep roots in the tradition, but the meaning of Genesis 1 is much debated, read variously as literal history, as a theological and liturgical poem, and as an ancient cosmology in pointed dialogue with the rival creation stories of the surrounding cultures. The philological point about dabar is real but should not be over-pressed, as though every occurrence carried the full metaphysical weight; Hebrew words, like all words, take their sense from their contexts. And the identification of the creative Word with a divine person is a specific Christian theological claim, read by John back into the older text. None of this diminishes the grandeur of the vision. It simply reminds us that we are reading a text of extraordinary depth and contested meaning, and that its power to illuminate the mystery of the creative word does not depend on settling every question about how it is to be read.
