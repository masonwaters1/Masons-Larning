We have seen that Genesis portrays creation as an act of speech — God said, and it was. But there is a detail in the text, easy to pass over, that Jordan Peterson has made the center of one of his most characteristic and influential readings, and that opens the creative word onto the whole drama of order and chaos which this course has explored from its beginning. Before the first "let there be," before the light, the text pauses to describe the state of things prior to creation, and the description is arresting. The earth, it says, was formless and void — in the Hebrew, tohu wa-bohu — and darkness lay upon the face of the deep, and the Spirit of God moved, or hovered, over the waters. Creation, in other words, does not begin from nothing in the tidy modern sense of empty space; it begins from chaos, from a formless, dark, watery deep, and the divine Word is the power that brings habitable order out of that chaos. This is the reading Peterson develops, and it turns Genesis from a remote account of physical origins into a meditation on the most fundamental task any conscious being faces — the confrontation with the formless, and the calling-forth of a world in which one can live.

## The Formless Deep

Look closely at what the creative word actually does across the six days, and a pattern emerges that the casual reader rarely notices.[^1] Overwhelmingly, the word separates, distinguishes, divides, and orders. God separates the light from the darkness; he separates the waters above from the waters below by the firmament; he gathers the waters so that the dry land appears, separating sea from land; he sets the lights in the heavens to divide the day from the night and to mark the seasons and the years; he brings forth the living creatures, each according to its kind, filling the ordered spaces with ordered and distinct life. Creation is not primarily the manufacture of stuff out of nothing; it is the imposition of form, boundary, distinction, and order upon a prior formlessness. The tohu wa-bohu, the formless watery chaos, is rendered, word by word and day by day, into a kosmos — a Greek term that means, precisely, an ordered and habitable whole, the opposite of chaos. And the instrument of this ordering is speech: God says, and the undifferentiated deep resolves into distinct, named, bounded, habitable regions where life can take hold.

Scholars of the ancient Near East recognize in this pattern a version of a widespread mythic structure, in which creation is the establishment of ordered, life-sustaining space against the ever-present threat of the chaotic waters — the same waters that, in the flood narrative, return to un-create the world when the ordering restraints are lifted, and that recede again so that dry land and life can re-emerge. This is important to register at the outset: the chaos-to-order reading of Genesis is not something Peterson conjured out of nothing. It has deep roots in serious biblical scholarship, in the study of the text's own imagery and of the ancient cosmologies that surround it. What Peterson adds is a further, psychological, and archetypal interpretation of the pattern — and it is this addition that makes his reading at once so powerful and, as we shall see, so contestable.

## Chaos and Order as Permanent Categories

For Peterson, chaos is not merely a state of the primordial past, a condition that existed once before the world was made and then was gone. It is a permanent category of experience, a face of reality that every conscious creature encounters daily. Chaos, in his usage, is the domain of the unknown, the unformed, the unexplored, the overwhelming — everything that has not yet been mapped, understood, named, or rendered navigable; the flood of raw potential and undifferentiated possibility that threatens at every moment to swamp us; the ground that opens beneath our feet when our assumptions fail and we do not know what is happening or what to do. Order is its complement and its counterweight: the known, the structured, the explored and habitable territory, the domain where our maps still work and our expectations still hold, where we can act with competence and predict the results.

And the Word — the Logos, which Peterson identifies with articulate speech, truthful consciousness, and focused attention — is the faculty that stands between the two and mediates them, the power that confronts chaos and extracts habitable order from it. God speaking the cosmos out of the formless deep is, on this reading, the archetypal image of consciousness itself confronting the unknown and, through the word, calling forth a world in which one can live. The three become a permanent structure of reality as we actually meet it: chaos, the unknown that surrounds and threatens; order, the explored world that shelters us; and the Logos, the speaking, attending consciousness that carves the second out of the first. Human beings live perpetually at the border between the two, and the whole of our experience is, in a sense, the ongoing work of the word turning chaos into order — of consciousness meeting what it does not understand and speaking it into a form it can inhabit.

## The Human Task: Naming the Chaos

The reason this reading has moved so many people is that it descends immediately from the cosmic to the intimate, and it does so through the very theme of this unit. What God does at the scale of the cosmos, Peterson argues, the human being does, in a small and derivative way, at the scale of a single life. Each of us is perpetually confronted with chaos — with problems we have not yet understood, emotions we have not yet sorted, situations that overwhelm and disorient us, the formless dread of what we cannot yet name. And our primary instrument for transforming that chaos into something habitable is articulate speech: the act of naming, distinguishing, and articulating.

Consider how concretely this works, for it is not a mere metaphor. A vague, pervasive anxiety is a kind of personal tohu wa-bohu — a formless dread with no edges, all the more terrible for being undefined, because a threat without a shape cannot be faced or fought. To put that dread into precise words — to say, at last, exactly what one fears, and why — is to begin to master it, because the naming gives it edges, renders it finite, bounded, and therefore addressable, where before it was an amorphous cloud that filled the whole sky. To articulate a problem clearly is to separate it from everything it is not, to distinguish the actual difficulty from the vast surrounding fog of dread it had gathered around itself — which is precisely to separate the light from the darkness in one's own experience, to impose form upon one's private chaos. This is the daily, practical work that Peterson witnessed constantly in his years as a clinical psychologist: the movement from an inarticulate, paralyzing distress to a spoken, specified, and therefore workable set of problems, a movement accomplished almost entirely through the patient, courageous act of putting the unspeakable into speech. The word is the tool by which we generate habitable order out of the chaos of raw experience — which is why speech is so extraordinarily powerful, and why this unit will end, many lessons from now, with the injunction to be precise in your speech. The precise word is a creative act; it is the human echo of the divine "let there be," calling a livable world out of the formless deep of one's own life.

The same principle explains one of the oldest and best-attested tools of healing: the sheer act of putting a burden into words. The sufferer carrying an unspoken grief or shame carries a formless weight, a darkness on the face of the deep, and it presses all the more heavily for never having been given shape. To speak it — to a friend, a counselor, a confessor, or even a private page — is to begin to order it, to lift it out of the undifferentiated interior murk and set it in the light where it has edges and can be looked at. This is why confession, testimony, and the talking cure recur across so many cultures and traditions, and why, as a later lesson will show, the simple discipline of writing honestly about one's troubles has measurable effects on body and mind. In each case the mechanism is the one Genesis dramatizes at the scale of the cosmos: the word goes out over the formless deep, and where there was chaos, a little habitable order comes to be.

## The Pattern Beneath the Myths

This structure, Peterson argues, is not only the deep meaning of Genesis but the deep structure beneath the great myths and stories that have gripped humanity across every culture and age. Over and over, the fundamental narrative recurs: a hero ventures out from the safety of the known world into the chaotic unknown — the dark forest, the underworld, the belly of the whale, the dragon's lair, the stormy sea — confronts there some embodiment of chaos itself, and returns, if he survives, bearing a treasure, a rescued victim, an ordering knowledge, some habitable gain wrested from the encounter. The dragon guards the gold; to win the gold one must face the dragon. The pattern is the same as Genesis writ in narrative form: order established, or re-established, or enlarged, through a courageous confrontation with chaos, mediated by the hero's competence and consciousness.

And it is, Peterson insists, the very pattern of psychological maturation. To grow up, to develop, to become capable, is precisely to venture repeatedly beyond the edge of what one already knows and can already do, to confront what one does not understand and cannot yet handle, and to return having enlarged the domain of order — having turned a piece of the overwhelming unknown into explored and masterable territory. The child who learns to read, the student who masters a difficult subject, the adult who faces a feared conversation, the sufferer who at last confronts a buried grief — each is enacting, in miniature, the archetypal descent into chaos and return with order. Genesis, on this reading, is not merely an ancient account of how the physical world came to be. It is a profound and compressed symbol of the eternal task of the conscious, speaking creature: to face the deep, and to speak an ordered world into being, again and again, across a whole life.

## The Border Between Order and Chaos

There is a further turn to Peterson's reading that gives it its ethical force and guards it against a crude misreading. The goal, he insists, is not the elimination of chaos and the achievement of total order, for pure order is its own kind of catastrophe — the frozen, tyrannical rigidity that admits nothing new, the sterile perfection that has crushed all life and possibility out of itself. Nor is the goal an embrace of chaos, which is dissolution, madness, the flood that drowns the world. The place to be, rather — the place where meaning itself is found — is precisely on the border between the two: one foot in the security of order and one foot in the vital, dangerous potential of chaos, at the exact line where the explored meets the unexplored and the known gives onto the unknown. It is there, at the meaningful edge, that life is most fully alive; there that the ordering word does its proper work, continually enlarging order without ever pretending that chaos has been finally defeated. The Logos is not the tyrant who abolishes chaos but the mediating way that walks the border, forever calling a little more habitable world out of the deep while remaining open to the renewing, terrifying potential the deep contains. This, for Peterson, is the pattern of a rightly lived life, and it is the same pattern he finds inscribed in the opening words of Genesis.

## The Honest Reckoning

This reading is illuminating, and it is important to be clear about what is solid in it and what is interpretive, because the two are easily run together, and the whole credibility of the approach depends on keeping them distinct. What is solid is that Genesis 1 genuinely does present creation as the ordering of a prior chaos; that tohu wa-bohu genuinely does denote a formless waste; and that this chaos-to-order structure genuinely does connect to well-attested patterns in ancient Near Eastern cosmology and to the text's own recurring imagery of the threatening waters. Peterson stands on firm scholarly ground when he reads the text as a drama of order established against chaos; he is not inventing this from nothing.

What is interpretive — and contested — is the further, psychological overlay: the identification of chaos with the unknown of subjective experience, of order with explored territory, and of the Logos with articulate human consciousness. This framework comes not from the text of Genesis itself but from Peterson's fusion of the text with the archetypal psychology of Carl Jung and the comparative study of myth, and it is read into the ancient words as much as out of them. Critics of Peterson's method, whom this course engages seriously elsewhere, press exactly here. The danger of the symbolic and archetypal approach, they argue, is that it is almost infinitely flexible — that a sufficiently ingenious interpreter can find nearly any psychological lesson latent in nearly any ancient story, so that the reading finally tells us more about the interpreter's own commitments than about the text or its original authors. An ancient writer meditating on divine sovereignty and cosmic order, they would say, was not encoding a modern theory of the confrontation between consciousness and the unknown, and to treat him as though he were is to project our psychology backward onto people who had entirely different concerns. Peterson's defenders reply that the archetypal reading recovers the experiential meaning the story has always carried — the reason it has gripped human beings for millennia — and that the deep structures of enduring myth do reflect deep structures of the human mind. Where the truth lies between these is a question this course takes up in earnest when it examines the limits of the mythological method directly.

For now it is enough to receive Peterson's reading for what it is: a genuinely powerful and, at its best, genuinely illuminating meditation on the creative, order-making power of the word — grounded in real features of the text, extended by an interpretive framework that is itself open to serious debate. Whichever way one finally weighs the method, the underlying insight it fastens upon is one this unit will keep returning to, and one that stands independent of any particular theory of myth: that to name the chaos is to begin to order it, and that in this the human word is a faint but real image of the Word that spoke the cosmos into being. To that image — the human being as a speaking maker in the likeness of the Maker — we now turn.
