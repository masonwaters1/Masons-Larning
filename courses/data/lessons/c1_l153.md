We have seen that Genesis portrays creation as an act of speech — God said, and it was. But there is a detail in the text, easy to pass over, that Jordan Peterson has made the center of one of his most characteristic and influential readings, and that opens the creative word onto the whole drama of order and chaos which this course has explored from its beginning. Before the first "let there be," before the light, the text pauses to describe the state of things prior to creation, and the description is arresting. The earth, it says, was formless and void — in the Hebrew, tohu wa-bohu — and darkness lay upon the face of the deep, and the Spirit of God moved over the waters. Creation, in other words, does not begin from nothing in the tidy modern sense; it begins from chaos, from a formless, dark, watery deep, and the divine Word is the power that brings habitable order out of that chaos. This is the reading that Peterson develops, and it turns Genesis into a meditation on the most fundamental task a conscious being faces.

## The Formless Deep

Look closely at what the creative word actually does across the six days.[^1] Overwhelmingly, it separates and distinguishes and orders. God separates the light from the darkness, the waters above from the waters below, the dry land from the sea; he sets the lights in the heavens to divide day from night and to mark the seasons; he brings forth distinct kinds, each after its kind, filling the ordered spaces with ordered life. Creation is not primarily the manufacture of stuff out of nothing; it is the imposition of form, distinction, boundary, and order upon a prior formlessness. The tohu wa-bohu, the formless watery chaos, is rendered into a cosmos — a word that means, precisely, an ordered and habitable whole. And the instrument of this ordering is the word: God speaks, and the undifferentiated deep resolves into distinct, named, habitable regions. Scholars of the ancient Near East recognize in this a version of a widespread mythic pattern, in which creation is the establishment of ordered, life-sustaining structure against the ever-present threat of the chaotic waters. Peterson did not invent the chaos-to-order reading; it has deep roots in the scholarship of ancient cosmology.

What Peterson adds is a psychological and archetypal interpretation of the pattern, and this is where his reading becomes distinctive and powerful. Chaos, for Peterson, is not merely a state of the primordial past; it is a permanent category of experience. It is the domain of the unknown, the unformed, the overwhelming — everything that has not yet been mapped, understood, or rendered navigable, the flood of raw potential and undifferentiated possibility that threatens to swamp us. Order is its complement: the known, the structured, the explored and habitable territory in which we can function. And the Word — the Logos, which Peterson identifies with articulate speech, truthful consciousness, focused attention — is the faculty that stands between them, the power that confronts chaos and extracts habitable order from it. God speaking the cosmos out of the deep is, on this reading, the archetypal image of consciousness confronting the unknown and, through the word, calling forth a world in which one can live.

## Naming the Chaos

The reason this reading has moved so many people is that it descends immediately from the cosmic to the personal, and it does so through the theme of this entire unit. What God does at the scale of the cosmos, Peterson argues, the human being does, in a small and derivative way, at the scale of a life. Each of us is perpetually confronted with chaos — with problems we have not yet understood, emotions we have not yet sorted, situations that overwhelm and disorient us, the formless dread of what we cannot yet name. And our primary instrument for transforming that chaos into something habitable is articulate speech: the act of naming, distinguishing, and articulating. To put a vague dread into precise words is to begin to master it. To articulate a problem clearly is to render it finite, bounded, and therefore addressable, where before it was an amorphous cloud of anxiety. To name what is happening to you is to separate the light from the darkness in your own experience, to impose form upon your personal tohu wa-bohu. The word is the tool by which we generate habitable order out of the chaos of raw experience — and this is why speech is so extraordinarily powerful, and why the unit will end, many lessons from now, with the injunction to be precise in your speech. The precise word is a creative act; it is the human echo of the divine "let there be," calling a livable world out of the formless deep.

This is also, Peterson argues, the deep structure beneath the great myths and stories that have gripped humanity — the pattern in which a hero ventures into the chaotic unknown, confronts the dragon or the flood or the abyss, and returns having wrested from it some treasure, some ordering knowledge, some habitable gain. It is the pattern of psychological maturation itself: the repeated confrontation with what we do not understand and the repeated extraction, through attention and articulation, of order we can live within. Genesis, on this reading, is not merely an ancient account of how the physical world came to be; it is a profound and compressed symbol of the eternal task of the conscious, speaking creature — to face the deep, and to speak an ordered world into being.

## The Honest Reckoning

This reading is illuminating, and it is important to be clear about what is solid in it and what is interpretive, because the two are easily run together. What is solid is that Genesis 1 genuinely does present creation as the ordering of a prior chaos, that tohu wa-bohu genuinely does denote a formless waste, and that this chaos-to-order structure genuinely does connect to well-attested patterns in ancient Near Eastern cosmology. Peterson is on firm scholarly ground when he reads the text as a drama of order established against chaos; he is not conjuring this out of nothing. What is interpretive — and contested — is the further, psychological overlay: the identification of chaos with the unknown of subjective experience, of order with explored territory, and of the Logos with articulate human consciousness. This framework comes not from the text of Genesis but from Peterson's fusion of the text with the archetypal psychology of Carl Jung and the mythological comparativism he draws upon, and it is read into the ancient words rather than straightforwardly out of them.

Critics of Peterson's method, whom this course engages seriously elsewhere, press exactly here. The danger of the symbolic and archetypal approach, they argue, is that it is almost infinitely flexible — that a sufficiently determined interpreter can find nearly any psychological lesson latent in nearly any ancient story, so that the reading tells us more about the interpreter's prior commitments than about the text. An ancient author meditating on divine kingship and cosmic order, they would say, was not encoding a theory of the confrontation between consciousness and the unknown, and to treat him as though he were is to project a modern psychology backward onto a text that had other concerns. Peterson's defenders reply that the archetypal reading recovers the experiential meaning the story has always carried, the reason it has gripped human beings for millennia, and that the deep structures of myth do reflect deep structures of the mind. Where the truth lies between these is a question this course takes up in earnest when it examines the limits of the mythological method directly. For now, it is enough to receive Peterson's reading for what it is: a genuinely powerful and, at its best, genuinely illuminating meditation on the creative, order-making power of the word — grounded in real features of the text, extended by an interpretive framework that is itself open to serious debate. Whichever way one finally weighs it, the underlying insight it fastens upon is one this unit will keep returning to: that to name the chaos is to begin to order it, and that in this the human word is a faint image of the Word that spoke the cosmos into being. To that image — the human being as a speaking maker in the likeness of the Maker — we now turn.
