If the previous lessons are right that the cosmos is spoken into being by a creative Word, then a question presses itself upon us, and it is the hinge on which this entire unit turns from the divine to the human. Genesis says that God, having spoken the world into being, made one creature in his own image and likeness. What does it mean to bear the image of a God whose defining act, in the opening of the story, is to create by speech? The answer this unit proposes — and it is an answer that a great imaginative writer spent his life exploring — is that to be made in the image of the speaking God is, among other things, to be a speaker who creates, a maker of worlds with words. We are, in J.R.R. Tolkien's beautiful and precise term, sub-creators.

## The Image of the Maker

The doctrine that human beings are made in the image of God has been interpreted in many ways across the long history of reflection upon it.[^1] Some have located the image in human rationality, some in the human capacity for moral choice, some in our dominion over the created order, some in our capacity for relationship and love. All of these have merit, and they are not exclusive. But this unit fastens on a dimension that the placement of the image within the Genesis narrative makes hard to ignore: the God in whose image we are made has just been revealed, in the preceding verses, as one who creates by speaking, who brings order out of chaos through the word, who calls light and life and world into being by utterance. To be made in the image of this God is, at least in part, to share, in a small and creaturely way, in that creative, speaking, world-making capacity. We are the creatures who speak, and in speaking, make. The human word is stamped with the image of the divine Word.

This is not a fanciful over-reading; it fits the whole shape of human life. Alone among the creatures, so far as we know, the human being possesses language of unlimited generativity — the power to say what has never been said, to describe what does not yet exist, to call into being through words new plans, new institutions, new stories, new worlds. We do not merely inhabit a given reality; we speak realities into being — as the coming lesson on social worlds will show in rigorous detail, and as the whole prior history of promises and vows and declarations has already suggested. The creative power of speech, which this unit has traced from the cosmos downward, reaches its human expression in this: that the image-bearer is a maker, and that the maker's chief instrument is the word.

## Sub-Creation

No one has explored this idea more deeply, or more beautifully, than J.R.R. Tolkien, who was not only the author of a vast invented mythology but a serious thinker about what it means to invent one. In his essay on fairy-stories and in a poem addressed to his friend C.S. Lewis, Tolkien developed the concept for which he is justly celebrated: sub-creation. The human being, he argued, made in the image of the Creator, is himself a maker of worlds — a sub-creator, whose imaginative creations are a genuine, if derivative and dependent, exercise of the world-making power that belongs supremely and originally to God. When a storyteller builds a coherent secondary world — a Middle-earth, a Narnia, or the humbler imagined worlds of any novel or tale — and populates it with life and law and history so that a reader can enter and believe and be moved, he is doing, in his small human way, something that images the divine act of creation. We make, Tolkien wrote in a line that distills the whole vision, still by the law in which we're made. The storyteller who speaks a world into being is exercising the very capacity for which he was fashioned, the sub-creative gift that is part of what it means to bear the image of the Word.

For Tolkien this was not escapism, and not a kind of licensed lying, as some had charged against the making of fantasy. It was, on the contrary, one of the highest and most proper exercises of the human vocation — a participation, however faint, in the creativity of God, and a way of glorifying the Creator by doing in miniature what he does in majesty. The writer sits down before the blank page, that small formless void, that private tohu wa-bohu, and speaks into it, and out of the emptiness a world takes ordered shape — characters who did not exist, places that were never seen, events that never happened and yet move us to tears or to courage. Fiction creates worlds. This is the imaginative form of the truth this unit has been pursuing: that words make worlds, and that the human maker, in speaking his stories, echoes the Maker who spoke the world.

## The Dignity and the Danger

To see human speech this way — as a creaturely participation in the divine creativity, the exercise of the image of the Word — is to grant it an extraordinary dignity, and with the dignity comes a corresponding weight of responsibility. If our words genuinely make worlds, then we are answerable for the worlds we make. The sub-creative power can be exercised to call into being what is habitable, beautiful, and true — the encouraging word that builds a person up, the honest account that orders a confusion, the story that ennobles and consoles. Or it can be turned to speak worlds that are ugly, false, and destructive — the lie that distorts reality, the contempt that tears a soul down, the propaganda that builds a nightmare. The image-bearer's word is powerful for creation and for destruction, and the later lessons of this unit, on the tongue as a fire and on the corruption of language, will trace the dark side of exactly the power celebrated here. To be a sub-creator is to hold, in one's speech, a real portion of the world-making power — and therefore to bear a real portion of the responsibility for what one's words bring into being.

A note of honesty belongs here, lest the vision be mistaken for a proof. That the image of God consists, even partly, in creative speech is one interpretation among the several the tradition has offered, not a settled doctrine; Tolkien's sub-creation is a theological aesthetic, a luminous idea rather than a demonstrated fact. And one need not share Tolkien's faith to recognize the phenomenon he named: the world-making power of storytelling, the way an author calls into being imagined realities that shape and move real people, is affirmed by secular writers and critics as readily as by religious ones. What the theological frame adds is not the fact of the power — that is plain to anyone who has been changed by a book — but an account of its source and its meaning: that we make worlds with words because we are made in the image of the One who did. Whether one accepts that account or not, the power itself is undeniable, and its most rigorous secular demonstration is the subject of the next lesson, where the philosopher John Searle shows that we speak into being not only the imagined worlds of fiction but the very social world we all inhabit — money and marriage and nations and law — every hour of our waking lives.
