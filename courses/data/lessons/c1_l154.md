If the previous lessons are right that the cosmos is spoken into being by a creative Word, then a question presses itself upon us, and it is the hinge on which this entire unit turns from the divine to the human. Genesis says that God, having spoken the world into being, made one creature — and only one — in his own image and likeness. What does it mean to bear the image of a God whose defining act, in the opening of the story, is to create by speech? The answer this unit proposes, and it is an answer that a great imaginative writer spent his life exploring, is that to be made in the image of the speaking God is, among other things, to be a speaker who creates — a maker of worlds with words. We are, in J.R.R. Tolkien's beautiful and precise coinage, sub-creators: small makers fashioned in the likeness of the great Maker, exercising in miniature the very power by which we ourselves were made.

## The Image of the Maker

The doctrine that human beings are made in the image of God has been interpreted in many ways across the long history of reflection upon it, and the interpretations are not mutually exclusive.[^1] Some have located the image in human rationality — our capacity to reason, which mirrors the divine Reason. Some have located it in our moral freedom, the capacity to choose between good and evil. Some have found it in our dominion, the delegated rule over creation that immediately follows the granting of the image in the text. Some have found it in our capacity for relationship and love, reflecting a God who is himself, in the Christian understanding, a communion of persons. All of these have real merit. But this unit fastens on a dimension that the placement of the image within the Genesis narrative makes hard to ignore: the God in whose image we are made has just been revealed, in the immediately preceding verses, as one who creates by speaking, who brings order out of chaos through the word, who calls light and life and world into being by utterance. To be made in the image of this God is, at least in part, to share, in a small and creaturely way, in that creative, speaking, world-making capacity.

And this fits the whole strange shape of human life, for it points to something genuinely unique about our species. Alone among the creatures, so far as we can tell, the human being possesses language of unlimited generativity — the capacity, as the linguists put it, to produce and understand an infinite variety of new sentences from finite means, to say what has never been said before, to describe what does not yet exist, to speak of the past and the future and the possible and the counterfactual, to call into being through words new plans, new institutions, new stories, new worlds. No other animal composes a symphony, drafts a constitution, or tells a tale of a world that never was. And Scripture marks this creative dimension of human speech from the very first: the earliest task given to the first human being is an act of language that shares in God's own ordering work — the naming of the animals, the imposition of order upon the living world by the giving of names, the human word taking up and continuing the divine word's work of distinction and order. We do not merely inhabit a given reality; we speak realities into being. The creative power of speech, which this unit has traced from the cosmos downward, reaches its human expression in this: that the image-bearer is a maker, and that the maker's chief instrument is the word.

## Sub-Creation

No one has explored this idea more deeply, or more beautifully, than Tolkien, who was not only the author of a vast invented mythology but a serious and self-conscious thinker about what it means to invent one. In a landmark essay on fairy-stories, and in a poem addressed to his friend C.S. Lewis, he developed the concept for which he is justly celebrated: sub-creation. The human being, he argued, made in the image of the Creator, is himself a maker of worlds — a sub-creator, whose imaginative creations are a genuine, if derivative and dependent, exercise of the world-making power that belongs supremely and originally to God. When a storyteller builds a coherent secondary world and populates it with life and law and history so that others can enter and believe and be moved, he is doing, in his small human way, something that images the divine act of creation. We make, Tolkien wrote in a line that distills the whole vision, still by the law in which we're made. The storyteller who speaks a world into being is exercising the very capacity for which he was fashioned — the sub-creative gift that is part of what it means to bear the image of the Word.

There is a moving piece of history behind this idea, for it was the instrument of one of the most consequential conversions of the twentieth century. Tolkien's friend Lewis, then an atheist, had objected that myths, however beautiful, were finally lies — breathing a lie through silver. Tolkien answered, in a long night's conversation that Lewis never forgot, that myths are not lies but the opposite: that human beings, making stories of redemption and sacrifice and the triumph of light, are not fabricating falsehoods but reflecting, however brokenly, a truth built into them by their Maker — splinters of the true light. And the Christian claim, Tolkien argued, was that in the Gospel this pattern of all the myths had, uniquely, entered history and become fact: the true myth, the one that really happened, toward which all the invented myths had been unconsciously reaching. Lewis was undone by the argument, and it moved him toward the faith he would spend the rest of his life commending. For our purposes, the episode reveals the seriousness with which Tolkien held his idea: sub-creation was not a charming literary conceit but a claim about the meaning of the human imaginative act — that when we make worlds with words, we are doing something sacred, echoing the Maker in whose image we are made.

## The Secondary World

Tolkien thought carefully about how this world-making actually works, and his analysis illuminates the whole unit's theme. A good story, he argued, does not merely ask the reader to suspend disbelief grudgingly; it creates a secondary world so internally consistent, so richly and coherently ordered, that the mind enters it and grants it a "secondary belief" — accepting, for as long as one is within it, that within that world these things are simply true. The maker of such a world is a kind of god of a small creation, responsible for its laws and its coherence, and the more real and consistent the world, the more powerfully it can move and change the reader who enters it. This is not idle; anyone who has wept at the death of a character who never existed, or been made braver by a tale of imagined courage, or had their sense of good and evil permanently shaped by a story, knows that these spoken worlds reach out of their pages and rearrange the real souls of real people.

The proof is in the effect Tolkien's own sub-creation has had. Millions of readers who will never study ethics in a lecture hall learned something enduring about it from a spoken world that does not exist — learned that the small and the humble may carry a burden the great and powerful cannot; that mercy shown to a wretched creature may prove, in the end, the hinge on which everything turns; that the refusal of power can be a form of strength; that courage is going on when there seems no hope. These convictions entered and reshaped real moral imaginations by way of an invented world of invented peoples speaking invented tongues — a world with no more physical existence than a dream, and yet powerful enough to form the values of generations. That is the sub-creative power at its height: not escapism from reality but a lever upon it, a made world that reaches back into the unmade one and changes the people who dwell in it for a while. The maker's word, even when it makes a world that never was, does genuine work in the world that is.

Tolkien practiced what he theorized with an almost unimaginable thoroughness. He did not merely write stories set in Middle-earth; he built the world beneath them — its geography, its ages of history, its peoples, its cosmology, and, most extraordinarily, its languages. He was a philologist by profession, a lover and scholar of ancient tongues, and he invented whole languages, with their own sound and grammar and history, before and beneath the tales, so that his imagined world rested on the deep foundation of imagined words. Here the theme of the unit reaches a kind of apex: a sub-creator literally speaking a world into being out of invented language, building a secondary reality on a substructure of words the way, in the theology, the primary reality rests upon the Word. The writer sits down before the blank page — that small formless void, that private tohu wa-bohu — and speaks into it, and out of the emptiness a world takes ordered shape: characters who did not exist, places that were never seen, events that never happened and yet move us to tears or to courage. Fiction creates worlds. This is the imaginative form of the truth this unit has been pursuing, that words make worlds, and the human maker, in speaking his stories, echoes the Maker who spoke the world.

## Every Speaker a Sub-Creator

We must not confine this power to novelists and poets, as though world-making with words were the special preserve of professional artists. The truth Tolkien named applies, in a quieter key, to every human being, for we are all, in speaking, making. We speak our relationships into being and sustain them by speech, as an earlier lesson showed. We speak our plans and projects into existence, calling futures into being with words before we build them with hands. We speak our shared social reality into being — the money and marriages and nations and laws that the next lesson will show are, quite literally, spoken structures upheld by collective utterance. And we speak our very selves into being, narrating our identities, telling ourselves and others the stories of who we are. Every human being is, in this sense, a sub-creator, a maker of worlds with words, because every human being bears the image of the Word. The professional storyteller merely does consciously and with great skill what all of us do constantly and unawares: call habitable realities out of the formless possible by the power of speech.

Consider how literally this holds even in the smallest domestic settings. A parent playing with a child speaks a world into being — the floor becomes a sea, the cushions an island, the ordinary room a place of dragons and treasure — and the child enters that spoken world and lives in it as though it were real, because for the space of the game it is. A group of friends builds, over years, a shared world of private references, inside jokes, and remembered stories, a whole small culture spoken into existence and inhabited by no one else. A community tells itself the story of who it is and where it came from, and lives inside that story, oriented and bound by it. None of these worlds has any physical existence; each is spoken into being and held up by continued speech, and each is genuinely a world — a place the mind can dwell, that shapes what its inhabitants feel and do. The sub-creative power is not reserved for genius; it is exercised, hour by hour, wherever human beings open their mouths and call the not-yet-real into shared existence. We are, all of us, always, speaking worlds.

## The Dignity and the Danger

To see human speech this way — as a creaturely participation in the divine creativity, the exercise of the image of the Word — is to grant it an extraordinary dignity, and with the dignity comes a corresponding and sobering weight of responsibility. If our words genuinely make worlds, then we are answerable for the worlds we make. The sub-creative power can be exercised to call into being what is habitable, beautiful, and true — the encouraging word that builds a person up and brings the struggling friend to tears, the honest account that orders a confusion into clarity, the story that ennobles and consoles and enlarges the soul that receives it. Or it can be turned to speak worlds that are ugly, false, and destructive — the lie that distorts reality and traps its hearers in a falsehood, the contempt that tears a soul down, the propaganda that builds a shared nightmare and calls a whole people into it. The later lessons of this unit, on the tongue as a fire and on the corruption of language, will trace in detail the dark side of exactly the power celebrated here. To be a sub-creator is to hold, in one's speech, a real portion of the world-making power — and therefore to bear a real portion of the responsibility for what one's words bring into being, in the world and in the people who hear them.

A note of honesty belongs here, lest the vision be mistaken for a proof. That the image of God consists, even partly, in creative speech is one interpretation among the several the tradition has offered, not a settled doctrine, and Tolkien's sub-creation is a theological aesthetic — a luminous and fruitful idea rather than a demonstrated fact. And one need not share Tolkien's Christian faith to recognize the phenomenon he named: the world-making power of storytelling, the way an author calls into being imagined realities that shape and move real people, is affirmed by secular writers and critics as readily as by religious ones, and stands on its own as a fact about human language whatever one makes of its ultimate source. What the theological frame adds is not the fact of the power — that is plain to anyone who has ever been changed by a book — but an account of its origin and its meaning: that we make worlds with words because we are made in the image of the One who did, and that our small creative speech is therefore charged with a dignity and a responsibility that reach beyond ourselves. Whether one accepts that account or not, the power itself is undeniable, and its most rigorous secular demonstration is the subject of the next lesson, where the philosopher John Searle shows that we speak into being not only the imagined worlds of fiction but the very social world we all inhabit — money and marriage and nations and law — every waking hour of our lives.
