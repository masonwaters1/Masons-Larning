The previous lesson spoke of the human being as a sub-creator who makes worlds with words, and illustrated the claim with the imagined worlds of fiction. But there is a way in which we speak worlds into being that is not imaginary at all — a way so pervasive and so fundamental that we swim in it unawares, the way a fish is unaware of water. We do not only tell stories; we speak into existence the entire social reality within which we conduct our lives. Money, marriage, governments, borders, laws, property, universities, corporations, nations, the very dollar in your pocket and the office your employer holds — none of these exists in the way mountains and molecules exist. They exist because we say they do, and they persist only as long as we keep saying so. The philosopher who has mapped this astonishing fact with the greatest rigor is John Searle, the same analyst of speech acts we met at the opening of this unit, and his account is the secular, hard-edged confirmation of everything the unit has been claiming: that words, quite literally, make worlds.

## Brute Facts and Institutional Facts

Searle begins from a distinction that, once grasped, permanently changes how one sees the human world.[^1] There are, he observes, two fundamentally different kinds of facts. On one side are brute facts — facts that would be true whether or not any human being existed to think about them. That a certain mountain is higher than another, that the sun has a certain mass, that hydrogen has one proton, that water is made of hydrogen and oxygen — these are brute facts, features of a mind-independent physical reality that was here before us and would remain if we vanished. On the other side are what Searle calls institutional facts, and these are of a wholly different character, for they exist only because human beings collectively believe, accept, and treat them as existing. That a certain piece of printed paper is a twenty-dollar bill, that two particular people are married, that a certain line running through a desert is an international border, that a particular person is the president, that a certain rectangle of grass and soil is somebody's private property — these are institutional facts. They are perfectly real, and they govern our lives with enormous, often decisive force; wars are fought and lives are spent over them. But they have no existence in the physical nature of the things themselves.

Here is the crux, and its strangeness deserves a moment's genuine astonishment rather than the shrug of familiarity. Take a twenty-dollar bill and examine it with every instrument physics and chemistry can provide. You will find paper of a certain composition, ink of a certain chemistry, fibers, threads, patterns of pigment. You will never, with any instrument, find its being-money — because its being-money is not a physical property of the object at all. Two bills may be physically almost identical, and one be legal tender and the other worthless counterfeit, the difference lying not in their physics but entirely in a web of collective human acceptance. The whole vast structure of institutional reality — the economy, the government, the legal system, the university, the family as a legal and social entity — rests on nothing physical. It rests on collective agreement, and collective agreement is constituted and sustained by language.

## X Counts as Y

Searle gives the mechanism a precise formula, and it connects directly to the speech acts we examined at the opening of this unit, closing a circle. Social reality is created, he argues, when we collectively impose what he calls a status function through a declaration, according to a simple and immensely powerful pattern: X counts as Y in context C. This piece of specially printed paper (X) counts as legal tender (Y) in the United States (C). This form of words spoken by these two people before this official (X) counts as a marriage (Y) in this jurisdiction (C). This person, having been through this procedure, counts as the president. This line counts as the boundary of the nation. This building counts as a courthouse, this rectangle of grass counts as your property, this sequence of marks counts as a valid signature.

In every case we take something — an object, a person, a place, a form of words — and impose upon it a status and a function that it does not possess by its physical nature, and it acquires that status purely through our collective acceptance of the imposition, an acceptance that lives in and is carried by our words. These are declarations in the precise sense Searle established years earlier: the speech acts that make something so by declaring it so. But they are now raised to the level of whole societies, and sustained not by a single momentary utterance but by the continuous, collective speech and acceptance of millions of people over time. A currency is not created and then left standing on its own; it is a shared declaration that a society keeps repeating with every transaction, every price, every act of trust. A nation is a reality held in being by the continual collective saying-so of its people and of the world that recognizes it. The entire edifice of civilization, Searle shows, is a structure of status functions imposed and maintained by language — spoken into being, and kept in being, by words.

## Civilization as a Spoken Structure

Sit with the magnitude of this, because it is easy to hear and hard to truly absorb. It means that the world most of us inhabit for the overwhelming majority of our waking hours — the world of work and money and law and marriage and citizenship and property and rank — is not a brute physical given but a collective human creation, spoken into existence and upheld, moment by moment, by our shared speech and agreement. When you hand over a card to buy coffee, you are participating in an immense, invisible, ongoing act of collective declaration: that these numbers count as money, that this transaction counts as valid, that this debt counts as settled. When a judge pronounces a sentence, a legislature passes a law, a couple exchanges vows, an official certifies a result, a nation recognizes another nation, they are all doing, at the scale of institutions, precisely what the opening lesson described at the scale of a promise: bringing a new reality into being by speaking, and sustaining it by continuing to speak.

Your Genesis intuition — that a world can be called into being by an utterance — turns out, then, to be not a poetic fancy confined to theology but a literal and rigorous description of how human society actually works, established by one of the most unsentimental analytic philosophers of the age, a man who is himself no theologian and intends no theology whatever. The image of the speaking God, this unit has argued, is a being who makes worlds with words. Searle, without meaning to and without believing any of the metaphysics, has shown us doing exactly that — all day, every day, on a civilizational scale. We are the creatures who speak reality into being, and the proof is not in the temple but in the marketplace, the courtroom, and the register office.

## The Institution Beneath the Institutions

There is a further depth to Searle's analysis that is easy to miss and worth pausing on, because it reveals language to be not merely one world-building tool among others but the ground floor beneath the entire structure. Notice that every institutional fact we have considered — money, marriage, property, government — presupposes language, while language does not presuppose any of them. You cannot have money without language, because money requires the collective imposition of a status ("this counts as legal tender"), and a status can only be imposed, represented, and sustained symbolically, which is to say linguistically. The same holds for property, for law, for nationhood, for every institution: each depends on symbols that mean, on a shared capacity to represent one thing as counting as another. But language itself is different. A community could have language and yet lack money, marriage as an institution, or private property; there are, and have been, human groups without any of these. What there has never been is a human society with institutions but without language, because language is the precondition of institutions as such.

This makes language, in Searle's account, the fundamental social institution — the one that makes all the others possible, the master status function underlying every particular status function. To have words at all is already to have the capacity to impose meaning on the meaningless, to make one thing stand for and count as another, and it is this very capacity, exercised collectively and cumulatively, that builds the entire edifice of civilization on top of the brute physical world. The human power of speech is therefore not just useful within an already-existing social world; it is the thing that calls the social world into existence in the first place. Strip a people of every institution but leave them language, and they can rebuild everything; strip them of language, and no institution is possible at all. The word is not merely a resident of the human world. It is its foundation.

## The Fragility of Spoken Worlds

Because social reality is constituted and held up by collective speech and acceptance, it has a feature that brute reality conspicuously lacks: it can dissolve when the speaking stops. A mountain does not cease to exist if people stop believing in it; the sun would burn on in an empty universe. But a currency can collapse in a matter of days when enough people stop believing it has value and stop treating it as money — the paper is suddenly just paper again, the status function evaporating the instant the collective declaration ceases. A government can fall not only to armies but to the simple, contagious withdrawal of recognition, when the collective acceptance that sustained its authority quietly evaporates and the soldiers and citizens stop treating its commands as commands. A marriage ends, in part, through the very speech acts that declare it over. The worlds we build with words are exactly as durable, and exactly as fragile, as the collective speech that sustains them — which is why moments of social collapse have a peculiar, vertiginous quality, the sensation of a solid world revealing itself to have been, all along, a shared agreement that can be un-agreed.

History supplies vivid illustrations of the sensation. A bank is perfectly solvent right up until enough depositors say, and act as though, it is not; the run that follows is a collective withdrawal of the declaration "this institution is sound," and it can destroy in an afternoon a thing that looked as permanent as stone. A regime can command armies and prisons and appear utterly immovable, and then, in the space of days, dissolve — not because it was defeated in battle but because the shared acceptance that made its authority real quietly drained away, and one morning the commands were simply no longer treated as commands. Onlookers describe such moments with the same word again and again: unreal. And in a precise sense they are witnessing the unreal made visible — watching an institutional fact, which existed only in and through collective speech and acceptance, cease to exist the moment the speaking and the accepting stopped. What had felt like the bedrock of the world showed itself to have been a spoken world all along.

This is the source of both the immense power and the immense responsibility that attach to public speech, and it illuminates one of the examples that set this whole unit in motion. When politicians and leaders speak, they are not merely describing the social world from outside it; they are acting upon its very fabric, reinforcing or eroding the shared acceptances on which institutions rest, declaring new realities into being or dissolving old ones, moving the collective speech that holds a society together or drives it apart. To name a thing publicly, to define it, to insist on a description until others adopt it, is to reach into the machinery of institutional reality and adjust it. This is a power for building shared worlds of trust and cooperation, and a power for corroding them; the later lessons on rhetoric and on the corruption of language will trace what happens when that world-shaping power is wielded to serve the truth and when it is wielded to deceive.

## What Searle Does and Does Not Show

A word of honest qualification keeps all this in proportion, and the distinction matters as much here as anywhere in the unit. Searle's account is a secular theory of social reality, and it neither presupposes nor establishes the theological vision of the earlier lessons; it is a striking and illuminating resonance with the Genesis intuition, not a proof of it. One can accept every word of Searle's analysis and remain a thoroughgoing atheist, for the analysis concerns how humans construct their shared institutions, not whether a divine Word underlies the cosmos. The resonance is real and worth marveling at — the pattern of "words creating worlds" turns up in the driest corner of analytic philosophy — but it is a parallel, not an argument, and to treat it as secret evidence for theology would be to overreach in exactly the way this course tries never to do.

The scope of Searle's claim must also be kept precise. It explains how money and marriage and nations and law are spoken into being; it does not explain how mountains or molecules are, for the brute physical world exists entirely without our word and would persist in our absence. "Words create worlds" is therefore literally and rigorously true of the social world, and only analogically or metaphorically true of the physical one — and the honest thinker holds those two registers apart even while noticing how suggestively they rhyme. Nor is every detail of Searle's theory beyond dispute; philosophers argue about the exact machinery of collective intentionality, about whether all institutional facts really fit the "X counts as Y" formula, about how status functions first get off the ground. But the core insight stands on its own secular feet, and it is remarkable enough without any embellishment: that the human world — the one we live in, argue in, marry and work and vote and grieve in — is a world we have spoken into being and that we hold in existence, together, with our words, and that would dissolve if we ever fell collectively silent. We are, whether we notice it or not, the sub-creators of the reality we inhabit.

Having traced the creative word outward, from the cosmos to the society, the unit now turns inward, to follow the same word into the depths of the self — beginning with the mystic who claimed to have heard the eternal Word being spoken, not in the heavens or the marketplace, but in the silent ground of his own soul.
