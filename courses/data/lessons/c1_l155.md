The previous lesson spoke of the human being as a sub-creator who makes worlds with words, and illustrated the claim with the imagined worlds of fiction. But there is a way in which we speak worlds into being that is not imaginary at all — a way so pervasive and so fundamental that we swim in it unawares, the way a fish is unaware of water. We do not only tell stories; we speak into existence the entire social reality within which we conduct our lives. Money, marriage, governments, borders, laws, property, universities, corporations, nations, the very dollar in your pocket and the office your employer holds — none of these exist in the way mountains and molecules exist. They exist because we say they do, and they persist only as long as we keep saying so. The philosopher who has mapped this astonishing fact with the greatest rigor is John Searle, and his account is the secular, hard-edged confirmation of everything this unit has been claiming: that words, quite literally, make worlds.

## Brute Facts and Institutional Facts

Searle begins from a distinction that, once grasped, permanently changes how one sees the human world.[^1] There are, he observes, two fundamentally different kinds of facts. On one side are brute facts — facts that would be true whether or not any human being existed to think about them. That a certain mountain is higher than another, that the sun has a certain mass, that hydrogen has one proton — these are brute facts, features of a mind-independent physical reality. On the other side are what Searle calls institutional facts — and these are of a wholly different character, for they exist only because human beings collectively believe and treat them as existing. That a certain piece of paper is a twenty-dollar bill, that two people are married, that a certain line running through a desert is an international border, that a particular person is the president, that a certain rectangle of grass is somebody's property — these are institutional facts. They are perfectly real; they govern our lives with enormous force. But they have no existence in the physical nature of the things themselves. Examine a twenty-dollar bill with every instrument of physics and chemistry and you will find paper, ink, fiber — you will never find its being-money, because its being-money is not a physical property at all. It is money only because we collectively declare and continually accept that it is.

Here is the crux, and its strangeness deserves a moment's astonishment. The whole vast structure of institutional reality — the economy, the government, the legal system, the university, the family as a legal and social entity — rests on nothing physical. It rests on collective agreement, and collective agreement is constituted and sustained by language. We assign to things and persons a status and a function that their physical nature does not give them, and they acquire that status purely through our shared acceptance of it, an acceptance that lives in and is carried by our words.

## X Counts as Y

Searle gives the mechanism a precise formula, and it connects directly to the speech acts we examined at the opening of this unit. Social reality is created, he argues, when we collectively impose a status function through a declaration, according to the pattern: X counts as Y in context C. This piece of specially printed paper counts as legal tender in the United States. This form of words counts as a marriage in this jurisdiction. This person, having been through this procedure, counts as the president. This line counts as the boundary of the nation. These are declarations in the precise sense established earlier — the speech acts that make something so by declaring it so — but raised now to the level of whole societies, and sustained not by a single utterance but by the continuous, collective speech and acceptance of millions of people. A currency is a shared declaration that a society keeps repeating. A nation is a reality held in being by the continual collective saying-so of its people and the world. The entire edifice of civilization, Searle shows, is a structure of status functions imposed and maintained by language — spoken into being and kept in being by words.

Sit with the magnitude of this. It means that the world most of us inhabit for most of our waking hours — the world of work and money and law and marriage and citizenship — is not a brute physical given but a collective human creation, spoken into existence and upheld, moment by moment, by our shared speech. Your Genesis intuition, that a world can be called into being by an utterance, turns out to be not a poetic fancy confined to theology but a literal description of how human society works, established by one of the most rigorous and unsentimental philosophers of the age, who is himself no theologian and intends no theology. The image of the speaking God, this unit has argued, is a being who makes worlds with words. Searle, without meaning to, has shown us doing exactly that, all day, every day, on a civilizational scale.

## The Fragility of Spoken Worlds

Because social reality is constituted and held up by collective speech and acceptance, it has a feature that brute reality does not: it can dissolve when the speaking stops. A mountain does not cease to exist if people stop believing in it, but a currency can collapse in days when enough people stop believing it has value and stop treating it as money; a government can fall when the collective recognition that sustained its authority evaporates; a marriage ends, in part, through the speech-acts that declare it over. The worlds we build with words are exactly as durable, and exactly as fragile, as the collective speech that sustains them. This is the source of both the immense power and the immense responsibility that attach to public speech — and it illuminates one of the examples that set this unit in motion. When politicians speak, they are not merely describing the social world; they are, for better or worse, acting upon its very fabric, reinforcing or eroding the shared acceptances on which institutions rest, declaring new realities into being or dissolving old ones, moving the collective speech that holds a society together. The later lessons on rhetoric and on the corruption of language will trace what happens when that world-shaping power is wielded well and when it is wielded to deceive.

A word of honest qualification keeps this in proportion. Searle's account is a secular theory of social reality, and it neither presupposes nor establishes the theological vision of the earlier lessons; it is a striking resonance with the Genesis intuition, not a proof of it. And its scope is precisely social: it explains how money and marriage and nations are spoken into being, not how mountains or molecules are — the brute physical world exists without our word, and "words create worlds" is therefore literally true of the social world and only analogically true of the physical. Nor is every detail of Searle's theory beyond dispute; philosophers argue about the exact machinery of collective intentionality and status functions. But the core insight stands on its own, secular feet, and it is remarkable enough without any embellishment: that the human world — the one we live in, argue in, marry and work and vote in — is a world we have spoken into being and that we hold in existence, together, with our words. We are, whether we notice it or not, sub-creators of the reality we inhabit. Having traced the creative word outward, from the cosmos to the society, the unit now turns inward, to follow it into the self — beginning with the mystic who heard the eternal Word spoken in the silent ground of the soul.
