We have followed the Word outward and upward — the Logos holding the cosmos together, the God who speaks creation into being, the human being made in the image of that speaking God and given the power to build worlds, social and imaginative, with words. Now the movement reverses and turns inward, and it turns with a mystic who is one of the strangest, deepest, and most vertiginous thinkers in the whole Christian tradition. Meister Eckhart, a Dominican friar and preacher of the late thirteenth and early fourteenth centuries, took the vast doctrine of the creative Word and located it in the most intimate place imaginable: the innermost ground of the individual human soul. His central and most electrifying teaching was that the eternal Word which the Father speaks — the Word through whom the galaxies were made — is spoken, continually, in you. To follow the Word from the cosmos into the self is to follow Eckhart, and it is a dizzying descent.

## The Birth of the Word in the Soul

Eckhart returned again and again, in his German sermons, to a single overwhelming idea, which he called the birth of the Word in the soul.[^1] The starting point was orthodox Christian doctrine: the Father eternally generates the Son, eternally speaks his Word, in the life of the Trinity — this utterance is not a past event but an eternal, timeless act, always happening. And the Word was spoken once into history, at Bethlehem, when the Word became flesh. But Eckhart pressed further, to a third birth that was the heart of his preaching: the same Word is born, here and now, in the ground of the soul of the person who is prepared to receive it. What happens eternally in God and once in history happens, Eckhart insisted, in the depths of the believer — the Father speaks his Word into the soul, and the soul becomes the place of the Word's birth. In one of his most startling formulations, he said that when this birth takes place, every soul becomes Mary — every soul becomes the one in whom the Word takes flesh.

This was not, for Eckhart, a poetic flourish or a pious metaphor. He meant it with a metaphysical seriousness that would later get him into serious trouble. The creative utterance of God, the speaking that makes and orders all things, was not merely an event out there in the cosmos or back there in history; it was an event that could occur, that in some sense was always occurring, in the innermost depth of a human person. The self, for Eckhart, was a place where the divine Word is spoken. This is the theme of this unit — the world-making power of the Word — driven to its most interior and astonishing conclusion.

## The Ground of the Soul

To understand where this birth takes place, one must understand Eckhart's most famous and elusive idea: the ground of the soul, the Seelengrund. Beneath all the ordinary faculties of the person — beneath the senses, beneath the emotions, beneath even the intellect and the will — Eckhart located a deepest center, a ground, a spark, a place so interior that it is, he suggested, the point where God and the soul are joined, where the divine and the human touch. This ground is silent, imageless, nameless; it is deeper than thought, prior to all the words and pictures and desires that fill the surface of the mind. And it is here, in this silent ground, that the Word is born. Not in the chattering intellect, not in the busy will, but in the still depth beneath them, where the soul is quiet enough and empty enough to receive the eternal speaking.

There is a profound resonance here with the depth psychology this whole course explores. Eckhart is describing, in the language of fourteenth-century mysticism, a substructure of the self far beneath the ego and its noise — a ground where meaning is generated, where the deepest realities of the person are constituted, beneath conscious awareness. When Peterson and the depth psychologists speak of a Self deeper than the ego, of an imago Dei in the substrate of the psyche, of meaning welling up from a source below deliberate thought, they are walking, in secular and psychological dress, a path that Eckhart walked in explicitly theological terms. The ground of the soul is where the Word is uttered, and to live from that ground rather than from the surface chatter is, for Eckhart, the whole of the spiritual life.

## The Condition: Silence and Letting Go

If the Word is born in the silent ground, then the condition for its birth is silence — and here Eckhart introduces the theme that will govern the back half of this unit. To receive the Word being spoken within, the soul must become still, must empty itself of its own clamor, its grasping, its images and words and willful striving. Eckhart called this condition Gelassenheit — a word that means releasement, letting-go, a serene detachment from one's own agenda and grip. The soul that would hear the eternal speaking must first fall silent; it must, in his arresting phrase, let go even of its own eager efforts to reach God, and become a still, empty, receptive ground in which the Word can be born. There is nothing, Eckhart said, so much like God as silence. The most creative speech — the divine Word itself — is received only in the deepest quiet.

This is a paradox that runs to the center of the whole unit, and it prepares the ground for the lessons on the custody of the tongue and the cloud of unknowing still to come. We have been celebrating the power of words to create, to order, to build worlds. Eckhart insists that the greatest Word of all is heard only by the one who has learned to be silent — that beneath all our speaking there must be a stillness, a ground emptied of our own noise, if the truly creative and life-giving Word is to be born in us. The power of speech and the discipline of silence are not opposites but partners; the one who would speak worlds into being must first become quiet enough to receive the Word.

## The Honest Reckoning

Eckhart must be handled with real care, and honesty requires several acknowledgments. He was, first of all, a controversial figure in his own time and after: he was investigated for heresy near the end of his life, and shortly after his death a papal bull condemned a number of propositions drawn from his work as heretical or dangerous, while allowing that others might be understood in an orthodox sense. The condemnation reflected a genuine difficulty — Eckhart pushed language to and past its breaking point, and his most extreme statements about the identity of the soul's ground with God can be read as sliding toward pantheism, the erasure of the distinction between creature and Creator. His defenders argue, plausibly, that he was straining ordinary language to express an orthodox mystical union and that his condemned propositions, read in context, remain within the bounds of the tradition; his critics see a genuine blurring of the line between the human and the divine. The interpretation of Eckhart is genuinely contested, and this lesson has presented a sympathetic reading that not every scholar or theologian would endorse.

Beyond the historical controversy lies a deeper caution. The claim that an eternal Word is literally born in the ground of the soul is a mystical assertion, not an empirical finding, and mystical experience is notoriously difficult to interpret and impossible to verify from the outside; contemplatives across very different traditions describe encounters with a nameless ground or depth, and what such experiences actually disclose — about God, about the mind, about reality — is a matter on which sincere and thoughtful people sharply disagree. One may find Eckhart's phenomenology of the inner depths illuminating, may recognize in it something true about the layered structure of the self and the silence beneath our chatter, without thereby accepting his full metaphysics of the divine birth. What Eckhart contributes to this unit is not a doctrine to be swallowed whole but a bridge and a provocation: the bridge from the cosmic Word to the inner word, and the provocation that the deepest speaking happens in the deepest silence. From the Word born in the ground of the soul, we turn to a thinker who described that inner word with more sobriety and system, and who anticipated modern psychology by more than a thousand years — Augustine, and the word of the heart.
