Eckhart carried us into the silent ground of the soul, where he said the eternal Word is born. But Eckhart was a mystic, straining language toward the edge of what can be said. The next lesson, on Vygotsky and inner speech, will be rigorous modern psychology, careful and empirical. Between them stands a figure who somehow belongs to both worlds — a thinker of such range that he anticipated, with startling precision, a discovery modern psychology would not make for another millennium and a half, while grounding it in a theology of the divine Word. Augustine of Hippo, writing at the turn of the fifth century, worked out a theory of an inner word beneath all spoken language, and tied it directly to the Logos. To read him is to watch ancient contemplation and modern cognitive science reach toward each other across sixteen hundred years.

## Words as Signs

Augustine came to Christianity as a trained rhetorician — a professional of words — and he never lost his fascination with how language works.[^1] In his handbook on interpreting Scripture, he laid out one of the first real theories of signs in the Western tradition. Words, he observed, are signs: they are not the things they name but stand in for them, point toward them, carry the mind from the sign to the thing signified. A word is a signum that directs attention to a res, a reality beyond itself. This seems obvious once stated, but its implications are deep, for it means that language is essentially a system of pointing — that words are always gesturing beyond themselves toward realities they are not, and that to use words well is to point accurately, to direct the mind reliably toward the things that are.

But Augustine pressed on to a more radical and surprising claim, worked out in an early dialogue on the nature of teaching. If words are signs that point beyond themselves, then words alone cannot actually teach us anything; they can only prompt us to look. When a teacher speaks, Augustine argued, the words do not deposit knowledge in the student like water poured into a jar; rather, the words prompt the student to consult a truth already available within, to turn inward and see for themselves whether what the words point to is real. The true teacher, he concluded, is not the human speaker but the Truth itself, dwelling within the mind and illuminating it from inside — Christ, the inner Teacher, the light by which we recognize what any word is pointing at. Words point; the inner light illumines. This was Augustine's doctrine of illumination, and it located the decisive event of understanding not in the external words but in an interior encounter with truth.

## The Word of the Heart

All of this was preparation for Augustine's most remarkable insight, developed in the great work of his maturity, his long meditation on the Trinity. There he asked a question so simple it is easy to miss its depth: when you think a thought, before you speak it aloud, is there already a word present in your mind? And he answered that there is — that beneath every spoken word in Latin or Greek or any human tongue lies a prior word, a word of the heart, the verbum cordis, which belongs to no language at all. When you understand something, Augustine argued, your mind forms an inner word — the thought itself, the meaning grasped — and this inner word exists before and beneath its clothing in the sounds of any particular language. The spoken word in English or Latin is the outer garment; the word of the heart is the naked thought it dresses. The same inner word could be spoken in a hundred languages; it is prior to all of them, the meaning that each of them merely translates into sound.

Sit with what Augustine has done here, and its audacity becomes clear. He has located a word-structure beneath thought itself — an inner articulation, a saying that happens in the mind before any external speech — and he did so at the dawn of the fifth century. The next lesson will show the psychologist Vygotsky arriving, through careful empirical study, at a strikingly similar conclusion: that there is an inner speech, deeper than and prior to spoken language, which constitutes the very structure of our thinking. Augustine got there sixteen centuries earlier, by introspection and theological reflection, and this is why he belongs precisely here, immediately before Vygotsky — the theology of the inner word setting the table for the psychology of inner speech. The two are describing, from opposite ends of history and by utterly different methods, what may be the same profound feature of the human mind: that we think in words before we speak them, that thought is at its root a kind of interior saying.

## The Trace of the Logos

For Augustine, this inner word was not merely a curiosity of cognition; it was a clue to the deepest structure of reality and of the self. The word of the heart, he argued, is a created image of the eternal Word — the Word the Father speaks in the life of the Trinity. As the Father generates the Son, his perfect Word, so the human mind, in the act of understanding, generates its own inner word, its imperfect and creaturely image of that divine utterance. The human person, made in the image of God, images God precisely as a speaking thing: as the divine mind eternally speaks its Word, so the human mind speaks its inner word in every act of knowing, and in this the trace of the Logos is stamped upon the very structure of human thought. To be a thinking creature is to be a speaking creature, even in the silence of the mind, and to be a speaking creature is to bear the image of the God who is himself eternally Word.

This gives the whole unit its anthropological foundation. If the cosmos is spoken into being by the Word, and if the human being is made in the image of that speaking God, then it should be no surprise that thought itself turns out to be word-shaped — that beneath our chatter runs an inner word, and that beneath the inner word runs the eternal Word whose image it is. The claim that we think in language, that ordering our words orders our minds, that speech reaches down into the very structure of the self — a claim this unit will develop through Vygotsky and the psychology of the inner voice — has, in Augustine, its ancient and profound charter. We are word-shaped all the way down, because we are made in the image of the Word.

We should be candid that not every part of this survives unchallenged. Whether human thought is fundamentally linguistic is a live and contested question in modern cognitive science; some thinking is clearly imagistic, spatial, or wordless, and the evidence of people who reason without inner speech complicates any simple claim that all thought is verbal. And the further step — that the inner word is the image of a divine Word — is a theological reading, not a finding that cognitive science could confirm or deny. But Augustine's core observation, that a word forms in the mind before it reaches the tongue, is something most of us can verify by simple attention to our own thinking, and it anticipated the science with uncanny accuracy. From the word of the heart, we turn to the modern psychologist who traced how that inner word is built — and who discovered that it is assembled, in large part, from the words that others speak over us.
