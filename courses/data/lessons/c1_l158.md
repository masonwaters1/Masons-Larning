Augustine, in the previous lesson, discovered by introspection a word beneath all spoken words — the verbum cordis, the word of the heart, the thought that forms in the mind before it is clothed in any language. But Augustine could only describe this inner word; he could not explain where it comes from or how it is built, for those are questions that require the patient empirical study of how a human mind actually develops, and that study lay fifteen centuries in his future. It was undertaken, with extraordinary originality, by a Soviet psychologist who died at thirty-seven, of tuberculosis, in 1934, leaving behind a body of work so far ahead of its time that it was suppressed for decades and only fully appreciated generations later. Lev Vygotsky has been called the Mozart of psychology, and his account of what he called inner speech is one of the most important discoveries ever made about the relation between language and the self. It takes Augustine's inner word and shows us how it is assembled — and the answer has profound and slightly unnerving implications for everything this unit has been building toward.

## The Voice in the Head

Begin with the plain phenomenon, the thing Vygotsky set out to explain.[^1] You have a voice in your head. Right now, reading these words, you may be hearing them in a kind of inner voicing; when you deliberate about a decision, you talk it over with yourself; when you rehearse what you will say, or replay what you should have said, or narrate your own actions, or encourage or scold yourself, you do it in an inner speech that no one else can hear. This silent inner voice is, for most people, the very medium of conscious thought — the workspace in which we reason, plan, remember, and above all relate to ourselves. It is so constant and so intimate that we rarely notice it, the way we rarely notice our own breathing. Vygotsky's question was deceptively simple: where does this inner voice come from? Is it simply innate, a faculty we are born with? Or does it have a history — a developmental origin that we could, if we watched closely enough, actually observe being built?

His answer, drawn from close study of how young children develop, was that the inner voice has a history, and that its history runs in a single, striking direction: from the outside in. The speech that becomes our most private possession, the voice in the head, begins its life as something entirely public — as speech between people, out loud, in the open air.

## From the Social to the Inner

Vygotsky traced a developmental sequence in three stages, and the sequence is the heart of his theory. In the first stage, speech is purely social. The infant and young child experience language as something that happens between the child and others — the caregiver's words, the back-and-forth of request and response, the running commentary of parents narrating the child's world. Language is, at this stage, wholly interpersonal, a bridge between two minds, with no inner dimension at all. It exists in the space between people before it exists inside anyone.

In the second stage comes a curious and telling phenomenon that any parent has witnessed: the child begins to talk out loud to himself. The four-year-old at play narrates his own actions — now the truck goes here, no, it doesn't fit, try the other way, there — carrying on a running monologue directed at no one, or at himself. Psychologists before Vygotsky had noticed this "egocentric speech" and largely dismissed it. The great Jean Piaget regarded it as a mark of the child's immaturity, a sign that the young child cannot yet take another's perspective and so talks pointlessly to himself, a habit that would simply fade as the child matured into properly social speech. Vygotsky made a brilliant reinterpretation that turned Piaget's reading on its head. This private speech, he argued, is not immature social speech destined to fade into nothing; it is a crucial way-station, speech in the very act of turning inward. The child is using out-loud self-talk to guide and organize his own behavior — to think, in effect, out loud — and this self-directed speech does not disappear as he grows. It goes underground.

In the third stage, that is exactly what happens: the private, out-loud speech becomes internalized as silent inner speech. The talking-to-oneself does not vanish; it is submerged, condensed, and transformed into the inner voice, the medium of verbal thought. What the child once did audibly — narrating and directing his own actions with words — he now does inaudibly, in the silent speech of the mind. The voice that was once out loud, between the child and the world, becomes the voice in the head. And this is why Vygotsky's account is so momentous: it means that the inner voice, the very instrument of our private thinking, is internalized social speech. It comes from outside. It is, in its origin, the speech of others and the speech we first spoke aloud, taken in and made the silent substance of the self.

## Every Function Twice

Vygotsky generalized this discovery into one of the most influential principles in the history of psychology, and it reaches far beyond speech. Every higher mental function, he proposed, appears twice in the course of development: first on the social plane, between people, and only later on the psychological plane, within the individual. First it is interpersonal — a relation between the child and others — and then it becomes intrapersonal, a structure within the child's own mind. The child first shares attention with a caregiver, then learns to direct his own attention; first is soothed by another, then learns to soothe himself; first is reasoned with, then learns to reason. In every case the movement is the same: what begins as a relationship between people becomes, through internalization, a capacity within a single person. The mind, on this account, is built from the outside in — assembled, in large part, out of internalized social relations, with language as the master tool by which the social becomes the mental.

The implications for this unit are hard to overstate, and they are the reason this lesson sits where it does. If the inner voice is internalized social speech, then the character of that inner voice — its tone, its stance, the way it addresses and evaluates the self — is shaped, at its foundation, by the speech that was directed at us, especially in the years when the internalization was taking place. The words spoken over a child do not simply influence the child from outside and then stop at the skin. They are taken in and become the child's own inner voice, the silent narrator of his experience, the tone in which he will speak to himself for the rest of his life. This is the mechanism, discovered by empirical developmental psychology, behind the ancient and universal intuition that the words spoken to a child shape the person he becomes — the intuition that the next lesson will explore in full. The parent who speaks constant encouragement is not merely making a child feel good in the moment; she is installing an inner voice of encouragement. The parent who speaks constant contempt is installing an inner voice of contempt, which will go on speaking, in the child's own head, long after the parent is silent or gone.

## The Grammar of Inner Speech

Vygotsky noticed, too, that inner speech is not simply outer speech spoken quietly; it has a distinctive character of its own, transformed by its journey inward. It is radically condensed and abbreviated — we do not think in full, grammatical sentences but in a compressed, elliptical shorthand, because we already know what we mean and need not spell it out to ourselves. It is predicative, tending to drop the subject (which is always obvious to the thinker) and keep only what is new. And it is saturated with personal, private meaning, freighted with associations that belong to us alone. Inner speech is a kind of dense, personal argot, an idiom of one, in which whole trains of thought can flash by in a word. This is why it can be so hard to put our own thoughts into words for others: we must unpack the condensed inner speech, restore what we had abbreviated, translate our private idiom back into the full public language from which it originally came. The word of the heart, in Augustine's phrase, must be re-clothed in the outer word before another can receive it.

## Listening In on the Inner Voice

Vygotsky worked in the 1920s and 30s with almost no way to observe the inner voice directly; he had to infer its existence and character from behavior, chiefly from the out-loud private speech of children on its way inward. Modern methods have allowed researchers to listen in more closely, and what they have found broadly confirms and enriches his account. When people engage in inner speech, brain-imaging studies show activity in the very regions involved in producing and perceiving outer speech — the areas that plan articulation and process heard language light up, as though the mind were quietly speaking and listening to itself. Inner speech is, quite literally, speech run internally, a rehearsal of outer speech with the volume turned off; some people even detect faint movements in the muscles of the throat and mouth during silent verbal thought, the ghost of an utterance that never fully escapes. The voice in the head is not a mere metaphor. It is the speech system running quietly on itself.

This intimate link between inner and outer speech has a striking and poignant edge, one that reveals how deeply the words of others become woven into us. Researchers who study auditory-verbal hallucinations — the experience, familiar in certain conditions, of hearing voices that are not there — have proposed that some such experiences arise when a person's own inner speech is, for one reason or another, no longer recognized as self-generated, and is heard instead as an alien voice speaking from outside. If that is right, it is a vivid illustration of just how much the inner voice is built from the internalized speech of others: the voices people hear are frequently the voices of judgment, command, or reproach — often echoing, in tone and content, the critical or authoritative figures of a life. The inner voice, assembled from the speech spoken over us, can under strain seem to detach and speak back at us in the accents of those who first shaped it. In the ordinary case it remains our own, the silent medium of thought; but even in the ordinary case it carries, woven into its very grain, the voices of everyone who ever spoke into the space where it was formed.

## The Scaffolding of the Mind

There is one more piece of Vygotsky's vision that bears directly on the power of speech, and it concerns how the words of others actively build a child's capacities. He observed that at any given moment a child can do certain things alone, and certain further things only with the help and guidance of a more capable person — a parent, a teacher, an older child. The gap between these two — between what the child can do independently and what he can do with skilled help — Vygotsky called the zone of proximal development, and he argued that it is precisely here, in this zone, that learning and development happen. The crucial point for us is how that help is delivered: overwhelmingly through language. The adult's words guide, prompt, structure, and scaffold the child's still-developing capacities — and then, in time, those words are internalized, so that what the child could once do only with the adult's spoken guidance he can now do alone, the external scaffolding of speech having become an internal structure of the mind. The words of others, in other words, do not merely inform the growing mind; they build it, laying down the very scaffolding on which the self is raised. We are, cognitively, constructed out of the speech of those around us.

## The Honest Reckoning

Vygotsky's core discoveries are well-supported and have proven enormously fruitful: that private speech internalizes into inner speech, that inner speech is a central medium of verbal thought, that higher mental functions have social origins, that the words of others scaffold the developing mind. These are not fringe claims but foundational contributions to developmental psychology. But the strongest versions of the thesis must be stated with care, because they overreach, and honesty requires marking the limits.

The chief caution is this: it is not true that all thought simply is inner speech, that thinking is nothing but silent talking. A great deal of human thought is plainly not verbal at all. We think in images, in spatial relations, in movements, in music, in feelings — the sculptor reasoning in three-dimensional form, the chess master seeing a position, the athlete thinking in motion, the composer thinking in sound. More strikingly still, recent research has confirmed that some people have little or no inner voice at all — a condition sometimes called anendophasia — and they think, reason, and function perfectly well; and infants and animals clearly engage in forms of thought before or without language entirely. Inner speech, then, is a major and central medium of human thought, especially of the deliberate, self-reflective, self-governing kind — but it is not the whole of thought, and the claim that it is goes beyond the evidence. Relatedly, the strong doctrine that language wholly determines the shape of thought — the strong form of what is called linguistic relativity — has been largely rejected; language influences and shapes cognition, sometimes in measurable ways, but it does not imprison it, and thought is not simply the helpless prisoner of the words available to us.

We should also remember that Vygotsky died young, his work unfinished and, for political reasons, suppressed in his own country for decades, so that parts of his theory rest on limited studies and were extended by later hands. None of this overturns his central insight, which has been confirmed and built upon many times over: that the inner voice has social origins, that it is assembled in significant part from the speech of others, and that the mind is built, to a remarkable degree, from the outside in. That insight is secure, and it is the one that matters most for this unit — for it means that the words spoken over us become, quite literally, part of the fabric of our minds. To the most important instance of this — the words a parent speaks over a child, day after day, across the years in which the inner voice is being built — we now turn.
