The previous lesson, on the letter of James, showed us the tongue as a fire — a small member that sets whole forests, whole lives, whole societies ablaze. If that is true, then a question presses itself upon anyone who takes the power of speech seriously: what does it look like to actually govern such a thing? The whole thrust of this unit has been the immense power of words to create, to order, to build worlds. But there is a counter-melody, sounded most clearly by the monastic tradition, and it is essential to the whole: the first mark of a person who truly understands the power of speech is not that they wield it constantly but that they have learned when to be silent. The desert fathers and the monks who followed them made a spiritual discipline of restraint of speech — the custody of the tongue — and they were convinced that a person who cannot govern their words cannot govern their soul.

## The Desert and the Discipline of Silence

The tradition begins in the deserts of Egypt in the fourth and fifth centuries, where men and women fled the cities to seek God in solitude, and where the collected sayings of these desert fathers and mothers became one of the treasuries of Christian wisdom.[^1] What is striking, reading these sayings, is how obsessed the desert dwellers were with the tongue. Again and again they return to the dangers of careless speech, idle talk, gossip, and above all the judging of others, and to the surpassing value of silence. One of the most famous of them, Abba Arsenius, left a line that distills the whole tradition: he had often been sorry, he said, for having spoken, but never for having kept silent. The desert fathers were not misanthropes who hated conversation; they were physicians of the soul who had observed, with unsparing clarity, how much damage the tongue does — how gossip corrodes community, how the judging word poisons the one who speaks it as much as the one spoken against, how the endless stream of idle talk scatters and dissipates the self. And they concluded that learning to govern the tongue was near the very center of the spiritual life, that the mastery of speech and the mastery of the soul were nearly the same thing.

This was not, for them, a merely negative discipline, a grim clamping-down on a dangerous faculty. It was understood as the clearing of a space. The person forever talking, forever reacting, forever emptying themselves into words, has no interior stillness, no depth from which anything true or considered can arise. The discipline of silence was meant to create an inner quiet in which the soul could listen — to God, to conscience, to the reality of things — rather than perpetually broadcasting its own noise. The one who has learned to be silent has learned to hear.

## Benedict and the Rule of Restraint

What the desert fathers discovered as a wisdom, the great monastic legislator Benedict, writing his Rule for monasteries in the sixth century, built into a way of life. Benedict devoted an entire chapter of his Rule to restraint of speech — taciturnitas — and made it one of the marks of the humble and well-ordered soul. He did not command absolute, perpetual silence; monks had to speak to work, to worship, to counsel one another. What he commanded was discipline: speaking little, weighing one's words, avoiding idle and frivolous and coarse talk, and above all avoiding the sin that Benedict, with a monk's realism about communal life, regarded as especially corrosive — grumbling, the murmured complaint that poisons the spirit of a community from within. The custody of the tongue, the guarding of the mouth, was for Benedict a daily practice, woven into the rhythm of the monastery, a training in which the governing of speech became a training in the governing of the whole self. To learn to hold one's tongue was to learn to hold one's soul.

Behind this lay the wisdom tradition of the Scriptures, which the monks knew by heart and which had said the same thing for centuries. The book of Proverbs is full of it: that the one who guards their mouth preserves their life, that even a fool who keeps silent is thought wise, that in the multitude of words sin is not lacking. The tongue is powerful and dangerous, the wisdom tradition insists, and therefore it must be disciplined, watched, restrained. The monks simply took this ancient wisdom and made it a school, a lifelong training in the government of speech.

## The Silence Before the Word

There is a deeper note beneath the practical discipline, and it connects the custody of the tongue to the very heart of this unit. Consider: before God speaks creation into being, there is silence. The creative Word emerges from a stillness; the "let there be" sounds into a prior quiet. When the prophet Elijah sought God, we are told, God was not in the wind or the earthquake or the fire, but in a sound of sheer silence, a still small voice that could be heard only by one who had grown quiet enough to hear it. The contemplative traditions of Christianity made much of this interior stillness — the Christian East developed a whole spirituality of it, the practice of hesychia, inner silence, as the ground from which the soul meets God. The insight running through all of this is that the truly creative, life-giving Word is heard and spoken only out of silence. Noise cannot make worlds; only the word that comes from stillness can.

This is why the custody of the tongue is not the enemy of the unit's celebration of speech but its necessary partner and precondition. The two lessons that will close this unit — the call to tell the truth, and the call to be precise in one's speech — describe a way of speaking that is disciplined, deliberate, weighed, and true. But such speech is simply impossible for a person who says everything that comes into their head, who empties themselves into a ceaseless stream of reaction and chatter and half-formed opinion. You cannot say only what is true and precise if you say everything. The discipline of restraint is the gate through which truthful, precise, world-building speech must pass. First one learns to be silent; only then can one learn to speak words that heal and order and create rather than words that wound and scatter and destroy. The custody of the tongue is the ancient monastic ancestor of the disciplined, careful, non-corrupting speech that this course, following Peterson, will hold up as an ethical ideal — and Peterson's counsel to speak deliberately, to refuse the careless and the false word, has its roots in this desert soil.

There is an honest tension here that the tradition itself knew and that we should not paper over. Silence is not always a virtue. There is a silence that is cowardice, a keeping-quiet in the face of injustice that is itself a kind of sin; the same Scriptures that praise the guarded tongue also command the prophet to cry aloud and spare not, to speak for those who have no voice. The custody of the tongue is not a vow of indifference or a counsel to never speak up when speaking is required. The monks sought not muteness but wisdom — the wisdom to know when to keep silent and when to speak, which the ancient teachers ranked among the highest and rarest of all. Restraint of speech and courageous truth-telling are not opposites but two disciplines of the same governed tongue, and the mature person, the tradition holds, has learned both: to be silent when words would only wound or scatter, and to speak, unflinchingly, when silence would be a betrayal. From the discipline that governs the tongue, we turn to a great non-Western voice who grasped the social stakes of speech with equal depth — Confucius, and the rectification of names.
