This unit has been, from its very first lesson, a sustained celebration of the power of words. Words do things; they build social worlds; they order the psyche; they raise or ruin a child; they move souls and cities; and, misused, they poison a civilization. We have seen speech as one of the most potent forces in all of human life, an echo of the divine Word that spoke the cosmos into being and holds it in existence. And now, before we descend at last to the practical climax of the unit, we must sound the necessary counter-note, the one that keeps all this celebration from tipping over into a kind of idolatry of language. It comes from the deepest contemplative tradition of Christianity, and its message is bracing and humbling: for all their extraordinary power, words finally fail. Before the ultimate reality, before the ineffable God, every word we possess falls infinitely short — and the mark of real maturity about language is to know exactly where it ends, and to fall silent there.

## The Way of Negation

The tradition has a name — the apophatic way, the via negativa, the way of negation — and its most influential ancient source is a figure of tantalizing mystery.[^1] Writing around the year 500, an anonymous theologian composed a small but extraordinary body of work under the borrowed name of Dionysius the Areopagite, a figure mentioned in the New Testament as an Athenian convert of the apostle Paul. The pseudonym lent the writings enormous authority for a thousand years, until scholarship revealed that the author must have lived centuries after Paul; and so he is known to us now as Pseudo-Dionysius, a voice without a face, one of the most consequential anonymous authors in the history of thought. His central insight, developed with great subtlety, was that God — the ultimate source and ground of all things — transcends everything we can say or even think. Whatever concept we form of God, whatever name we apply, is drawn from the created world and therefore falls short of the Creator, who is beyond all creation and all the categories our minds can supply.

Pseudo-Dionysius distinguished two ways of speaking about God. There is the affirmative or cataphatic way, which names God from his creatures — God is good, God is wise, God is powerful, God is light — attributing to the source the perfections found in what he has made. This way is legitimate and even necessary; Scripture itself speaks so. But it is, Dionysius insisted, the lower way, and it must always be surpassed by the negative or apophatic way, which denies these very names — God is not good in the way we understand goodness, not wise as we are wise, not existent in the way created things exist — because God transcends every one of them, dwelling beyond assertion and beyond denial alike. And the negations must themselves finally be negated: God is not merely the negation of our concepts either, but beyond both the affirmation and the negation, in a darkness that is not the absence of light but its overwhelming excess, too bright for the mind's eye to bear. This is no mere skepticism or vagueness; it is a rigorous discipline of humility before the infinite, a recognition that the reality we call God is not one more object in the universe about which we can form adequate ideas, but the very ground of being, which no idea can contain.

## The Ladder of Negations

The apophatic way is best understood not as a doctrine but as an ascent, a ladder the soul climbs by letting go. One begins with the affirmations, the names and images of God drawn from Scripture and creation; one does not discard them carelessly, for they are the necessary first rungs. But then one begins to negate — to recognize that God exceeds each name, that he is more than good, more than wise, more than being — and with each negation one rises higher, stripping away another concept, another image, another handhold, ascending by subtraction rather than addition. Pseudo-Dionysius found the perfect emblem of this ascent in the figure of Moses climbing Sinai. Moses leaves the people below, then passes beyond even the sights and sounds of the holy mountain, and finally enters alone into the thick darkness at the summit where God is — not seeing God, for God cannot be seen, but entering the cloud of unknowing where the divine presence is met precisely in the failure of sight and speech. The higher one climbs, the less one can say, until at the summit the soul falls wholly silent, united to the God who is beyond all knowing in a darkness that is really the fullness of light. The way up is a way of letting go, and its destination is a luminous silence in which words have no more work to do.

## The Cloud and the Arrow of Love

Nine centuries after Pseudo-Dionysius, an anonymous English monk gave this austere and difficult tradition its most beautiful and practical expression, in a small book of spiritual counsel written for a young disciple and titled, from its central image, The Cloud of Unknowing. Writing in the plain and tender Middle English of the fourteenth century, the author described the situation of the soul that seeks God with unforgettable concreteness. Between you and God, he told his pupil, there hangs a cloud of unknowing — a darkness that your thinking simply cannot penetrate, for God cannot be grasped by thought, cannot be captured by any concept or reached by any reasoning, however subtle or sincere. Beneath you, meanwhile, you must place a cloud of forgetting, deliberately putting down and out of mind not only the world and its distractions but even your own good and pious thoughts about God, for these too, if you cling to them as though they were God himself, become obstacles between you and the reality they merely point toward. The intellect, with all its words and images, must be left behind, laid under the cloud of forgetting, so that the soul may reach up, through the cloud of unknowing, with an entirely different faculty.

And what is that faculty? Not thought, but love. The author's most famous teaching is the beating heart of the whole book: God cannot be reached by the mind, but he can be reached by love; by thought he can never be grasped or held, but by love he can. The thinking intellect, for all its power, halts at the cloud; but a naked intent of the will, a longing love reaching up blindly into the darkness, pierces where thought cannot go. The soul is to send up toward the hidden God, again and again, what the author calls a sharp dart of longing love — a wordless arrow of desire shot into the dark toward the God who cannot be seen or said but can be loved and, in loving, in some sense touched. This is the apophatic way turned into a livable practice, a method of prayer: the deliberate laying-aside of words, concepts, and images, so that the soul, emptied of its own understanding, may reach in pure love toward the reality that lies beyond all of them.

## Where Words End

Now we can see clearly why this lesson is the indispensable counterweight to everything else in the unit. We have celebrated, and rightly, the power of words to reveal, to create, to order, to build. But the apophatic tradition reminds us of the other half of the truth: that words also fail; that they are penultimate rather than ultimate; that the deepest reality of all lies beyond the reach of any language we can command. Words point, as Augustine taught us many lessons ago — but the pointing finger is not the moon, and the greatest realities are precisely the ones toward which our words can only gesture, never grasp. To know this is not to despise words or to abandon the unit's high estimate of their power; it is to understand words truly, to hold their power and their limit together in a single view, which is the whole of wisdom about speech. The person who understands that words build worlds, and also that before the ultimate mystery all words fail, has grasped both halves of the truth. The one who knows only the first half is in perpetual danger of mistaking their own eloquence for reality, of supposing that whatever cannot be said clearly does not exist, and of shrinking the world down to the size of their vocabulary.

## The Paradox of Speaking the Unspeakable

There is a lovely and instructive paradox lying at the center of this whole tradition, and it is worth dwelling on rather than hurrying past. The apophatic theologians used a great many words to say that words fail. Pseudo-Dionysius wrote treatises, subtle and elaborate, to argue that God is beyond all treatises. The author of the Cloud wrote a whole book to counsel his reader to put books aside. This looks, at first, like simple hypocrisy or self-contradiction — but it is neither; it is the very nature of the enterprise. Language is being made to turn back upon itself, to point beyond its own limit, to use the word as an arrow aimed past all words toward the silence in which alone the ineffable can be met. The words of the apophatic writers are not descriptions of God, which they insist is impossible, but instruments for leading the soul to the place where description gives out and something else — silence, love, unknowing — takes over. This connects the lesson directly to what we have already seen: to Eckhart's insistence that the Word is born only in a silent ground, and that he too used daring words to point beyond words; and to the monks' custody of the tongue, their conviction that the truest and deepest speech comes only out of stillness. All of these traditions are circling the same recognition from different sides: that beneath and beyond all our speaking lies a silence, and that the deepest things are approached, in the end, not by saying more but by falling reverently quiet.

## Peterson, the Ineffable, and the Tacit

This apophatic humility has a secular cousin that connects it back to the concerns of this course, for the recognition that the most important realities exceed our capacity to state them is not the property of mystics alone. The philosopher Michael Polanyi built a whole account of human knowledge on the observation that we know more than we can tell — that the deepest and most important things we know, from how to recognize a face to how to ride a bicycle to the tacit judgments of a master in any craft, cannot be fully reduced to explicit, spoken rules, and that all our articulate knowing rests on a vast foundation of the inarticulate. Peterson has been drawn throughout his work to this same frontier: to the numinous and the transcendent, which he insists cannot be captured in mere propositions; to the meaning that is enacted, embodied, and lived rather than stated; to the sense that the deepest realities are gestured at by myth, ritual, and story precisely because they cannot be set down in a list of literal claims. The apophatic tradition is the ancient, explicitly religious form of this same recognition — that reality, and above all its ultimate ground, overflows what language can hold, and that a wisdom which knows only how to assert, and never how to fall silent before the unsayable, is a shallow wisdom. The most articulate person is not the one who can put everything into words, but the one who knows, with precision, what cannot be.

## The Honest Reckoning

The apophatic way is profound, but it is not without genuine difficulties, and honesty requires naming them plainly. The sharpest is a problem of self-consistency. If God is truly beyond all words and concepts, how can we say even that — how can one assert that God is "beyond being" or "ineffable" without those very assertions being words and concepts applied to God? The tradition has answers, holding that such negations function differently from ordinary claims, that they unsay rather than say, point rather than describe, and finally cancel themselves; but the worry is real and has been pressed hard by critics. A God about whom literally nothing can be affirmed threatens, they argue, to collapse into a God about whom nothing meaningful can be said at all — and there is a version of the negative way that is genuine humility before the infinite, and a version that is merely a fog, a retreat into the unsayable that conveniently immunizes every claim from scrutiny. The two are not always easy to tell apart, and the apophatic writer must work to stay on the right side of the line.

There is a further honest point, and it cuts to the heart of the matter. The experience of the ineffable — of a reality that exceeds all words — is not the property of Christianity alone. Contemplatives across many and mutually incompatible traditions, and some people of no tradition at all in moments of overwhelming awe, beauty, grief, or crisis, report an encounter with something that language cannot hold; and these experiences are interpreted in radically different ways, as the Christian God, as the emptiness at the heart of Buddhist meditation, as the impersonal Absolute of the Vedantic tradition, or, by the skeptic, as a natural and explicable state of the human brain. That so many roads arrive at the limits of language does not, by itself, tell us what lies beyond those limits, and one should be wary of reading a specific and detailed theology out of an experience that is, almost by definition, beyond specification. The silence at the summit does not obviously announce whose mountain it is.

Yet even granting all of this, the core contribution of the apophatic tradition to this unit stands, and it is deeply valuable. It is the recognition that words, for all their genuine world-making power, are not the last thing; that reality exceeds our capacity to state it; and that the person who has learned both to speak worlds and to fall silent before the mystery has learned something the merely eloquent never will. This is not a retreat from the unit's high view of language but its necessary completion — the humility that keeps the celebration of the word from hardening into the pride that thinks it has said everything. With that humility established, we are ready at last for the practical heart of the unit, the two lessons toward which everything has been building — the ethics of speech, and the question you have been waiting all along to have answered: how, exactly, does learning to speak truly and precisely change a life?
