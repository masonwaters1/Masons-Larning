The story of early depth psychology is usually told as a drama of two men — Freud the father and Jung the rebellious son — but there was a third figure in the founding circle, and his ideas may have shaped the modern understanding of the self more quietly and more thoroughly than either. Alfred Adler broke with Freud before Jung did, and he built a psychology that rejected the primacy of sex and the tyranny of the past in favor of something at once simpler and more hopeful: the idea that human beings are driven, above all, by the struggle to overcome their own weakness. His concept of the inferiority complex entered the language of the world; his emphasis on goals, competence, and social contribution anticipated much of what later psychology would confirm. If Jung is the most mystical of the founders, Adler is the most practical — and, in some respects, the one whose insights have aged the best.

## The Third Man

Alfred Adler was a Viennese physician who joined Freud's inner circle in its earliest years, one of the first to gather around the new psychoanalysis.[^1] But he was never a comfortable disciple, and by 1911 the tensions had become irreconcilable. Adler could not accept Freud's insistence that sexuality was the fundamental engine of the psyche, the hidden force behind all human motivation. The break was sharp and, in the manner of the early psychoanalytic movement, permanent; Adler departed to found his own school, which he called Individual Psychology — a name meant to signal his conviction that each person must be understood as an indivisible whole, a unified self oriented toward a goal, rather than as a battleground of warring drives.

The disagreement with Freud went to the root. Where Freud looked *backward* — explaining the present by the buried past, the childhood traumas and repressed desires that determine us — Adler looked *forward*, to the goals and purposes that pull us onward. Where Freud saw us pushed by drives we cannot control, Adler saw us striving toward ends we set ourselves. And where Freud made sex the master motive, Adler proposed something he thought both more universal and more human: the drive to overcome inferiority, to move from a felt sense of smallness and inadequacy toward competence, mastery, and significance. This single idea would become the foundation of his entire psychology.

## From Inferiority to Striving

Adler's starting point was a fact so obvious it is easily overlooked: every human being begins life small, weak, and utterly dependent. The infant is helpless among giants, unable to feed or protect itself, at the mercy of a world it cannot control. This universal condition of childhood, Adler argued, plants in every person a deep and formative sense of *inferiority* — a felt gap between what one is and what one needs to be — and the whole of psychological development can be understood as the effort to close that gap. The fundamental human motive is the *striving from inferiority toward superiority*: not superiority over others, necessarily, but the movement from felt weakness toward competence, from helplessness toward mastery, from a minus toward a plus.

This striving, Adler held, is the engine of human growth, and in its healthy form it is the source of all achievement. The child learns to walk, to speak, to master its world, driven by the urge to overcome its initial helplessness; the adult pursues skill, accomplishment, and significance out of the same root motive. Adler added the important observation of *compensation*: a felt weakness in one area often drives intensified development, sometimes in that very area, sometimes in another. The sickly child may become the devoted athlete; the boy with the stammer may become, like the ancient orator Demosthenes, a master of speech. Weakness, felt and confronted, can be the spur to strength. The sense of inferiority is not in itself a disorder; it is the universal human starting point, and the creative response to it is the story of a life.

## When Striving Goes Wrong

But the striving can go wrong, and Adler's most famous contributions describe how. When the normal sense of inferiority deepens into an overwhelming, paralyzing conviction of inadequacy, it becomes what he called an *inferiority complex* — a settled belief in one's own fundamental deficiency that cripples rather than motivates, producing discouragement, avoidance, and the retreat from life's tasks. And this complex can flip into its mirror image: the *superiority complex*, a compensating drive to dominate, to prove oneself above others, to seek the appearance of mastery precisely because one feels, underneath, so small. The bully, the tyrant, the relentless self-aggrandizer are, in Adler's reading, often driven not by real strength but by a desperate flight from felt weakness — superiority worn as a mask over inferiority.

The crucial distinction, for Adler, is *how* the striving is directed. The healthy person channels the drive to overcome inferiority into genuine competence and useful contribution; the neurotic channels it into the sterile pursuit of personal superiority, seeking to feel above others rather than to become genuinely capable. This is why Adler paid close attention to what he called the *style of life* — the unique, largely unconscious pattern each person develops, early in childhood, for pursuing their goal of significance. The style of life is oriented toward a self-created ideal, a "guiding fiction" of what one must become to overcome one's inferiority, and it shapes everything: how a person interprets their world, what they pursue, how they meet the tasks of living. To understand a person, for Adler, was to grasp the goal toward which their whole life secretly aimed.

## Social Interest

Adler's mature thought added a further element that distinguishes it sharply from the more self-enclosed psychologies of his contemporaries, and it may be his deepest insight. Mental health, he came to believe, lies not merely in overcoming inferiority but in the direction the overcoming takes — and the healthy direction is *outward*, toward others. He gave this the name *social interest*, or in his German, *Gemeinschaftsgefühl* — the feeling of community, the sense of connection with and concern for one's fellow human beings, the disposition to contribute to the common good. The mature, healthy person, Adler argued, channels the drive for significance into socially useful contribution: they find their significance in belonging, cooperating, and giving, rather than in a lonely striving to rise above.

The neurotic, by contrast, is turned *inward* — absorbed in personal superiority, private grievance, and self-concern, cut off from the community of others. For Adler, this self-absorption was the very signature of psychological trouble, and social interest the very mark of health. Meaning and wellbeing, on this view, are found not in the self's triumph but in the self's connection and contribution — in taking up one's tasks alongside others and giving something to the shared human enterprise. This was a strikingly social and moral vision of the psyche, one that located flourishing not in the resolution of private drives but in the quality of one's engagement with the world. It anticipated, by decades, the later psychological recognition that belonging and contribution are among the deepest human needs.

## The Adlerian Inheritance

Adler's influence is enormous and often unrecognized, precisely because so much of it has been absorbed into common sense. The very phrase "inferiority complex" is his, passed from clinical theory into the everyday speech of the world. His teleological emphasis — that we are pulled by goals and purposes, not merely pushed by past causes — anticipated the goal-directed, future-oriented models of much later psychology. His stress on competence, mastery, and the confidence that one can meet life's tasks foreshadowed the modern concept of self-efficacy. His insistence that meaning lies in contribution and belonging anticipated the findings of later research on the human need to belong. And his holistic, purposive picture of the person as a unified self oriented toward significance influenced humanistic psychology, cognitive therapy, and the whole tradition of self-help.

The resonance with the concerns of this course is direct. Adler's vision — that the antidote to felt inadequacy is not the pursuit of dominance but the movement toward competence and contribution, that a person becomes whole by aiming at a worthy goal and taking up their responsibilities alongside others — echoes strongly in the moral psychology this course will examine in Jordan Peterson, with its emphasis on adopting responsibility, developing competence, aiming at something meaningful, and finding significance in the burdens one takes up rather than in the gratifications one seizes. The Adlerian conviction that the striving self is healed by turning outward, toward useful contribution and genuine connection, is among the most durable insights of the whole depth-psychological tradition, and it runs quietly through much that came after.

## The Honest Reckoning

Adler's psychology, like Freud's and Jung's, was built largely from clinical intuition and broad interpretation rather than controlled evidence, and it inherits the corresponding weaknesses. Its central concepts are difficult to test and easy to apply after the fact: almost any behavior can be read as either a striving for superiority or a flight from inferiority, which makes the theory hard to falsify. Some of Adler's specific claims have fared poorly — his ideas about the determining influence of birth order on personality, once widely popular, find only weak and inconsistent support in modern research. And the framework as a whole is a broad interpretive scheme rather than a precise scientific theory, sharing the unfalsifiability that limits the entire depth-psychological tradition.

Yet Adler may be the founder whose core insights have aged the best. The importance of *goals and purpose* in human motivation, the reality of *self-efficacy* and the confidence of competence, the deep human *need to belong* and to contribute, the value of a future orientation over mere excavation of the past — these are not merely plausible; they are broadly supported by later, more rigorous psychology, which arrived independently at much that Adler had seen. His picture of the person as a unified, purposive self, striving to overcome felt inadequacy and healed by turning outward toward others in useful contribution, is at once psychologically penetrating and morally serious, and it has proved remarkably fruitful. Where Freud can seem dated and Jung mystical, Adler often seems simply ahead of his time — less a relic of early psychoanalysis than a quiet anticipation of much that we now take for granted. Having traced the founders and their heirs, we are ready to step back and ask what this whole tradition amounts to, and where its true legacy lies.
