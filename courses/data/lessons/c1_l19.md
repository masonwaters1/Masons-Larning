The psychologists we have studied descended into the depths of the mind and found there a self divided against itself — hidden from its own view, driven by forces it did not understand, seeking a wholeness it had lost. But the same century that produced depth psychology produced another great current of thought that arrived at the human predicament by a wholly different road. Where the psychologists began from the clinic and the case study, this current began from philosophy; where they asked what unconscious forces move us, it asked what it means to exist at all, to be free, to face our own death, and to seek meaning in a universe that offers none for free. This is existentialism, and it is the second great tradition on which this course is built. It took human existence itself — concrete, individual, suffering, choosing — and made it the center of thought.

## Philosophy of the Existing Individual

Existentialism is notoriously difficult to define, in part because it was never a single doctrine held by a school but a family of thinkers united more by a shared preoccupation than by a shared set of conclusions. Yet at its heart lies a single decisive move: the turn from abstract systems to the *concrete, existing individual*. Traditional philosophy had tended toward the general and the universal — building grand systems of thought that sought to comprehend all of reality in abstract categories, to explain the world *sub specie aeternitatis*, from the viewpoint of eternity. Existentialism rebelled against this. It insisted that such systems, however impressive, leave out the one thing that matters most: the actual, living, particular human being who exists, who must choose, who suffers and dies, and who cannot be reduced to a term in anyone's theory.

The existentialists began, therefore, not from the cosmos or the categories but from *existence* — from the situation of the human being thrown into the world, forced to live, forced to choose, and confronted with the raw facts of freedom, anxiety, meaning, and mortality. Philosophy, on this view, is not primarily a matter of solving abstract puzzles but of confronting the predicament of being a self: What am I? Am I free? What should I do with my freedom? How do I face my death? Where, if anywhere, is meaning to be found? These are not academic questions; they are the questions a person cannot avoid simply by being alive, and existentialism made them the whole of philosophy's proper concern.

## Existence Precedes Essence

The most famous slogan of the movement, coined by Jean-Paul Sartre, captures its central claim: *existence precedes essence*.[^1] To grasp it, consider a manufactured object — a paper knife. Before the knife exists, there is a concept of it in the mind of its maker: its purpose, its design, what it is *for*. Its essence — the definition of what it is — precedes its existence; it is made to fulfill a nature fixed in advance. For most of Western thought, the human being was understood on the same model: created by God, or defined by a fixed human nature, to fulfill a purpose set in advance. Essence came first; we exist to be what we already, in principle, are.

Existentialism reverses this for the human being. We have, it claims, no fixed, pre-given essence that determines in advance what we are and what we are for. We simply *exist* first — thrown into the world without instructions — and then, through our choices and actions, we *make* ourselves into whatever we become. There is no blueprint, no design, no human nature waiting to be fulfilled; there is only the raw fact of existence and the terrible freedom to define oneself. Man, in Sartre's formulation, is nothing else but what he makes of himself. This is the foundation of existentialist thought, and from it everything else flows: if we have no given essence, then we are radically free, radically responsible, and radically without excuse for what we become.

## The Weight of Freedom

This freedom is the great existentialist theme, and it is not the cheerful freedom of the advertisement but a heavy and vertiginous thing. If there is no fixed nature and no external authority to tell us what we must be, then we are wholly responsible for defining ourselves and our values — and there is no one to blame, no ground to stand on, no script to follow. We are, in Sartre's stark phrase, *condemned to be free*: condemned, because we did not choose to exist, and yet, having been thrown into existence, we are utterly responsible for what we make of it. Every choice is ours; every value is one we must affirm; the whole weight of a self-defined life rests on shoulders that have no external support.

From this freedom arises the characteristic existentialist mood: *anxiety*, or dread — not the fear of any particular thing, but the deeper unease that comes from confronting our own groundless freedom, our mortality, and the absence of any guaranteed meaning. Kierkegaard called it the dizziness of freedom, and a later lesson will examine it closely. Alongside anxiety comes the demand for *authenticity*: the existential task of *owning* one's freedom and finitude — living deliberately, as the author of one's own life — rather than fleeing into the comfortable evasions that Sartre named *bad faith*: the pretense that we are not free, that we merely play a fixed role, that we are carried along by circumstance or convention and cannot do otherwise. To live authentically is to refuse these evasions and to bear the weight of freedom with open eyes; to live in bad faith is to hide from it, losing oneself in the anonymous crowd, the assigned role, the borrowed opinion.

## After the Death of God

Behind all of this stands the great crisis that gives existentialism its urgency: the collapse of any given, external, guaranteed source of meaning. In the traditional world, meaning was underwritten from outside the self — by God, by a divinely ordered cosmos, by a fixed moral order woven into the nature of things. The modern condition, as the existentialists diagnosed it, is the condition in which these guarantees have failed — the situation Nietzsche named with the phrase "the death of God," which a later lesson will confront in full. When the external sources of meaning fall away, the individual is left facing a universe that provides no meaning of its own, and the terrible question arises: is existence, then, meaningless? Must the honest person confront an absurd cosmos indifferent to human longing, and either despair or somehow create meaning where none is given?

This is the problem of *nihilism*, and it is the dark sun around which existentialism orbits. The movement is, in large part, the record of the attempt to face this problem honestly — to confront the possible meaninglessness of existence without flinching, and to ask whether and how a human being can live a meaningful, authentic life in its shadow. The responses varied enormously, and this is why existentialism includes both the devoutly religious and the resolutely atheistic. On the religious side stand thinkers like Kierkegaard and Dostoevsky, for whom the confrontation with anxiety and absurdity drives toward faith. On the atheistic side stand Nietzsche, Sartre, and Camus, for whom no return to God is possible, and meaning, if it is to exist at all, must be forged by the individual out of nothing. What unites them across this vast divide is the shared starting point — the existing individual, free, anxious, and facing the question of meaning — and the shared conviction that this question is the one that matters most.

## Why It Matters Here

The relevance of all this to the concerns of this course could hardly be more direct, for existentialism poses the problem of meaning and nihilism more sharply than any other tradition, and it is the philosophical soil from which much of Jordan Peterson's thought grows. Peterson stands squarely within this lineage. His central preoccupation — the confrontation with nihilism and the search for meaning in a world where the old certainties have collapsed, the burden of freedom and the necessity of taking responsibility for one's existence, the demand for an authentic and courageous engagement with life rather than a flight into distraction or ideology — is the existentialist preoccupation, inherited and made his own. The thinkers this unit will examine, Kierkegaard and Nietzsche and Dostoevsky above all, are among his primary philosophical sources, standing alongside Jung as the deepest wells he draws from.

To understand Peterson, then, one must understand existentialism, and to understand existentialism one must feel the weight of the questions it refused to evade: that we are free and therefore responsible, that our freedom brings anxiety, that meaning is not handed to us and must somehow be found or made, and that the alternative to meaning is not comfort but the abyss of nihilism. These are the questions the rest of this unit will pursue through the thinkers who asked them first and asked them best — beginning with the solitary Danish philosopher who, before the word "existentialism" existed, had already made the existing individual the center of everything.

## The Honest Reckoning

Existentialism has its critics, and their objections deserve a hearing. As a philosophy, it can seem less a rigorous system than a *mood* — an orientation, a sensibility, a way of feeling the world — and its central concepts are often vague, more evocative than precise. Its emphasis on anguish, dread, and the burden of freedom can tip into a kind of self-indulgent melodrama, a romanticizing of despair that mistakes intensity for depth. Its radical claims about freedom, especially in Sartre's version, strain credibility: we are plainly not as unconditioned as the doctrine of absolute freedom suggests, shaped as we are by biology, circumstance, and the unconscious that the previous unit explored. And its subjectivism — its emphasis on individual choice as the ground of value — raises the worry that it cannot distinguish good choices from bad ones, authentic commitment from committed atrocity, leaving value dangerously untethered.

These are real limitations, and a serious engagement with existentialism must hold them in view. Yet the questions the tradition raised are not thereby dissolved; they are, if anything, confirmed as inescapable. Whatever the imprecision of its concepts, existentialism identified the defining predicament of modern life with unmatched force: the individual thrown back on their own freedom, deprived of guaranteed meaning, anxious before the void, and charged with the task of living authentically in the face of it. Its diagnosis of the modern condition — of what it is to seek meaning after the collapse of the old certainties — remains penetrating and, for many, exactly right. One may reject its answers and still find that it named the question one cannot stop asking. That is the mark of a philosophy that matters, and it is why existentialism, for all its vagueness and excess, remains one of the indispensable traditions for anyone who wishes to think honestly about how to live. With its shape before us, we turn to the man who drew it first.

[^1]: Existentialism is a philosophical movement, flourishing in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, centered on concrete human existence — freedom, choice, responsibility, anxiety, authenticity, and the problem of meaning. Its most famous formula, "existence precedes essence," was articulated by Jean-Paul Sartre in *Existentialism Is a Humanism* (1946): human beings, unlike manufactured objects, have no pre-given essence and must define themselves through their choices, which makes them "condemned to be free" and wholly responsible. Sartre also developed the notion of "bad faith" (self-deceiving flight from freedom). The movement spans religious existentialists (Kierkegaard, Dostoevsky, Marcel, Tillich) and atheistic ones (Nietzsche, Sartre, Camus), united by the starting point of the existing individual and the confrontation with meaninglessness or "nihilism" after the collapse of guaranteed sources of meaning (Nietzsche's "death of God"). Precursors include Kierkegaard, Nietzsche, and Dostoevsky; twentieth-century developments include Heidegger, Sartre, Camus, and de Beauvoir. Common criticisms are its vagueness, its emphasis on anguish, the implausibility of its stronger claims about freedom, and its potential subjectivism; its enduring value lies in its diagnosis of the modern predicament of meaning.
