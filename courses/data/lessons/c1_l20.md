Before there was a word for it, there was a man who lived it. Søren Kierkegaard, writing in Copenhagen in the middle of the nineteenth century, decades before "existentialism" entered anyone's vocabulary, had already made the existing individual the center of his thought and staked everything on the questions the movement would later inherit. He wrote against the great philosophical system of his age, against the complacent religion of his city, and against the whole tendency of thought to lose the living person in abstraction. He was strange, solitary, and often unbearable; he poured out a torrent of books under invented names, quarreled with his times, and died at forty-two, exhausted by his own intensity. And he left behind a body of work so penetrating in its psychology and so radical in its account of faith that he is rightly called the father of existentialism.

## Against the System

Kierkegaard's great philosophical antagonist was Hegel, whose vast System claimed to comprehend the whole of reality — history, nature, mind, God — within a single grand movement of rational thought. To the intellectual world of Kierkegaard's day, the System was the crowning achievement of philosophy, the mind's triumphant grasp of the Absolute. Kierkegaard found it absurd, and his objection went to the root of what philosophy is for. The System, he charged, *forgets the existing individual*. In its magnificent abstraction, it leaves out the one who is doing the philosophizing — the actual, particular, living person who must eat and choose and suffer and die, and who cannot be dissolved into a moment of the world-historical dialectic. You may build a palace of pure thought, Kierkegaard said, but you live in a shack beside it; the system-builder constructs a cathedral of ideas and then resides, absurdly, outside it.

The deeper point is that *existence cannot be captured in a system* because existence is not a matter of abstract thought at all but of concrete living — of choosing, committing, becoming, in time and in passion. Abstract objective thought surveys reality from nowhere, from the viewpoint of eternity; but the existing individual is always somewhere, always in the midst of a life that must be lived forward and decided now, without the luxury of the completed view. To think that one has comprehended existence in a system is to have missed existence entirely — to have exchanged the burning reality of a life that must be chosen for the cool abstraction of a thought that merely contemplates. Philosophy, Kierkegaard insisted, must return to the existing individual, or it becomes a grand evasion of the very thing it claims to understand.

## Truth Is Subjectivity

From this came Kierkegaard's most provocative and most misunderstood claim: that *truth is subjectivity*. It is easy to hear this as a crude relativism — as if he meant that whatever you feel to be true is true, that there are no facts. He meant nothing of the kind. He was making a distinction between two kinds of truth. For objective matters — mathematics, the facts of nature — truth is objective, a matter of correspondence, indifferent to the knower. But for the deepest matters of existence — how to live, what to believe, how to face death, whether there is a God — Kierkegaard argued that truth is not a detached, objective proposition one contemplates from a safe distance, but something that must be *appropriated*, *lived*, held in passionate personal commitment. Here what matters is not merely *what* you believe but *how* you hold it — whether you grasp it with the whole of your existence or merely entertain it as an idea.

Consider the difference between a person who can recite the correct doctrines about God with perfect objective accuracy but holds them coolly, at arm's length, changing nothing in their life — and a person who stakes their whole existence on God in fear and trembling, though they cannot prove a word of it. For Kierkegaard, the second person stands in the truth in a way the first does not, even though their "objective" beliefs might be identical, because existential truth is a matter of *inwardness*, of the passion and commitment with which one lives what one believes. This is not to abolish objective fact; it is to insist that in the matters that decide a life, correctness of proposition is not enough. Truth must be *become*, not merely known — enacted in the passionate inwardness of an existing self. To reduce the ultimate questions to objective information one might survey without being changed is, once again, to miss the entire point of being a self who must actually live.

## The Leap

All of this converges on Kierkegaard's central and most famous idea: the *leap of faith*. Faith, he insisted, is not the conclusion of an argument. It cannot be reached by reason, cannot be established by proof, cannot be arrived at by the accumulation of objective evidence — and any attempt to make it so fundamentally misunderstands what faith is. For the object of Christian faith is, to reason, an offense and an absurdity: the claim that the eternal, infinite God entered time as a particular, finite man — what Kierkegaard called the *absolute paradox* — cannot be made rationally palatable, cannot be smoothed into a reasonable proposition. It stands before reason as a scandal. And this means that faith can never be a matter of being persuaded; it can only be a *leap* — a passionate act of commitment made in objective uncertainty, without the security of proof, over what Kierkegaard memorably pictured as seventy thousand fathoms of water.

To have faith, then, is to *risk* — to commit the whole of oneself to what cannot be rationally guaranteed, to venture out over the abyss without a net. Objective certainty would abolish faith, not fulfill it; if the truths of religion could be proven like a theorem, there would be no need for faith and no room for it, for one does not have *faith* in what one can demonstrate. Faith lives precisely in the gap that proof cannot close, in the passionate embrace of what remains, to the intellect, uncertain and even absurd. It is the highest passion of a human life, and it is not a retreat from the difficulty of existence but the most demanding possible confrontation with it: to stake everything, in fear and trembling, on what one cannot see.

## The Knight of Faith

Kierkegaard dramatized this in his extraordinary meditation on the biblical story of Abraham, commanded by God to sacrifice his own son Isaac. Abraham is the *knight of faith*, and his situation reveals the terror and grandeur of what faith demands.[^1] By every ethical standard, the command is monstrous: a father must not kill his son, and the universal moral law condemns Abraham utterly. Yet Abraham obeys, in what Kierkegaard called a *teleological suspension of the ethical* — a setting-aside of the universal moral law in obedience to a private, absolute relation to God that cannot be justified in universal terms. Abraham cannot explain himself, cannot appeal to any principle that would make his act comprehensible or right to others; he stands utterly alone before God, and he obeys.

What makes Abraham the knight of faith rather than merely a would-be murderer is that he believes, "by virtue of the absurd," that he will receive Isaac back — that God will somehow make it right, though every rational expectation says otherwise. He gives up Isaac completely and yet expects him back completely, holding both at once in a movement of faith that reason cannot follow. This is faith at its most extreme: a solitary, absurd, passionate commitment to God that transcends the ethical, that cannot be shared or justified or understood from outside, that leaves the individual radically alone before the Absolute. Kierkegaard did not present Abraham as a comfortable example to imitate lightly; he presented him as an object of fear and trembling, to shatter the complacent assumption that faith is an easy or reasonable thing. Faith, in this vision, is the hardest and most fearful venture a human being can make.

## The Father of a Lineage

Kierkegaard died largely unread outside Denmark, but the twentieth century discovered him, and his influence became immense. He is the acknowledged father of existentialism, and both its wings descend from him: the religious existentialism of thinkers like Karl Barth and Paul Tillich, and, remarkably, the atheistic existentialism of Heidegger and Sartre, who took over his analysis of the existing individual, anxiety, and authentic choice while discarding his faith. His penetrating studies of anxiety, despair, and the inner life anticipated depth psychology itself; his critique of "the crowd" and "the public" — the anonymous mass in which the individual loses their selfhood and their responsibility — anticipated a century of reflection on conformity and mass society. Nearly every theme this unit will pursue was sounded first by this solitary Dane.

His resonance with the concerns of this course is strong and direct. The insistence on the individual against the crowd; the conviction that the deepest truths must be lived, not merely known; the account of faith as a courageous commitment made in the face of uncertainty rather than a set of propositions one can prove; the demand that a person stake their existence on something rather than drift in comfortable abstraction — all of this echoes powerfully in the moral psychology this course will examine, in which acting with commitment in the face of uncertainty, and taking responsibility for a life one cannot fully justify in advance, are central. Kierkegaard gave the modern world its first and in some ways its deepest portrait of what it is to be an existing individual who must choose, and choose in fear and trembling.

## The Honest Reckoning

Kierkegaard's thought is profound, and it is also genuinely vulnerable, and the vulnerabilities cluster around the leap itself. To the critic, the leap of faith can look like *irrationalism* — a licensing of belief without evidence, even against reason, that could justify committing oneself to anything at all, however false or dangerous, so long as one does so with sufficient passion. If faith is a leap beyond reason made in objective uncertainty, what distinguishes the knight of faith from the fanatic, or from the merely deluded? Kierkegaard's "truth is subjectivity," similarly, can be pressed toward the worrying conclusion that intensity of commitment matters more than correctness — that a passionately held falsehood is somehow superior to a coolly held truth, which cannot be right. And the story of Abraham, with its suspension of the ethical, raises the darkest question of all: how is the private, unjustifiable command that Abraham obeys to be distinguished from the voice that drives a fanatic to atrocity in the name of God?

These are serious objections, and Kierkegaard does not fully disarm them; the leap of faith remains, by design, a scandal to reason, and one may finally judge it a step too far. Yet even the critic should recognize what Kierkegaard saw with unmatched clarity. He grasped, before anyone, the predicament of the modern individual — thrown back on their own freedom, unable to reach the deepest truths by pure reason, anxious before the void, and compelled to commit without the security of proof. His psychology of anxiety and despair is astonishingly acute; his critique of the crowd and of complacent, propositional religion still cuts; his insistence that the ultimate questions must be *lived* rather than merely thought is a permanent corrective to the evasions of abstraction. Whether or not one can follow him into the leap, he identified the existential situation of modern humanity more deeply than any thinker before him, and everything that followed in this tradition — including the very different confrontation with the death of God that comes next — is in some measure a response to him.

[^1]: Søren Kierkegaard (1813–1855), the Danish philosopher regarded as the father of existentialism, wrote against Hegel's comprehensive rational "System" for forgetting the concrete existing individual, and argued that in matters of existence "truth is subjectivity" — appropriated in passionate inwardness rather than grasped as detached objective proposition. His central religious idea is the "leap of faith": since the object of Christian faith (the "absolute paradox" of the eternal God become a finite man) is objectively uncertain and offensive to reason, faith cannot be reached by proof but only by a passionate commitment made in uncertainty. In *Fear and Trembling* (1843, under the pseudonym Johannes de Silentio) he presented Abraham, commanded to sacrifice Isaac, as the "knight of faith," whose obedience requires a "teleological suspension of the ethical" and belief "by virtue of the absurd." His analyses of anxiety (*The Concept of Anxiety*, 1844) and despair (*The Sickness unto Death*, 1849) anticipated depth psychology, and his critique of "the crowd" anticipated later reflection on mass society. His influence extends to both religious existentialism (Barth, Tillich) and atheistic existentialism (Heidegger, Sartre). The principal criticisms are that the leap of faith risks irrationalism and that "truth is subjectivity" and the suspension of the ethical struggle to distinguish authentic faith from fanaticism.
