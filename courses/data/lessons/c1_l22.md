Before we leave Kierkegaard for the thinker who would confront the death of God, we must dwell on one more of his discoveries — perhaps the most psychologically penetrating of all, and the one that most directly anticipates both the existentialism to come and the depth psychology we have already studied. Kierkegaard was, among his other gifts, an extraordinary psychologist of the inner life, and in a small, strange book called *The Concept of Anxiety* he offered an analysis of dread so acute that it shaped a century of thought about the human condition. His central image has become one of the most famous in all of philosophy: anxiety is *the dizziness of freedom*. To understand what he meant is to understand something fundamental about what it is to be a free and finite creature who must choose.

## Fear and Anxiety

Kierkegaard begins with a distinction that seems simple but proves profound: the difference between *fear* and *anxiety*.[^1] Fear, he observed, always has a *definite object*. You fear a specific thing — the growling dog, the coming exam, the dark alley. Fear points at something; it can be named, located, and in principle escaped, because it is fear *of* something particular. Anxiety is different. Anxiety has *no definite object*. It is a diffuse, objectless dread — an unease that is not about any particular thing, a disquiet that hangs over us without a name, a mood rather than a response to a threat. When we are anxious, we cannot always say what we are anxious about, because anxiety is not *about* anything in the way fear is. It is a dread that arises from within the structure of existence itself.

This distinction, which Kierkegaard drew in the 1840s, became foundational. It reappears at the center of twentieth-century existentialism, in Heidegger's analysis of *Angst* and Sartre's account of anguish, and it anticipates the modern psychological interest in anxiety as distinct from fear. But Kierkegaard's interest was not merely descriptive. He wanted to know *what* this objectless dread is about — for even if anxiety has no particular object, it must be about *something*, some feature of existence itself that discloses itself in this strange, unsettling mood. And his answer is the heart of the matter: anxiety is about *freedom*.

## The Dizziness of Freedom

Here is Kierkegaard's famous image. Imagine standing at the edge of a high cliff, looking down into the abyss. You feel fear, certainly — the fear of falling, of the drop, of the definite danger. But Kierkegaard noticed that there is *something else* mixed into the experience, something stranger and more disturbing than fear. Along with the fear of falling, there arises a kind of vertigo that comes from the awareness that *you could throw yourself off* — that nothing, in this moment, actually prevents you from leaping, that the choice is entirely yours, that you are *free* to do this terrible thing. This dizziness — not the fear of the drop but the dread that rises from the awareness of one's own freedom before it — is anxiety. Anxiety is the dizziness of freedom.

What the cliff-edge reveals in a heightened form is present, Kierkegaard argues, in all of existence. Anxiety is the mood in which our *freedom* discloses itself to us — the vertiginous awareness that we are free, that our future is genuinely open, that nothing determines what we will do or become, that the possibilities before us are real and unactualized and *ours to choose*. This is exhilarating and terrifying at once, and the terror is anxiety. To be free is to face an open future in which anything might happen and in which I must decide; and the awareness of this open, undetermined possibility — the sheer weight of my own freedom — produces the objectless dread Kierkegaard names. Anxiety is freedom become conscious of itself, and dizzy at the sight.

## Anxiety and Possibility

This is why Kierkegaard connects anxiety so intimately with *possibility* and the *future*. Fear responds to an actual, present threat; anxiety hovers over the *possible*, the not-yet, the open horizon of what might be. It is oriented toward the future, toward the undetermined space in which I must project myself through choice. The more acutely we become aware of our freedom — of the fact that we are not fixed, that we might be otherwise, that our future is genuinely open and genuinely ours — the more we are exposed to anxiety. A creature with no freedom, wholly determined, would feel no anxiety; it is precisely because we are free, because our existence is a task and not a given, that we are anxious.

Kierkegaard developed this in his subtle and difficult analysis of Adam before the Fall. In the state of innocence, he suggests, Adam exists in a kind of untroubled immediacy — until the prohibition arrives ("you shall not eat"), and with it the *awakening of possibility*: the dawning awareness that he *could* disobey, that he is free, that a choice lies before him. This awakening of the possibility of freedom is what produces anxiety, and the anxiety *precedes* the actual choice; it is the psychological presupposition of the fall, the dizziness before the leap. Whatever one makes of the theological framing, the psychological insight is striking: anxiety arises at the threshold of freedom, in the awareness of possibility, before and beneath any particular decision. It is the mood of a creature who has become aware that it must choose.

## Anxiety as Teacher

It would be easy to conclude that anxiety is simply a torment to be escaped, and much of human life is organized precisely around escaping it — fleeing into distraction, into busyness, into the anonymous safety of the crowd, into anything that will quiet the dizzying awareness of our own freedom. But Kierkegaard makes a bold and characteristic move: he argues that anxiety, faced rightly, is a *teacher*. Because anxiety discloses our freedom, our possibility, and our finitude, it teaches us the truth about our existence — a truth we cannot learn while fleeing it. "Whoever has learned to be anxious in the right way," he wrote, "has learned the ultimate." To face anxiety is to face the reality of one's freedom and one's condition; to flee it is to flee oneself.

This reframes anxiety from a mere affliction into a doorway. To flee anxiety — into the distractions and conformities that let us forget we are free — is to live inauthentically, to hide from the truth of our own existence in the comfortable anonymity of the crowd. To face anxiety, by contrast, is to accept the disclosure it brings: that we are free, responsible, finite creatures who must choose, and who cannot offload that burden onto anyone else. For Kierkegaard, this confrontation is the path toward authentic selfhood and, ultimately, toward faith — for it is only the self that has faced the abyss of its own freedom and finitude that can make the leap. Anxiety, rightly understood, is not the enemy of a meaningful life but one of its preconditions: the mood in which we are forced to confront what we are, and thereby given the chance to become it.

## The Modern Resonance

The relevance of Kierkegaard's analysis to the concerns of this course, and to the thought of Jordan Peterson in particular, is direct and deep. The theme that anxiety signals the encounter with the *unknown*, with *possibility*, with the open and undetermined — and that this anxiety must be *voluntarily confronted* rather than fled if one is to grow into a genuine self — runs powerfully through Peterson's psychology. The idea that the voluntary confrontation with what we dread is the path to development, that fleeing anxiety through distraction and comfort is a flight from the truth of our own being, and that the courageous acceptance of the burden of freedom and possibility is central to a meaningful life — all of this has a Kierkegaardian root. When Peterson speaks of voluntarily facing the chaos and the unknown, of confronting rather than avoiding what makes us anxious, he is drawing on a lineage that begins with the Danish philosopher's discovery that anxiety is the dizziness of freedom.

Kierkegaard, remarkably, anticipated much of the depth psychology we studied in earlier units — the recognition that the inner life has hidden structures, that we flee difficult truths about ourselves, that the confrontation with what we avoid is the path to wholeness. He wrote before psychology existed as a discipline, yet his analyses of anxiety and despair read like the work of a master psychologist, and they fed directly into both the existentialism and the psychology that followed. The dizziness of freedom is his permanent contribution: the insight that our characteristic dread is not a malfunction but a disclosure — the reality of our freedom, made felt.

## The Honest Reckoning

Kierkegaard's phenomenology of anxiety is psychologically acute and enormously influential, but honesty requires two important qualifications. The first is that his analysis is framed within his theological project: anxiety is bound up, for him, with sin and the Fall, and it is ultimately resolved in faith — a framework not everyone shares, and one that colors his interpretation of what anxiety fundamentally *is*. The claim that anxiety is essentially *about freedom* rather than, say, about threat or uncertainty, is a philosophical interpretation, not an established fact, and modern clinical models of anxiety often frame it quite differently, as a response to perceived danger rather than a disclosure of freedom. Kierkegaard's account is a profound reading of a certain dimension of human dread; it is not the whole story, and it should not be mistaken for a complete psychology of anxiety.

The second qualification is more practical and more urgent. Kierkegaard's *existential* anxiety — the dizziness of freedom, the dread that discloses our condition — must not be conflated with *clinical* anxiety disorders, which are a different matter altogether. The romantic notion that anxiety is a teacher to be embraced can be genuinely harmful if applied to someone suffering from a debilitating anxiety disorder, for whom the responsible counsel is treatment and care, not the philosophical embrace of dread. Existential anxiety and pathological anxiety are not the same phenomenon, and Kierkegaard's profound insights about the former say nothing about the appropriate response to the latter. With that distinction firmly in place, his core contribution stands: the recognition that a certain fundamental human dread is objectless because it is about freedom itself, and that to face it honestly is part of what it means to become a self. It is a discovery that shaped everything that followed — and it prepares us for the thinker who would take the confrontation with freedom, meaning, and the abyss further than anyone: the philosopher who announced that God is dead.
