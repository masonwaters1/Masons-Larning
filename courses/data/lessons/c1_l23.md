We arrive now at the thinker who stands at the center of this course's deepest concern, the philosopher who diagnosed the spiritual crisis of the modern world more profoundly and more disturbingly than anyone before or since. Friedrich Nietzsche announced an event that he believed would define the coming centuries — an event so vast that most of his contemporaries could not yet perceive it, though it had already occurred. He called it the death of God. It is one of the most famous phrases in the history of thought and one of the most misunderstood, and everything in the modern confrontation with meaning and nihilism — everything this course is ultimately about — flows from it. To understand Nietzsche's death of God is to understand the question to which the rest of this course, and much of modern life, is an attempted answer.

## The Madman in the Marketplace

Nietzsche announced the death of God not in a treatise but in a parable, one of the most haunting passages he ever wrote.[^1] In *The Gay Science*, he tells of a madman who lights a lantern in the bright morning, runs into the marketplace, and cries out, "I seek God! I seek God!" The bystanders, many of whom do not believe in God, laugh and mock him. And then the madman turns on them with a terrible announcement: "God is dead. God remains dead. And we have killed him." He asks how they could have done it — how they drank up the sea, unchained the earth from its sun, sent it plunging through an endless nothing, into a cold and darkness in which they must now wander. The crowd falls silent and stares at him in astonishment. And the madman, seeing that they do not comprehend, throws down his lantern and says: "I have come too early. This tremendous event is still on its way — it has not yet reached the ears of men."

The parable is carefully constructed, and every element matters. The madman seeks God among people who no longer believe — and it is he, the madman, who grasps the enormity of what their unbelief means, while they, the comfortable unbelievers, do not. "We have killed him" — not God's enemies, not the wicked, but *we*, the whole of modern Western civilization, have done this deed, and mostly without noticing. And the madman has "come too early": the event has happened, but its meaning has not yet dawned. Nietzsche's point is precisely this — that the death of God is an accomplished fact whose consequences have not yet been felt, a catastrophe already underway that the world has not yet understood.

## What the Death of God Means

It is essential to grasp what Nietzsche did and did not mean, because the phrase is so easily misread. He did not mean that a literal deity had literally died; the statement is not a metaphysical claim about the existence of a being. It is a *cultural and historical diagnosis*. What Nietzsche meant is that *belief* in God — and with it the entire framework of transcendent meaning, objective values, cosmic purpose, and moral order that Christianity had provided to Western civilization for nearly two thousand years — had become, for modern people, no longer genuinely tenable, no longer a living and load-bearing faith. The foundation on which Western meaning and morality had rested was crumbling, whether or not people acknowledged it, and the whole edifice built upon it was therefore in peril.

And "we have killed him" identifies the killers as modern Western civilization itself. It was not the enemies of Christianity but its own children — the scientific revolution it helped birth, the Enlightenment's critical reason, the relentless will to truth that Christianity itself had cultivated, the whole movement of secularization — that had eroded the foundations of belief from within. The critical, questioning, scientific spirit of modernity had, over centuries, dissolved the plausibility of the faith that once grounded everything, and it had done so largely unconsciously, so that people went on holding the *values* Christianity had bequeathed — compassion, human dignity, equality, the very ideal of truth — while quietly abandoning the God who alone had made sense of them. This is the killing the madman describes: not a murder in a moment, but the slow, unintended, civilization-wide dissolution of a faith by the forces that faith had itself unleashed.

## The Coming of Nihilism

Here we reach Nietzsche's most important and most prophetic insight, the reason the madman has "come too early." The comfortable unbelievers in the marketplace do not grasp what they have done, because they continue to live as though nothing has changed — holding onto Christian-derived morality, meaning, and values while having removed the divine foundation that once supported them. They believe they can keep the fruits of Christianity while discarding its roots, retain the morality while abandoning the God. Nietzsche saw that this was profoundly unstable, and that when the implications finally sank in, the result would be *nihilism* — the collapse of all values, the dawning sense that there is no objective meaning, no cosmic purpose, no ground for morality, no answer to the question "why?" Nothing would matter, because nothing could any longer be shown to matter.

This is the "cold and darkness," the "endless nothing" into which the unchained earth plunges. Nietzsche foresaw nihilism as the defining spiritual crisis of the coming centuries — the crisis that would confront a civilization that had lost its God but not yet reckoned with the consequences. And he was, by any honest measure, a prophet in this. The twentieth century, with its collapse of traditional certainties, its desperate search for substitute faiths, and its catastrophic political ideologies that rushed to fill the void left by God, unfolded very much as Nietzsche warned it might. He saw the crisis coming while it was still gathering on the horizon, and he named it with a clarity no one has surpassed. The death of God, for Nietzsche, was not an occasion for atheist celebration but the announcement of an approaching abyss.

## Catastrophe and Opportunity

Yet Nietzsche did not regard the death of God as *only* catastrophe. The very passage that mourns the loss of the sun also glimpses, terrifyingly, a new horizon. If God is dead, then the old sources of meaning and value are gone — but this also means that human beings are, for the first time, radically *free* to create meaning and value themselves, to become the authors of their own significance where before they had received it from above. The death of God is at once the greatest loss and the greatest opportunity: the loss of the given meaning that oriented human life for millennia, and the opening of a terrifying new possibility — that humanity might rise to become the creator of its own values, might live without the comforting illusions, might affirm existence on new and self-made grounds. Nietzsche's later ideas — the will to power, the Übermensch, the revaluation of all values — are his attempt to describe how one might live, and even flourish, in the aftermath of the death of God. Those attempts will occupy the lessons to come.

## The Question at the Heart of the Course

We can now see why Nietzsche stands at the center of this course. The death of God, the collapse of the traditional framework of meaning, and the threat of nihilism that follows — this is the central problem that the entire course circles, and it is the problem to which the thought of Jordan Peterson is, above all, a response. Peterson takes the Nietzschean diagnosis as substantially correct and desperately urgent: the traditional foundations of Western meaning have eroded, the danger of nihilism is real and present, and the twentieth century's totalitarian ideologies were precisely the catastrophic pseudo-religions that rushed to fill the void the dead God left behind. The question of how to find or create meaning, how to avoid the abyss of nihilism and its murderous political substitutes, after the death of God — this is the question that animates Peterson's work, and it is Nietzsche's question. Everything else in this course can be read as one attempt after another to answer it.

## The Honest Reckoning

Nietzsche's diagnosis is powerful and genuinely prophetic, but it is not beyond challenge, and honesty requires marking the disputes. First, his central claim — that Western morality and meaning cannot survive the loss of their religious foundations — is contested. Many philosophers argue that morality can be grounded on secular bases, whether reason, human flourishing, evolved sentiment, or social agreement, without collapsing into nihilism; whether they are right is one of the deepest open questions in modern thought, and it connects directly to debates this course has met elsewhere about the grounding of values. Second, Nietzsche's assumption that belief in God has simply "died" is empirically questionable: religion has not vanished but persists and in places flourishes across the globe, and the secularization he took to be inexorable has proved more complicated than he supposed. Third, his own proposed alternatives to nihilism, as later lessons will show, are deeply problematic in their own right.

There is also a crucial matter of historical honesty that must be stated plainly, because it bears on how Nietzsche is received. After his mental collapse in 1889 and death in 1900, his work was edited, distorted, and appropriated by his sister Elisabeth, a German nationalist and antisemite, who twisted his philosophy into something congenial to the ideologies that would culminate in Nazism. This appropriation was a grotesque falsification. Nietzsche himself was a fierce critic of German nationalism, held antisemitism in contempt, and broke with the composer Wagner partly over exactly these prejudices. The Nietzsche who was later claimed by the worst movements of the twentieth century was in large part his sister's forgery, and the real Nietzsche must be recovered from beneath it. With that caution registered, his central achievement stands undiminished: he saw, earlier and more clearly than anyone, that the modern world faced a profound crisis of meaning as its ancient foundations dissolved, and he refused every comfortable evasion of it. Whether or not one accepts his diagnosis in full, no one who wishes to think honestly about the modern condition can avoid confronting the death of God. Having named the crisis, Nietzsche spent the rest of his thought trying to answer it — beginning with his account of the fundamental drive of all life.
