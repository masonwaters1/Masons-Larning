We come now to Nietzsche's most audacious and most disturbing project: not to ask what is good, but to ask where our very ideas of good and evil came from — to write a *history* of morality itself, and to expose the hidden origins of the values we take to be self-evident and eternal. In *On the Genealogy of Morals* and *Beyond Good and Evil*, Nietzsche argued that morality is not a fixed and heavenly given but a human creation with a traceable history, and that at the root of Western morality lies a great and largely forgotten struggle between two fundamentally opposed value systems, which he named *master morality* and *slave morality*. This genealogy is among the most influential ideas in modern thought, and among the most dangerous — and it connects, remarkably, to a story we have encountered in another course entirely.

## The Genealogical Question

Nietzsche's method was itself a revolution. Previous moral philosophy had mostly asked what actions are good, what duties we owe, what the good life consists in — taking for granted that the *categories* of good and evil were stable and given. Nietzsche asked a different and more unsettling question: *where did these categories come from?* What is the origin and history of our moral values? Whose interests did they serve, and out of what psychological soil did they grow?[^1] He proposed to treat morality not as an eternal truth handed down from above but as a historical and psychological *phenomenon* — something that came to be, that developed and changed, that could be traced to its sources and its motives. This is the *genealogical* method, and it would prove enormously influential, shaping later thinkers who sought to unmask the hidden histories and interests behind ideas presented as timeless and neutral.

The genealogical question is inherently suspicious, even subversive. To ask where a value came from, and whose interests it served, is to open the possibility that our most cherished moral convictions are not the voice of eternal truth but the residue of ancient struggles, the victory of particular parties, the disguised expression of particular psychological needs. Nietzsche pursued this possibility to its most provocative conclusion, and what he claimed to find at the root of Western morality was a war.

## The Morality of Masters

The first of the two value systems Nietzsche describes is *master morality* — the morality of the strong, the noble, the powerful: the aristocratic warrior classes of the ancient world. The masters, secure in their strength and confident in their vitality, defined "good" straightforwardly as *what they themselves were* — noble, strong, powerful, proud, capable, life-affirming. And they defined "bad" as what the weak and lowly were: contemptible, base, cowardly, pitiable. The fundamental distinction of master morality is thus *good versus bad*, where "good" means noble and strong and "bad" means base and weak — a distinction rooted in self-affirmation, in the powerful saying *yes* to themselves and their own qualities.

Master morality is, in this sense, primary and spontaneous: it arises from the strong affirming their own existence, valuing the qualities they possess — courage, strength, pride, power, honesty, the capacity for great deeds. It does not begin by condemning others; it begins by celebrating itself, and only secondarily regards the weak as "bad" in the sense of lesser, contemptible, of no account. It is the morality of those who have power and do not resent their condition, who look upon their own strength and find it good. Whatever one thinks of it, Nietzsche presents master morality as fundamentally *affirmative* — a saying-yes to oneself and one's own vitality.

## The Revolt of the Slaves

The second value system is *slave morality* — and here Nietzsche's genealogy becomes explosive. Slave morality is the morality of the weak, the oppressed, the powerless — those who are dominated by the masters and cannot prevail against them. Unable to act, unable to discharge their hostility in deeds, unable to affirm themselves through strength, the "slaves" accomplish something extraordinary: an *imaginary revenge*, a revaluation of values carried out in the realm of the spirit. They take the qualities of the masters — strength, power, pride — and rebrand them as *evil*. And they take their own qualities — weakness, humility, meekness, poverty, suffering, patience, obedience — and rebrand them as *good*. The distinction shifts from *good versus bad* to *good versus evil*, and now the "good" are the weak and humble, while the "evil" are the strong and proud.

This is the *slave revolt in morality*, and Nietzsche traces its psychological engine to what he calls *ressentiment* — the festering, impotent resentment of those who cannot act, a subject the next lesson will examine in depth. Where master morality begins with a spontaneous *yes* to oneself, slave morality begins with a *no* to the other, to the enemy, to the strong — and only derivatively affirms itself. It is *reactive* rather than active, born of resentment rather than self-affirmation, and it achieves in imagination and in morality the victory it cannot achieve in reality. The weak, unable to defeat the strong, defeat them by declaring them *evil* — and thereby win a moral superiority that compensates for their real powerlessness. Slave morality, on this account, is ressentiment become creative: resentment that has given birth to values.

## The Inversion of the Ancient World

Nietzsche's most provocative historical claim is that the triumph of Christianity was precisely this slave revolt, victorious on a civilizational scale. The values that the modern West takes as self-evidently moral — compassion, humility, meekness, equality, concern for the weak and the suffering, the exaltation of the poor and the lowly — are, Nietzsche argues, the triumphant values of the slave revolt: an *inversion* of the noble values of the ancient aristocratic world, in which the last became first and the meek inherited the earth. Where the ancient masters had exalted strength, pride, and power, Christianity exalted the humble, the poor, the suffering, and the meek, and condemned pride and strength as sin. This great transvaluation, which overthrew the aristocratic morality of antiquity and installed the morality of the weak in its place, is for Nietzsche the decisive event in the moral history of the West.

Readers of this course's companion study of Christianity and the modern world will recognize this claim with a start, for it describes *exactly* the same historical transformation that the historian Tom Holland traces in his account of how Christianity remade Western values — the revolutionary inversion by which the crucified victim was exalted over the conquering Caesar, and the dignity of the weak and lowly placed at the center of morality. Nietzsche and Holland are describing the *same event*: the Christian overturning of the ancient hierarchy of values. But they evaluate it in opposite ways. Where Holland regards the inversion with a complex admiration, seeing in it the source of the modern West's most cherished moral commitments, Nietzsche regards it with hostility, as the triumph of ressentiment and weakness over vitality and strength — a "life-denying" catastrophe that exalted the mediocre and crippled the highest human possibilities. The same revolution appears, through the two lenses, as the birth of everything good or the poisoning of the noble. That two such profound thinkers can describe an identical historical event and draw opposite verdicts is itself one of the most illuminating facts in the whole study of Western morality.

## Nietzsche's Verdict and Its Nuances

Nietzsche's own judgment is critical: he regards slave morality as life-denying, born of weakness and resentment, hostile to strength and vitality and excellence, and productive of a leveling mediocrity that drags the exceptional down to the common. He calls for a *revaluation of values* — a critical overcoming of slave morality and a recovery of a more life-affirming relation to existence. But his position is subtler than a simple endorsement of the masters against the slaves. He acknowledges the *cleverness* of the slave revolt, its astonishing spiritual creativity, and even credits it with having deepened the human soul, made human beings more *interesting*, more inward, more complex than the healthy but shallow masters ever were. He does not call for a naive return to master morality, and he is not, whatever his misreaders claim, straightforwardly endorsing cruelty or the oppression of the weak. His critique is a diagnosis and a provocation, aimed at making us question the origins and effects of values we had taken for granted — not a political program of domination.

## The Honest Reckoning

Master and slave morality is among Nietzsche's most influential ideas and among his most dangerous, and both must be acknowledged. The dangers first: the framework was catastrophically misused to justify contempt for the weak, cruelty, and authoritarian domination — readings that, as the previous lessons noted, distort Nietzsche's subtler intent but are made possible by his provocative language and his hostility to the "morality of pity." The historical genealogy is also speculative and contested; historians question whether the neat story of a "slave revolt" accurately captures the tangled real history of Western morality. And the argument arguably commits a version of the *genetic fallacy* — the error of thinking that exposing the lowly *origin* of a value refutes its *validity*. Even if Christian morality did arise from ressentiment, that historical fact would not, by itself, show that compassion, humility, or concern for the weak are *false* or *bad*; where an idea comes from is a separate question from whether it is true or good, and Nietzsche too often blurs the two.

Yet the psychological insight at the core is genuinely penetrating, and it changed how we think. The recognition that moral values have *hidden histories*, that they can serve *disguised psychological functions*, that some apparent moral goodness may mask envy, weakness, or a covert will to power, and that our most cherished values are worth examining critically rather than accepting as eternal givens — this is a real and lasting contribution, one that shaped Freud, Foucault, and the whole modern practice of moral suspicion. And the central historical observation — that Christianity inverted the values of the ancient world, exalting the weak and humble over the strong and proud — is, whatever one makes of Nietzsche's verdict upon it, historically real, as the parallel with Holland's very different assessment confirms. The framework is a powerful instrument of insight and a dangerous instrument of contempt, and the honest reader wields it with both hands: taking the genuine illumination while refusing the genetic fallacy and the disdain for the vulnerable that Nietzsche's rhetoric too easily invites. At the psychological root of the whole analysis lies the poisonous emotion Nietzsche named as the engine of the slave revolt, and to that emotion — ressentiment — we turn next.

[^1]: Nietzsche developed the distinction between "master morality" and "slave morality" in *Beyond Good and Evil* (1886) and *On the Genealogy of Morals* (1887), applying his "genealogical" method of tracing the historical and psychological origins of moral values. Master morality, the morality of the strong and noble, is structured as *good versus bad* (noble/strong versus base/weak) and is fundamentally self-affirming. Slave morality, the morality of the weak and oppressed, is structured as *good versus evil*: driven by *ressentiment*, it inverts the noble values, branding the strong "evil" and the weak "good," in an imaginary revenge. Nietzsche identifies the triumph of Christianity as a "slave revolt in morality" that overturned the aristocratic values of antiquity — the same historical inversion that Tom Holland traces in *Dominion*, though Holland evaluates it far more favorably than Nietzsche, who condemns it as "life-denying." The honest criticisms are that the genealogy is speculative and contested, that it was gravely misused to justify contempt for the weak (misreading Nietzsche's subtler intent), and that it risks the genetic fallacy of treating a value's origin as a verdict on its validity. Its enduring value lies in the insight that moral values have hidden histories and can serve disguised psychological functions.
