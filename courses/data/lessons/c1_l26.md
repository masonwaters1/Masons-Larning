Nietzsche was not content to diagnose the death of God and dissect the origins of morality; he wanted to answer the terrible question his own diagnosis posed. If God is dead, if the old sources of meaning and value have collapsed, if nihilism is the abyss opening beneath modern humanity — then how is one to live? What could take the place of the vanished God as a source of meaning and value? Nietzsche's two most famous positive answers, both introduced in his strange masterpiece *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*, are among the most demanding and most misunderstood ideas in philosophy: the *Übermensch* and the *eternal recurrence*. They are his attempt to describe how a human being might not merely survive the death of God but rise, through it, to a new and higher form of existence.

## Overcoming the Human

The *Übermensch* — translated variously as "overman," "superman," or "beyond-man" — is Nietzsche's ideal of a future human type who has overcome nihilism by becoming the *creator of their own values*.[^1] After the death of God, Nietzsche saw, humanity stands at a crossroads. It can sink into nihilism, or into the comfortable, meaningless mediocrity he elsewhere calls the "last man"; or it can rise to something higher — a human being who no longer needs God or any external authority to ground meaning, but who creates meaning and value out of their own strength, affirms life fully and without illusion, and stands as the author of their own significance. "Man is something that shall be overcome," Zarathustra proclaims. "I teach you the Übermensch." Humanity, on this vision, is not an end but a *bridge* — "a rope stretched between the animal and the Übermensch, a rope over an abyss" — a transition toward a higher form of life.

It is essential to say clearly what the Übermensch is *not*, because it is Nietzsche's most grotesquely distorted idea. It is *not* a master race; it is *not* a biological superman in the comic-book sense; it is *not* a warrant for the domination of the weak by the strong. That reading, propagated by the Nazi appropriation of Nietzsche, is a falsification. The Übermensch is fundamentally an ideal of *self-overcoming* and *value-creation* — of the individual who masters their own drives, creates their own values rather than passively receiving them from a dead religion or a herd society, and affirms existence fully. It is the highest expression of the will to power turned inward and upward: the creative self-overcoming that produces a new and higher humanity. Where God was once the source of meaning, the Übermensch proposes that *we* might become the creators of value — "the meaning of the earth," a this-worldly ideal to replace the otherworldly ideals that the death of God has swept away.

## The Meaning of the Earth

This phrase — "the meaning of the earth" — captures something crucial about Nietzsche's vision. The religious ideals he sought to replace were, in his view, *otherworldly*: they located meaning, value, and hope in a beyond — in heaven, in God, in an afterlife — and thereby, he charged, devalued *this* world, this life, this earth, treating it as a mere vale of tears to be endured on the way to something better. Against this, the Übermensch is emphatically *this-worldly*. "Remain faithful to the earth," Zarathustra urges, "and do not believe those who speak to you of otherworldly hopes." The task is to find and create meaning *here*, in this life, in this world, without appeal to any beyond — to affirm earthly existence fully rather than fleeing it toward an imagined heaven. The Übermensch is the human being who has learned to say yes to the earth, to the body, to this life, and to create meaning within it rather than beyond it.

This is Nietzsche's most affirmative and, in its way, most hopeful teaching — a call not to despair at the death of God but to seize the terrifying freedom it opens: the freedom, and the burden, of becoming the creators of our own values and the authors of an earthly meaning. But affirmation of *this* life, exactly as it is, with all its suffering, is a fearsomely demanding thing to ask of anyone. And so Nietzsche devised a second idea, stranger than the first, to serve as the ultimate test of whether one had truly achieved it.

## The Heaviest Weight

The *eternal recurrence* is Nietzsche's most haunting thought-experiment, and he called it "the heaviest weight." Imagine, he proposes, that a demon comes to you in your loneliest moment and says: this life, as you have lived it and are living it, you will have to live once more and innumerable times more — every pain and every joy, every thought and sigh, everything unspeakably small and great, all in the same succession, eternally, an infinite number of times. Would you throw yourself down and gnash your teeth and curse the demon? Or would you answer, "You are a god, and never have I heard anything more divine"? The eternal recurrence poses this question: if you had to live your life over and over, exactly the same, forever — would the thought crush you, or would you embrace it?

The eternal recurrence functions as a *test* of life-affirmation, the supreme criterion of whether one has truly said yes to existence. To be able to *will* the eternal recurrence — to embrace one's life so fully, including all its suffering, its failures, its griefs, that one would gladly live it again and again for eternity — is the ultimate affirmation, the complete overcoming of nihilism and of all resentment against existence. Nietzsche gave this attitude a name: *amor fati*, the love of fate — to love one's life and one's fate so totally as to will its eternal return, to want nothing to be different, not forward, not backward, not in all eternity. This is the opposite of the life-denying, otherworldly hope that devalues this life in favor of a beyond; the eternal recurrence demands the total affirmation of *this* life, exactly as it is, with nothing subtracted and nothing deferred to any heaven.

## The Existential Imperative

Whether Nietzsche meant the eternal recurrence as a literal cosmological doctrine — an actual claim that time is cyclical and all events truly recur — or as a thought-experiment and existential test, scholars still debate; he presents it in both ways at different moments. But its philosophical power lies less in its truth as cosmology than in its force as an *imperative*. Even if it is not literally true, it poses a demand: *live in such a way that you could will the eternal return of your life*. Live so that, faced with the demon's question, you could say yes — that you would embrace your life so fully as to want it repeated forever. It becomes a criterion for a life truly affirmed: to live each moment as though you would have to live it again eternally, and to shape a life you could bear, and even bless, to have return without end.

Together, the Übermensch and the eternal recurrence form Nietzsche's constructive response to the death of God. The Übermensch says: overcome yourself, create your own values, become the author of an earthly meaning. The eternal recurrence says: affirm your life so totally, including its suffering, that you would will its eternal return. Both are heroic, demanding, individualistic ideals — a vision of how to live meaningfully, even joyfully, in a world without God, without sinking into the abyss of nihilism. They ask everything of a person, and they offer, in place of the vanished divine guarantee, the terrible dignity of self-creation and total affirmation.

## The Response to Nihilism

The relevance to this course's central concern could not be greater. Nietzsche has posed the problem of meaning after the death of God more sharply than anyone, and here he offers his answer — an answer with which the thought of Jordan Peterson is in deep and critical dialogue. The theme of *amor fati*, of total life-affirmation including the affirmation of suffering, resonates strongly with Peterson's insistence that one must accept and even embrace the tragic and painful nature of existence, saying yes to being despite its brutality. The call to self-overcoming, to the voluntary bearing of responsibility, to the creation of meaning through one's own effort, echoes throughout Peterson's work. Yet Peterson is also a sharp *critic* of Nietzsche's solution, and the point of disagreement is crucial: Peterson doubts that individuals can simply *create their own values* out of nothing, by an act of will. He argues that values are grounded in deeper structures — in biology, in tradition, in the archetypal patterns the earlier units explored — and that the project of self-generated values is incoherent or, worse, dangerous, opening onto exactly the arbitrary self-assertion that produced the murderous ideologies of the twentieth century. For Peterson, Nietzsche brilliantly diagnosed the disease but prescribed a cure that could not work and that helped clear the way for catastrophe. This disagreement is one of the pivots of the entire course.

## The Honest Reckoning

The Übermensch and the eternal recurrence are profound responses to the deepest of problems, and they are also genuinely vulnerable. The Übermensch is Nietzsche's most misused idea, and the distortion into a racial or dominating "superman" — however contrary to Nietzsche's actual meaning — has done real and terrible harm. More philosophically, the central proposal that one can simply *create one's own values* by an act of individual will is deeply contested: if I merely invent my values, why are they binding, why do they command me, why are they anything more than arbitrary preferences dressed as commitments? Many critics, Peterson among them, argue that self-generated values collapse back into the very nihilism they were meant to overcome, and that meaning cannot be manufactured by fiat. The eternal recurrence, as cosmology, is scientifically dubious; as an existential test it is powerful but its exact status remains unclear. And the heroic individualism of both ideals can shade into an isolating elitism, a contempt for the ordinary and the communal, that neglects the goods of tradition, belonging, and shared life.

Yet the power of these ideas is undeniable, and it lies in their refusal to flinch from the hardest question. Nietzsche would not accept the comfortable evasions — neither a return to a faith he thought no longer tenable, nor a complacent nihilism that pretends nothing has changed. He insisted on facing the death of God directly and asking how a human being might affirm life and create meaning without external guarantees, and his answer, whatever its flaws, is among the most bracing ever given. *Amor fati* — the total affirmation of one's life, including its suffering — remains a genuinely powerful ideal, and the demand to overcome the complacent nihilism of the "last man" through self-overcoming and life-affirmation still speaks with force. Nietzsche posed the problem of post-religious meaning more sharply than anyone and offered a heroic, flawed, and dangerous answer to it — and both the sharpness of the question and the audacity of the answer are given their fullest and strangest expression in the book where these ideas first appear, the prophetic poem to which we turn next.
