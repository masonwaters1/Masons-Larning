Nietzsche considered it his masterpiece, the deepest book ever given to humanity, and it is certainly the strangest thing he ever wrote. *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* is not a work of philosophical argument at all but a poem, a prophecy, a mock-scripture — the story of a prophet who descends from his mountain solitude to teach a humanity that mostly refuses to listen. In it, Nietzsche gathered his central ideas — the death of God, the Übermensch, the eternal recurrence — and cast them not as propositions to be proven but as visions to be proclaimed, in a language deliberately echoing the Bible he sought to overturn. To read *Zarathustra* is to encounter Nietzsche at his most poetic, most ambitious, and most ambiguous, and to meet one of his most enduring and disturbing prophecies: the warning of the "last man."

## The Prophet Comes Down

The book opens with Zarathustra, having spent ten years in solitude on his mountain, deciding to descend among human beings to share the wisdom he has gathered.[^1] The name is a deliberate provocation: Zarathustra is the Persian prophet Zoroaster, one of the first to divide the world into good and evil — and Nietzsche makes him the mouthpiece for the *overcoming* of exactly that moral division, a prophet who returns to undo his own original teaching. The scriptural style is unmistakable and intentional. The book is written in the cadences of prophecy and gospel, full of parables, speeches, and symbolic episodes, parodying the sacred texts of the tradition it means to supersede while offering itself as a kind of new gospel for a post-religious age. Nietzsche chose this form deliberately: his ideas were not meant to be cool arguments but transformative visions, calls to a new way of living, and the prophetic voice was the only one grand enough to carry them.

Zarathustra descends with a teaching to give, and it is the teaching we met in the previous lesson: "I teach you the Übermensch. Man is something that shall be overcome." He comes to announce that God is dead and to proclaim the new ideal that might take God's place — the human being who overcomes themselves and creates new values, faithful to the earth. But when he arrives in the town and proclaims this to the assembled crowd, he discovers that they do not understand him, and do not want to. And what happens next is one of the most prophetic passages Nietzsche ever wrote.

## The Last Man

Seeing that the crowd does not respond to the Übermensch, Zarathustra tries to reach them by describing its opposite — the most contemptible thing he can imagine, the figure he calls the *last man*. The last man is the human being who has abandoned all higher aspiration, who no longer strives, no longer creates, no longer risks anything, but seeks only comfort, security, and the avoidance of all difficulty. The last man has "his little pleasure for the day and his little pleasure for the night." He is risk-averse, mediocre, comfortable, and self-satisfied; he has made everything small; he no longer dreams of anything great, no longer aspires to anything beyond his own contentment. "What is love? What is creation? What is longing? What is a star?" — so asks the last man, and blinks. He is the human being who has given up on meaning itself in exchange for comfort, and who is quietly proud of having done so.

Nietzsche's genius, and his warning, comes in the crowd's response. When Zarathustra describes the last man, meaning to horrify them into aspiring to something higher, the crowd is delighted. "Give us this last man!" they cry. "Make us into these last men!" They *want* to be the last man; they prefer comfort to greatness, security to striving, the little pleasures to the terrible dignity of self-overcoming. This is the chilling heart of the passage: the danger, after the death of God, is not only the dramatic abyss of nihilistic despair but something quieter and more insidious — that humanity might sink not into creative self-overcoming but into a comfortable, contented, meaningless mediocrity, and might even *cheer* its own diminishment. The last man is nihilism wearing the face of contentment, and the crowd embraces it gladly.

## The Three Metamorphoses

Among the book's most famous teachings is Zarathustra's parable of the three metamorphoses of the spirit — a compressed image of the path toward the free creation of values. The spirit, Zarathustra says, becomes first a *camel*, then a *lion*, and finally a *child*. The camel is the spirit that bears burdens: reverent, dutiful, it kneels to take on the heavy weight of tradition, obligation, and the "thou shalt" of received values, carrying them out into the desert. This is a necessary stage — one must first bear the weight of one's inheritance, master the discipline of what has been handed down. But it is not the final stage.

In the desert, the camel becomes a *lion*, whose task is to win freedom by fighting the great dragon named "Thou Shalt," on whose every scale a received value gleams. The lion cannot yet create new values, but it can say a sacred *No* — it can destroy the old values, break the authority of the "thou shalt," and clear a space of freedom through its defiant "I will." Yet even the lion is not the end, for destruction and freedom are not the same as creation. And so the lion must become a *child* — for the child is innocence and forgetting, a new beginning, a "sacred Yes." Only the child can create new values, can begin again, can play the game of creation with the innocence of one who has passed through duty and defiance and come out the other side. The three metamorphoses trace the path of the free spirit: from the bearing of tradition, through the destruction of the old, to the creation of the new — a compressed vision of how one might become a creator of values in the wake of the death of God.

## Faithful to the Earth

Throughout the book, Zarathustra returns to a handful of central exhortations that distill Nietzsche's affirmative vision. "Remain faithful to the earth," he urges, "and do not believe those who speak to you of otherworldly hopes" — the insistence, against all religious flight into a beyond, that meaning must be found and made *here*, in this life, this body, this world. "Become who you are" — the call to self-creation, to becoming fully and authentically oneself rather than a copy of the herd. And "man is a rope stretched between the animal and the Übermensch, a rope over an abyss" — the vision of humanity as a bridge, a transition, a dangerous crossing toward something higher, not an end in itself.

The book also contains Zarathustra's struggle with his "most abysmal thought" — the eternal recurrence, which he can barely bring himself to affirm, which sickens and terrifies him before he can rise to embrace it. This is significant: even Zarathustra, the prophet, finds the total affirmation of life almost unbearable, and must wrestle with it as with a mortal illness before he can say his great Yes. Nietzsche does not present life-affirmation as easy or automatic; he presents it as the hardest of all spiritual achievements, won only through struggle with the abyss. The book ends not in triumphant resolution but in ongoing striving, with Zarathustra going out once more to his work — for the overcoming Nietzsche envisions is not a destination reached but a task never finished.

## The Warning We Live Inside

Of all the book's teachings, it is the *last man* that has proved most prophetic and most relevant to this course's concerns, and it is a favorite theme of Jordan Peterson's. The danger Nietzsche identified — that a civilization, having lost its God and its higher aspirations, might sink not into dramatic despair but into a comfortable, risk-averse, pleasure-seeking mediocrity, abandoning meaning for security and even priding itself on the exchange — is a warning that many now feel describes a real feature of modern life. Peterson invokes the last man as a diagnosis of a genuine modern decadence: the temptation to trade the difficult pursuit of meaning for the easy pursuit of comfort, to make everything small and safe, to blink rather than to reach for a star. The three metamorphoses, too, resonate with Peterson's themes of maturation — the bearing of responsibility, the critical overcoming of inherited constraints, and the creative renewal that lies beyond. And the mode of the book itself — teaching through myth, symbol, and story rather than dry argument — is one Peterson has adopted in his own work, and on which he has lectured directly. *Zarathustra* is not a museum piece; it is a warning many believe we are living inside.

## The Honest Reckoning

*Thus Spoke Zarathustra* is a work of extraordinary power, and its very form generates its deepest difficulties. Because it teaches through poetry, prophecy, and symbol rather than argument, it is radically *ambiguous* — resistant to precise interpretation, open to many readings, and therefore easily misread, including in dangerous directions. The ideas it dramatizes carry all the difficulties the previous lessons noted: the misusable Übermensch, the problematic project of self-created values, the elitism latent in the heroic ideal. And the book's contempt for the "herd" and the "last man" can shade into an ugly disdain for ordinary people and ordinary happiness — a contempt for the very comforts and securities that most human lives are rightly built around. It was, moreover, central to the Nazi appropriation of Nietzsche, its images torn from context and pressed into service for a cause its author would have loathed.

Yet as a diagnosis of the modern spiritual condition, the book is unmatched, and its central warning has only grown more urgent with time. The figure of the last man — the danger that a civilization might abandon meaning for comfort and cheer its own diminishment — is a prophetic insight of disturbing accuracy, and it names a temptation that any honest observer of modern affluence can recognize. The three metamorphoses offer a genuinely illuminating image of spiritual development; the call to remain faithful to the earth and to affirm life in the face of the abyss retains real power; and the book's refusal to offer easy comfort, its insistence that life-affirmation must be wrested from a struggle with despair, gives it a hard-won seriousness. One may reject Nietzsche's answers and recoil from his contempt for the ordinary, and still find in *Zarathustra* a diagnosis of the modern predicament that no comfortable philosophy has surpassed. Having followed Nietzsche's constructive vision to its strangest expression, we return to the dark psychological root beneath his critique of morality — the poisonous emotion that, he claimed, gave birth to the whole world of "good and evil."
