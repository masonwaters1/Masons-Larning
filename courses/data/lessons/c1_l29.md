The philosophers we have studied confronted the death of God, the burden of freedom, and the crisis of meaning through argument and analysis. But there was a Russian novelist working in the same decades who confronted these questions in a different and, many would say, deeper way — not through abstract philosophy but through the creation of unforgettable human beings, souls placed under such spiritual pressure that the deepest questions of existence were forced out of them. Fyodor Dostoevsky is often called a proto-existentialist, and he is arguably the greatest psychologist in all of literature; the thinker at the center of this course, Jordan Peterson, reveres him above almost anyone. And Dostoevsky's first great existential creation, the nameless narrator of a short, savage book called *Notes from Underground*, is nothing less than the definitive portrait of the resentful modern soul — the very psychology of ressentiment we have just examined, given flesh and a voice.

## The Man from Under the Floor

Dostoevsky's life was itself a school of extremity.[^1] Arrested as a young man for belonging to a circle of political radicals, he was subjected to a mock execution — led out before a firing squad, made to believe he was about to die, and reprieved only at the last possible moment — before being sent to years of hard labor in a Siberian prison camp. He suffered from epilepsy, struggled with a ruinous gambling addiction, and forged, through all of it, a profound and hard-won Orthodox Christian faith. His novels are not tidy philosophical treatises but philosophical dramas of the most intense kind, in which ideas are not argued but *incarnated* — lived out, and tested to destruction, in the souls of his characters.

*Notes from Underground*, published in 1864, is the book that Nietzsche admired and that is frequently called the first work of existentialism. Its narrator, the "Underground Man," is a nameless, bitter, intensely self-conscious former civil servant who has withdrawn from the world into a squalid isolation — the "underground" of the title — from which he addresses the reader in a long, contradictory, self-lacerating confession. He is spiteful, envious, humiliated, and consumed by wounded vanity; he takes a perverse, almost gleeful pleasure in his own suffering and spite; he is paralyzed by an excess of self-consciousness that makes action impossible and turns every impulse back on itself in an agony of second-guessing. He is, in short, the resentful soul dramatized with a psychological precision that had never been achieved before and has rarely been equaled since.

## Ressentiment Given a Voice

The Underground Man is the literary incarnation of everything the previous lesson described. Unable to act, unable to prevail, unable to discharge his hostility in the world, he has turned it inward, where it festers and poisons — exactly the pathology Nietzsche named as ressentiment. His resentment has no outlet and therefore no end; it circles endlessly, feeding on humiliations real and imagined, curdling into a spite that harms no one so much as himself. He is reactive to the core: he defines himself against others, against the men of action he envies and despises, against the whole world that has slighted him, and he cannot construct a self out of anything but grievance. When he does finally act — in the novel's second half, cruelly toward a young prostitute named Liza who shows him kindness — he can only wound, because kindness itself is unbearable to a soul organized around resentment, and he drives away the one person who might have redeemed him.

Dostoevsky's achievement here is to have made the abstract psychology of ressentiment vivid, particular, and horribly recognizable. We do not merely understand the resentful soul; we are trapped inside it, forced to inhabit its logic from within, to feel the awful pleasure it takes in its own degradation and the way it poisons every possibility of connection and love. This is why Peterson and others return to the Underground Man again and again: he is the definitive case study of the resentment that the previous lesson analyzed, the demonstration in flesh and blood of what it is to be consumed by an impotent, festering grievance — and of how such a soul, offered love, will spit it back.

## The Assault on the Crystal Palace

But *Notes from Underground* is not only a psychological portrait; it is also a philosophical bombshell, and its central argument is what makes it a founding text of existentialism. The Underground Man launches a furious assault on the confident rationalism of his age — the belief, associated with the radical thinkers of nineteenth-century Russia, that human beings are fundamentally rational creatures who pursue their own advantage, that if you simply show people their true self-interest they will act on it, and that a perfectly rational society could therefore be engineered to produce universal happiness. This utopian dream Dostoevsky mocks under the image of the "Crystal Palace" — the gleaming, rational, scientifically ordered society in which every human need is calculated and met, and everyone is finally, rationally, happy.

The Underground Man will have none of it, and his refusal is the heart of the book. Human beings, he insists, are *not* fundamentally rational, and do *not* simply pursue their own advantage. There is something a person values more than happiness, more than prosperity, more even than their own interest — and that is their *freedom*, their own free will, their capacity to choose. And to prove that they are free, that they are not a mere mechanism, human beings will deliberately act *against* their own advantage; they will choose suffering, folly, and destruction, precisely to demonstrate that they are not predictable machines. Show a man the rational calculation that two times two makes four, hand him a life ordered entirely by such calculations, and he may reject it out of sheer spite, may insist that "two times two makes five is sometimes a very charming thing too" — not because five is correct, but because the freedom to say it is dearer to him than the truth.

## The Refusal to Be a Piano Key

Behind this provocation lies a serious and prophetic argument. The Underground Man refuses to be reduced to what he calls a "piano key" or an "organ stop" — a thing played upon by the laws of nature, a mechanism whose every action can be predicted and controlled. What he values above all is the assertion of his own irreducible will, his freedom to be unpredictable, capricious, even self-destructive, because that freedom is the mark of his humanity, the one thing that cannot be taken from him and folded into anyone's rational system. Better to be free and miserable than to be a contented cog in a perfectly rational machine. This is the primacy of freedom over reason, of the individual will over the system — and it is a foundational existentialist theme, the refusal to let the human being be dissolved into a term in someone's theory of universal happiness.

Dostoevsky's target was the utopian socialism of his day, and his warning was prophetic. He saw that the dream of engineering a perfectly rational, happy society ignored the deepest fact about human beings — their need for freedom — and that the attempt to force people into the Crystal Palace, to make them happy against their nature, would not produce paradise but tyranny. The rational utopia, imposed on creatures who value their freedom above their happiness, could only become a prison. This insight — that utopian rationalism runs aground on the irreducible human demand for freedom, and turns coercive when it tries to override it — anticipates the entire twentieth-century critique of totalitarian ideology, and it connects directly to the theme of ideological possession that a later lesson in this cluster will explore.

## The Diagnosis, Not the Cure

It is essential to understand that the Underground Man is *not* a hero, and that Dostoevsky does not endorse him. This is where careless readings go wrong. The Underground Man's assertion of freedom is real and philosophically serious, but it is bound up with spite, cruelty, paralysis, isolation, and self-torment; the freedom he defends so fiercely he uses only to wound himself and others. He is a diseased soul, and the disease is precisely the resentful, isolated self-assertion cut off from love, faith, and genuine connection with others. Dostoevsky presents him as a warning, not a model: this is what the human being becomes when freedom is exercised in resentment and isolation, when the irreducible will affirms itself only in spite and never in love.

The Christian answer that Dostoevsky believed in is present in the book only by its absence — in the shape of the hole where love and faith should be. The Underground Man's misery is the misery of a freedom that has found no fulfillment in love, a self-consciousness that has curdled into self-torment, a will that asserts itself only negatively. What redeems freedom, Dostoevsky implies, is not the spiteful self-assertion of the underground but the free gift of oneself in love — the very thing the Underground Man cannot do, and whose absence damns him. The novel thus diagnoses the resentful modern soul with terrifying accuracy while pointing, by contrast, toward the cure it withholds: the freedom that fulfills itself not in resentment but in love, a theme Dostoevsky's later novels would take up directly.

## The Honest Reckoning

*Notes from Underground* is a work of extraordinary psychological penetration, and its portrait of resentment and its critique of naive rationalism are both profound and prophetic. Yet honesty requires noting its limits and ambiguities. The celebration of irrational freedom — the claim that a person will and even should act against their own interest to prove they are free — is philosophically double-edged: Dostoevsky presents it critically, since the Underground Man is plainly miserable, but detached from that context it can read as a dangerous irrationalism, a license for caprice and self-destruction dressed up as authenticity. The assault on rationalism can also be overstated. Reason and rational self-interest do in fact explain a great deal of human behavior, and the sweeping claim that human beings are fundamentally irrational is one-sided; the truth is that we are *both* rational and something more, and the Underground Man, in his fury, sees only the something more.

It must also be remembered that this is a work of literature, not a systematic philosophy: its "arguments" are dramatizations voiced by a deliberately unreliable and diseased narrator, not demonstrations Dostoevsky asks us to accept as proven. What the book offers is not a proof but a *seeing* — a vision of the resentful soul and of the human demand for freedom so vivid that it has the force of revelation. And as such a vision it is unsurpassed: it named the psychology of ressentiment before Nietzsche gave it a name, anticipated the existentialist primacy of freedom, and foresaw the coming collision between utopian rationalism and the irreducible human will. Its influence on Nietzsche, Sartre, Camus, and modern psychology alike is profound. Dostoevsky would go on to explore the consequences of the ideas glimpsed here — the freedom that becomes destruction, the self that places itself beyond good and evil — in the great novels to come, beginning with the story of a young man who murdered an old woman to prove that he was extraordinary.
