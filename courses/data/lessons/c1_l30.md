If the Underground Man is Dostoevsky's portrait of the resentful soul in paralysis, his next great creation is that soul in action — a young man who does not merely stew in his alienation but acts on a terrible idea, and discovers, at catastrophic cost, what that idea actually means. *Crime and Punishment*, published in 1866, follows Rodion Raskolnikov, an impoverished former student in the sweltering slums of St. Petersburg, as he murders an old pawnbroker to test a theory — and then suffers the psychological consequences of having placed himself, deliberately, beyond good and evil. It is one of the supreme achievements of the psychological novel, and it dramatizes, years before Nietzsche formalized the idea, exactly what happens when a human being tries to become a superman above morality.

## The Theory of the Extraordinary Man

Raskolnikov is possessed by an idea, and the idea is the engine of the novel.[^1] He has written an article, and worked out a theory, according to which humanity divides into two fundamentally different classes. There are the "ordinary" people — the vast mass of humanity, who exist merely to reproduce their kind and who are bound to obey the moral law and the conventions of society. And there are the "extraordinary" people — the rare few, the Napoleons and lawgivers and world-historical figures, who possess the right to *transgress*: to step over the ordinary moral boundaries, to shed blood if necessary, in the service of a great idea or a higher purpose. For the extraordinary man, conventional morality does not apply; he is permitted what is forbidden to others, because his greatness and his mission place him above the common law. The ordinary man who transgresses is a criminal; the extraordinary man who transgresses is a benefactor of humanity.

Raskolnikov's murder of the pawnbroker is, at least in part, an *experiment* — a test to determine which class he belongs to. Is he an extraordinary man, one of those who has the right to transgress, the will to shed blood and step over the corpse and feel no remorse? Or is he merely ordinary, a "louse" like everyone else, trembling before a moral law he lacks the strength to break? He murders the old woman, and her innocent sister who stumbles in, to find out whether he is a Napoleon or a nobody — whether he has the right and the will to place himself beyond good and evil. The crime is committed not primarily out of need or passion but out of an *idea*, and this is the deepest theme of the book: a man driven to murder by an abstract theory.

## The Superman Before Nietzsche

There is something remarkable here that no reader of the previous lessons can miss. In Raskolnikov's theory of the extraordinary man who stands above conventional morality, Dostoevsky articulated a version of the very idea that Nietzsche would later formalize as the Übermensch and master morality — the strong individual who transcends the "slave morality" of the herd and legislates his own values *beyond good and evil* — and Dostoevsky did so *before* Nietzsche, and to devastatingly critical effect. Where Nietzsche would present the self-overcoming individual who creates his own values as an aspiration, Dostoevsky put the idea into the hands of an actual human being, let him act on it, and then dramatized the *consequences* with pitiless honesty. *Crime and Punishment* is, in effect, a preemptive refutation of the Nietzschean superman — a demonstration, worked out in the soul of one young man, of what happens when a person genuinely attempts to place himself beyond the moral law.

And what happens is catastrophe — but not the catastrophe one might expect. Raskolnikov is not caught by clever detective work; he is not undone by external circumstance. He is destroyed from *within*, by his own conscience, and this is Dostoevsky's profound answer to the superman idea.

## The Refutation from Within

Having committed the murder to prove he is extraordinary, Raskolnikov discovers that he *cannot bear it*. The theory fails him not intellectually — he continues, for much of the novel, to defend it in argument — but *existentially*, in the depths of his own being. He is seized by a horror he cannot rationalize away: a feverish guilt, a suffocating dread, a paranoid terror of discovery, and, most tellingly, a profound *alienation* from the rest of humanity, a sense of having cut himself off from all human connection by his act. He becomes physically ill; he cannot think straight; he is haunted, tormented, unable to rest. His conscience, which his theory told him he should be able to override, rebels against him with a force he never anticipated and cannot suppress.

This is the heart of Dostoevsky's argument, and it is an argument made through psychology rather than logic. Raskolnikov cannot become the extraordinary man because his own *moral nature will not permit it*. The murder does not liberate him into a higher freedom; it destroys him, isolates him, and poisons his soul. What the theory treated as a mere convention to be stepped over — the prohibition against murder — turns out to be written into the very structure of the human soul, so that to violate it is to violate oneself, to tear something at the root of one's own being. Dostoevsky's claim, dramatized rather than argued, is that a human being cannot simply place himself beyond good and evil without catastrophic inner consequences — that the moral law is not an external rule imposed from outside but a reality woven into the constitution of the human person, and that the attempt to transcend it destroys the one who tries.

## Sonya and the Way Back

If Raskolnikov's descent shows the destruction wrought by the transgressive idea, the path of his redemption shows Dostoevsky's Christian answer. That path runs through Sonya Marmeladov, a young woman driven into prostitution to save her destitute family, who becomes, in her humility, suffering, and faith, the moral center of the novel. Sonya has committed a terrible sin of her own, yet she has not lost her soul; she bears her degradation with a self-giving love and an unshaken faith that stand as the exact opposite of Raskolnikov's proud, isolating theory. Where Raskolnikov transgressed out of pride, to prove himself extraordinary, Sonya has sacrificed herself out of love, and her suffering has deepened rather than poisoned her.

It is Sonya who shows Raskolnikov the way back — not through argument, but through love and the summons to accept his suffering. She urges him to confess, to bow down and kiss the earth he has defiled, to accept the punishment that will begin his regeneration. Through her, and through the gradual acceptance of his own guilt and the suffering it demands, Raskolnikov is led at last to confession and to Siberian prison — where, in the novel's closing pages, under the influence of Sonya's love, the first stirrings of spiritual rebirth begin. Dostoevsky's answer to the destruction wrought by the transgressive idea is redemption through the acceptance of guilt and suffering, and through love: the way back from having placed oneself above humanity is to bow down, to accept one's shared humanity and one's guilt, and to be restored through suffering and self-giving love.

## Everything Is Permitted

Raskolnikov's theory is, at bottom, a version of a proposition that will haunt all of Dostoevsky's great novels: that for the man who has placed himself beyond God and conventional morality, *everything is permitted* — the strong may do as they will, unbound by any law they have not chosen. *Crime and Punishment* is Dostoevsky's first sustained dramatization of the falsity and horror of this idea. The man who tries to live as though everything were permitted is not liberated but destroyed; the abolition of moral limits leads not to a higher freedom but to psychological and moral catastrophe. This is Dostoevsky's response to the nihilism that the death of God threatens to unleash — not a philosophical proof that morality is objective, but a searing demonstration, in the soul of one young man, that the human being who abolishes the moral law abolishes himself.

The relevance to this course's concerns, and to the thought of Jordan Peterson, is direct and deep. Here is the dramatization of the consequences of the "superman beyond morality" idea — a preemptive critique of the Nietzschean and, later, the ideological self-deification that the twentieth century would enact on a mass scale. Here is the psychology of transgression, guilt, and conscience, and the claim that the moral law is written into the soul. Here is the theme of a man driven to atrocity by an *abstract idea*, an idea that possesses him and overrides his humanity — the theme of ideological possession that a later lesson will pursue. And here is the vision of redemption through the acceptance of suffering, responsibility, and love. Peterson returns to *Crime and Punishment* repeatedly, precisely because it stages, with unmatched psychological depth, the collision between the nihilistic idea and the moral structure of the human soul.

## The Honest Reckoning

*Crime and Punishment* is a psychological and moral masterpiece, and its dramatization of conscience, transgression, and redemption is profound. But its status as an *argument* must be understood honestly. It is a literary dramatization, not a philosophical proof: it shows one man destroyed by his transgression, but it does not, and cannot, prove that all who transgress must be so destroyed, or that morality is objective. A committed immoralist might reply that Raskolnikov simply lacked the strength to bear what he had done — that his collapse reveals not the objectivity of the moral law but only the weakness of a particular conscience, and that a truly extraordinary man would have felt no such torment. Dostoevsky cannot refute this from within the novel; he can only offer his vision, which is that conscience is universal and its violation ruinous. The Christian redemption arc, likewise, reflects Dostoevsky's own faith and is offered as a witness rather than a neutral demonstration.

Yet as an exploration of the moral psychology of transgression — of guilt, conscience, isolation, and the possibility of redemption — the novel is unsurpassed, and its central insight has a force that mere argument rarely achieves. It saw, and dramatized, what the twentieth century would confirm on a horrifying scale: that the idea of the man who stands beyond good and evil, licensed by his greatness or his cause to shed blood for a higher purpose, is not a liberation but a catastrophe, both for its victims and for the soul that embraces it. Dostoevsky put that idea on trial in a single human heart and found it guilty. He would put it on trial again, on a still larger stage, in his final and greatest novel — where the proposition that "everything is permitted" is spoken aloud, and leads to a murder that implicates one of the most powerful minds Dostoevsky ever created.
