There is one more Dostoevsky novel we must examine, and it is the darkest and most politically prophetic of them all. Where *Crime and Punishment* dramatized the nihilistic idea destroying a single soul, and *The Brothers Karamazov* showed an idea issuing in a single murder, *Demons* — published in 1872, and variously translated as *The Possessed* or *The Devils* — dramatizes what happens when the nihilistic idea seizes hold not of one person but of a whole society. It is a savage, prophetic novel about a circle of revolutionary radicals who bring chaos, manipulation, and murder to a provincial Russian town, and it introduces a concept that has become, in the hands of this course's central thinker, one of the most important and most contested ideas in modern political psychology: *ideological possession*.

## The Meaning of the Title

The novel takes its title and its governing image from a passage in the Gospels, which Dostoevsky places as its epigraph: the story of a man possessed by demons, whom Jesus heals by casting the demons out into a herd of swine, which then rush over a cliff and drown.[^1] The meaning Dostoevsky intends is unmistakable and chilling. The radical, atheistic, nihilistic *ideas* sweeping through nineteenth-century Russia are, he suggests, like *demons* — and they *possess* people. They enter a person's mind, drive out their humanity and their moral sense, take command of them, and drive them, like the swine, toward destruction and death. The novel's diagnosis is that ideology can function as a kind of possession: a person seized by an abstract idea — revolution, the perfect society, the total remaking of humanity — can lose their individual judgment, their compassion, their very self, becoming an instrument of the idea rather than a free human being. The idea thinks *through* them, and it drives them off a cliff.

This is a startling and profound conception. Dostoevsky is claiming that certain ideas are not merely mistaken but *dangerous in a specific way* — that they have the power to take over a human being, to hollow out the self and replace it with the logic of an abstraction, and to turn ordinary people into agents of destruction. The novel is his attempt to anatomize this possession: to show how it happens, what kind of people are susceptible, and where it leads.

## The Possessed

The circle of radicals Dostoevsky portrays is a gallery of the possessed, and each illustrates a facet of the pathology. At the center stands Nikolai Stavrogin — magnetic, intelligent, and utterly hollow, a man who has exhausted every experience and believes in nothing, whose spiritual emptiness radiates outward and corrupts everyone around him. Around him orbits Pyotr Verkhovensky, the scheming, manipulative organizer of the revolutionary cell — modeled on a real figure, the revolutionary Sergei Nechaev, who had orchestrated the murder of one of his own followers — a man for whom human beings are merely material to be used and, if necessary, destroyed in the service of the cause. There is Kirillov, obsessed with an idea we will come to. And there is Shatov, who has begun to turn away from the radicals toward faith and Russia, and whom the cell therefore murders — binding themselves together in blood, as Nechaev's real circle had done.

What Dostoevsky captures in these figures is the psychology of the ideologue with pitiless accuracy. He shows how devotion to an abstract idea — the love of "humanity" in the abstract, the vision of a perfected future society — can coexist with, and even feed, a chilling contempt for actual human beings; how the revolutionary who claims to love mankind can treat the particular men and women in front of him as expendable material; how the logic of the cause, once it possesses a person, licenses lying, manipulation, and murder in its name. The abstraction devours the concrete. The love of the future justifies the destruction of the present. And the person possessed by the idea, believing themselves to be serving humanity's highest good, becomes capable of atrocity.

## The Man Who Would Be God

One character deserves special attention, because he reveals the metaphysical root of the whole catastrophe. Kirillov is a gentle, tormented engineer obsessed with a single idea: that if God does not exist, then *man himself* must become God. Kirillov reasons that the highest assertion of human freedom, the ultimate proof that man has taken God's place, would be to kill oneself for no reason at all — to exercise the pure, absolute self-will that only a god possesses. He plans his own suicide as a philosophical act, a demonstration that man, freed from God, has become divine through the exercise of unlimited self-will. Kirillov is the logic of the death of God carried to its terminus: with God abolished, man must ascend to the vacant throne, and the mark of his divinity is that everything is now permitted to him, up to and including the absolute disposal of his own life.

Kirillov's "man-god" is the mirror image of everything Dostoevsky feared, and it ties *Demons* to the whole current of thought this course has traced. It is the nihilistic self-deification we met in Raskolnikov's extraordinary man and in Ivan's "everything is permitted" — the human being who, in the absence of God, claims God's authority for himself, and thereby licenses himself to transgress every limit. This self-deification, Dostoevsky suggests, is the hidden engine of the revolutionary project: the usurpation of God's place by man, which does not elevate humanity but unleashes destruction, because the man who has made himself god acknowledges no law above his own will.

## The Prophecy Fulfilled

*Demons* is, by common consent, one of the most prophetic novels ever written. Dostoevsky foresaw, decades before it happened, the essential character of the totalitarian revolutions of the twentieth century — and he foresaw it with uncanny precision. He saw that a utopian, nihilistic ideology, seizing hold of people like a possession, would lead not to the paradise it promised but to mass murder, terror, and the destruction of the very humanity it claimed to serve. One of his characters, the theorist Shigalyov, proposes a system for the perfect society and confesses that, starting from unlimited freedom, he has arrived at unlimited despotism — that the logic of the perfect society ends, inexorably, in total tyranny. This is the totalitarian trajectory named in advance: the dream of perfect liberation collapsing into perfect enslavement. When the revolutions came — when whole societies were seized by the idea of remaking humanity through the abolition of God, tradition, and the old morality — they fulfilled Dostoevsky's warning with a terrible fidelity, producing exactly the terror, the mass death, and the contempt for the actual human being that *Demons* had prophesied.

This is why the novel stands at the center of Jordan Peterson's political thought, and why the concept it dramatizes has become one of his signature ideas. "Ideological possession" is a term Peterson uses constantly, and it descends directly from this lineage: the claim that abstract ideologies can seize and possess people, driving out their individual judgment, their compassion, and their humanity, and turning them into instruments of an idea that leads to atrocity. Peterson's central political warning — about the dangers of utopian ideology, the totalitarian temptation, and the catastrophes that follow when human beings are possessed by a totalizing abstraction — draws heavily on Dostoevsky, and on Solzhenitsyn after him. The connection between the death of God, nihilistic self-deification, the conviction that everything is permitted, and the descent into ideological mass murder is, for Peterson as for Dostoevsky, the darkest and most important story of the modern age.

## The Honest Reckoning

*Demons* is prophetically powerful, and its diagnosis of ideological possession and the dangers of utopian nihilism is stunningly accurate about the century that followed. But it must be read with real caution, for it is also a *polemical* work, and its power can mislead. Dostoevsky was a conservative Christian reacting with horror against the radicals of his day, and the novel is a savage, one-sided caricature of them — not a fair portrait of all reform, all radical thought, or all who hope to improve society. Read carelessly, it can seem to tar every progressive or revolutionary aspiration with the brush of nihilistic demonism, and it has sometimes been used that way; but the honest reader must distinguish Dostoevsky's genuine insight into a real pathology from his reactionary tendency to see that pathology everywhere the established order is challenged. Not every strong political conviction is a demon, and not every hope for a better society ends at the Gulag.

This bears directly on the concept of "ideological possession" itself, which is as double-edged as the ressentiment we examined earlier. As a diagnosis of a real and terrible phenomenon — the way certain totalizing ideologies genuinely can seize people and drive them to atrocity — it is penetrating and was tragically vindicated. But the concept is easily *weaponized*: it can be turned into a way of dismissing any strongly held conviction one dislikes as mere "possession," which is both unfair and a conversation-stopper, a means of refusing to engage with an opponent's actual reasons by diagnosing them instead. And the implicit slippery slope — from holding firm ideas, to fanaticism, to totalitarian murder — is not inevitable; most people with strong political commitments do not become mass murderers, and treating conviction itself as a step toward the abyss would be its own error. The honest use of the idea requires discrimination: recognizing the real and dangerous phenomenon Dostoevsky named — the totalizing abstraction that hollows out the self and licenses atrocity — while refusing to deploy it as a blanket smear against all conviction and all hope for change. Wielded with that care, *Demons* remains one of the most important warnings ever written; wielded without it, it becomes exactly the kind of weapon its own logic should make us wary of. With Dostoevsky's darkest prophecy before us, we have completed the great confrontation between the existentialist thinkers and the crisis of meaning, and the unit turns next to the twentieth-century philosophers who carried the existentialist project forward into a new age.
