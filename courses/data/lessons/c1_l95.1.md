How does it happen? How does a single human being — Hitler, Stalin, Pol Pot, Che Guevara, Khomeini — rise from obscurity to a power so total that he can organize the murder of millions? The question haunts anyone who studies the twentieth century, and it is one Jordan Peterson has returned to again and again, because the depth psychology and existential philosophy of the earlier units in this course converge, terribly, on exactly this point. The tyrant is not, it turns out, simply a monster from another species. He is a human being in whom certain recognizable psychological patterns have run to their catastrophic extreme — and understanding those patterns is not an academic exercise but a warning, because the tendencies they magnify are latent, in smaller measure, in all of us.

## Not Madmen but Believers

The first temptation is to dismiss the great tyrants as lunatics, and it is a temptation we must resist, because it is both false and dangerous.[^1] Most of the men who built the murderous regimes of the twentieth century were not raving psychotics; many were disciplined, capable, intelligent, and — this is the disturbing part — sincerely convinced that they were serving a great good. Lenin and the Bolsheviks believed they were liberating humanity; Pol Pot's cadres believed they were purifying a corrupted society; the architects of the Nazi state believed they were saving a race and a nation. The horror of the tyrant is not usually the horror of madness but the horror of *conviction* — of a human being gripped by a totalizing idea so completely that it has silenced his conscience and licensed any atrocity in its name.

This connects directly to what the Dostoevsky unit called ideological possession. The tyrant is, in a sense, the supremely possessed man — the one whose entire self has been reorganized around an abstract idea that explains everything, justifies everything, and demands everything. Whether the idea is racial rebirth, the classless utopia, revolutionary purity, or the perfect religious order, its structure is the same: it offers a total account of what is wrong with the world, identifies who is to blame, and promises a paradise on the far side of whatever destruction is required to reach it. The man possessed by such an idea does not experience himself as evil. He experiences himself as an instrument of history, justice, or God — and that is precisely what makes him so dangerous.

## Resentment and the Promise of Paradise

Beneath the ideology, in figure after figure, runs the emotion this course has already examined at length: resentment. The Nietzschean and Dostoevskian analysis of *ressentiment* — the festering, impotent grievance that turns creative and builds a whole moral world around the hatred of an enemy — is nowhere more visible than in the psychology of the tyrant and the movements he leads. Hitler rose by channeling the collective humiliation of a defeated Germany, the resentment of Versailles, and a poisonous scapegoating hatred; his movement offered a wounded people someone to blame and a promise of restored greatness. Again and again, the aspiring tyrant appears as the man who gives organized voice to a mass grievance, who names an enemy responsible for all suffering, and who promises that the destruction of that enemy will usher in paradise.

This is the recurring architecture: a utopian promise, a designated enemy, and the mobilization of resentment. The tyrant does not usually seize a contented, flourishing society; he rises where there is humiliation, grievance, and the hunger for someone to blame. He offers a total solution — and the totality of the solution is the tell, because a promised paradise justifies unlimited means. If the destruction of a class, a race, or a faith will bring heaven on earth, then no atrocity is too great, and the ordinary restraints of conscience become mere sentimentality standing in the way of salvation. The utopian promise and the murderous means are not opposites; the first licenses the second.

## The Man Who Would Be God

There is a further pattern, and this course has met it before under other names. Raskolnikov's "extraordinary man," who has the right to transgress the moral law for a higher purpose; Kirillov's "man-god," who proves his divinity through absolute self-will; Nietzsche's vision, distorted by his misusers, of the figure beyond good and evil who legislates his own values — all of these are dramatizations of the psychology that the tyrant embodies in the flesh. The tyrant places himself, or his cause, *above* the moral law that binds ordinary human beings. He has, in the deepest sense, deified himself or his idea: he has claimed the authority that belongs to God, the authority to decide who shall live and who shall die, to remake human nature, to stand as the final measure of good and evil.

This self-deification is the metaphysical root of the tyrant's capacity for evil, and it is why the death of God that Nietzsche diagnosed matters so much here. When the transcendent framework that once limited human authority collapses, the vacancy it leaves does not stay empty; something rushes to fill it. And what rushes in, in the totalitarian century, was the ideology and the leader who claimed the vacant throne — the man or the movement that would now decide, with godlike finality and without appeal, the fate of everyone. The tyrant is the human being who has answered the death of God by attempting to *become* God, and who has discovered, in that attempt, that everything is now permitted.

## The Conditions of the Rise

Psychology alone, however, does not make a tyrant; the rise requires conditions, and honesty demands that we attend to them, lest we imagine that evil is simply a matter of wicked individuals. The great tyrannies arose out of *crisis* — military defeat, economic collapse, social chaos, the disintegration of a traditional order. A demoralized, frightened, humiliated population, cut loose from its old certainties and desperate for meaning and security, is the soil in which the tyrant grows. Hitler rose amid defeat, hyperinflation, and depression; Lenin and Stalin amid war, collapse, and revolution; Pol Pot amid the devastation of a wider war; Khomeini amid the upheaval of a rapidly modernizing society and a discredited monarchy. Add to the crisis a disciplined vanguard willing to use violence, a monopoly on a compelling promise, and a suitable scapegoat, and the conditions for the rise are assembled.

The figures themselves differed enormously, and we should not flatten them. Some were ideological true believers; some were cynical accumulators of power who wore ideology as a garment; most were some combination, the proportions shifting over a career. Che Guevara was a revolutionary romantic who openly embraced hatred as a tool of struggle and the necessity of killing; Stalin was a bureaucratic master of the party apparatus whose paranoia turned the machinery of terror inward; Khomeini fused a theological vision with charismatic authority and the aura of the returning exile-redeemer. To understand each requires attending to the specific man, the specific idea, and the specific crisis — not a single formula. But across the differences, the shared psychological threads recur with unsettling regularity: the possessing idea, the mobilized resentment, the utopian promise, the designated enemy, and the self above the law.

## The Honest Reckoning

The "psychology of the tyrant" is a genuinely illuminating lens, but it must be handled with real caution, and several dangers deserve naming. The first is the temptation to *armchair diagnosis* — to reduce complex historical figures to a tidy psychological label, or to claim a confident knowledge of the inner life of men long dead whom we can never examine. We should be wary of any account that says the tyrants were "all just" resentful, or "all just" narcissists, as though a single trait unlocked them; the reductive formula flatters us with false understanding. The second danger is *monocausality*. The rise of a tyrant is always multi-causal — a convergence of individual psychology, ideological currents, structural crisis, institutional failure, and sheer historical contingency — and any explanation that fastens on one factor alone, whether the man's psyche or the society's condition, distorts by omission.

A third danger is more subtle and more political: the word "tyrant," and the whole apparatus of tyrant-psychology, can be turned into a rhetorical weapon, deployed to demonize any leader or movement one opposes by assimilating them to Hitler or Stalin. This cheapens the analysis and the memory of the real horror, and it should make us cautious about how readily the label is applied. Yet when all these cautions are registered, the core insight stands, and it is one Peterson rightly presses: the tyrant is not an alien monster but a human being in whom recognizable human tendencies — the hunger for a totalizing idea, the seduction of resentment, the appetite for a scapegoat, the impulse to place oneself above the moral law — have run to their catastrophic end. The point of studying him is not to congratulate ourselves on our distance from him, but to recognize, soberly, the smaller versions of those same tendencies in ourselves, and to understand that the line between the ordinary citizen and the accomplice of atrocity is thinner than we would like to believe. That line, and how whole societies are marshaled across it, is the subject of the lessons that follow.
