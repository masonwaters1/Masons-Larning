We come now to the most uncomfortable question of all, and the one that matters most. The tyrant is a rarity; the tyrannical society is built once in a generation. But neither could accomplish anything without the participation of vast numbers of ordinary people — the clerks who processed the paperwork, the police who made the arrests, the neighbors who informed, the soldiers who pulled the triggers, the citizens who looked away. The death camps and the gulags were not staffed by monsters imported from some other planet. They were staffed by people who, in another life, were unremarkable — husbands, fathers, churchgoers, men who loved their children and were kind to their dogs. How is that possible? How do ordinary, normal human beings become the instruments of atrocity? This is the question Hannah Arendt made unavoidable, and it is central to Jordan Peterson's moral vision, because its answer implicates not only the perpetrators of the past but each of us.

## The Banality of Evil

In 1961, Arendt traveled to Jerusalem to report on the trial of Adolf Eichmann, the SS officer who had organized the transport of millions of Jews to the death camps.[^1] She expected to find a monster; what she found, or believed she found, disturbed her far more. Eichmann struck her not as a raging fanatic or a sadistic demon but as something almost worse — a mediocre, shallow, thoughtless functionary, a careerist who had done his monstrous work not out of demonic hatred but out of ambition, obedience, and a stunning inability or refusal to *think* about what he was actually doing. He spoke in clichés and bureaucratic jargon; he seemed genuinely more concerned with his stalled career than with the mountain of corpses his efficiency had produced. From this observation Arendt coined the phrase that has echoed ever since: the *banality of evil*. The greatest horrors, she suggested, are often perpetrated not by exceptional villains but by unexceptional people who have surrendered their capacity for moral judgment — who have stopped thinking, and simply do their job within a system that has redefined atrocity as duty.

This is a profound and terrible idea, and it must be handled with care, for its meaning is easily distorted. Arendt did not mean that Eichmann's crimes were trivial, nor that he bore no guilt — she believed he deserved to hang, and said so. She meant that the *source* of such evil is often not a monstrous depth but a monstrous shallowness: the failure to think, to imagine the reality of the other, to ask whether what one is doing is right. Evil of this kind requires no grand wickedness. It requires only ordinary people who have handed their conscience over to authority and ideology, and who no longer permit themselves to see what is in front of them.

## The Experiments in Obedience

Arendt's philosophical insight was soon paralleled by a body of psychological research that seemed to confirm how readily ordinary people can be led into harmful acts. In the early 1960s, the psychologist Stanley Milgram conducted his famous obedience experiments, in which ordinary volunteers, instructed by an authority figure in a lab coat, proved willing to administer what they believed were increasingly dangerous, even lethal, electric shocks to another person, simply because they were told to continue. A majority went all the way. Solomon Asch had earlier shown how far people would deny the evidence of their own eyes to conform to a group's obvious error. Later, Philip Zimbardo's Stanford prison study seemed to show ordinary students descending rapidly into cruelty when assigned the role of guards. Together, these studies suggested a sobering conclusion: that the power of situation, authority, and conformity to override individual conscience is far greater than we like to believe, and that the capacity for participation in cruelty is widely distributed among normal people.

These experiments are famous for good reason, but honesty requires flagging immediately that they are also seriously contested, and we will return to that. For now, the historical evidence matters more than the laboratory, and it points in a similar direction. The historian Christopher Browning studied a unit of middle-aged German reserve policemen — Reserve Police Battalion 101 — who carried out mass shootings of Jewish civilians in occupied Poland. These were not fanatical SS men or committed Nazis; they were ordinary working-class and middle-class men, many of them older, from a city with a weak Nazi tradition. They were even given, on occasion, the option to step aside. Most did not. Browning concluded that what turned these ordinary men into mass killers was not primarily ideology or coercion, but a tangle of ordinary human pressures: deference to authority, conformity to the group, the fear of being thought a coward by one's comrades, careerism, and the numbing power of incremental escalation.

## The Machinery of Compliance

From all of this emerges a recognizable set of mechanisms by which ordinary people are led, step by step, into complicity — and naming them is itself a form of moral vigilance. There is *incremental escalation*: the descent proceeds by small steps, each only slightly worse than the last, so that no single moment feels like the decisive crossing of a line, and by the time the horror is fully visible, one is already deep within it. There is the *diffusion of responsibility*: in a vast bureaucratic machine, each person performs only a small, specialized task — signing a form, scheduling a train, guarding a fence — and can tell himself that he is not really responsible for the whole, that the guilt belongs to someone else, higher up or further down the line.

There is *dehumanization*, which the ideology supplies: once the victims have been redefined as vermin or enemies, the ordinary moral response to their suffering is short-circuited. There is *euphemism* — the "special treatment," the "final solution," the "resettlement" — language engineered to hide the reality of the act from the one performing it. There is *conformity* and the terror of social exclusion, the deep human dread of standing alone against the group. And beneath them all is the fundamental move that this course has traced from the beginning: the *surrender of individual moral judgment* to the authority, the ideology, or the crowd — the abdication of the responsibility to think and to see for oneself, which is precisely what Arendt found missing in Eichmann. The machinery of compliance works by relieving the individual of the burden of conscience, one small permission at a time.

## The Line Through Every Heart

Against the comforting belief that evil is done only by evil others stands the witness of Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn, who had seen the Soviet machinery from inside its camps. In the most quoted passage of *The Gulag Archipelago*, he refused the easy division of humanity into good people and evil people. The line separating good and evil, he wrote, does not run between nations or classes or parties; it runs through the middle of every human heart. He had known interrogators and camp guards, and he understood that they were not a separate species; and he knew, too, that under other circumstances he might have been one of them. This is the moral core of the entire inquiry, and it is the point Peterson presses with particular force: the recognition that *you*, too, under the right conditions, might have been a camp guard — that the capacity for complicity in atrocity is not safely located in some other kind of person, but latent in yourself.

This recognition, far from being a counsel of despair, is the beginning of moral seriousness. For if evil is something only monsters do, then I, being no monster, need not examine myself; I am safe, and vigilance is unnecessary. But if the line runs through my own heart — if I, too, am capable of the thoughtlessness, the conformity, the deference, the incremental surrender that made ordinary people into accomplices — then I am called to a lifelong watchfulness over my own soul, to the cultivation of the moral courage to see clearly and to refuse. Peterson's insistence that each person take responsibility, tell the truth, and resist the seductions of ideology is, at bottom, the practical answer to this lesson: the antidote to becoming an accomplice is the refusal to surrender one's conscience, the determination to think and to see, and the willingness to stand alone when standing alone is required.

## The Honest Reckoning

This lesson's insights are among the most important the twentieth century taught, but several of its most famous supports are genuinely contested, and a serious treatment must say so plainly. Arendt's "banality of evil" thesis has been challenged by later scholarship; the historian Bettina Stangneth, drawing on Eichmann's own recorded words in exile, argued that he was in fact a far more committed and ideologically fanatical antisemite than the thoughtless functionary Arendt described — that his mildness at trial was partly a performance. If so, Eichmann may be a poor emblem for "banality," even if the broader phenomenon Arendt named is real. The psychological experiments, too, have come under heavy criticism: Milgram's have been faulted on methodological and ethical grounds and their results reinterpreted, and Zimbardo's prison study has been substantially discredited by revelations that participants were coached and the results stage-managed. These famous studies should therefore be cited with caution, as suggestive rather than conclusive.

There is a deeper ethical caution as well. The thesis that "anyone could become a perpetrator" carries a real danger: pressed too far, it can dissolve individual responsibility into situational determinism, offering the perpetrator the very excuse he reaches for — that he had no choice, that anyone would have done the same. But this is not true, and the historical record refutes it. In Browning's own battalion, some men *did* step aside; throughout the tyrannies, there were those who refused, who resisted, who rescued at mortal risk to themselves. Their existence proves that the situation did not compel, that a margin of choice remained, and that those who did evil are therefore genuinely responsible for it. The truth lies in holding both halves together: ordinary people are indeed capable of great evil under the right conditions, *and* they retain moral agency and responsibility for what they choose. To collapse either half — to imagine ourselves incapable of complicity, or to excuse complicity as inevitable — is to miss the lesson. The sober conclusion, and the one this unit has been building toward, is that mass evil is largely the work of ordinary people who surrender their conscience by degrees; that the capacity for such surrender lives in each of us; and that the whole moral task is to recognize it there, and to build in ourselves the truthfulness and the courage to refuse.
