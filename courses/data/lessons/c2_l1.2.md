The previous lesson assembled the biblical raw material; this one watches four generations of Protestant leaders forge it into the most economically consequential moral doctrine in Western history. The sequence matters, because each figure solved a problem the previous one created: Luther demolished the medieval hierarchy of callings and dignified all lawful work as divine vocation — but left the doctrine static, a matter of faithfully keeping one's station; Calvin dynamized it — diligence, improvement, thrift, and the fateful legitimation of interest — and attached it to a doctrine of election whose psychological pressures would fascinate Weber; the Puritans systematized it into a full spirituality of "redeeming the time," producing, by their own diagnosis, the paradox that godly industry breeds the wealth that corrupts godliness; and John Wesley stated that paradox with such clarity — gain all you can, save all you can, give all you can, and watch the first two devour the third — that he stands as both the ethic's greatest preacher and its most prescient critic. Benjamin Franklin then closes the arc by secularizing the whole inheritance, detaching the discipline from the doctrine — which is precisely where Weber's famous thesis (next lesson) picks the story up.

## Luther: Every Station a Calling

The revolution begins with a translation choice. Rendering 1 Corinthians 7:20 ("Let every man abide in the same calling wherein he was called"), Luther reached for the German Beruf — and proceeded to pour into that word a doctrine that flattened a thousand years of spiritual hierarchy.[^1] Medieval Christendom, as the previous lesson noted, reserved vocatio for the religious life: monks, nuns, and priests had "callings"; everyone else merely had jobs, spiritually second-class. Luther's twin doctrines — justification by faith alone and the priesthood of all believers — detonated the distinction: if salvation is God's gift and every believer a priest, then the cloister holds no higher spiritual ground than the kitchen, and the milkmaid, the magistrate, the father changing diapers (Luther's own homely example: God and the angels smile when a man washes diapers in faith) serve God as fully in their stations as any monk in his cell. His formulation, developed in the treatises of the early 1520s and preached relentlessly: all lawful stations — Stände — are divine assignments; God himself works through them (he "milks the cows through the vocation of the milkmaid," as the tradition summarizes Luther's vivid doctrine of vocation as God's mask, larva Dei — God feeding, clothing, and governing the world through human hands at work); and therefore the test of work is not its prestige but its faithfulness and its service to the neighbor. The doctrine's economic significance is hard to overstate — it conferred sacred dignity on ordinary labor across an entire civilization — and its limit must be marked with equal precision: Luther's vocation was conservative and static, an ethic of remaining faithfully where God had placed you, suspicious of ambition, mobility, and commerce (Luther retained scholastic hostility to large-scale trade and interest). He sanctified the calling; he did not yet dynamize it. That required Geneva.

## Calvin and the Puritans: Diligence, Time, and the Sentry Post

Calvin took Luther's doctrine and gave it engines. The calling remains divine assignment — his Institutes describes it as "a sentry post" assigned by God "so that he may not heedlessly wander about throughout life" — but the accent shifts from station-keeping to active stewardship: the believer as steward of gifts, time, and property that are God's, obliged to improve them (the parable of the talents doing heavy doctrinal work), with diligence, sobriety, and frugality as the visible grammar of gratitude. Two Genevan moves proved economically fateful. First, the rehabilitation of interest: against the Aristotelian-scholastic doctrine that money is barren and usury therefore unnatural, Calvin's famous letter on the question argued that Scripture's usury prohibitions targeted the grinding of the poor, not moderate interest on productive commercial loans between equals — a carefully hedged permission (he attached conditions most modern lenders would flunk) that nonetheless broke the theoretical dam: credit, the bloodstream of the economy this course studies, acquired theological legitimacy in Reformed Europe first, and Geneva, Amsterdam, and Edinburgh became what they became partly on that permission. Second, the discipline: Geneva's consistory made industry and sobriety enforceable community norms — the work ethic as social system, not merely private virtue. The Puritans — Calvin's English and American heirs, whom this course's sister volume follows across the Atlantic — then built the full spirituality. William Perkins's Treatise of the Vocations taught two callings held together: the general calling (to Christ) and the particular calling (the trade), each judged by service and neither dissolving the other — Cotton Mather's image made it proverbial: the Christian at his two callings is a man rowing a boat with two oars, pulling only one and he goes in circles. Richard Baxter's enormous Christian Directory — the source Weber would quarry — codified the ethic's maxims: time is the most precious of commodities and its waste "the first and greatest of sins" in the practical order (redeeming the time, per Ephesians 5:16); labor diligently in the meanest calling as unto God; and — the sentence that carries the whole paradox — if God shows you a way to lawfully gain more than in another way, and you refuse it, "you cross one of the ends of your calling, and you refuse to be God's steward": the duty to prosper, hedged (as Baxter hedged everything) with the duty to give, but a duty nonetheless. Add the Puritan hatred of idleness (the "sin of Sodom"), the diaries auditing hours like account books, and the sanctified week — and the ethic stands complete: disciplined, time-hoarding, improvement-seeking labor as worship, with consumption restrained by holiness. Diligence plus asceticism equals accumulation; the arithmetic was about to become a problem.

## Wesley: The Triad and the Trap

John Wesley, a figure the sister volumes meet as revivalist, belongs to this lesson as the ethic's perfecter and prophet. His sermon "The Use of Money" distilled the whole tradition into the most quotable economic homily ever preached: Gain all you can — by honest industry, harming neither soul nor body nor neighbor (his exclusions of exploitative trades are half the sermon and usually omitted by quoters); Save all you can — waste nothing on vanity, "despise delicacy," cut off every needless expense; Give all you can — for the Christian is not an owner but a steward, and the first two rules without the third make a man "twofold more a child of hell." Wesley practiced the arithmetic with famous rigor — as his income multiplied across his life he held his expenses nearly flat, giving away the enormous difference, and died leaving little but his books and a legend. And he saw, with a clarity that makes him this lesson's second author, exactly what the ethic he preached was doing at scale. His late reflection deserves full quotation, because it states the trap the Puritans had built and Weber would name: "I fear, wherever riches have increased, the essence of religion has decreased in the same proportion. Therefore I do not see how it is possible, in the nature of things, for any revival of true religion to continue long. For religion must necessarily produce both industry and frugality, and these cannot but produce riches. But as riches increase, so will pride, anger, and love of the world in all its branches." Religion breeds diligence; diligence breeds wealth; wealth kills religion — the self-consuming cycle, diagnosed by the ethic's own greatest evangelist, whose proposed cure (give all you can, and so grow in grace as you grow in goods) he admitted few would take. When Methodists rose, within two generations, from persecuted enthusiasts to respectable mill-owners, the prophecy graded itself.

## Franklin: The Ethic Without the Faith

The arc closes in Boston and Philadelphia, where the Puritan candle-maker's son who had Proverbs 22:29 drilled into him at table grew into the man Max Weber would exhibit as the "spirit of capitalism" in its chemically pure form. Benjamin Franklin kept the whole Puritan discipline — the time-auditing (his famous daily schedule with its morning question, "What good shall I do this day?"), the thirteen virtues methodically drilled (Industry: "Lose no time; be always employ'd in something useful"; Frugality: "Waste nothing"), the maxims of Poor Richard ("Early to bed and early to rise..."; "Time is money"; "Remember, that credit is money") — and quietly dropped the theology that had generated it: the calling became usefulness, providence became prudence, election became reputation, and the glory of God became "the most acceptable service of God is doing good to man." Franklin is thus the hinge figure of this entire cluster: living proof that the ethic could survive the death of its doctrine — for a generation or three — and the specimen on which Weber built his question: where did this strange, methodical, ascetic devotion to gain-as-duty come from, if not from the theology it had visibly outlived? That question, its famous answer, its critics, and the modern evidence are the next lesson.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The champions require the same audit the course gives everyone. The ethic had coercive edges from birth: Geneva's consistory and the Puritan colonies enforced industry by law — idleness was punishable, and the line between forming character and policing the poor was crossed regularly; by the industrial era, critics (E.P. Thompson's classic account) could document employers and Methodist moralists wielding the work ethic as labor discipline — sermons on diligence preached at mill hands by the mill's owners, the doctrine's class uses in full view. Luther's static "station" theology, for its part, sanctified existing hierarchies and served, in the Peasants' War, to bless their bloody restoration. Wesley's exclusions — no gain from trades that harm — were honored in the breach by heirs who kept "gain all you can" and dropped the footnotes; the prosperity gospel, this tradition's modern heresy, is Wesleyan arithmetic with the third rule deleted and the second inverted. And the ethic's gender ledger deserves a sentence: the celebrated callings were long imagined male, the household labor that underwrote every diligent tradesman largely unsung — though Proverbs 31, the tradition's own text, praises precisely a woman's commercial industry, a resource the tradition used less than it might have. None of this cancels the achievement: a civilization taught for three centuries that ordinary work is holy, time is a trust, thrift is stewardship, and wealth is for giving built habits — and, the next lesson will argue, human capital — that changed the material world. But the champions themselves, Wesley above all, would insist the audit continue: an ethic this powerful is safe only while its hedges hold. Whether the ethic actually caused capitalism — the most famous thesis in social science, its demolitions, and the surprising thing the data finally said — is next.
