The two previous lessons assembled the doctrine and its champions; this one examines the most famous claim ever made about them — Max Weber's 1905 thesis that the Protestant ethic supplied the "spirit" of modern capitalism — together with the century-long critique storm it provoked and the remarkable place the evidence finally landed. The story is a model of how big ideas should be handled, which is why this course gives it a full lesson: a brilliant hypothesis, stated with more nuance than either its fans or its executioners remember; a demolition campaign that scored real hits; and then, a century later, a resolution from an unexpected direction — modern econometrics confirming that Protestantism did produce measurable, large, durable economic advantages, while overturning Weber's mechanism and identifying the real one, which turns out to be the very engine this curriculum's Bible-and-literacy lessons document: sola scriptura made readers, and readers made human capital. The lesson ends with the civilizational ledger — what the work ethic, whatever its precise causal wiring, actually helped build — and the honest accounting of the concept's abuses.

## The Thesis, as Weber Actually Stated It

Begin by rescuing Weber from his summarizers.[^1] The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism opens not with theology but with a puzzle in the Baden occupational statistics — Protestants overrepresented among owners, skilled workers, and technical personnel — and with a definition: the "spirit of capitalism" is not greed (avarice is universal, "common to all sorts and conditions of men at all times") but something historically strange — the methodical, disciplined pursuit of gain as a duty, an end in itself, joined to restrained consumption. His exhibit is Franklin (the previous lesson's hinge figure): time is money, credit is money, the diligent tradesman's virtues — an ethic of acquisition as moral obligation, visibly persisting after its religious scaffolding had fallen away. Where did it come from? Weber's answer runs through the doctrine of the calling (Luther's contribution, which he judged necessary but too traditionalist to do the work) to Calvinism's psychological pressure-chamber: predestination. If God has elected some to salvation and the decree is hidden, the believer faces an "unprecedented inner loneliness" — and pastoral practice, Weber argued, channeled the anxiety into a proof-seeking discipline: tireless labor in a calling as the means of assurance, worldly success read (cautiously, against the theologians' own warnings) as election's sign. The result was innerweltliche Askese — "this-worldly asceticism," the monk's methodical self-discipline relocated from the cloister to the counting-house: work without rest, gain without enjoyment, wealth accumulated because consumption was sin and idleness worse — Baxter's Directory quarried for a hundred quotations. And accumulation restrained from consumption becomes, mechanically, capital. Weber's hedges deserve their sentence, because the critique storm often blew down a strawman: he explicitly disclaimed the "foolish and doctrinaire thesis" that the Reformation created capitalism (capitalist forms long predated it); he claimed an "elective affinity" — one causal thread among many, shaping capitalism's spirit and personnel rather than inventing its machinery; and his famous close is a lament, not a triumph: the ethic built an order that no longer needs it, an "iron cage" (or "steel-hard casing") of compulsory economism inhabited by "specialists without spirit, sensualists without heart" — the Puritan wanted to work in a calling; we are forced to.

## The Critique Storm

The demolition crews arrived promptly and worked for a century, and their real hits must be recorded. Chronology: capitalism's great early centers — Venice, Florence, Genoa, Augsburg, Antwerp — were Catholic, and sophisticated banking, double-entry accounting, and merchant capitalism flourished centuries before Wittenberg; if anything, the Belgian historian Pirenne and others showed, the techniques migrated north with trade routes and refugees, not theology. Tawney — whose Religion and the Rise of Capitalism (1926) gave this cluster's Friedman lesson its deliberately echoed title — reversed much of the arrow: economic change reshaped religious teaching at least as much as the reverse, the Puritan ethic itself evolving as commercial society advanced; Kurt Samuelsson's Religion and Economic Action (1957) pressed the statistical and biographical record hardest, arguing the Protestant-prosperity correlations dissolved under scrutiny; Trevor-Roper relocated the phenomenon to Erasmian and refugee networks; specialists in Calvin's Geneva noted how remote the actual pastoral record stood from Weber's anxiety mechanism; and the Japanese, Chinese-diaspora, and later East Asian growth miracles supplied the standing comparative rebuke — formidable "capitalist spirits" with no Calvin in sight (Confucian-ethic literature arose to explain them, unintentionally parodying the genre). By the 1970s the respectable verdict was that Weber's thesis was a magnificent ruin: historically stimulating, empirically unsupported. That verdict, it turned out, was itself premature — because no one had yet run the numbers properly.

## The Literacy Resolution

The modern resolution came from economics, and it is one of the most satisfying findings in social science because it vindicates the phenomenon while replacing the mechanism — and because the true mechanism is one this curriculum has already documented from the source-texts side. Sascha Becker and Ludger Woessmann's landmark study ("Was Weber Wrong?", 2009) exploited a natural experiment: using county-level data from nineteenth-century Prussia and distance-from-Wittenberg as an instrument (the Reformation spread in rough concentric fashion, giving quasi-random variation in Protestantism), they confirmed the raw fact Weber began from — Protestant regions were significantly more prosperous — and then tested the channel. The result: the entire Protestant economic advantage was statistically accounted for by literacy and schooling. Protestants read; sola scriptura — the conviction, traced through this curriculum's Reformation and Bible-and-language lessons, that every believer must encounter Scripture directly — had made Protestant territories build schools (Luther's 1524 appeal to the councilmen; the Scottish parish system; the catechetical drilling) and produce, by the nineteenth century, measurably more literate populations; and literate populations, in an industrializing economy, earn more, adapt faster, and accumulate human capital whose returns compound. Control for education, and the "Protestant effect" on prosperity disappears — not because Protestantism didn't matter, but because this is how it mattered: not a mysterious ascetic spirit, but readers. The finding has been extended and stress-tested across settings — and it joins hands with Robert Woodberry's celebrated parallel result on the global stage: across the colonized world, the historical presence of "conversionary Protestant" missionaries (the alphabet-makers of this curriculum's missionary lesson, with their schools, presses, and mass-literacy drives) is among the strongest statistical predictors of modern literacy, education, civil society, and stable democracy — an effect that survives batteries of controls and has reshaped the sociology of development. The century-long argument thus closes with a precise verdict: Weber was right that the Reformation had large economic consequences and right that they ran through formed character and conduct in a broad sense — and wrong about the specific engine; the engine was the Book demanding readers, exactly as the cluster's other lessons watched it demand alphabets, schools, and standards from Wittenberg to Wales to Korea.

## The Civilizational Ledger

Whatever the wiring, tally what the ethic-and-literacy complex helped build, because the ledger is the point of the cluster. The Dutch Republic — Calvinist, print-saturated, the seventeenth century's economic miracle — and the English and Scottish takeoffs; the North Atlantic literacy belt (Protestant Europe, Scotland, New England — the same map the signature-literacy studies draw) that became the industrial revolution's human-capital reservoir; the American case the sister course documents — a colonial population reading at rates that astonished Europeans, Tocqueville's frontier households with their Bible and newspaper, the work-ethic vocabulary so native to American self-understanding that the phrase "Protestant work ethic" functions in ordinary speech as a synonym for diligence itself; and the habits — time-keeping, deferred gratification, thrift-as-virtue, the moralized workweek — that economic historians identify among industriousness's cultural preconditions. The course states the claim at its defensible strength: the Protestant complex was not capitalism's sole parent (the machinery, finance, and much of the spirit predate and outflank it — the critique storm's standing contribution), but it was among the most powerful accelerants in economic history, and its measurable core — mass literacy motivated by theology — is as well-established as anything in the discipline. The reader who wants the mechanism in a sentence can have it in scripture's own: the entrance of thy words giveth light — and light, the economists added a century later, compounds.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The concept's abuses require the same candor as its achievements, and the Bible-lesson's own warnings (the cluster's first installment) supply the standard. The work ethic has been wielded to moralize inequality — poverty read as sloth, full stop — in defiance of the tradition's own texts (the Proverbs that condemn the rich oppressor; the Torah's structural mercies; Wesley's excised third rule), and the prosperity gospel, which converts Wesleyan stewardship into divine wealth-entitlement, is the heresy of this cluster: the ethic with its hedges burned off, gain-as-duty rebranded as gain-as-blessing-on-demand. The secular inheritance has its own pathologies — the burnout culture that treats rest as weakness is the Sabbath commandment's revenge, Weber's iron cage furnished with laptops — and the ethic's historical class uses (Thompson's mill-owners preaching diligence downward) recur in every era's management literature. Scholarly humility belongs in the counterpoint too: cultural-economic causation remains genuinely hard — the Becker-Woessmann and Woodberry results are strong but contested at the margins like all instruments-based findings, replications and boundary conditions are an active literature, and the course reports a settling consensus, not a closed case. And one inversion deserves its sentence: the same literacy the Reformers built to bind readers to the Book eventually equipped readers to question it — the Scottish Enlightenment's skeptics were the parish schools' graduates, a dialectic this curriculum meets repeatedly and hides from no one. The ethic, in sum, is a powerful inheritance with its correctives written into its own founding texts; kept whole — work and rest, diligence and justice, gain and give — it built much of the modern world's prosperity; broken into fragments, it has justified some of that world's characteristic sins. How the theology's softening — not its ethic but its doctrine of God and man — opened the door to Adam Smith himself is the cluster's final installment, waiting at the Scottish Enlightenment lesson: the Friedman thesis.
