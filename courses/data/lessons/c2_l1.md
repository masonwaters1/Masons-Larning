There was no such thing as an economist in 1700. The word did not exist in anything like its modern sense, the profession did not exist, and the body of systematic knowledge we now call economics had not been assembled. And yet human beings had been trading, lending, pricing, taxing, and arguing about wealth for thousands of years. They had built and ruined fortunes, debased currencies, cornered markets, and impoverished nations through bad policy. What they had not done was step back and ask, as a distinct and disciplined question: *how does this whole system actually work?*

This lesson is about the long pre-history of that question — the centuries in which the materials of economic thought lay scattered across moral philosophy, theology, and the practical art of statecraft, waiting for someone to gather them into a science. To understand why Adam Smith was a revolutionary, you have to understand the world of thought he was revolutionizing. The free-market tradition did not spring from nowhere. It emerged, slowly and against resistance, from a much older conversation about justice, virtue, and the proper ordering of human life.

## Aristotle and the Household

The very word *economics* gives the game away. It descends from the Greek *oikonomia*, which means, quite literally, household management — *oikos* (household) plus *nomos* (law or rule). For Aristotle, who gave the subject its first serious philosophical treatment in the fourth century BC, economics was the art of running an estate well: managing the land, the family, the slaves, the stores of grain and wine.[^1] It was a branch of ethics and politics, concerned with the good life of the household within the good life of the city. It was emphatically not a study of markets, prices, or national wealth as we would understand them.

Aristotle did, however, plant two seeds that would grow for two thousand years. The first was a distinction he drew between two kinds of getting. There was the natural and legitimate acquisition of what a household genuinely needs — food, shelter, the goods of a flourishing life. And there was *chrematistics*, the unlimited pursuit of money for its own sake, which Aristotle regarded with deep suspicion as unnatural and potentially boundless, a desire with no built-in stopping point. This suspicion of commerce-for-profit, and especially of money-making-money through lending at interest, would echo through Western moral thought for millennia. Aristotle famously condemned usury on the grounds that money is barren — a coin cannot breed more coins — so to charge interest was to make money from money in violation of its nature.

The second seed was subtler and more fruitful. In analyzing exchange, Aristotle wrestled with the puzzle of how dissimilar goods — a house and a pair of shoes, say — could be made commensurable enough to trade. He gestured toward the idea that *need* or *demand* was the common measure that made exchange possible. He did not develop this into a theory of value, but he noticed the problem, and the problem he noticed would not be properly solved until the marginalist revolution of the 1870s, which is the subject of this course's second unit. Aristotle asked the right question and left it open for two thousand years.

## The Schoolmen and the Just Price

When Christian Europe inherited Aristotle through the great medieval synthesis of Thomas Aquinas in the thirteenth century, his economic intuitions were absorbed into a sophisticated moral theology. The Scholastics — the schoolmen of the medieval universities — were not businessmen, but they were the confessors and counselors of businessmen, and they had to answer hard practical questions. Was it sinful to sell a thing for more than you paid for it? What price could a merchant justly charge? Was all lending at interest damnable, or only some?

Out of this pastoral pressure came the doctrine of the *just price*. Aquinas, following Aristotle and Roman law, held that there was a price at which a transaction was fair and above or below which it became a kind of theft.[^2] But — and this is the part too often missed by those who imagine the medievals as naive price-fixers — Aquinas and his successors increasingly identified the just price not with some mystical intrinsic worth but with the *common estimation of the market*, the going price that emerged from the ordinary buying and selling of a community. They understood that scarcity raised prices and abundance lowered them. They debated whether a merchant who brought grain to a starving city, knowing more grain was on the way, was obliged to reveal that fact or could sell at the current high price. These were not crude minds. They were doing careful, casuistic economic reasoning inside a moral framework.

On usury they were stricter, and the long medieval prohibition on lending at interest shaped European finance for centuries — though it also generated an enormous body of clever exceptions, partnerships, and instruments designed to achieve the economic effect of interest while staying within the letter of the law. The history of finance is in part the history of getting around the usury ban, and that pressure itself drove real innovation.

## The Hidden Economists of Salamanca

The most remarkable and least famous chapter in this pre-history unfolded in sixteenth-century Spain, at the University of Salamanca. As Spanish galleons hauled the silver of the New World back across the Atlantic, a group of theologians confronted economic phenomena that demanded explanation, and their explanations were astonishingly modern.

The Salamanca scholars watched prices rise across Spain and Europe as American silver flooded in, and several of them — Martín de Azpilcueta foremost among them — articulated a clear version of what economists now call the quantity theory of money: that the value of money falls as its quantity increases, so that more silver chasing the same quantity of goods drives prices up.[^3] They observed that money, like any good, was more valuable where it was scarce and less valuable where it was abundant, which explained why the same coin bought more in some countries than others. This is genuine monetary economics, worked out by theologians a century and a half before Adam Smith was born.

On value, the Salamancans went further than the medievals. They argued forcefully that the just price was simply the market price arrived at by common estimation, and that this value derived not from the labor or cost embodied in a good but from human *need and desire* — from utility and scarcity as perceived by buyers. A good was worth what people would give for it, full stop. This is a striking anticipation of the subjective theory of value that would not become orthodox economics for another three hundred years. The free-market tradition has deep Catholic roots that its more secular modern champions sometimes forget.

## Mercantilism: The Doctrine to Be Defeated

By the time we reach the seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries — the era immediately before Adam Smith — the dominant economic *policy* of the European states was not scholastic moral theology but something far more practical and, Smith would argue, far more wrongheaded. We call it mercantilism, and it is the great intellectual antagonist against which classical economics defined itself.

Mercantilism was less a single theory than a cluster of assumptions held by merchants, pamphleteers, and royal ministers. At its core lay the belief that the wealth of a nation consisted in its stock of gold and silver, and that the path to national power was therefore to accumulate as much precious metal as possible. From this followed a whole program: maximize exports, minimize imports, run a persistent trade surplus so that bullion flows in rather than out. Protect domestic industry with tariffs and prohibitions. Found colonies to supply cheap raw materials and serve as captive markets. Grant monopolies to favored companies. Treat international trade as a zero-sum struggle in which one nation's gain was necessarily another's loss.

The deep error embedded here — the one Smith would devote his life to exposing — was the conflation of money with wealth. The mercantilists treated the gold itself as the prize. But gold is not wealth; it is a claim on wealth, a medium of exchange. The true wealth of a nation, Smith would insist, is the stream of goods and services its people can produce and consume — its annual produce of land and labor. A country stuffed with gold but poor in productive capacity is a country waiting to be impoverished. And because trade is not zero-sum but mutually beneficial — both parties trade precisely because each values what it gets more than what it gives — the whole mercantilist apparatus of tariffs, prohibitions, and trade wars was, in Smith's view, a vast machine for making everyone poorer in the name of making one's own nation rich.

Yet even mercantilism contained dissenting currents that pointed toward the coming revolution. There were writers — Sir Dudley North, Nicholas Barbon, the brilliant and disreputable John Law — who glimpsed pieces of the truth: that low interest rates reflected abundant capital rather than caused prosperity, that consumption drove production, that the free movement of trade enriched all parties. The ground was shifting beneath mercantilist orthodoxy even at its height.

## The Physiocrats and the Order of Nature

The final and most important bridge to Adam Smith was built in France by a circle of thinkers who called themselves *les économistes* and whom history calls the Physiocrats. Led by François Quesnay, court physician to Louis XV, they produced in the 1750s and 1760s the first attempt at a systematic *model* of an entire economy.

The Physiocrats made two contributions that fed directly into the classical tradition. The first was the very idea that the economy is a *natural system* governed by discoverable laws — like the circulation of blood, which Quesnay the physician understood well. Wealth, in their famous *Tableau Économique*, flowed through the social body in a circuit between farmers, landowners, and artisans. If wealth obeyed natural laws, then the task of the statesman was not to command the economy but to align policy with those laws — to stop interfering. This gave them their immortal slogan, *laissez faire, laissez passer* — let it be, let it pass. Leave the economy alone and it will find its own productive order.

Their second contribution was an error so productive it deserves respect: the conviction that all real wealth came ultimately from the land, from agriculture, the one sector they believed produced a genuine surplus over its costs. They were wrong about the unique productivity of land — Smith would correct them by locating the source of wealth in labor and productive capacity across all sectors. But in searching for the true *source* of a nation's wealth, rather than fixating on its stock of gold, they had reframed the entire question in the way Smith would answer it.

## The Moral Shadow That Never Left

Before we cross the threshold into Smith, it is worth noticing something about this pre-history that will matter for the entire course: the moral suspicion of commerce that runs from Aristotle through the medieval church never actually disappeared. It went underground, and it surfaces still, often in people who would be astonished to learn they are echoing a Greek philosopher and a medieval friar.

The intuition is deep and durable. It is the intuition that there is something a little disreputable about making money from money, something grasping about the pursuit of profit, something morally superior about producing tangible things with one's hands compared to the abstract maneuvers of the merchant and the financier. It is the intuition that a fair price is a fixed and knowable thing, and that someone who charges more than that — the "price gouger" who raises the cost of water before a storm, the "speculator" who profits from scarcity — is committing a kind of theft, regardless of what supply and demand might say. It is the intuition that trade between unequal parties must involve exploitation, that one side's gain implies the other's loss, that the wealthy have grown rich by taking from the poor.

Every one of these intuitions has an ancient pedigree, and every one of them is, from the standpoint of the economic tradition this course will develop, a fallacy — or at least a serious confusion. Aristotle's condemnation of money breeding money lives on in modern hostility to finance. The medieval anxiety about the just price lives on in modern price controls and anti-gouging laws, which economists across the political spectrum recognize as counterproductive. The zero-sum vision of the mercantilists lives on in the conviction that imports cost "us" jobs and that trade deficits represent a nation being cheated. These are not new errors. They are some of the oldest errors in the world, and they recur in each generation precisely because they flow so naturally from moral intuitions that predate economics itself.

This is the central reason the free-market tradition has to be deliberately *learned*. Its core propositions cut against the grain of moral instinct. The mind does not arrive naturally at the understanding that a voluntary exchange benefits both parties, that a rising price is a signal performing useful work rather than an act of greed, that the speculator hoarding grain before a famine is, by raising its price now, conserving it for when it will be needed most. These insights are counterintuitive, and the intuitions they must overcome are thousands of years old and morally charged. That is why economic illiteracy is the natural state and economic literacy the hard-won exception — and why the thinkers in this course had to fight, against the moral common sense of their times, to establish truths that remain contested today.

## The Threshold

So we arrive at the doorstep of 1776, the year Adam Smith published *The Wealth of Nations* — and, by no coincidence this course will explore, the year a new nation was founded across the Atlantic on related principles of natural liberty. The materials were all lying there waiting. Aristotle had asked how exchange creates commensurability. The Scholastics had tied price to common market estimation. Salamanca had grasped subjective value and the quantity theory of money. The Physiocrats had conceived the economy as a natural, self-ordering system best left alone. And the towering edifice of mercantilism stood ready to be demolished by anyone with the analytical power to see through it.

What had not happened was the synthesis. No one had taken these scattered insights and woven them into a single, systematic, persuasive account of how a market economy actually generates and distributes wealth — an account powerful enough to overturn the reigning policy of every European state and to found a new discipline. That is precisely what Smith would do, beginning not with self-interest, as the caricature has it, but with sympathy and moral sentiment, which is where our next two lessons begin. It is no small irony that the man remembered as the prophet of self-interest was a professor of moral philosophy whose first great book was about fellow-feeling — a sign of how badly the tradition is understood even by those who invoke it.

The crucial thing to carry forward from this opening lesson is a sense of how *recent* and how *hard-won* the basic insights of economics are. The propositions that now seem obvious to anyone who has taken an introductory course — that trade benefits both sides, that prices coordinate dispersed activity, that money is not the same as wealth, that intervention has unseen costs — were the achievements of centuries of difficult thought, frequently against the grain of moral intuition and political interest. They were not obvious. They are still not obvious to most people, which is why this tradition needs to be learned and re-learned in every generation. We are about to learn it from the man who first put it all together.
