In the last lesson we took Marx seriously, presenting his critique of capitalism at full strength. Now we take him apart — not by dismissal or insult, but on the merits, by examining the foundation on which his whole economic edifice rests. That foundation is the *labor theory of value*, the claim that the value of a thing is determined by the labor required to produce it. Upon this single premise Marx built his theory of surplus value, and upon surplus value his theory of exploitation, and upon exploitation his prophecy of capitalism's doom. If the foundation cracks, the structure above it cannot stand. And the foundation, as economics discovered within a generation of Marx's death, was already crumbling.

## The Labor Theory of Value

The labor theory of value was not Marx's invention; he inherited it from the classical tradition, from Adam Smith and especially David Ricardo, who had treated the labor embodied in a good as the key to its value. Marx took this inherited theory and pressed it to a revolutionary conclusion. If the value of a commodity is the labor required to make it, and if the worker is the source of that labor, then the value the worker creates over a day exceeds the wage he is paid, and the difference — the surplus value — is appropriated by the capitalist as profit. Profit, on this account, is congealed unpaid labor; exploitation is the system's very engine.

The argument is internally tight, and that is its seduction: grant the labor theory of value, and the rest follows with a kind of remorseless logic. But this means that everything depends on the premise. Is value, in fact, determined by labor? The classical economists had assumed so, but they had never been able to make the theory work cleanly, and it harbored difficulties that would prove fatal.

## The Paradox at the Heart of It

The deepest difficulty is captured in an old puzzle: the paradox of value, sometimes called the diamond-water paradox. Water is essential to life; without it we die in days. Diamonds are useless ornaments; no one needs them to live. Yet water is nearly free and diamonds are extravagantly dear. If value were determined by usefulness, water should command the higher price; if by labor, the comparison still fails, for the relationship between labor and value breaks down again and again on inspection. A good can absorb enormous labor and be worth nothing — labor poured into something nobody wants creates no value whatever. A child may spend all day making mud pies; the labor is real, and the value is zero. Conversely, a thing requiring little labor may command a great price if it is scarce and desired.

The lesson of the paradox is that value is *not* an intrinsic property poured into an object by the labor expended on it. Labor does not, by itself, create value. Something else determines what a thing is worth — and until economics identified that something else, the labor theory of value remained an unexplained muddle, and theories built upon it stood on sand.

## The Marginal Revolution

The solution arrived in the 1870s, in one of the great turning points in the history of economic thought — the *marginal revolution*. Independently and almost simultaneously, three economists working in different countries — William Stanley Jevons in England, Carl Menger in Austria, and Léon Walras in Switzerland — arrived at the same decisive insight.[^1] Value, they showed, is determined not by labor or cost of production but by *marginal utility*: the subjective usefulness, to a particular person, of one additional unit of a good.

Two ideas are bundled in this. First, value is *subjective* — it resides not in the object but in the mind of the valuer, in how much a person wants the thing. There is no value without a valuer; worth is a relation between a good and a human desire, not a substance embedded in the good by labor. Second, value is determined *at the margin* — by the usefulness of the next or last unit, not by the total. This dissolves the diamond-water paradox at a stroke. Water is cheap not because it lacks value but because it is abundant: with so much available, the marginal unit — the next glassful — has low utility, since our most urgent needs for water are already met. Diamonds are dear because they are scarce: each additional diamond is highly valued precisely because there are so few. Total usefulness is irrelevant to price; marginal usefulness, against the backdrop of scarcity, sets it. With this, the centuries-old puzzle was solved, and the labor theory of value was rendered obsolete.

## The Subjective Turn

It is worth pausing to register how profound the marginalist shift was, for it did far more than refute one of Marx's premises — it reoriented the whole of economics. The classical tradition, including Marx, had sought the source of value in something *objective* and embedded in the good itself: the labor it contained, the cost of producing it. The marginal revolution relocated value to something *subjective*: the valuations of human beings, their wants and preferences, weighed against scarcity. Value ceased to be a property that goods *possess* and became a relation that people *create* — an expression of human choice rather than a measurable substance.

This subjective turn is the foundation of nearly all modern economics. It makes the consumer, not the producer, the ultimate source of value, and it makes economics a study of human action under scarcity — of how people choose among competing ends with limited means — rather than a kind of social physics measuring quantities of embodied labor. From it flow the modern understanding of prices as signals of relative scarcity and desire, the analysis of how markets coordinate the dispersed knowledge of millions of valuers, and the case that no central authority can replicate what the price system accomplishes. Marx's labor theory was not merely a wrong answer to the question of value; it was a wrong *kind* of answer, looking for value in the object when it lived all along in the human mind. The marginalists did not just patch a hole in Marx; they rebuilt the discipline on a new and firmer foundation.

## What This Does to Marx

The consequences for Marx are devastating, and they follow directly. If value flows from subjective marginal utility rather than from embodied labor, then the labor theory of value is false — and with it, Marx's theory of surplus value and exploitation collapses. Profit cannot be defined as the "unpaid labor" of the worker, because labor is not the source of value in the first place. Value arises from the meeting of subjective wants with scarce means, and the return to capital reflects genuine and distinct contributions: the provision of capital itself, the bearing of risk and uncertainty, the entrepreneurial foresight that anticipates what people will want, the coordination of production, and the passage of time. The worker is paid for the value his labor actually contributes at the margin; the capitalist's return is not theft from the worker but compensation for contributions the worker does not make. The charge of systemic exploitation, so powerful in Marx's framing, rests on a theory of value that economics abandoned.

Nor was this only an external critique. Within Marxian economics itself, the attempt to reconcile labor values with the actual prices that goods command in the market — the notorious "transformation problem" — proved a technical morass that occupied Marxist economists for a century without satisfactory resolution. The system was troubled from within as well as refuted from without. And the empirical record compounded the theoretical failure. Marx's grand predictions did not come true: in the advanced capitalist nations the working class did not sink into ever-deeper misery but grew dramatically more prosperous; capitalism did not collapse under its contradictions but proved remarkably adaptable and reformable; and the revolutions made in Marx's name erupted not in advanced industrial societies, as his theory required, but in backward agrarian ones, where they produced not the promised liberation but tyranny and mass death. Wrong about value, wrong about prices, wrong about the future — the system failed at every level.

## What Survives

To refute Marx's economics is not to dismiss everything he touched, and intellectual honesty requires saying what survives. The labor theory of value falls; the theory of exploitation falls; the predictions fall. But the *moral* concerns Marx raised can be detached from the failed economics and considered on their own. The experience of alienating, dehumanizing labor is real; inequality is real; the instability and recurrent crises of unregulated markets are real; the concentration of economic power raises genuine questions. One can take these seriously — as the classical liberal tradition, from Mill onward, already did — without accepting the labor theory of value or the revolutionary cure that brought catastrophe wherever it was applied. This is the distinction, drawn in the last lesson, between Marx's diagnosis and his prescription: parts of the diagnosis remain worth pondering; the prescription, and the economic theory beneath it, do not survive examination.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Fairness requires hearing the strongest replies on Marx's behalf. Some Marxist economists argue that the marginalist critique misses its target — that Marx was not offering a theory of *price* determination at all, but an analysis of the deep *social relations* of capitalist production, for which the labor theory of value functions differently than the marginalists assume. On this reading, marginal utility may govern day-to-day prices while Marx's labor analysis still illuminates the underlying structure of class and surplus. Others contend that the transformation problem has been solved by later reformulations, and nearly all hold, rightly, that the analysis of alienation stands independent of the labor theory of value altogether. These rejoinders deserve respect, and a serious engagement with Marx cannot wave them away.

But two points hold against them. First, even granting that Marx aimed at social relations rather than prices, his specific charge of *exploitation* — the extraction of surplus value as unpaid labor — does depend on labor's being the source of value, and that claim does not survive the marginal revolution; relocating Marx to the level of "social relations" tends to make the theory less falsifiable rather than more true. Second, the empirical verdict is not in serious doubt: the predictions failed, and the societies built on Marx's prescription failed catastrophically. The honest conclusion is neither to caricature Marx nor to rehabilitate his economics, but to recognize that he asked real questions, built his answers on a mistaken theory of value, and prescribed a cure that proved worse than any disease — and that the market tradition is stronger for having met him on the merits rather than fearing to engage him. With Marx understood and answered, the unit can draw its classical thread to a close, gathering up the legacy of the tradition before moving on.
