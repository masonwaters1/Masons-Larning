Of the three economists who independently launched the marginalist revolution, one did more than solve the problem of value: he founded a whole tradition of economic thought that would become, in the century to follow, the most rigorous defender of free markets and the most penetrating critic of central planning. That economist was Carl Menger, and the tradition he founded is the Austrian School. To study Menger is to encounter not only the subjective theory of value at its source but the distinctive way of doing economics — focused on the individual, on human choice, on causes rather than equations — that would run through Mises and Hayek and shape the free-market tradition this course exists to transmit.

## The Founding Text

In 1871, the same year that Jevons published his marginal analysis in England, Carl Menger published in Vienna his *Principles of Economics* — the founding document of the Austrian School.[^1] Like Jevons and Walras, Menger had arrived independently at the concept of marginal utility and the subjective theory of value. But where Jevons reached for mathematics and Walras for the grand modeling of general equilibrium, Menger took a different path, and that difference would define the tradition he founded.

Menger developed his economics not through equations but through *causal reasoning* — tracing, step by step, how economic phenomena arise from the choices and valuations of individual human beings seeking to satisfy their needs. He sought to explain economic life as the outcome of human action, understood from the inside, rather than to model it as a system of mathematical relationships observed from the outside. This "causal-realist" method, verbal and logical rather than mathematical, became a hallmark of the Austrian approach and set it apart from the increasingly mathematical mainstream that the other marginalists helped to create.

## Subjective Value

At the center of Menger's economics stands the subjective theory of value, which he stated with particular clarity and force. Value, Menger insisted, is *not* a property residing in objects — not the labor they contain, not their cost of production, not any intrinsic quality. Value arises entirely from the *relationship* between a good and a human being's needs. A thing has value only because, and only insofar as, some person recognizes it as capable of satisfying a need they have. Value is conferred by the valuer; it does not inhere in the valued.

From this Menger derived diminishing marginal utility through the structure of human needs, and he did so without a single equation. Human beings, he observed, satisfy their most urgent needs first. The first unit of a good is devoted to the most pressing want; the second to a less pressing one; and so on. Therefore each additional unit serves a less urgent need and is valued less than the one before — and the value of any unit is determined by the least urgent need it satisfies, the need "at the margin." A man dying of thirst values his first cup of water immensely; his tenth cup, used to wash his hands, hardly at all. Value flows from the satisfaction of subjectively ranked human needs, and it diminishes at the margin because our wants are ordered by urgency. The whole theory grows from the realities of human choice, reasoned out causally rather than computed.

## The Orders of Goods

One of Menger's most original contributions was his analysis of the *orders of goods*, which inverted the classical understanding of where value comes from. Menger distinguished *goods of the first order* — consumer goods that directly satisfy human wants, like bread — from *higher-order goods*, the means of production: the flour, the oven, the labor, the wheat, and the land that go into making the bread. The crucial question is where the higher-order goods get their value.

The classical economists, with their labor and cost theories, had value flowing *forward*: the labor and resources embodied in production gave the final good its value. Menger reversed the arrow. Value, he argued, flows *backward*, from the consumer's valuation of the final good to the factors that produce it. Flour, ovens, and farm labor have economic value *because*, and only because, consumers value the bread they help to make. If no one valued bread, the wheat and the ovens dedicated to making it would be economically worthless, however much labor they had absorbed. This "imputation" of value from final ends back to productive means is a foundational Austrian idea, and it completes the subjectivist revolution: not only consumer goods but the entire structure of production derives its value, ultimately, from the subjective valuations of consumers. Production serves consumption, and value originates in the wants it exists to satisfy.

## The Spontaneous Origin of Money

Menger also gave a famous account of the origin of money that became a model of a whole style of economic explanation. How did money come to exist? The naive answer is that some ruler or authority invented and decreed it. Menger showed that money need not have been deliberately created at all — that it could, and most likely did, *emerge spontaneously* from barter, without anyone designing it, through the self-interested actions of countless individual traders.

In a barter economy, Menger reasoned, some goods are more easily and widely traded — more "saleable" — than others. A rational trader, seeking to acquire what he wants, will find it advantageous to accept these more marketable goods even if he does not want them for their own sake, because he can more easily trade them onward for what he does want. As more traders, each pursuing their own advantage, do the same, the most saleable commodities become ever more widely accepted, until one or a few emerge, through this cumulative and unintended process, as a universal medium of exchange — as money. No one planned it; it arose as the unintended outcome of many individuals pursuing their own ends. This is a paradigm of *spontaneous order* — the emergence of a useful social institution from human action but not from human design — and it connects Menger directly to the deepest theme of the classical liberal tradition, the insight, descending from Adam Smith's invisible hand, that the most important institutions of society often arise unplanned from the free interactions of individuals.

## The Austrian School

From Menger's *Principles* grew the Austrian School, carried forward by his followers Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk and Friedrich von Wieser, and later by Ludwig von Mises and Friedrich Hayek, who would become the school's most famous figures. The tradition Menger founded has several distinctive marks, all visible already in his work. It is radically *subjectivist*, grounding all economic value in individual valuation. It is *methodologically individualist*, explaining economic phenomena through the choices of individual human beings rather than through aggregates or collectives. It is *causal-realist* and skeptical of excessive mathematization, preferring to trace the logic of human action rather than to model equilibrium with equations. And it emphasizes *process* over static equilibrium — the market as an unfolding process of human action, discovery, and adjustment through time.

These commitments would bear remarkable fruit. The Austrian emphasis on subjective knowledge, individual action, and the spontaneous coordination of dispersed information would lead, in the hands of Mises and Hayek, to the most powerful critiques of socialism and central planning ever formulated — the argument that a central planner cannot replicate the knowledge and coordination achieved by the price system and the free choices of millions of individuals. The seeds of that argument are already present in Menger's vision of value arising from individual valuation and order emerging spontaneously from individual action. The Austrian School became the great intellectual home of the case for economic freedom, and Carl Menger is its founder.

## The Methodenstreit

Menger also fought, late in his career, a famous methodological battle that helped define the Austrian stance — the *Methodenstreit*, or "conflict of methods," with the German Historical School led by Gustav Schmoller. The Historicists held that economics should be primarily an empirical, historical discipline, describing the particular economic conditions of particular societies, and were skeptical of universal economic laws. Menger insisted, against them, that economics could and should be a *theoretical* science, deriving universal laws from the logic of human action — laws that hold across all times and places because they follow from the nature of human choice itself.

This defense of economic theory as a science of universal principles, grounded in the logic of human action rather than in mere historical description, became a defining commitment of the Austrian tradition. It set the Austrians against both the Historical School and, later, against the purely empirical and statistical approaches that would come to dominate mainstream economics. For Menger and his heirs, the foundation of economic science was not the accumulation of data but the rigorous reasoning out of the implications of human beings acting to satisfy their wants — a conviction that would distinguish, and to some extent isolate, the Austrian School ever after.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Fairness requires noting that the Austrian path Menger charted became, over time, a *heterodox* one, and its distinctive commitments are genuinely contested. Mainstream economics took the mathematical and empirical road of Jevons and Walras, not Menger's verbal causal-realism, and many economists regard the Austrian skepticism of mathematics and formal testing — carried to an extreme in Mises's later insistence on deriving economics from self-evident axioms of human action — as unscientific or unfalsifiable. The Austrian School today is a minority tradition, influential in certain circles but outside the economic mainstream, and its strong free-market conclusions are intertwined with a libertarian politics that critics see as ideologically motivated rather than dispassionately derived.

These criticisms have force, and a defender of the tradition should not pretend otherwise. Yet much of Menger's specific achievement is accepted far beyond the Austrian school. The subjective theory of value is now simply part of economics; the imputation of value from ends to means is uncontroversial; and Menger's account of the spontaneous emergence of money remains a celebrated model of how institutions can arise unplanned from individual action. Even economists who reject the broader Austrian method and politics acknowledge Menger as one of the founders of modern value theory. The honest conclusion is that Menger launched both a universally accepted revolution in the theory of value and a distinctive, contested tradition of economic method and free-market thought — and that the free-market tradition this course follows owes him an immense and direct debt. With the Austrian stream flowing from Menger, the unit turns next to the other great current of the marginalist revolution and to the developments that built modern economics upon the new foundation of subjective value.
