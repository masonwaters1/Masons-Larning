The previous lesson followed Carl Menger in Vienna, arriving at marginal utility through words, causes, and the logic of human needs. This lesson crosses to England, where in the very same year — 1871 — William Stanley Jevons published The Theory of Political Economy and announced the same revolution in a completely different language. Where Menger reasoned in prose, Jevons reasoned in calculus; where Menger built a causal account of valuing minds, Jevons built equations, declaring on his first page that economics, if it is to be a science at all, must be a mathematical science. The two men had never read each other; a third, Léon Walras in Lausanne, would arrive at the same principle independently within three years. This extraordinary triple discovery is the subject of the next two lessons, but Jevons matters for more than his share in it. He set economics on the mathematical road it still travels — a turn that gave the discipline enormous analytical power and, as the Austrians never stopped arguing, cost it something real. Understanding what was gained and what was lost in the mathematical turn is essential to everything that follows in this course, because the split between the two roads — Menger's and Jevons's — becomes the split between the Austrian school and the mainstream, and it begins here.

## The Man in a Hurry

Jevons's biography explains something of his urgency.[^1] Born in Liverpool in 1835 to a merchant family ruined in the crash of 1848, he broke off his studies for financial reasons and spent five years as an assayer at the mint in Sydney, Australia — a job that immersed him daily in the precise measurement of value in metal. He returned to England determined to rebuild economics on exact foundations, and he worked with the intensity of a man who suspected his time was short; it was — he drowned at forty-six. Along the way he did foundational work in logic and statistics, built one of the first logical machines, pioneered the study of business cycles (including a famous, flawed attempt to tie them to sunspots), and wrote on coal, currency, and scientific method. But his central conviction, formed early and held with missionary force, was that political economy in the classical style — the literary, discursive economics of Ricardo and Mill — had wandered into a dead end precisely because it refused to become quantitative. "Our science," he wrote, "must be mathematical, simply because it deals with quantities." Pleasure and pain, utility and disutility, come in degrees; whatever comes in degrees can in principle be treated with the calculus of variations; and a science that talks about more and less while refusing the mathematics of more and less is, Jevons thought, simply an underdeveloped science.

## The Final Degree of Utility

Jevons's core theoretical move is the same one Menger made, dressed in different clothes. Value, he argued, does not come from labor or cost of production, as the classical school had taught; it comes from utility — and not from total utility, but from what he called the final degree of utility: the increment of satisfaction yielded by the last small addition to one's stock of a good. Water has immense total utility and, in ordinary circumstances, negligible final utility, because the last gallon serves a trivial want; hence its low price. The final degree of utility diminishes as quantity increases — Jevons's version of the law of diminishing marginal utility — and a rational consumer allocates spending so that the final increments of utility per unit of money are equalized across all goods, since otherwise he could gain by reallocating. From this single principle Jevons derived the theory of exchange: two parties trade until the ratios of their final degrees of utility equal the ratio of exchange, at which point no further mutually beneficial trade exists. Labor enters the story not as the source of value but as its shadow: we work until the pain of the last unit of effort equals the utility of the last unit of reward. The classical edifice is thus inverted. Cost does not determine value; the anticipated utility of outputs determines what costs producers will bear. Jevons stated the break with characteristic bluntness, declaring Ricardo a wrong-headed genius who had shunted the car of economic science onto a wrong line — and value, he insisted, depends entirely upon utility.

## Economics as Mechanics

What distinguished Jevons from Menger was not the principle but the program. Jevons modeled economics explicitly on physics — he called his theory "the mechanics of utility and self-interest" — treating pleasure and pain as the economic analogues of energy, and the equilibrium of exchange as the analogue of the equilibrium of forces. He knew utility could not yet be measured directly, and conceded the point candidly, but argued that markets reveal its effects the way instruments reveal invisible forces: prices are the readings on the dial. The program had immediate, permanent consequences for the discipline. It made economic argument checkable in a new way — an equation's derivation can be verified or refuted line by line, where a chain of prose can hide its slippages. It opened the door to the marriage of theory and statistics that Jevons himself pioneered and that would become econometrics. And it redefined the economist's ideal from the philosopher-observer of Smith's kind toward the scientist-engineer, a transformation so complete that within three generations the mathematical method was not a school within economics but the definition of the discipline, and a paper without a model would struggle to be read at all. Whether that transformation was a triumph, a wrong turn, or both at once is one of the deepest running arguments in the tradition this course follows.

## What the Equations Assume

The power of the mathematical method was purchased by assumptions, and it is essential to see them clearly, because they became invisible precisely as they became standard. To write consumer behavior as maximization, Jevons had to posit an agent with given, stable preferences, choosing over known alternatives — the creature later economists would call rational economic man. To make the calculus tractable, goods had to be treated as divisible, markets as frictionless, information as available. None of this described any actual market participant, and Jevons knew it; the claim was that the idealization captures the essential tendency, as frictionless planes do in mechanics. But notice what quietly drops out of an economics built this way: the ignorant, groping, discovering human being; the entrepreneur who notices what others have missed; the passage of real time in which expectations are formed and dashed; the institutions within which choice happens. Menger's prose kept those things in view because prose can carry them; Jevons's equations could not carry them, and so the mathematical tradition set them aside — at first as simplifications to be relaxed later, then, as generations passed, as matters simply outside economics. The Austrian school's entire later quarrel with the mainstream — Mises on calculation, Hayek on knowledge, Kirzner on entrepreneurship — is, at bottom, an insistence that what the equations left out was the heart of the subject. That quarrel begins here, in 1871, in the difference between two books announcing the same principle.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The Austrian-flavored telling above must now face the strongest case for Jevons's road, and it is very strong. First, the mathematical turn delivered: the precision it enforced exposed genuine errors in classical reasoning that prose had protected for decades, and the twentieth century's real analytical advances — from demand theory to game theory to auction design that allocates spectrum and matches kidney donors — are children of the formal method, not the literary one. Second, the charge that mathematics smuggles in false assumptions cuts both ways: prose smuggles too, and less visibly; an equation at least declares its assumptions where a paragraph can hide them, which is why formalization so often reveals that a verbal argument was subtly circular or incomplete. Third, the exclusions were not permanent: information economics, behavioral economics, and institutional economics — all mathematical — eventually brought ignorance, psychology, and rules back into the models, suggesting the method was capacious rather than crippling, even if the return took a century. Fourth, Jevons himself was no naive scientist: his philosophy of science was sophisticated, he insisted on the provisional character of all theory, and his empirical work on coal and currency shows a mind fully engaged with the world's friction. What survives the counterpoint is a calibrated claim, and it is the one to carry forward: the mathematical turn was a genuine scientific advance that came with a genuine narrowing — power gained on the questions equations could reach, attention lost to the questions they could not — and the history that follows in this course, from the calculation debate to the 2008 crash, is in large part the story of economics rediscovering, at intervals and at cost, the things the equations had set aside. Menger and Jevons found the same principle; the tradition split over what to do with it. The third co-discoverer took the mathematical road further than Jevons ever dreamed — toward a complete system of equations for an entire economy — and it is to Léon Walras, and the idea of general equilibrium, that we turn next.

[^1]: William Stanley Jevons (1835–1882), *The Theory of Political Economy* (1871), published the same year as Menger's *Principles* — with Walras's *Éléments* (1874) completing the independent triple discovery of marginal utility. Biography: Liverpool merchant family ruined in 1848; five years as gold assayer at the Sydney mint; drowned at 46; parallel work in logic (the "logical piano"), statistics, business cycles (the sunspot theory), and resource economics (*The Coal Question*, 1865). Core doctrine: value derives from the "final degree of utility" (marginal utility), which diminishes with quantity; consumers equalize final utility per unit of expenditure across goods; exchange proceeds to the equality of utility ratios with the price ratio; labor is balanced at the margin against the utility of its product — inverting the classical cost-of-production theory ("value depends entirely upon utility"; Ricardo as the "able but wrong-headed man" who "shunted the car of Economic science on to a wrong line"). Program: economics as "the mechanics of utility and self-interest," explicitly modeled on physics, with prices as the observable readings of unmeasurable utility — the fountainhead of the discipline's mathematization and, via his statistical work, of econometrics. The assumptions of tractability (given stable preferences, known alternatives, divisibility, frictionlessness) define the later *homo economicus*; the Austrian counter-tradition (Mises, Hayek, Kirzner) is the standing argument that the mathematical idealization set aside the essential subject matter — ignorance, discovery, time, institutions. Counterpoint: formalization's exposure of verbal error, the demonstrable achievements of the formal method (demand theory, game theory, mechanism design), the later mathematical recovery of information, psychology, and institutions, and Jevons's own methodological sophistication.
