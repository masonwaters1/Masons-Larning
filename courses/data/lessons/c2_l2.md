If you know one sentence of Adam Smith, it is probably the one about the butcher, the brewer, and the baker — the claim that we get our dinner not from their benevolence but from their regard to their own interest. From that single, often misquoted line a whole caricature has been built: Smith the prophet of greed, the apostle of selfishness, the man who taught that the pursuit of private gain is the only engine society needs and that morality can be left to take care of itself. It is one of the most consequential misreadings in the history of ideas, and this lesson exists to correct it at the root.

Because here is the fact that the caricature cannot accommodate. Before Adam Smith was an economist — indeed, before economics existed as a discipline for him to belong to — he was a professor of **moral philosophy** at the University of Glasgow. His first great book, published in 1759, seventeen years before *The Wealth of Nations*, was not about markets at all. It was about the foundations of morality. It was called *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*, and it opens not with self-interest but with its opposite. To understand the free-market tradition, you have to begin where its founder began: with the question of how human beings come to care about one another.

## The First Sentence

The very first sentence of *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* dismantles the caricature before it can form. Smith observes that however selfish we may suppose man to be, there are evidently principles in his nature that make him care about the fortune of others and take pleasure in their happiness, even though he gets nothing from it but the pleasure of seeing it.[^1] Read that again, because it is the cornerstone of everything. The man supposedly devoted to selfishness opens his life's work by insisting, as a plain observation about human nature, that we are *not* purely selfish — that concern for others is woven into us at the root, that we are built to be moved by the joys and sorrows of people from whom we stand to gain nothing.

This is not a throwaway pleasantry. It is the foundation of a complete theory of morality, and Smith spends the book building the structure that rests on it. His central concept — the thing that does for his moral philosophy what gravity does for Newton's physics — is **sympathy**.

## Sympathy and the Mechanism of Fellow-Feeling

We have to be careful with the word, because Smith means something more precise and more interesting than ordinary pity. By **sympathy** he means our capacity to enter imaginatively into the situation of another person and to feel, in a fainter and secondhand way, what we imagine they are feeling. When we see a man about to be struck, Smith notes, we flinch and draw back our own leg. When we watch someone in grief, something in us echoes the grief. We cannot feel another's sensations directly — there is no nerve that runs between us — but we can place ourselves, by imagination, in their position, and our imagination produces a shadow of their emotion in our own breast.

This act of imaginative projection is, for Smith, the basic engine of all moral life. And from it he derives morality through a single elegant move. We do not merely sympathize with others; we constantly check whether others can sympathize with *us*. When I feel a surge of anger or grief or joy, I instinctively ask — often without realizing it — whether a bystander, watching me, could go along with what I feel. If my reaction is proportionate, if an onlooker could enter into it and approve, I feel it is appropriate. If my reaction is excessive — if I rage over a trivial slight, or collapse over a small loss — I sense that no reasonable observer could share it, and I feel a flush of impropriety. Morality, in this account, is born in the gap between what I feel and what others can feel along with me. We are social creatures who are perpetually, mostly unconsciously, adjusting our conduct to what we sense others can endorse.

## The Impartial Spectator

But Smith knew the obvious objection. If morality is just doing what actual onlookers approve of, then it collapses into mere conformity, the tyranny of public opinion — and a man alone, or a man surrounded by corrupt company, would have no morality at all. So Smith makes the move that elevates his whole system from sociology into genuine ethics. Over time, he argues, we internalize the perspective of the observer. We develop within ourselves an imagined witness — neutral, well-informed, and fair — who watches our conduct from the outside and judges it without partiality to our own interests. Smith calls this internal judge the **impartial spectator**, and it is one of the most beautiful concepts in the history of moral philosophy.

The impartial spectator is conscience, but conscience explained — not handed down as a mysterious faculty but built up from the raw material of sympathy. It is the man within the breast, the figure who lets us step outside our own cravings and excuses and ask what a fair observer, who could see everything and favored no one, would make of what we are about to do. It is what allows a person to do right when no one is watching, to resist the approval of a corrupt crowd, to feel the sting of guilt over a secret act that brought public praise. Smith draws a sharp and lovely distinction here: there is a difference between being *praised* and being *praiseworthy*, between being *blamed* and being *blameworthy*. The shallow person wants only to be praised; the person of real character wants to be *worthy* of praise, and would rather be justly blamed than unjustly admired. That orientation toward praiseworthiness — toward what the impartial spectator would actually approve, regardless of what the crowd happens to say — is the mark of genuine virtue.

This is not the writing of a man who thinks society runs on self-interest alone. It is the writing of a man who thinks the moral life is a demanding, lifelong discipline of learning to see ourselves honestly through the eyes of a fair judge we carry within.

## So What About the Butcher and the Brewer?

Now we can return to the famous line and read it correctly. When Smith says in *The Wealth of Nations* that we get our dinner from the butcher's regard to his own interest rather than his benevolence, he is making a specific and limited point about how to organize the *economic* coordination of a vast society of strangers — not a sweeping claim that self-interest is the whole of human motivation or the foundation of the good life.

The point is this. Benevolence is real and precious, but it is also scarce and short-range. I can rely on my friends and family to act from love toward me, but a great commercial society requires the cooperation of thousands of strangers I will never meet and who have no reason to love me — the farmer who grew the wheat, the miller, the baker, the shipper. We cannot run a civilization on the expectation that millions of strangers will feel benevolence toward us; there is not enough benevolence in the world to go around, and it cannot reach that far. What the market does — and this is its moral marvel, not its moral failing — is arrange things so that strangers serve one another's needs *even without* loving one another, by making it in each person's interest to provide what others want. The baker need not love me for me to get bread; he need only want my custom. This is a way of harnessing the limited supply of human benevolence by supplementing it with something more plentiful, and thereby extending cooperation across a whole society. Far from dispensing with morality, the market presupposes it: it works only within a framework of trust, honesty, and lawfulness that the moral sentiments supply.

So the two books are not in contradiction. The *Moral Sentiments* describes the inner moral life of the human being and the close relationships governed by sympathy and love. The *Wealth of Nations* describes how a society of such beings can coordinate the production of material goods across distances too vast for love to bridge. The first is about how we ought to live and feel; the second is about how we can eat. Smith saw both as parts of a single picture of human flourishing.

## The "Adam Smith Problem"

It is worth naming the famous scholarly muddle here, because you will meet it. In the nineteenth century, particularly among German scholars, it became fashionable to allege a deep contradiction between the two books — to claim that the Smith of 1759, who founded morality on sympathy, could not be reconciled with the Smith of 1776, who built economics on self-interest. They called it *Das Adam Smith Problem*, and they generally concluded that Smith had either changed his mind or been incoherent.

The verdict of careful modern scholarship is that there is no problem at all — that it was an artifact of reading each book in isolation and misunderstanding what each was trying to do.[^2] We now know that Smith revised *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* repeatedly throughout his life, producing a substantially expanded sixth edition in 1790, the year of his death and fourteen years *after* the *Wealth of Nations*. He never abandoned the moral philosophy; he deepened it, late in life, with the economics already long behind him. The two works were, in his own mind, complementary halves of one continuous inquiry into human nature and human society. The man who is supposed to have replaced morality with markets spent his final year refining his theory of conscience.

## Justice and Beneficence: The Pillar and the Ornament

There is a further distinction in *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* that turns out to be quietly foundational for everything Smith would later say about economics and government, and it concerns two different kinds of virtue. Smith separates **beneficence** — active kindness, generosity, the doing of positive good to others — from **justice** — the refraining from harm, the respecting of others' lives, property, and rights.

The two, he argues, have completely different standing in social life. Beneficence is the ornament of society; it is what makes life beautiful and warm, and we rightly admire it. But it cannot be *compelled*. You cannot force a man to be generous, and a society does not collapse merely because its members are less charitable than they ought to be. Justice is different. Justice, Smith says, is the main pillar that upholds the whole edifice; remove it, and the great fabric of human society dissolves into nothing. Justice — the basic security of person and property, the assurance that others will not rob, defraud, or assault you — *can* and *must* be enforced, because without it no society can stand at all. A community can survive a shortage of kindness; it cannot survive a collapse of justice.

This distinction does enormous work later. It is why Smith, the moral philosopher of sympathy, can also be the theorist of a limited government: the indispensable, enforceable task of the state is to secure justice — to protect people and their property from force and fraud — while beneficence is largely left to the free play of sympathy, conscience, and voluntary association rather than to compulsion. The market and the wider civil society are where beneficence and cooperation flourish; the law's hard core is justice. A great deal of the free-market tradition's view of the proper scope of government is already implicit in this single distinction between the pillar and the ornament.

## The Man of System

One passage from *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* deserves special attention, because it reveals how seamlessly Smith's moral philosophy flows into his suspicion of overweening power. Smith warns against a particular and dangerous type he calls the **man of system** — the reformer so enamored of his own ideal blueprint for society that he imagines he can arrange a whole population as easily as a hand arranges pieces on a chessboard.[^3]

The fatal error of the man of system, Smith observes, is that he forgets the pieces on a real chessboard have no motion of their own beyond what the player imposes, whereas in the great chessboard of human society every single piece has a principle of motion entirely its own — its own purposes, its own judgments, its own will. When the planner's grand design runs against the countless individual motions of real people, the result is not the harmonious order he imagined but misery and disorder, as he tries to force living human beings into positions they have every reason to resist. This is one of the deepest and most enduring insights of the whole tradition: that society is not a machine to be engineered from above by a clever designer, but an order that emerges from the free interaction of millions of self-directing people, and that the arrogance of the central planner — however benevolent his intentions — collides ruinously with the irreducible agency of the governed. Written in 1759, it is a warning the twentieth century would learn at catastrophic cost, and it grows directly out of Smith's view of the human being as a creature of conscience and self-direction, never a mere piece to be moved.

## Why This Matters for the Whole Tradition

Why begin a course on the free-market tradition with a book about empathy and conscience? Because the deepest defenders of markets have always understood — even when their loudest popularizers forgot — that a free economy is not a machine that runs on greed and requires no virtue. It is exactly the reverse. A free society demands *more* moral self-government from its members than an authoritarian one, not less, precisely because it relies less on external compulsion. When people are free to make their own contracts, keep their own promises, and pursue their own ends, the whole arrangement depends on their being, by and large, honest, trustworthy, and restrained — on there being a working impartial spectator inside most people most of the time. Markets do not manufacture this moral capital; they presuppose it, draw on it, and, if they are wise, take care not to corrode it.

This is the thread that runs through the entire tradition we are about to study. Later figures will disagree fiercely about money, regulation, and the proper scope of the state. But the soundest of them never forgot Smith's starting point: that *homo economicus*, the cold calculating self-interested agent of the textbooks, is a useful simplification for certain narrow problems and a disastrous error if mistaken for a complete account of the human being. The real Adam Smith knew that man is a creature of sympathy before he is a creature of calculation, a being who longs not merely to be wealthy but to be worthy — and that any social order which forgets this is building on sand.

We have, in other words, recovered the foundation. In the next lesson we will build the famous structure upon it — the division of labor, the invisible hand, the critique of mercantilism, and the system of natural liberty laid out in *The Wealth of Nations*. But you will read all of it differently now, knowing that the man who wrote it began his life's work by insisting that we are made to care about one another, and ended it still refining what it means to deserve, rather than merely to receive, the approval of an honest witness. The popular Adam Smith — the cartoon apostle of greed — turns out to be very nearly the opposite of the real one. The real one is a moralist first and an economist second, and his economics makes full sense only as the work of a moralist. Keep that order in mind; it is the key that unlocks the entire tradition that follows.
