Unit 1 gave Marx two full lessons and took his challenge seriously: the labor theory of value, the doctrine of surplus extraction, the charge that profit is unpaid labor time. This lesson completes that arc, because the answer to Marx was written by the man just studied. In 1896, following the posthumous publication of the third volume of Capital, Böhm-Bawerk published Karl Marx and the Close of His System — the most famous critique of Marxian economics ever written, and one of the most consequential book-length rebuttals in the history of ideas. Its central claim is not that Marx was immoral or his followers dangerous; it is narrower and deadlier: that Marx's system fails on its own terms — that the third volume of Capital contradicts the first, that the contradiction was structural rather than incidental, and that the labor theory of value on which the entire edifice of exploitation rests had already been dissolved by the marginal revolution. A century and a quarter later, the debate this book opened is still the reference point for every discussion of whether Marxian economics survives as economics. This lesson reconstructs the argument with the care it deserves — including, at the end, the strongest things Marxians have said back.

## The Promise of Volume Three

To feel the force of Böhm-Bawerk's attack, recall the problem Marx had set himself.[^1] Volume one of Capital (1867) rests everything on the labor theory of value: commodities exchange in proportion to the socially necessary labor time embodied in them, and profit arises because labor-power is the unique commodity that produces more value than it costs — surplus value, extracted in the unpaid portion of the working day. But volume one contained a time bomb, and Marx knew it: if profit comes only from living labor, then industries using much labor and little machinery should yield higher profit rates than mechanized ones. In reality, competition equalizes profit rates across industries regardless of their labor-intensity. Marx acknowledged the difficulty and promised its resolution in a later volume. For nearly three decades — through Marx's death in 1883 — the movement waited, and critics were told the answer was coming. Engels published volume three in 1894, containing the promised solution: the transformation of values into prices of production. Böhm-Bawerk read it, and his verdict gave his book its title: the system had not been completed; it had been closed — shut down by its own third volume.

## The Contradiction

The transformation solution runs as follows. Individual commodities do not, after all, exchange at their labor values; they exchange at "prices of production" — cost plus the average rate of profit — which deviate systematically from labor values, above them in capital-intensive industries and below in labor-intensive ones. The labor theory holds only in aggregate: total profit equals total surplus value, total prices equal total values, so that the sum of exploitation is conserved even as competition redistributes it among capitalists. Böhm-Bawerk's response was a philosopher's dissection of what had just been conceded. The law of value was advanced in volume one as the law governing the exchange of commodities — that is its content and its only content. Volume three now admits that individual commodities do not, even as a tendency, exchange at their values. Then the law governs nothing: a law of exchange ratios that fails for every actual exchange ratio, and is rescued only "in the aggregate," asserts, in the aggregate, a tautology — the sum of prices equals the sum of prices, once value is whatever the sum is redefined to preserve. Marx's followers, Böhm-Bawerk wrote, hail as a solution what is in fact the abandonment of the theory with its flag still flying: volume three does not answer the contradiction with volume one; it enacts it. And he pressed the diagnosis deeper. Marx had derived the labor theory not from observation of exchange but from a dialectical sieve at the opening of volume one — abstracting from commodities every property except being products of labor, and declaring the residue the substance of value. But the sieve was rigged: scarce goods, natural goods, and the services of waiting and risk were excluded from consideration before the abstraction began, so labor emerged as the common substance only because everything else had been shown the door. The system's foundation, Böhm-Bawerk concluded, was not an established economic law but a postulate defended by exclusion — and when reality (equal profit rates) collided with the postulate, it was reality that volume three tried to transform.

## The Marginal Solvent and the Missing Dimension

Beneath the internal critique lay the positive theory of the two previous lessons, and Böhm-Bawerk deployed both of its blades. Value, the marginal revolution had shown, is not a substance embodied in goods by production; it is a relation between goods and valuing minds at the margin. Labor is valued because its products are valued, not the reverse — the direction of causation the water-diamond paradox had already settled — and a theory that derives price from labor content has the arrow backward, which is why it must treat the pricing of rare wines, unimproved land, and everything scarce-but-unlabored as awkward exceptions. And the specific engine of exploitation, surplus value, dissolves under the time analysis of the previous lesson. The worker is paid today; the product sells in a year. Marx's arithmetic compares the wage with the product's eventual price as if the interval were nothing — as if present and future goods were the same goods. They are not: present goods carry an agio over future goods everywhere and always. The capitalist advances present wages against future output, carrying the interval and its risk; the difference the Marxian calls unpaid labor is, on Böhm-Bawerk's analysis, the discount on time itself — an income that would have to be paid, to someone, under any system that did not abolish the future. The worker who wants the full future value of his product can have it, by waiting for the sale; what he cannot have is the future value now, because now and later are not equal, and no revolution can make them equal. Exploitation, as an economic (not moral) theorem, thus rests on ignoring the one dimension — time — in which production actually occurs.

## The Aftermath

The debate did not end in 1896; it professionalized. The Marxian economist Rudolf Hilferding replied in 1904 that Böhm-Bawerk had misread the project — Marx's value theory was social and macroscopic, a theory of the relations behind prices, not a predictor of individual ratios — a defense that concedes the economics while relocating the doctrine, and that set the pattern for a century of Marxian responses. Ladislaus von Bortkiewicz showed in 1906–07 that Marx's own transformation arithmetic was internally flawed (he transformed outputs but not inputs), and that corrected versions could not preserve both of Marx's aggregate equalities at once — a technical wound reopened repeatedly through the twentieth-century "transformation problem" literature, and pronounced fatal again in the 1970s when Piero Sraffa's framework allowed Ian Steedman to show prices and profits derivable from physical data with no detour through labor values at all: the labor theory was not wrong so much as redundant. Within economics as a discipline, the verdict Böhm-Bawerk sought became the settled one: no serious school builds on the labor theory of value, and Marxian economics survives principally in departments other than economics. Whether that verdict settles Marx — the historian of capitalism, the theorist of class and crisis — is a different question, and this course has already insisted the answer is no. What Böhm-Bawerk closed was the system: the claim to have demonstrated, scientifically, that profit is theft.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The strongest Marxian and skeptical replies deserve their hearing. First, Hilferding's relocation is not mere retreat: read as social theory — a claim that market prices veil relations of power and dependence between classes — Marx's value analysis raises questions (Who owns? Who must sell labor to live? Who commands the surplus, whatever we call it?) that survive the destruction of the price theory, and Böhm-Bawerk's own framework, critics note, takes the existing distribution of property as given rather than justified. Second, time preference explains why an interval commands a price; it does not explain why one class habitually owns the present goods and another habitually waits — the historical question of primitive accumulation on which Marx's fire was concentrated. Third, some modern Marxians (the "New Interpretation" and related schools) have reconstructed value theory as an accounting framework immune to the Bortkiewicz-Steedman results, though at the price, their critics reply, of making it unfalsifiable. Fourth, Böhm-Bawerk's polemic occasionally hardens Marx's subtler formulations — Marx himself called the value-price relation a tendency operating through deviations — though Böhm-Bawerk's rejoinder, that a tendency contradicted by the systematic pattern of deviations is no tendency, retains its bite. What survives the counterpoints is the core verdict, and it is one of the great settled results in the history of economic thought: as a theory of price and profit, the labor theory of value is dead — killed by the marginal revolution's account of value and by the internal contradiction its own third volume exposed — and with it dies the scientific (as opposed to moral or historical) demonstration of exploitation. The Austrian school had produced its first world-historical result. Its next great figure, Wieser, would give the school — and all of economics — its most universal concept: opportunity cost. He is next.
