The marginal revolution's last founding figure is the least famous and, for the twentieth century's macroeconomic wars, arguably the most important. Knut Wicksell, working in Sweden at the century's turn, asked a question none of the schools had answered: what connects the world of the previous lessons — real capital, time preference, Böhm-Bawerk's roundabout production — to the world of banks, credit, and money in which actual interest rates are quoted? His answer introduced a distinction on which whole schools would later be built: between the natural rate of interest, set by the real productivity of capital and society's willingness to save, and the market rate, set by the banking system's creation of credit. When the two rates coincide, the price level is stable and saving matches investment; when banks hold the market rate below the natural rate, credit expands, prices rise cumulatively, and investment is fed with purchasing power no one saved. That single idea is the hinge between nineteenth-century value theory and twentieth-century macroeconomics: Hayek's business cycle theory and Keynes's early work both descend directly from it, and every modern central bank that speaks of a "neutral rate," r-star, is speaking Wicksell whether it knows it or not.

## The Scandalous Professor

Wicksell's biography is the strangest in this course so far.[^1] Born in Stockholm in 1851 — the same year as Böhm-Bawerk and Wieser — and orphaned young, he came to economics late and sideways: his first fame in Sweden was as a radical public lecturer, a neo-Malthusian who scandalized the country in 1880 by advocating contraception, and a lifelong provocateur who attacked the monarchy, the church, and marriage (he lived in a deliberate common-law union to protest the institution). He took up economics in his thirties to give his social criticism foundations, studied Böhm-Bawerk's work deeply, and produced his masterpieces — Value, Capital and Rent (1893) and Interest and Prices (1898) — before he held any academic post; Swedish law required a law degree for a chair in economics, so he earned one in middle age, becoming a professor at Lund at fifty. At fifty-seven he served two months in prison for a satirical lecture blaspheming against the virgin birth, and worked on economics in his cell. Yet this incorrigible radical wrote economics of scrupulous, almost conservative rigor — synthesizing Walras's general equilibrium, Böhm-Bawerk's capital theory, and marginal productivity theory into a unified system — and founded, through his students, the Stockholm School that made small Sweden a great power in interwar economics.

## The Two Rates

Wicksell's problem was the quantity theory of money, which he accepted in substance and found mechanically empty: saying that more money raises prices explains nothing about how — about the concrete chain by which new money enters and moves an economy. His answer runs through the banking system, and it begins with the distinction that bears his name. Imagine first an economy where all lending is in kind — real capital lent directly, seed corn to farmers. There the interest rate would settle at what the marginal productivity of capital and the supply of real saving jointly determine: the natural rate. It is Böhm-Bawerk's world — the price of time, set by roundaboutness and patience. Now introduce modern banks, which do not merely transfer existing savings but create credit — deposits conjured in the act of lending. The rate banks charge, the market or money rate, is a policy of the banking system, held wherever the banks choose within wide limits, because their reserves constrain them loosely and a pure credit economy would constrain them not at all. Nothing guarantees the two rates coincide, and everything interesting happens when they do not. Hold the money rate below the natural rate, and every entrepreneur sees projects whose expected yield (the natural rate) exceeds the cost of funds: borrowing expands, investment spending rises, factors of production are bid away from consumption goods, and prices rise. Crucially, the rise does not stop: as long as the gap persists, the incentive persists, and the price level climbs in Wicksell's famous cumulative process — inflation feeding on itself not because money rained from the sky but because the price of credit is wrong. Hold the money rate above the natural rate and the process runs in reverse, a cumulative deflation. Price stability, on this analysis, is not automatic; it is the knife-edge on which the banking system keeps the money rate tracking an invisible, shifting natural rate it can never directly observe.

## Forced Saving and the Bridge to the Cycle

Buried in the cumulative process is a further insight the Austrians would seize: when banks lend created credit at below the natural rate, the investment that results is financed by no one's voluntary saving. The new purchasing power lets entrepreneurs outbid consumers for real resources; consumers, facing higher prices, involuntarily consume less than they intended. Wicksell and his successors called this forced saving — the diversion of real resources to investment through inflation rather than thrift — and it means the credit expansion does not merely raise prices, it redirects the economy's real structure: more roundabout projects are begun than society's actual patience (its true time preference, its natural rate) stands ready to fund. Wicksell drew the monetary conclusion — instability of the price level. Mises, reading Interest and Prices as he wrote his own Theory of Money and Credit, and Hayek after him, drew the further, darker conclusion the Austrian business cycle lesson will develop in full: the malinvested structure must eventually be liquidated when the credit stops, so the artificial boom carries its bust inside it. Keynes, meanwhile, built the Treatise on Money (1930) explicitly on Wicksellian foundations — savings, investment, and the rate that would equate them — before breaking the frame in the General Theory. The two great warring macroeconomics of the twentieth century are, to a remarkable degree, rival readings of the same Swedish book.

## The Ghost in the Central Bank

Wicksell's practical proposal was disarmingly simple: since the natural rate cannot be observed, use prices as the telltale — when the price level rises, the money rate is too low; raise it; when prices fall, lower it. That rule — steer the policy rate toward an unobservable neutral rate, using inflation as the signal — is, in essence, the operating doctrine of every inflation-targeting central bank in the modern world, and the contemporary apparatus of a "neutral rate of interest" (r-star) that policy should neither exceed nor undershoot is Wicksell's construct under a new name, complete with his difficulty: the star is invisible, it moves, and the estimates of it command policy while carrying error bars wide enough to drive a business cycle through. The course will return to this machinery in the monetary-policy lessons; what matters here is the intellectual position Wicksell occupies. He is the point where the marginal revolution's real analysis — value, capital, time — is joined to money and the aggregate economy; the point where price theory becomes macroeconomics. Every school ahead stands on this junction: the Austrians reading the two-rate gap as the seed of the cycle, the Keynesians reading the saving-investment nexus as the seed of demand failure, the monetarists and the New Keynesians alike running policy by the ghost of the natural rate.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The Wicksellian construct has taken serious hits, and they should be recorded. The concept of a single natural rate presupposes that "the" real return on capital is well-defined; the Cambridge capital controversies showed that in a many-goods world with heterogeneous capital there may be no unique natural rate independent of the price system itself — Sraffa pressed exactly this against Hayek in their famous 1932 exchange, arguing there are as many "natural" rates as commodities, a wound to the tidy two-rate story that Austrians and mainstream alike have had to answer with reformulations. Keynes's mature position was more radical: in the General Theory he renounced the natural rate outright, arguing there is a different one for every level of employment and no mechanism ensuring the market gravitates to the full-employment one — for Keynesians, the Wicksellian frame assumes the coordination it should be proving. Empirically, modern estimates of r-star are so uncertain and unstable that some economists, and occasionally central bankers themselves, have suggested retiring the concept as a policy guide. And Wicksell's price-level rule, applied mechanically, can mislead: productivity-driven deflation and demand-driven deflation call for opposite responses, a distinction his telltale cannot see — a critique the Austrians themselves press, since in their story the damage of the 1920s was done while prices were stable. What survives is nonetheless foundational: the insight that the interest rate is not just a price among prices but the coordinating price between present and future; that a banking system can hold that price away from what real saving and productivity warrant; and that when it does, the consequences arrive not as a simple price-level fact but as a distortion of the economy's structure in time. On that hinge the course now turns from the founders to the retrospective: what, in sum, did the marginal revolution change — and what two roads led out of it?

[^1]: Knut Wicksell (1851–1926): Stockholm-born, orphaned early; neo-Malthusian radical lecturer (the 1880 contraception scandal); law degree acquired in middle age to qualify for an economics chair (Lund, 1901); two months' imprisonment (1908) for a blasphemy satire; founder-figure of the Stockholm School (Lindahl, Myrdal, Ohlin). Works: *Value, Capital and Rent* (1893), synthesizing Walras and Böhm-Bawerk; *Interest and Prices* (*Geldzins und Güterpreise*, 1898); *Lectures on Political Economy* (1901, 1906). Doctrines: the natural rate of interest (determined by the marginal productivity of real capital and real saving — the in-kind, Böhm-Bawerkian benchmark) vs. the market/money rate (a policy of the credit-creating banking system, loosely constrained in a "pure credit economy"); the cumulative process — a sustained gap between the rates driving self-reinforcing inflation or deflation until the gap closes; forced saving — resources shifted to investment via credit-financed bidding rather than voluntary thrift, redirecting the real structure of production; the price-level rule for tracking the unobservable natural rate. Descent: Mises (*The Theory of Money and Credit*, 1912) and Hayek (*Prices and Production*, 1931) building Austrian business cycle theory on the two-rate gap and forced saving; Keynes's *Treatise on Money* (1930) as explicitly Wicksellian before the *General Theory*'s (1936) renunciation of a unique natural rate; the modern "neutral rate"/r-star apparatus of inflation-targeting central banks (e.g., Laubach-Williams estimates) as Wicksell's construct with acknowledged massive estimation uncertainty. Criticisms: Sraffa's 1932 attack on a unique natural rate in a heterogeneous-capital world (the "own-rates" argument against Hayek), the Cambridge capital results, Keynes's employment-relativity of the natural rate, r-star instability as a policy guide, and the price-level telltale's blindness to the productivity-vs-demand sources of deflation (pressed by the Austrians regarding the 1920s).
