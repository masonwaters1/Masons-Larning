This unit began with classical economics in crisis and ends with a transformed discipline. Before moving into the twentieth century, it is worth stepping back — as this course periodically does — to take stock of what actually changed between 1871 and 1900, and why it mattered beyond the seminar room. Revolutions in ideas are easy to announce and hard to assess; the marginal revolution has been called both the founding of scientific economics and a wrong turn from which the discipline never recovered, sometimes by the same critics. The sober accounting, attempted here, is that the revolution accomplished four permanent things: it replaced a broken theory of value with a working one; it relocated the foundations of economics from classes and aggregates to choosing individuals; it converted economics from a theory of wealth into a logic of choice under scarcity, vastly enlarging its domain; and it destroyed the scientific basis of the era's most powerful critique of the market order. Each deserves its reckoning — as does the honest ledger of what the revolution obscured, postponed, and got wrong.

## A Theory of Value That Works

The first achievement is the one the unit's opening lessons documented: the classical labor-and-cost theories of value, strained by a century of anomalies — the water-diamond paradox, rent, scarce and unreproducible goods, joint products — were replaced by an account that handles every case with one principle.[^1] Value flows from marginal utility under scarcity; costs are opportunity costs, themselves reflections of value in alternative uses; prices of inputs are imputed backward from the consumer goods they help produce. The direction of explanation reversed — from cost-determines-value to value-determines-what-costs-are-worth-incurring — and with the reversal, the anomalies dissolved rather than multiplied, which is the signature of a superior theory. It is easy, from inside a discipline that has taken this for granted for a century and a half, to miss the scale of the event: the central concept of economics — what value is and where it comes from — was rebuilt, and the rebuild held. No school in this course, from Vienna to Cambridge to Chicago, works with any other foundation.

## The Individual at the Foundation

The second achievement is methodological and quieter. Classical economics reasoned about classes and aggregates — landlords, laborers, capital, the wage fund, the national product — with the individual chooser appearing, if at all, as a specimen of his class. Marginalism inverted the architecture: the elementary unit of analysis became the individual valuing, choosing, economizing mind, and everything larger — prices, markets, distributions, institutions — became something to be explained as the outcome of interacting individual choices. This is the principle later named methodological individualism, and its consequences ran everywhere. Distribution ceased to be a struggle among classes over a fixed product and became the imputation of value to contributions at the margin — landlords, workers, and capitalists all earning, in competitive conditions, the marginal product of what they supplied, an analysis that dethroned the classical class-war framing (and, as the counterpoint below notes, acquired ideological uses of its own). Aggregates lost their self-evidence: "capital," "labor," and "the price level" became summaries of heterogeneous individual facts, summaries that can mislead — a suspicion the Austrians would radicalize and the Keynesians would defy, in a quarrel that structures the century ahead. And economics acquired its enduring philosophical center of gravity: whatever else divides the schools in this course, all of them explain social outcomes from individual choices, and their deepest disputes — Hayek against Keynes above all — are disputes about how much the individual chooser knows and how aggregates behave when no one intends them.

## From Wealth to Choice

The third achievement redefined the subject itself. Classical political economy was the science of wealth — production, accumulation, distribution of the material product. Marginalism's foundation in valuing choice contained no reference to material wealth at all: it described any allocation of scarce means among competing ends. The redefinition took decades to be made explicit — Lionel Robbins gave it canonical form in 1932: economics is the science which studies human behavior as a relationship between ends and scarce means which have alternative uses — but the substance was present from Menger's first pages, and its consequences were imperial. Time, attention, effort, risk, information, marriage, crime, votes: anything scarce and chosen fell, in principle, within the analysis, and the twentieth century duly saw economics expand into law, politics, family, and discrimination — the Becker and Buchanan lessons ahead are direct heirs of this move. So is the everyday transformation in how educated people reason: thinking at the margin, counting opportunity costs, expecting incentives to matter — the mental equipment this unit has been installing is the marginal revolution domesticated into common sense. A theory of pin factories became a logic of human action; whether that enlargement was insight or imperial overreach is itself one of the standing debates the course will visit, but no one disputes that it happened here.

## The Political Stakes

The fourth consequence was the one Böhm-Bawerk made explicit: the revolution destroyed the scientific foundation of Marxian socialism. This must be stated with the precision the course practices. Marginalism did not refute the moral critique of capitalism, the historical indictment of its cruelties, or the possibility of preferring another system; those live on other ground. What it destroyed was the claim to have demonstrated, from within economic science, that profit is unpaid labor — because that demonstration required the labor theory of value, and the labor theory of value did not survive the marginal analysis, either as an account of price (the anomalies) or as an account of surplus (the time analysis). After the 1890s, the scientific-socialist position had to fight on new terrain — and did: the great socialist arguments of the twentieth century, from the calculation debate to market socialism, are conducted in marginalist language, conceding the foundation while contesting the conclusions. That is the deepest measure of the revolution: even its adversaries had to adopt its tools. At the same time — and the course insists on the symmetry — marginalism supplied no automatic vindication of laissez-faire. The same apparatus that dissolved exploitation theory generated, in Marshall's and Pigou's hands, the economics of externalities and market failure; the same equimarginal logic underwrites the case for corrective taxation; and the revolution's three founders held politics ranging from Walras's land-nationalizing socialism to Wieser's interventionist sympathies. The marginal revolution armed every side of the coming century's arguments, which is exactly why this course must follow those arguments rather than declare them settled in 1900.

One further measure of the achievement deserves its own paragraph: durability under attack. Every subsequent upheaval in economics — the Keynesian revolution, the econometric transformation, the behavioral challenge, the repeated crises of confidence after depressions and crashes — has overturned conclusions, models, and policy doctrines, and not one has touched the marginalist foundation. Keynes revolted against the classical conclusions while reasoning about marginal propensities and marginal efficiencies; the behavioral economists documented how real choosers deviate from the marginal ideal, using experimental designs whose very logic — comparing choices at the margin under varied conditions — is marginalist through and through; even the harshest modern critics of the discipline attack its assumptions of rationality and equilibrium, not its account of value. A framework that survives a hundred and thirty years of internal revolution, absorbing each rebellion as a refinement, has passed the only test theories can pass. Whatever the twentieth century did to economics — and the rest of this course is the story of what it did — it did it on marginalist ground.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The ledger's other side, assembled from critics the course will meet at length. The revolution narrowed as it sharpened: in centering the allocating chooser it marginalized what the classicals did best — growth, development, institutions, the dynamics of accumulation — and questions of why some nations are rich returned to the discipline's center only generations later, a detour some historians of thought count as the revolution's largest cost. The static bias was real: equilibrium allocation of given resources under given preferences became the canonical problem, and process, discovery, and novelty — the things Menger's own version kept alive — were largely lost to the mathematical mainstream until the Austrians forced them back. Marginal productivity as a theory of distribution slid, in careless hands, from analysis into apologetics — "everyone earns their contribution" treated as a moral verdict rather than a competitive-equilibrium theorem hedged with assumptions, a slide John Bates Clark's critics rightly attacked and that serious economists of every school disown. The utilitarian scaffolding proved shakier than the structure: measurable pleasure gave way to ordinal preference, and the behavioral lessons ahead will document how far real choosers stray from the maximizing chooser the theory idealized. And historians note deflationarily that the "revolution" was slower, messier, and more national than the legend — a simultaneous triple discovery followed by decades of mutual incomprehension, consolidated less by intellectual victory than by textbook standardization. All of this stands, and the verdict survives it: the marginal revolution gave economics a working theory of value, a coherent foundation in individual choice, a vastly enlarged domain, and a decisive settlement of the era's central scientific-political dispute — at the price of narrowed questions and postponed problems whose recovery is the story of the century ahead. That story begins with a fork: two roads led out of the revolution, and the next lesson maps them.
