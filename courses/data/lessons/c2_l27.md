Every school has a founder and a rock. Menger founded the Austrian school; Ludwig von Mises was its rock — the man on whom the tradition survived when history did its best to end it. His life spans the school's whole arc of glory, catastrophe, and exile: born in the Habsburg empire at its height, trained in Böhm-Bawerk's seminar, the intellectual center of Vienna between the wars, driven across Europe by the Nazis, and finally an old man in New York, teaching an unpaid seminar to a remnant, having outlived — it seemed — the relevance of everything he believed. He was also, by wide agreement of friend and enemy, the most unbending figure in the history of economics: a man who did not soften conclusions for colleagues, funders, or the spirit of the age, whose personal motto was Virgil's tu ne cede malis — do not give in to evil, but proceed ever more boldly against it. This lesson is a portrait: the life, the formation, the battles, and the mission that organized them. The next three take up his system — the calculation argument, the praxeology, the theory of money — but the system is not intelligible without the man, because Mises understood his economics as a single, lifelong argument for civilization itself.

## Vienna

Mises was born in 1881 in Lemberg, in Austrian Galicia, to a prosperous, recently ennobled Jewish family, and educated in Vienna at the moment Vienna was the intellectual capital of Europe.[^1] At the university he came under the spell that converted so many in these lessons: reading Menger's Principles, he later said, made him an economist. The decisive formation was Böhm-Bawerk's seminar, where the empire's finest minds dissected value theory and where Mises absorbed the master's capital theory, his rigor, and his combativeness. Barred by the era's academic antisemitism from a paid professorship, he made his career outside the university — as economist and then chief economist of the Vienna Chamber of Commerce, one of the most influential economic-policy posts in Austria — while teaching as an unpaid Privatdozent. In 1912, at thirty-one, he published The Theory of Money and Credit, the book that joined Menger's value theory to money and banking and planted the seed of the Austrian business cycle theory; a later lesson gives it full treatment. After the war — in which he served three years as an artillery officer at the front — he turned to the work of his life: the running critique of every form of the century's collectivism, begun with the 1920 calculation essay and the 1922 treatise Socialism, conducted from his famous private seminar, which met in his Chamber office and gathered the future of Austrian and much non-Austrian economics: Hayek, Machlup, Haberler, Morgenstern, the philosopher Alfred Schütz. In those same years, as advisor to the Austrian government, he fought the inflation and — by Hayek's testimony — did as much as any man to keep Austria from the hyperinflationary abyss into which Germany fell.

## The Mission Takes Shape

To understand Mises, place him in his moment. He came of age when the educated world was concluding, almost unanimously, that the market economy was a transitional stage — that the future belonged to planning, in one of its competing costumes: Marxist socialism to the east, "war socialism" and then National Socialism in Germany, corporatism, interventionism, and the planned economy's respectable admirers throughout the democracies. Against this consensus Mises set a single thesis, elaborated for fifty years: that the market economy is not one option among several but the only possible form of advanced social cooperation — that private property in the means of production, market prices, and monetary calculation are the preconditions of rational economic order, so that socialism is not merely inefficient but impossible as a rational economy, and interventionism is not a stable middle way but an unstable process whose failures generate demands for further intervention, tending toward full control. The 1920 essay made the impossibility argument (next lesson); Socialism (1922) expanded it into a comprehensive critique that, by their own accounts, broke the socialist convictions of a generation of young intellectuals — Hayek foremost; Liberalism (1927) stated the positive program — property, peace, free trade — with a candor that spared no nationalist or interventionist piety; a stream of works on method defended economic theory itself against the historicists and positivists who denied its possibility. The mission had a further, darker clarity that most contemporaries lacked: Mises saw early that the fashionable statisms were converging on catastrophe. Sitting in Vienna, advising a rump republic squeezed between red revolution and brown reaction, he wrote that the age's anti-capitalist policies must issue in impoverishment and war — and few now dispute that on the largest questions of his time and place, the unbending man was right and the reasonable consensus was wrong.

## Exile

The catastrophe he predicted drove him out. In 1934, with Austro-fascism in power and Hitler next door, Mises — a Jew and the German-speaking world's most prominent anti-socialist — left Vienna for Geneva and a chair at the Graduate Institute, where he wrote his systematic treatise Nationalökonomie (1940). When France fell, he and his wife Margit fled across a Europe closing around them — their bus threading Nazi checkpoints through the Pyrenees — to New York, arriving in 1940: fifty-nine years old, without position, savings, or, it appeared, a future; the Gestapo had seized his Vienna papers (rediscovered, in one of history's ironies, in Soviet archives decades later). American academia, then at the high tide of Keynesianism and planning-enthusiasm, had no place for him: the man who had held Europe's premier economics seminar was never offered a paid professorship, subsisting on foundation grants and a visiting post at NYU's business school, where from 1945 to 1969 — into his late eighties — he taught a seminar attended by students, businessmen, and disciples, unpaid by the university to the end. There he rewrote his treatise in English as Human Action (1949), the summa the praxeology lesson examines, and gathered the remnant that would become the American Austrian revival: Rothbard, Kirzner, Sennholz, Hazlitt in the front row. He died in 1973, months before the Nobel committee honored his student Hayek for ideas the two had built together — a laurel most Austrians believe belonged, first, to the dead man.

## The Character of the Mission

Mises's temperament is inseparable from his achievement, and both its faces must be shown. The steadfastness was heroic by any measure: he maintained, without institutional shelter, through exile and eclipse, a body of doctrine that events progressively vindicated, and he paid the career price of never trimming — colleagues who bent with the Keynesian age prospered; Mises taught for free. The same iron had costs. He could be intolerant of deviation — famously storming out of the founding meeting of the Mont Pèlerin Society, the free-market international Hayek assembled in 1947, with the words "You're all a bunch of socialists!" flung at men who merely accepted more state than he did — and the seminar culture he inspired could shade into discipleship, with the sectarian dynamics the previous lesson flagged. His rationalism was total: he regarded his conclusions as demonstrated, not probable, which gave his prose its granite authority and his critics their standing charge of dogmatism. The fairest summary may be that the twentieth century got the Mises it needed: a consensus-resistant absolutist was precisely what the survival of a truth against a unanimous age required, and the vices of the character were the price of its function. Whether the doctrine justifies the certainty is the business of the next three lessons, beginning with the argument on which his fame rests — the claim, made in 1920 while half the world's intellectuals were saluting the Soviet dawn, that socialism cannot calculate, and therefore cannot work.

## The Honest Counterpoint

A portrait this admiring owes the reader the case against, beyond temperament. Critics note that Mises's political judgment was not uniformly prophetic: passages of Liberalism written amid the street-fighting of the 1920s credit Italian fascism with having "saved European civilization" from Bolshevism — in context a claim about emergency violence, immediately followed by the warning that fascism was a catastrophic error as a governing idea, but a passage his detractors quote and his admirers must own rather than explain away. His utilitarian-rationalist framework, which grounds liberty on social utility rather than natural right, struck even allies as thin — Rothbard's natural-rights revision, ahead, is an in-house rebellion on exactly this point. His "impossibility" thesis, critics argue, was vindicated in a weaker form than he stated: really-existing socialisms did calculate, badly, for decades — the next lesson weighs whether that observation refutes the argument or illustrates it. And the exile's marginalization was not solely the age's philistinism: his refusal of the discipline's emerging empirical standards guaranteed estrangement from colleagues who could not check his claims by their methods. All of this belongs in the record. What also belongs there is the verdict of the century itself: on the central question — whether comprehensive planning could sustain a modern economy — the marginal, unpaid, unbending exile was right, and nearly everyone who condescended to him was wrong. How he knew is the next lesson.
