The calculation argument of the previous lesson displayed Mises's method at work: a world-historical conclusion reached by pure chains of reasoning, without an equation or a data set in sight. This lesson examines the method itself — because Mises, alone among the great economists, made the method explicit, gave it a name, and defended it as a complete philosophy of economic science. He called it praxeology: the deductive science of human action, whose theorems are derived logically from the concept of action itself and hold, he claimed, with a certainty no empirical hypothesis can possess. His summa, Human Action (1949) — nearly nine hundred pages composed in his New York exile, the largest systematic treatise on economics ever written by one hand — opens not with markets or money but with the acting human being, and builds all of economics upward from that single foundation. Praxeology is the most admired and most attacked thing in the Austrian tradition: to its defenders, the secret of how Mises could be right when every dataset-wielding contemporary was wrong; to its critics, a declaration of independence from the standards of science. Both cases deserve their full statement, and this lesson gives them.

## The Axiom of Action

Everything begins from one proposition: human beings act.[^1] Action, in Mises's precise sense, is purposeful behavior — the employment of means to attain chosen ends; not reflex, not mere movement, but conduct aimed at substituting a preferred state of affairs for a less preferred one. The proposition is not, Mises argued, an empirical generalization that observation might overturn; it is a category we cannot coherently deny, since the denial is itself an action — a means (arguing) deployed toward an end (persuading). In the Kantian idiom of his Vienna formation, action is a priori: the framework through which conduct is intelligible at all. And packed inside the concept, awaiting deduction, is a surprising amount of structure. To act is to prefer — so value exists, and is subjective, revealed in choice. To act is to choose — so alternatives are forgone, and cost (opportunity cost) is a category of all action. Action employs means that are scarce — were they not, no action would be needed — so economizing is universal. Action takes time and aims at the future — so time preference is real, and with it interest. Action presumes the actor believes his means can influence outcomes — so causality and uncertainty are built in: where everything is certain, there is nothing to do. From the bare concept, the fundamental categories of economics — value, cost, scarcity, time, uncertainty, and, with more steps, diminishing marginal utility, exchange, and the downward-sloping demand curve — unfold as implications, the way geometry unfolds from its axioms. Economics, on this view, is not the statistics of behavior; it is the logic of choice, and its core theorems are as immune to statistical refutation as the Pythagorean theorem is to sloppy triangles.

## What Praxeology Forbids

The method's positive claims are matched by prohibitions, and the prohibitions define the Austrian quarrel with the century. If economic law is logical, then it cannot be established or overturned by measurement — and the entire program of positivist economics rests, Mises argued, on a category mistake. There are no constants in human action: unlike atoms, people learn, expect, and change, so yesterday's measured "elasticity" is a fact of economic history, not a parameter of economic law; econometrics, on this reading, produces descriptions of the past dressed as physics. Prediction, likewise, has strict limits: praxeology yields qualitative laws — price ceilings below market prices cause shortages; credit expansion misdirects production — but never magnitudes or dates, because the future depends on future choices and future knowledge, which are not data now. Mises accordingly distinguished theory (praxeology, universal and certain) from history (the record of unique, complex events, to be understood through theory but never able to test it) — and understanding history, including forecasting entrepreneurially, requires a different faculty he called thymology, the interpretive knowledge of human motives, closer to the historian's judgment than the physicist's law. The architecture explains the man: Mises's granite certainty about socialism and inflation, and his indifference to the mounting empirical fashion of the discipline, were not temperament alone — they were the method, which locates economics' authority in logic and confines data to illustration.

## The Treatise

Human Action executes the program at full scale, and its sweep should be felt even in summary. From the action axiom it derives the pure logic of choice; introduces exchange and builds catallactics, the theory of the market, where prices emerge from the meeting of subjective valuations; incorporates money via the theory the next lesson examines; erects the capital-and-interest structure inherited from Böhm-Bawerk; and then turns the machinery outward into the great polemics — the impossibility of calculation restated, the interventionist dynamic (each control breeding the distortions that call forth the next), the business cycle as credit-born malinvestment, and a closing vision of economics as the discipline on which civilization's survival depends: "Economics deals with society's fundamental problems; it concerns everyone and belongs to all." The book's fate mirrored its author's: ignored or derided by an academy then converging on Keynesian aggregates and econometric models, it became the underground scripture of the American free-market revival — the text through which Rothbard, and thousands after him, entered economics — and it remains the one complete, single-author system the tradition possesses: every later Austrian is, in one way or another, glossing it.

## The Method in Action

To feel what praxeology actually does, run one chain at working speed. Impose a price ceiling below the market price of a good — rent control, to take the case a later lesson treats at length. From the logic of action alone: at the lower price, more people demand the good (marginal buyers for whom the old price exceeded their valuation now enter), and fewer supply it (marginal suppliers whose costs the new price no longer covers now exit). Quantity demanded rises while quantity supplied falls; the gap is a shortage, and the shortage is not a prediction that might fail if the data misbehave — it is entailed, given only that the ceiling binds and that buyers and sellers act. The deduction continues: with price forbidden to ration, something else must — queues, waiting lists, favoritism, side payments, quality deterioration (the supplier cuts what he can no longer charge for) — each a form the suppressed price takes when driven underground. What praxeology delivers here is the structure of the outcome, with certainty; what it cannot deliver is magnitude or timing — how large the shortage, how fast the deterioration — which depend on the particular valuations of particular people at a particular time. That division of labor is the method in miniature: logic supplies the skeleton no data can break; history and judgment estimate the flesh. And the example shows why Mises regarded the method as practically urgent rather than philosophically fussy: a policymaker armed with the skeleton knows in advance what every rent-control episode will rediscover expensively, while one who waits for the local data to come in mistakes the latest measurement of the flesh for the absence of the bone.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Now the attack, at full strength, because nothing in the Austrian corpus has drawn more fire. The central charge is unfalsifiability: a science whose core theorems no observation can refute has, critics argue, purchased certainty by abandoning empirical content — Karl Popper's demarcation criterion, formulated in the same Vienna years, seems designed for the case, and methodologists like Mark Blaug pronounced praxeology "cranky and idiosyncratic," a relic of pre-positivist apriorism. The reply — that the theorems are not empty because they are derived from a substantive truth (action) and gain empirical traction the moment their conditions are instantiated — is serious, but it concedes the practical point: praxeology cannot adjudicate quantitative disputes (how much unemployment a minimum wage causes, and for whom), which is where most policy argument lives, and a school that forswears measurement cedes that terrain by design. Second, the deductions are less watertight than the geometry metaphor suggests: critics from Hutchison onward have shown auxiliary empirical assumptions (about leisure, about the direction of time preference) smuggled into the chains, so that the certainty of the axiom does not transmit undiluted to the theorems. Third, the a priori status of the axiom itself is contested even in-house: Hayek quietly declined the strong apriorism, treating the core as empirical though nearly trivial; Rothbard re-grounded it in Aristotelian realism; the family disagreement suggests the foundation is philosophy, with philosophy's permanent disputes, not bedrock. Fourth, behavioral economics has documented systematic departures from consistent purposefulness — though Misesians reply, with some force, that praxeology assumes only purposefulness, not correctness, and that error is one of its native categories. What survives the attack is best stated as a doubled verdict. As imperialism — the claim that praxeology is the whole of economic science and empirical work a confusion — the method has persuaded almost no one outside the school, and the loss of contact with the discipline's evidential standards is a real and self-inflicted cost. As core — the insistence that economics possesses a logical skeleton of choice, cost, and incentive that data can flesh out but never overturn, and that forgetting this skeleton produces statistical sophistication about nonsense — the method is not only defensible but quietly victorious: every economist who says "demand curves slope down" without running a regression, every teacher who derives opportunity cost from the concept of choice, is doing praxeology without the name. Mises built the skeleton into a system; the system's money-and-credit wing, where deduction meets the business cycle, is the next lesson.
