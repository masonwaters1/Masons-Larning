The spontaneous-order lesson ended on a paradox: if the most valuable social orders grow rather than being made, what exactly should be made? Hayek's answer occupied his last three decades: the framework should be made — the constitutional structure of general rules within which grown orders can form and persist — and designing that framework is the legitimate, demanding work of political reason. The Constitution of Liberty (1960), written for the centenary of Mill's On Liberty and intended as its successor, is the fullest statement: a systematic philosophy of freedom, law, and the state — the book Hayek considered his most important, the book Margaret Thatcher famously slammed on a table at a Conservative policy meeting with the words "This is what we believe." It is also, and this surprises both his admirers and his enemies, a book that accepts a substantial role for government, including a guaranteed minimum income — which is why reading what it actually says, rather than what its reputation says, is essential to locating Hayek accurately on the map this course is drawing.

## Freedom as the Absence of Coercion

The book opens by defining its subject with a precision the liberty tradition had often lacked.[^1] Freedom, for Hayek, is one specific thing: the condition of not being subject to coercion by the arbitrary will of another — coercion meaning the intentional bending of one person's action to another's purposes by threat, making the coerced person a tool. This is deliberately narrow. It is not political freedom (a share in choosing rulers: a free people can vote itself into servitude, and a benevolent autocracy could in principle leave individuals uncoerced); not inner freedom (mastery of one's passions); and above all not freedom as power or wealth — the redefinition Hayek regarded as the century's most consequential verbal maneuver, by which "I am not free to vacation in Monte Carlo" (a limit of means) is assimilated to "I am not free to speak" (a subjection to will), so that every redistribution of resources can be marketed as an expansion of liberty. Poverty, on Hayek's definition, is a grave evil but not unfreedom; the beggar and the billionaire are equally free if neither is coerced, and unequally almost everything else. The definition's payoff is the book's central move: since coercion cannot be eliminated (someone must restrain the coercers), the task of a free society is to minimize coercion by monopolizing it in the state and then binding the state's use of it by known general rules — so that citizens confront, ideally, no arbitrary will at all, only laws they can know in advance and navigate like features of nature. Freedom under law, in the old formula the book rehabilitates, is not freedom's dilution but its institutional form.

## The Rule of Law, Fully Stated

The book's core is the fullest modern statement of what the rule of law means, gathered into three requirements. Laws must be general and abstract — rules applying to unknown future cases and persons, not directives aimed at particular people or outcomes; a rule that names its targets is administration wearing law's costume. Laws must be equal — applying to all, including, crucially, the government itself and the officials who enforce them; and Hayek adds a shrewd test: inequalities of law are least dangerous when those inside the favored or burdened group include those who make the rules. Laws must be certain — known, prospective, and stable enough that individuals can plan; retroactivity is legislative time-travel that makes obedience impossible in principle. Rules meeting these tests coerce minimally even while binding absolutely, because — this is the Kantian heart of the argument — a general, known rule is something an individual can treat as a fact of the world and plan around, like winter; it bends no one to anyone's purposes, since its makers cannot know whom it will help or hurt. Discretionary commands invert every property: aimed, unequal by function, unknowable in advance. The distinction cuts across the usual big-government/small-government axis, and Hayek insists on the point: a state doing few things arbitrarily is more dangerous to freedom than a state doing many things by general rule — which is the bridge to the book's most misread section.

## What Government May Do

Part III of The Constitution of Liberty applies the framework, and its conclusions scandalize the Hayek of caricature. The test throughout is not size but kind: does the measure operate through general rules compatible with the market's signaling, or through discretionary command and monopoly? By that test Hayek accepts — explicitly — a large service agenda: the enforcement framework itself (courts, police, defense); the provision of genuine public goods; sanitary and health infrastructure; a monetary framework; certified information (standards, statistics); public financing of education (via vouchers rather than state schools where possible); and, most strikingly, "the assurance of a certain minimum income for everyone... a sort of floor below which nobody need fall" — a safety net he defends as an obligation of the Great Society precisely because market order has dissolved the older securities of kin and guild, and which he distinguishes sharply from the thing he opposes: redistribution aimed at a pattern, at "social justice," at making relative positions match anyone's design. The same test condemns: progressive taxation (unequal rule by definition — proportional taxation he accepts); state monopolies of services others could supply; controls of prices and entry (discretion at the signal wire); and the discretionary macroeconomic management then ascendant. Later, in Law, Legislation and Liberty, he pushed to constitutional design proper — a model constitution separating the making of general rules from the direction of government, his diagnosis being that modern legislatures had fused the two roles and become, inevitably, auction houses for organized interests. The details persuaded few; the diagnosis — democracy unbound by higher rules degenerating into a bazaar of privileges — became common property of the public-choice economics two units ahead.

## Why I Am Not a Conservative

The book closes with a postscript whose title has done as much work as some entire chapters, because it locates Hayek — and the tradition this course follows — against the map's usual poles. Conservatism, Hayek argues there, is by its nature a brake: legitimate distrust of rapid change, but no direction of its own — it cannot say where society should go, only how fast; and so it is always dragged along the path others choose, protesting the pace. Worse, the conservative characteristically lacks principles that limit power as such: he objects to coercion when his opponents wield it and reaches for it comfortably when the state is in hands he trusts — fondness for authority, Hayek writes, and lack of understanding of economic forces are his family traits. The liberal (Hayek fought all his life for the old word, conceding in America it was lost) differs in kind, not degree: he has a direction — the progressive discovery enabled by freedom under general rules — and his principles bind his own side's power as strictly as the adversary's. The postscript also names what liberalism shares with the socialist and against the conservative: confidence in argument over authority, indifference to hierarchy's comforts, and no wish to freeze any moment's arrangements. The essay unsettles the book's political adoption to this day — the movement that brandished The Constitution of Liberty was, by its own postscript's taxonomy, substantially the thing its author declined to be — and it sets up a tension the course will meet again in Sowell, whose "conflict of visions" cuts the map differently. Hayek's placement of himself is exact and awkward: a party of liberty that conserves the framework precisely so that everything within it may change.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The system draws fire from every direction, and the best shots land. From the left: the coercion definition is tendentious — if my alternatives are starvation or your terms, the "voluntariness" of my agreement is formal, and critics from Hale onward argue that property itself coerces non-owners, so that Hayek has defined the market's pressures out of the indictment by fiat; his reply (market outcomes are unintended, coercion requires intent) is coherent but contestable, and the debate is genuinely philosophical, not settled. The "social justice is a mirage" argument (developed fully in Law, Legislation and Liberty) — that justice predicates of conduct, not of outcomes no one intended — is the system's boldest and most attacked claim: even sympathetic philosophers reply that societies do choose their frameworks, and framework choice can be just or unjust though no particular outcome is intended. From the right and from within: Hayek's minimum income and service agenda strike Misesians and Rothbardians as unprincipled concession — where is the limiting principle? — and his admission of exceptions has been used, they note, to launder programs he opposed; the man who wrote "why I am not a conservative" as the book's postscript (liberty as a party of movement and principle, conservatism as mere brake) satisfied neither camp he declined to join. Constitutional scholars add that the general-rules test is less determinate than advertised — clever drafting can dress targeted measures in general language, and courts applying "generality" must exercise exactly the discretion the theory distrusts. And Thatcher's tribute cuts both ways: the book's adoption as a governing manifesto attached Hayek's name to programs and methods (not least the illiberal means of some "liberalizing" regimes he was slow to condemn — the Chile episode remains a real stain on the record his defenders must own) that its own principles, honestly applied, indict. What survives is the architecture, and it has proved permanent: the definition of the stakes (arbitrary will versus known rules); the three-part rule-of-law standard against which every statute can still be measured; the demonstration that the size-of-government debate obscures the kind-of-government question; and the honest admission — rare in the tradition — that a free society's framework must be built, maintained, and paid for. Hayek's last book distilled the whole system into a single accusation against socialism's deepest premise — and it is next: The Fatal Conceit.
