Every piece is now on the board: Böhm-Bawerk's roundabout production and time preference, Wicksell's two rates, Mises's fiduciary media and non-neutral money, Hayek's prices as knowledge. This lesson assembles them into the Austrian school's most famous applied doctrine — the Austrian Business Cycle Theory (ABCT), the account of booms and busts that Mises seeded in 1912, Hayek built into a system at the LSE, and partisans and critics have fought over through every crisis since. Its claim is radical in the precise sense: the business cycle is not native to capitalism. Markets do not spontaneously generate the boom-bust rhythm; the rhythm is injected, by credit expansion that falsifies the one price coordinating the economy's relationship with time. The boom is the disease, the bust is the recovery, and the policy conclusions run exactly opposite to the century's instincts. This is also the theory on which the Austrians staked their duel with Keynes and, by the reckoning of most of the profession, lost the 1930s — a verdict the school disputes and 2008 reopened. The lesson builds the machine, runs it through history, and then gives the opposition its full turn.

## The Machine

Begin where Wicksell left the pieces.[^1] There is a natural rate of interest — the rate that would equate saving with investment, expressing society's actual time preference: how much present consumption people will truly forgo to fund production for the future. And there is the market rate, set in the loan market — which banks, creating credit, and central banks, steering them, can hold below the natural rate. Now run Hayek's signal analysis on the gap. The interest rate is a price like the tin price: entrepreneurs read it as information about a real circumstance — how patient society is, how many resources have been released from present consumption for long projects. A rate lowered by genuine saving tells the truth: people are waiting; build for the future. A rate lowered by credit creation tells the same story falsely: no one is waiting; nothing has been released; the signal has been counterfeited. Entrepreneurs — rationally, on the information given — respond as Böhm-Bawerk says a lower rate warrants: they lengthen the structure of production, launching projects whose payoffs lie further out — mines, factories, towers, housing developments — because at the lower discount rate, distant returns now clear the hurdle. This is malinvestment, and the term's precision matters: not overinvestment (too much in general) but wrong investment — a capital structure stretched toward a future the society's real saving will not carry, like a builder who lays foundations for a mansion on a bricks budget sized for a cottage, the deficiency invisible until the walls rise. The boom is this stretching in progress, and it feels like prosperity: the new credit bids up capital goods, wages in the expanding sectors, then — as those incomes are spent by people whose time preference never changed — consumer goods too. The tug-of-war begins: consumers pulling resources back toward the present, half-built elongations pulling toward the future, costs rising beneath projects budgeted at boom prices. The end comes by one of two doors. Either the credit expansion accelerates to keep the illusion ahead of the reckoning — the path that runs toward the currency's destruction — or it slows, rates rise toward truth, and the stretch snaps back: the distant projects are revealed as unfundable, abandoned half-built; their workers and suppliers are released; the bust arrives. And the bust, in this theory, is the correction — the painful liquidation of errors and reallocation of resources back to sustainable lines — which yields the doctrine's most scandalous corollary: recessions do necessary work, and the true policy failure happened earlier, in the boom everyone enjoyed.

## The Theory in History

Hayek arrived in London in 1931 carrying exactly this analysis of the decade just ended, and it made his name. The 1920s, on the Austrian reading, were the textbook case with a twist: prices were roughly stable, so the mainstream saw no inflation — but productivity was surging, so prices should have been falling; the Fed's credit expansion showed up not in the CPI but in the signal wires, the stock market and real estate and the capital-goods boom, precisely where ABCT looks and price-level orthodoxy doesn't. The 1929 break and the depression were the snap-back — though Austrians themselves concede, and the honest telling insists, that the depression's full horror required additional blunders the theory does not celebrate: the monetary collapse of 1930–33 (a third of the money supply gone as banks fell — the contraction Friedman's lessons will indict) and the policy war on adjustment (Hoover's wage-maintenance, Smoot-Hawley, then the NRA cartels) turned a needed correction into a decade. The stable-price boom returned as Exhibit B in the 2000s: CPI quiet, the Fed at one percent after the dot-com bust, and the elongation appearing exactly on script — in housing, the most interest-sensitive, longest-duration asset ordinary people buy — followed by the snap. Austrians claim 2008 as vindication (several, notably at the BIS, warned in ABCT terms before the crash); critics reply that a theory predicting "low rates breed bubbles" shares that prediction with half of macroeconomics, and the sorting of that dispute belongs to the crisis lessons late in the course. The historical record honestly summarized: the theory illuminates credit booms — the 1920s, Japan's 1980s, the 2000s — better than almost anything else on the shelf, and it has never carried a consensus about the busts that follow, because the bust is where its rival takes the stage.

## The Duel with Keynes — First Statement

The Keynes unit will fight this battle at full length; the cycle lesson owes the reader the shape of it. Keynes's diagnosis inverts the Austrian one at the joint: the slump's problem is not a distorted structure needing reallocation but deficient total demand — idle men and idle machines side by side, resources unemployed not because they must migrate but because spending has fallen into a self-feeding hole; and the cure is to refill demand, by cheap money and public spending, the very instruments ABCT names as the disease's origin. The theories are not merely different; each reads the other's medicine as poison — reflation, to the Austrian, re-falsifies the signals and aborts the reallocation (the "zombie" economics of propped-up error); liquidation, to the Keynesian, is the fallacy of composition in action, a doctrine for turning corrections into catastrophes while the patient bleeds. The 1930s verdict went to Keynes, for reasons the honest Austrian admits included a real explanatory gap: ABCT explains why the boom's projects fail; it explains far less well why the ensuing contraction should spiral — why the release of resources from malinvested lines should become years of economy-wide idleness — and into that gap ("the secondary deflation," Hayek's later term, conceding it) Keynes drove the General Theory. Hayek's own retrospective was rueful on the politics and unbending on the economics; the course adjudicates in Unit 6.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The standing objections, at strength. Theoretical: Sraffa's 1932 demolition-attempt — in a money economy there is no single "natural rate" (each commodity has its own), so the two-rate machinery lacks its benchmark; Austrians reply (with Lachmann) that the point misfires against a monetary-equilibrium construct, but the exchange bruised the theory's Wicksellian foundations in full view of the profession. Rational expectations: if credit booms systematically end in busts, why do entrepreneurs — the market's alert discoverers, by the school's own account — keep falling for the counterfeit signal? The Austrian answers (individual entrepreneurs cannot profitably sit out a boom whose timing is unknowable; the signal is genuinely uninterpretable because sustainable and unsustainable rate-falls look identical from inside) are serious, but the tension between Kirznerian alertness and cyclical gullibility is a real internal strain. Empirical: the predicted capital-structure dynamics (relative price swings between production stages, resource migration patterns) have mixed econometric support — partly, Austrians note, because the aggregated data are built to miss them, an answer that risks unfalsifiability; and the theory fits some cycles (credit booms) far better than others (the 1970s stagflation is monetarist territory; some recessions follow no visible elongation). Policy: pure liquidationism has few defenders left even in-house — modern Austrians (White, Horwitz, the monetary-equilibrium wing) accept preventing secondary monetary collapse while opposing bailouts of malinvestment, a position closer to Friedman than the rhetoric admits; and the school's post-2008 predictions of imminent high inflation from QE — pressed loudly by some, resisted by others — cost credibility the honest ledger records. What survives the audit is what the century keeps re-teaching: that the interest rate is the economy's master signal about time; that holding it artificially low does not create resources, only the coordinated illusion of them; that the damage of a credit boom is done in the boom, is structural rather than merely aggregate, and cannot be cured by the instrument that caused it — and that any macroeconomics which looks only at price indices and output gaps, while the signal wires hum with counterfeit patience, will be surprised on schedule, as it was in 1929, 1990, and 2008. The machine assembled, the unit turns to the school's account of its human engine — the entrepreneur — in Israel Kirzner, next.
