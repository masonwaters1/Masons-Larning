We have spent two lessons with Adam Smith, and it would be easy to imagine him as a solitary genius who conjured the science of economics out of nothing. He was nothing of the kind. Smith was the brightest star in a remarkable constellation — the Scottish Enlightenment — and his ideas grew directly out of a particular intellectual soil, tended above all by his closest friend, the philosopher David Hume. To understand the foundations of the free-market tradition, we have to understand that soil, because some of the tradition's deepest ideas — the self-correcting market, the limits of reason, and above all the concept of spontaneous order — were worked out by Hume and his Scottish contemporaries before Smith gave them their famous economic form. This lesson goes back to the ground from which *The Wealth of Nations* grew.

## A Small Country's Astonishing Harvest

In the middle of the eighteenth century, Scotland was a poor, cold, recently impoverished nation on the edge of Europe — and it produced, in the span of a few decades, one of the most extraordinary concentrations of intellectual genius in human history. From the universities and clubs of Edinburgh and Glasgow came David Hume in philosophy, Adam Smith in economics and moral philosophy, Adam Ferguson in sociology, Francis Hutcheson in ethics, Thomas Reid in the theory of knowledge, the chemist Joseph Black, the geologist James Hutton, and many more, all known to one another, arguing in the same societies, reading one another's manuscripts. Voltaire remarked, with only mild exaggeration, that it was to Scotland that one looked for the ideas that were reshaping civilization.

What gave the Scottish Enlightenment its distinctive character was a temperament: empirical, practical, skeptical of grand abstractions, deeply interested in how human societies actually work rather than how they ought ideally to be arranged. The Scots were less interested than their French counterparts in toppling the existing order and rebuilding it on rational blueprints, and far more interested in understanding the slow, organic, often unintended ways that real institutions — law, money, language, morals — come into being and do their work. This bent of mind would prove decisive for the tradition we are studying.

## Hume and the Limits of Reason

David Hume is best known as a philosopher, and one of the most formidable who ever lived. His philosophical work matters to economics in a way that is easy to miss but profoundly important, because at its heart lies a particular attitude toward human reason — and that attitude shaped everything the tradition would later say about the dangers of trying to plan a society from the top down.

Hume was a great skeptic about the power of reason. In his most famous and provocative formulation, he argued that reason is, and ought only to be, the servant of the passions — that reason by itself sets us no goals and moves us to no action; it merely calculates the means to ends that our desires, not our logic, supply.[^1] Just as important, Hume showed how much of our most basic knowledge rests not on rational proof but on **custom and habit**. We believe the sun will rise tomorrow not because we can prove it but because habit, built from experience, leads us to expect it. Reason cannot even justify this expectation; custom does the work that logic cannot.

This sounds abstract, but its consequence for social thought is enormous. If human reason is limited — if it cannot ground even our simplest expectations without the help of inherited habit — then the dream of redesigning society from first principles, by pure rational planning, is a dangerous illusion. We are not nearly as rational, or as knowledgeable, as the would-be planner assumes. We depend, far more than we realize, on inherited practices we did not design and could not justify from scratch. This is the seed of an idea that runs straight through the tradition to its great twentieth-century champion, Friedrich Hayek: that the central error of the social engineer is overconfidence in reason, a failure to grasp how much wisdom is stored in evolved institutions that no mind invented.

## The Self-Correcting Market

Hume was also, in his *Political Discourses* of 1752, one of the finest economic thinkers of his age — and he did as much as anyone to demolish the intellectual foundations of mercantilism before Smith delivered the final blow.[^2]

Recall the mercantilist obsession: pile up gold and silver through a perpetual trade surplus. Hume exposed why this goal is not merely mistaken but self-defeating, through an elegant piece of reasoning now called the **price-specie-flow mechanism**. Suppose a nation succeeds in accumulating a great hoard of gold by running a trade surplus. With more money circulating among the same quantity of goods, prices in that country rise. But now its goods are expensive compared to those of other nations — so its exports fall and its imports rise, and gold begins to flow back *out* to the cheaper countries. Meanwhile those countries, gaining gold, see their own prices rise, and the flow reverses again. The system automatically tends toward balance. The mercantilist project of accumulating money without limit is therefore impossible: the very inflow of gold sets in motion the forces that disperse it. No planner arranges this; it happens on its own, through the ordinary responses of buyers and sellers to changing prices. It is one of the first clear demonstrations of a self-regulating market mechanism, and it shattered the logic of the entire mercantile system.

Hume drew the humane conclusions that followed. In an essay on what he called the "jealousy of trade," he argued against the zero-sum vision in which nations are commercial enemies and one country's gain is another's loss. On the contrary, Hume insisted, the prosperity of your neighbors *benefits* you: rich neighbors are better customers for your goods and better sources of useful innovations than poor ones. He went so far as to say that, as a man and as a citizen, he would pray for the flourishing commerce of Germany, Spain, Italy, and even France. Trade is mutual enrichment, not war by other means — the same conviction that would anchor Smith's case for free exchange.

## The Great Idea: Spontaneous Order

But the deepest contribution of Hume and the Scots to the free-market tradition is an idea larger than any single economic argument: the idea of **spontaneous order** — that the most important institutions of human society arise not from deliberate design but from the gradual, unplanned accumulation of countless individual actions and adaptations over time.

Hume developed this most powerfully in his account of the origin of justice and property.[^1] Where earlier thinkers imagined that the rules of property must have been laid down by some wise founding legislator or agreed in a formal social contract, Hume argued that they emerged *gradually*, by a slow growth of convention, because they proved useful. People came, through repeated experience, to recognize the advantage of stable rules about possession, and the rules took hold and spread not because anyone decreed them but because societies that stumbled into them flourished. Justice, in Hume's account, is an evolved convention, not an invention — like a path worn across a field by many feet, which no one designed but which serves everyone.

The same insight applies to money, which arose spontaneously as a medium of exchange long before any government coined it; to language, which no committee designed; to the common law, built case by case over centuries; to manners, morals, and markets alike. These are, in the Scottish phrase, grown rather than made. And Adam Ferguson gave the idea its most memorable formulation, in a line that has echoed through the tradition ever since: nations, he wrote, stumble upon establishments that are indeed the result of human action, but not the execution of any human design.[^3] Order without a designer; institutions that are the product of human action but not of human intention — this is the master idea.

## Hutcheson, the Moral Sense, and Smith's Teacher

The Scottish Enlightenment also supplied the moral foundation on which Smith built, and here a third figure deserves mention: Francis Hutcheson, who held the chair of moral philosophy at Glasgow before Smith and was Smith's own teacher. Hutcheson was the leading voice of the "moral sense" school, which held that human beings are equipped by nature with an innate faculty for perceiving right and wrong — a moral sense analogous to our other senses, by which we directly feel approval of benevolence and disapproval of cruelty, prior to any calculation of self-interest. Against the bleak view, associated with Thomas Hobbes, that man is fundamentally selfish and morality merely a useful convention imposed on a war of all against all, Hutcheson insisted that we are naturally fitted for benevolence and that virtue consists in promoting the general happiness.

The line from Hutcheson to Smith's *Theory of Moral Sentiments* is direct: Smith took his teacher's conviction that morality is rooted in natural human feeling rather than cold calculation, and refined the crude notion of a single "moral sense" into his far subtler mechanism of sympathy and the impartial spectator. The point worth carrying forward is that the founder of economics was trained, from the start, in a school that regarded human beings as naturally sociable and morally equipped creatures — not the isolated, calculating egoists of caricature. The free-market tradition was born, in Glasgow lecture halls, out of an optimistic and humane view of human nature.

## The Four Stages of Society

One more Scottish contribution deserves attention, because it shaped how the whole tradition would come to think about economic development and the growth of institutions. The Scots pioneered what is sometimes called conjectural or stadial history — the theory that human societies tend to pass through a sequence of stages defined by their mode of subsistence: first hunting, then herding (the pastoral stage), then settled agriculture, and finally commerce.

The power of this framework was that it tied a society's laws, customs, and even its ideas to its material foundation. As Smith and his colleagues argued, the form that property, government, and law take in a society depends heavily on how that society makes its living. Among hunters, with little to accumulate, there is scant property and scant need for elaborate government; as herds, then land, then commercial wealth become the dominant forms of property, law and government grow correspondingly complex to define and protect them. This was a genuine intellectual breakthrough: an attempt to explain institutions not as the timeless dictates of reason or the arbitrary decrees of rulers, but as evolving adaptations to changing material conditions. It reinforced the spontaneous-order insight by showing that whole legal and political systems develop, stage by stage, in response to economic life rather than being imposed upon it from above. And it placed commercial society — the world Smith was analyzing — not as an unnatural deviation but as the most advanced stage in a long, natural progression of human development, an argument that dignified commerce against the ancient suspicion we examined in the very first lesson of this course.

## How the Soil Produced Smith

Now Smith comes into focus as the heir of this tradition rather than its sole originator. The **invisible hand** is precisely the idea of spontaneous order applied to the economy: a complex, productive, self-coordinating order emerging from the self-interested actions of millions, intended by none of them and designed by no one. Smith's relentless attention to the *unintended consequences* of human action — the way private vices and private interests produce public goods that no one aimed at — is pure Scottish Enlightenment. And his warning against the arrogant "man of system" who would arrange society like pieces on a board is the political expression of Hume's skepticism about reason: a recognition that society is too complex, and human reason too limited, for any planner to redesign from the top down what evolved from the bottom up. Smith's economics is, in a real sense, the philosophy of his Scottish friends turned toward the question of wealth.

## The Friendship That Shaped a Discipline

It is worth pausing on the human relationship at the center of all this, because the birth of economics was, in a real sense, the fruit of a friendship. David Hume and Adam Smith were the closest of friends for some twenty-five years — Hume the elder by a dozen years, the more famous and more controversial figure in his lifetime, the genial skeptic whose religious doubts scandalized respectable Scotland; Smith the more reserved and cautious man, who shared many of Hume's views but guarded them more prudently. They read each other's work, sharpened each other's arguments, and shaped each other's thought across decades of conversation and correspondence.

The depth of the bond shows most movingly at its end. When Hume was dying, facing death with a serenity and cheerful courage that appalled the pious, who expected a notorious unbeliever to be terrified, it was Smith who described his friend's final weeks. Smith wrote that Hume met death with such calm good humor, and bore his decline with such gentleness, that he approached as nearly to the ideal of a perfectly wise and virtuous man as human frailty permits. It was a daring tribute — to praise the character of a man famous for his irreligion, at the very moment when his lack of religious fear was scandalizing the public — and it cost Smith some grief. But it captures something essential about the tradition we are studying: that it was born not among cold calculators but among friends who prized virtue, character, and the examined life, and who built their science of wealth on a deeply humane understanding of what human beings are. The intellectual partnership of these two friends did more to found modern economics than any institution or decree.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Intellectual honesty requires two cautions. First, the idea of spontaneous order, powerful as it is, can be pushed too far. Not everything that grows spontaneously is good — slavery and feudal serfdom were also evolved institutions, persisting for centuries because they served the powerful, and it took deliberate moral reform to abolish them. The fact that an institution emerged without design tells us nothing, by itself, about whether it is just; "it evolved naturally" is not a moral defense, and the tradition is at its weakest when it treats every existing arrangement as wise simply because it is old. Spontaneous order is a profound description of how much of social life works, not a blanket endorsement of whatever exists. Second, these were men of the eighteenth century, with its serious blind spots; Hume in particular expressed views about human races that are indefensible and were challenged even in his own day. To learn from a thinker is not to canonize him, and this course studies the tradition's ideas on their merits, not its authors as saints.

With the soil now understood — the limits of reason, the self-correcting market, and the great idea of order without a designer — we are ready to follow the tradition forward. In the generation after Smith and Hume, the new science of economics would grow more rigorous and, in some hands, far gloomier, as thinkers like Malthus and Ricardo pressed its logic toward conclusions that earned economics its lasting nickname: the dismal science. The sunny Scottish confidence that free exchange would steadily lift the condition of the common man was about to collide with a darker arithmetic of population, scarcity, and rent. That harder, more sobering chapter is where we go next.
