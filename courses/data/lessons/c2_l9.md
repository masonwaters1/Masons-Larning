In the last lesson we met Frédéric Bastiat as the great popularizer of the seen and the unseen — the economist-journalist who taught a generation to look past the visible effects of an action to its hidden consequences. But Bastiat was more than an economist; he was a moralist and a satirist of genius, and his deepest concern was not merely with efficiency but with *justice* — with the proper purpose of law and the ways that purpose is perverted. In this second lesson on Bastiat we take up the work for which he is best remembered: his devastating satires against protectionism, and his great pamphlet *The Law*, in which he set out a theory of natural rights, legitimate government, and what he called "legal plunder." Here Bastiat moves from economics to political philosophy, and lays down ideas that would echo through the whole later tradition of classical liberalism.

## The Candlemakers' Petition

Bastiat's most famous satire is a short piece, written around 1845, known as the Candlemakers' Petition.[^1] It takes the form of a mock petition addressed to the French Chamber of Deputies, supposedly from the manufacturers of candles, lamps, candlesticks, and everything connected with lighting. These manufacturers, the petition complains, are suffering ruinous competition from a foreign rival — a rival who floods the market with light at a price so low as to be impossible to match. That rival is the *sun*.

The candlemakers therefore humbly request that the Chamber pass a law requiring the closing of all windows, skylights, shutters, and openings through which sunlight enters houses — so that the nation, deprived of free natural light, will be obliged to buy artificial light instead, to the great enrichment of the domestic lighting industry. The petition then argues, with a perfectly straight face, that this would benefit everyone: the candlemakers would prosper, the tallow and wax industries would boom, shipping and agriculture would flourish to supply them, and employment would rise across the land. The logic is precisely the logic of every protectionist argument ever made — that blocking a cheaper competitor "creates jobs" and "protects industry." Bastiat's genius was to follow that logic to its absurd but faithful conclusion: if shutting out cheap foreign goods enriches a nation, then shutting out the *cheapest and most foreign* light source of all, the sun, must enrich it most. The satire's force lies in its consistency; it grants the protectionist premise entirely and shows where it leads. If you would block the sun, you have understood protectionism perfectly. If you would not, then you must abandon the premise.

## The Negative Railroad

Bastiat deployed the same reductio elsewhere, and another instance is worth recalling because it sharpens the point. Confronting an argument that a proposed railway from Paris to Spain should have a deliberate *break* at the town of Bordeaux — so that goods and passengers would have to stop there, employing porters, hotels, and tradesmen, to the town's benefit — Bastiat pointed out that the logic, if sound, could not stop at Bordeaux. If a gap at Bordeaux enriches Bordeaux, then a gap at every town along the line would enrich every town; the most prosperous railway of all would be one composed entirely of gaps — a "negative railroad" that never actually connected anything to anything. The absurdity exposes the fallacy: obstruction is not wealth. What enriches a particular town at others' expense, by forcing inefficiency, impoverishes the whole. Once again Bastiat takes a respectable-sounding argument, presses it to consistency, and watches it collapse. These satires are not mere jokes; they are precise instruments for revealing that protectionist reasoning, followed honestly, demands the destruction of the very efficiencies that create wealth.

## The Law: The Purpose of Government

Bastiat's deepest and most enduring work is the pamphlet *The Law*, written in 1850 in the last months of his life, as France convulsed with competing schemes of state-directed reform.[^2] Here Bastiat set out to answer the most basic political question: what is law *for*? His answer begins from a theory of natural rights. Life, liberty, and property, he argued, do not exist because laws created them; on the contrary, it is because life, liberty, and property already exist — as gifts of God and facts of human nature, prior to all legislation — that men make laws in the first place. Rights come first; law comes after, to defend them.

From this Bastiat drew his definition of the proper role of law and government. Law, he said, is nothing but the *collective organization of the individual right to lawful self-defense*. Each person has the natural right to defend his life, liberty, and property; law is simply the pooling of that right, the substitution of a common force for the defensive force of each individual. The legitimate purpose of law, therefore, is strictly limited: to protect persons and property from aggression, to secure justice, to guarantee to each the peaceful enjoyment of his rights. Law exists to prevent injustice — to restrain plunder and violence — not to *do* anything positive beyond that defense. When the law confines itself to this protective function, it serves justice and liberty together; the citizen, secure in his rights, is left free to pursue his own ends.

## The Perversion: Legal Plunder

But law, Bastiat observed, has a fatal tendency to be turned from its proper purpose into its opposite. Instead of protecting life, liberty, and property, the law is too often used to *violate* them — to take from one person and give to another, to organize injustice rather than prevent it. This perversion he named with a memorable and deliberately provocative phrase: *legal plunder*.

Plunder, in Bastiat's vocabulary, is simply the taking of another's property without his consent. When an individual does it, we call it theft and the law punishes it. But when the law *itself* does it — when the machinery of the state is used to transfer wealth from those who have earned it to those who have not, on whatever pretext — the plunder is clothed in legality, and so escapes the moral judgment it deserves. This, Bastiat argued, is the great corruption of politics: the law, which was instituted to prevent plunder, becomes the very instrument of plunder, and the state becomes the prize over which competing groups struggle, each seeking to use the law to live at others' expense. He offered a simple test for identifying legal plunder: see whether the law takes from some persons what belongs to them and gives it to others to whom it does not belong; see whether the law does for one citizen what, if he did it himself, would be a crime. Where the law does what would be theft in private hands, there is legal plunder, however legal it may be.

## The Two Consequences of Plunder

Bastiat warned that legal plunder, once established, produces two grave consequences for a society. The first is *moral*: it confuses the very ideas of justice and injustice in the public mind. When the law itself plunders, people lose the ability to distinguish right from wrong, for they have been taught that whatever the law commands is just — and so they come to see plunder, when legalized, as legitimate, and even virtuous. The conscience of a nation is corrupted when its law is corrupted.

The second consequence is *political*: legal plunder turns politics into a war of all against all. Once it is accepted that the law may be used to enrich some at others' expense, every group in society has an interest in capturing the law for itself — to become one of the plunderers rather than one of the plundered. Political life degenerates into a scramble for control of the state's redistributive power, each faction seeking legal privileges, subsidies, and protections at the expense of the rest. The result is endless conflict, mutual hostility, and the steady expansion of the state as more and more of life is drawn into the struggle over who shall plunder whom. The only escape, Bastiat insisted, is to restore law to its proper and limited function: the equal protection of every person's life, liberty, and property, and nothing more. Let the law protect rights and refuse to redistribute them, and the war of plunder loses its prize.

## The Seen Liberty and the Unseen

It is worth connecting this pamphlet to the lesson that preceded it, for *The Law* applies, in the political realm, the very method of seen-and-unseen that Bastiat taught in economics. The schemes of legal plunder always advertise their *seen* benefit — the protected industry, the subsidized group, the favored interest, visibly enriched by the law. What goes *unseen* is the cost: the wealth taken from those who earned it, the enterprises that never start, the liberty quietly diminished, the corruption of justice itself. Bastiat's whole project, in economics and in politics alike, was to make the unseen visible — to insist that we count not only the beneficiaries of an intervention but its hidden victims, and not only its immediate effect but its long-run consequences for justice and liberty. *The Law* is, in this sense, the political capstone of his economic vision: a sustained argument that the same blindness which makes us cheer the broken window makes us cheer the legalized plunder, and that clear sight in both cases points toward freedom.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Bastiat's argument is powerful, but it has provoked serious and sustained objection, and a fair treatment must give the critics room. The central difficulty is this: Bastiat's concept of "legal plunder" appears to condemn, as a kind of theft, *any* use of the law to transfer resources — and that would sweep in not only the cronyism and special privileges he had in mind, but also public education, social insurance, public health, infrastructure, and the entire apparatus of the modern welfare and regulatory state. Critics charge that this *begs the question*. Bastiat defines legitimate law as the mere protection of pre-existing property rights, and then declares everything beyond that to be plunder — but whether the protection of property is the *only* legitimate function of law, and what the rightful distribution of property is in the first place, are precisely the questions in dispute. To call redistribution "plunder" is to assume the libertarian conclusion rather than to argue for it.

The deeper objection runs to the natural-rights foundation itself. Bastiat treats life, liberty, and especially *property* as natural, pre-political rights that law merely recognizes. But many philosophers argue that property rights are not given by nature at all; they are themselves *created and defined* by law and social convention — there is no "natural" property line that exists prior to the legal and political order that establishes it. If that is so, then taxation and redistribution are not the violation of some pre-existing natural property right, but simply part of how a society defines property and its obligations in the first place; and the question becomes not "plunder or not" but "what distribution is just?" — a question Bastiat's framework forecloses rather than answers. Defenders of Bastiat reply that some notion of natural right and self-ownership is indispensable if liberty is to have any secure foundation, and that the practical danger he identified — the capture of law by organized interests seeking privilege at others' expense — is no mere abstraction but a permanent and observable feature of political life, whatever one concludes about the theory of property. The debate between these positions is, in a real sense, the central debate of modern political economy, and it remains unresolved. Bastiat does not settle it; but few writers have stated the libertarian side of it with more wit, clarity, or moral seriousness, and that is why he is read still.

With Bastiat we close our long examination of the classical foundations laid down from Smith through the French liberals. In the lessons ahead we cross into the great challenge that would dominate the next century of economic thought — the challenge of those who looked at the same industrial world and concluded that the market was not the guarantor of liberty but the engine of exploitation. We turn next toward the critics of capitalism, and supremely toward Karl Marx.
