There is an old parable, made famous by the writer David Foster Wallace, about two young fish swimming along who meet an older fish swimming the other way. The older fish nods at them and says, "Morning, boys, how's the water?" The two young fish swim on for a bit, and eventually one of them looks at the other and asks, "What the hell is water?"

The point is almost too simple to state, which is exactly why it needs stating. The most pervasive and important realities in our lives are often the hardest to see, precisely because they are everywhere. They are not objects in our environment; they are the environment itself. We do not notice them because we have never experienced their absence. A fish has no concept of water because a fish has never been dry. And a person raised inside a particular moral world has no concept of that world's strangeness, because he has never stood outside it.

This course makes a single large claim, and this first lesson is devoted to making sure you understand exactly what that claim is — and, just as importantly, what it is not. The claim is that the moral and intellectual world inhabited by modern Western people, including those who consider themselves entirely secular, is a fundamentally *Christian* world. Christianity is the water we swim in. And like the fish, most of us have no idea we are wet.

## The Convictions That Feel Obvious

Begin with an inventory of your own moral intuitions — the ones that feel not like opinions you arrived at through argument but like self-evident facts about reality. Consider how many of the following strike you as simply, obviously true.

That every human being possesses an equal and inviolable dignity, regardless of their strength, status, intelligence, or usefulness. That the weak and the vulnerable have a special claim on our care, rather than deserving their fate. That it is wrong to take pleasure in cruelty. That we owe something — compassion, even obligation — to suffering strangers we will never meet, on the far side of the world. That slavery is a monstrous evil. That power should be accountable and the humble should be lifted up. That forgiveness is a virtue and vengeance a vice. That the arc of history can and should bend toward justice.

These convictions feel like the moral common sense of any decent, thinking person. They feel like the conclusions you would reach by simply reflecting clearly on the human situation. They feel, in a word, *obvious*.

And that feeling of obviousness is the water. Because here is the difficulty that this entire course is built to confront: these convictions are not obvious at all. They are, by the standards of human history, bizarre, anomalous, and historically peculiar. The overwhelming majority of human civilizations, including some of the most intellectually sophisticated the world has ever produced, looked at the same human situation and reached precisely the opposite conclusions. To see that, we need a tool.

## The Strangeness Test

Call it the Strangeness Test. If our deepest moral convictions are simply the natural conclusions of clear thinking — universal truths available to any reflective mind — then we should expect to find them, at least in rough outline, across the great civilizations of history. The brilliant minds of other cultures, reasoning carefully about human life, should have stumbled onto roughly the same moral landscape. The convictions should look *familiar* wherever we look.

Apply the test, and the result is shocking.

Travel to the Rome that Western civilization has revered for two thousand years — the Rome of law and engineering and Stoic philosophy, of Cicero and Marcus Aurelius. A respectable Roman would have regarded our tender concern for the weak not as a virtue but as a contemptible weakness, a failure of manly self-command. The Latin word for it carried a sneer. Pity was something a serious person learned to overcome. The exposure of unwanted infants — leaving a newborn on a hillside to die or be taken as a slave — was an unremarkable feature of ordinary life, a sensible household management decision, condemned by almost no one.

Travel to the Athens of Aristotle, the keenest analytical mind of the ancient world, the man who all but invented the systematic study of ethics. Aristotle taught, calmly and as a matter of natural fact, that some human beings are slaves by nature — born to be owned, lacking the full rational soul that would make them fit for freedom. He taught that women were naturally subordinate to men. These were not the prejudices he failed to examine; they were conclusions he reached through careful philosophical reasoning about human nature and the proper ordering of the city.

Travel anywhere in the ancient world, and you find that morality was overwhelmingly a matter of honor and shame, of strength and status, of loyalty to your own people and indifference or hostility to outsiders. Hierarchy was not a problem to be solved but the natural and admirable structure of reality. The strong ruled because it was right that the strong should rule. The idea that a slave and an emperor possessed the same fundamental worth in the eyes of ultimate reality would have struck a thoughtful pagan as not merely false but unintelligible — a category error, like saying a pebble and a mountain weigh the same.

So either every great civilization before ours, including the ones we most admire, was populated by moral idiots who somehow missed truths obvious to us — or our convictions are not obvious at all. They are inheritances. Something *happened* to produce them. They have a history, and a source.

## Tom Holland's Discovery

The historian who has done the most to put this argument before a wide modern audience is Tom Holland, and his own intellectual journey is the best illustration of the thesis. Holland is not a Christian apologist; he is a popular historian who made his name writing vivid narratives of the ancient world, and he began as someone who frankly preferred the pagans.[^1]

As a boy and a young man, Holland was thrilled by the grandeur of antiquity — by the Spartans and the Caesars, by the sheer scale and confidence of pre-Christian civilization. He found the ancient world bracing and admirable. But the more deeply he studied it, the more a disturbing recognition crept over him. He realized that he did not actually share the values of the people he was writing about. He recoiled from the Roman appetite for cruelty as spectacle, from the casual brutality toward slaves and the conquered, from a moral universe in which the suffering of the weak simply did not register as a wrong. His own instincts — his assumption that the victims mattered, that cruelty was shameful, that the vulnerable had worth — were not Roman instincts at all.

Where, then, had they come from? Holland traced them, against his own initial expectations, to the very religion he had drifted away from. His sympathy for the underdog, his horror at cruelty, his bone-deep assumption of human equality — these were not the natural conclusions of a secular modern mind thinking freely. They were the residue of two thousand years of Christianity, so deeply absorbed into the Western imagination that they had come to feel like simple human nature. He had thought he was standing on neutral, secular ground. He discovered he was standing in a cathedral whose walls had become invisible to him.

This is the heart of the matter. Holland's argument, developed at length in his book *Dominion*, is that Christianity so thoroughly rewrote the moral assumptions of the West that even the people who most loudly reject Christianity continue to think in its categories. The atheist moved by the plight of refugees, the activist who insists every person has inalienable rights, the humanist who takes for granted the dignity of all — each is, in Holland's account, reaching for fruit grown on a Christian tree, often while denying the tree exists.

## What This Course Is — and Is Not

Now we must be extremely careful, because this argument is constantly misunderstood in two opposite directions, and the entire value of the course depends on avoiding both.

This is *not* a course in apologetics. We are not arguing that Christianity is *true* because of its cultural effects. That would be a confusion of categories, and a serious one. Whether the central claims of the Christian faith — that there is a God, that he became man, that he rose from the dead — are *true* is a completely separate question from whether Christianity *shaped Western morality*. A set of beliefs can transform a civilization without being true; plenty of false beliefs have moved history. And a set of beliefs could be true while having little cultural footprint at all. Cultural influence is a question for the historian; truth is a question for the philosopher and the theologian. We are keeping them rigorously apart. This course establishes the historical claim and leaves the question of truth to you.

But it is also *not* a course in secular dismissal. The opposite error is to wave the whole thesis away — to insist that human equality and compassion are just obvious, that they would have emerged anyway, that the Enlightenment or human reason or natural moral progress would have delivered them without any help from a Galilean carpenter and his followers. That dismissal turns out to be, on close inspection, profoundly unhistorical. It assumes precisely what the Strangeness Test calls into question: that our values are the default output of clear thinking, when the entire weight of human history suggests they are nothing of the kind. To say "we'd have gotten here anyway" is to make a sweeping counterfactual claim with no actual civilization to point to as evidence.

The honest position, the one this course occupies, is the historian's: to trace where these ideas actually came from, how they actually spread, and how thoroughly they have actually soaked into the modern mind — and to do so whether or not one believes the underlying religion. It is worth noting that the scholars converging on this thesis are a remarkably mixed group. Some, like the New Testament scholar Bart Ehrman, are not believers and find the historical transformation undeniable nonetheless. Some began as critics of religion. Holland himself describes his relationship to Christian faith as complicated and tentative even after writing the book that made this argument famous. When believers and unbelievers, working from opposite starting points, keep arriving at the same conclusion, that convergence is itself a kind of evidence worth taking seriously.

## The Convergence of Unlikely Witnesses

One of the most striking features of this argument is the company that has assembled behind it. If the thesis were merely the special pleading of believers eager to claim credit for their religion, it would be easy to dismiss. What makes it harder to wave away is the remarkable range of scholars, working from utterly different starting points and disciplines, who have arrived at versions of the same conclusion — frequently to their own surprise, and sometimes against their own preferences.

Tom Holland, as we have seen, is a popular historian whose sympathies began with the pagans. Bart Ehrman is one of the most prominent New Testament scholars in the world and a former evangelical who lost his faith; he is no friend of Christian apologetics, yet he argues that the ethical teaching attributed to Jesus and spread by the early church genuinely transformed Western moral assumptions in ways that would not otherwise have occurred. Larry Siedentop is a political philosopher who traces the very concept of the individual — the rights-bearing person at the foundation of modern liberalism — to developments in medieval church law. Rodney Stark was a sociologist who applied the cold tools of his discipline to the growth of the early church and concluded that its distinctive ethic of care was central to its triumph. Kyle Harper, a classical historian, has documented how Christianity overturned the sexual norms of the ancient world. Charles Taylor, a Catholic philosopher, has written the most ambitious account of how the secular age itself is a Christian production. And the anthropologist Joseph Henrich, approaching the question from evolutionary psychology and cross-cultural data, has argued that the peculiar psychology of Western people was shaped by the medieval church's reforms.

Some of these figures are Christians; several are not; a few are actively hostile to religion. They work in history, sociology, philosophy, classics, and anthropology. They do not agree with one another about much. Yet they keep converging on the same large fact: that the moral world of the modern West is a Christian artifact. When witnesses with such different interests and starting assumptions independently testify to the same thing, their convergence carries an evidential weight that no single advocate could supply. It is the intellectual equivalent of unrelated witnesses describing the same event — the agreement is hard to explain except by the reality of what they are describing.

## Inheriting the Categories, Not Just the Conclusions

There is a subtlety here that is easy to miss and crucial to grasp, and it sharpens the whole thesis. The claim is not merely that modern secular people happen to hold some moral *conclusions* that Christianity also taught — that would be a thin and unremarkable observation, since traditions overlap. The claim is deeper: that we have inherited the entire *framework* within which our moral arguments take place, the very categories we reach for when we reason about right and wrong.

Consider how a thoroughly secular person argues about justice today. He appeals to the equal dignity of all persons; he sides instinctively with the victim against the powerful; he treats the relief of suffering as a self-evident good and cruelty as a self-evident evil; he narrates history as a struggle toward greater inclusion and liberation; he assumes that the moral circle should keep expanding to embrace those previously left outside it. He may be a committed atheist who regards Christianity as a discredited superstition. And yet every one of these moves is made on a board that Christianity built. The instinct to take the victim's side rather than the victor's; the assumption that the lowly have a moral claim on the mighty; the very idea of moral progress as the expansion of compassion — these are not neutral features of rational thought. They are the deep grammar of a particular tradition, so completely absorbed that they now feel like the rules of thinking as such.

This is what it means to inherit the categories and not just the conclusions. A person can reject every doctrine of the Christian creed and still do all of his moral reasoning in a Christian key, the way a person can forget every rule of grammar he was ever taught and still speak the language fluently. He has not escaped the tradition by disbelieving it. He has simply forgotten where the language came from. And that forgetting — that fluent, unconscious use of an inheritance one no longer recognizes as an inheritance — is the precise condition this course is designed to make visible.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Intellectual honesty requires flagging, from the very first lesson, the chief danger of this entire genre — and we will return to it repeatedly. The danger is *overclaiming*. Once you start seeing Christianity as the hidden source of modern values, it becomes tempting to see it *everywhere*, to credit it with every good thing and attribute every modern moral achievement to its influence alone. That is intellectually lazy and historically false.

Other forces shaped the modern world too. Greek philosophy contributed the tools of reason and the ideal of disciplined inquiry. Roman law bequeathed concepts of justice and contract. The Enlightenment, however much it drew on Christian assumptions, also pushed against the church and developed ideas of its own. Economic change, the printing press, the encounter between civilizations, sheer historical accident — all played their part. A serious version of this argument does not claim that Christianity is the *only* root of modernity. It claims something more specific and more defensible: that a particular and astonishing cluster of moral convictions, the ones that feel most obvious and most universal to us, are in fact distinctively Christian in origin, and that they did not and would not arise naturally from human reason alone. Throughout this course, every unit will close with exactly this kind of counterpoint — the strongest case against the thesis we have just built — because an argument that cannot survive its critics is not worth holding.

## Seeing the Water

For now, the task is simply to begin seeing the water. To notice that the moral air you breathe has a particular chemistry, that it was manufactured over many centuries by a specific tradition, and that its composition is so familiar to you that you have mistaken it for the natural atmosphere of any thinking being.

Once you have genuinely seen this — once the Strangeness Test has done its work and you feel the genuine peculiarity of your own deepest convictions against the backdrop of human history — you cannot unsee it. You will find it changing how you read the news, how you understand political arguments, how you hear the moral claims that fill the modern air. You will start to notice the cathedral around you, even in the places that insist most loudly that no cathedral is there.

The fish, once it learns the word *water*, can never again swim in simple unawareness. That awareness is the beginning of understanding. It is where this course begins.
