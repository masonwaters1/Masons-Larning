In the last lesson we examined the apologetics trap — the believer's temptation to slide from a historical claim about where our values came from to a theological claim about whether the religion that produced them is true. We noted that the trap has two forms, and we examined the believer's version in detail. This lesson takes up the mirror image, the version that ensnares the skeptic: call it the *reverse trap*. If the believer is tempted to over-credit Christianity, drawing illegitimate theological conclusions from genuine historical influence, the skeptic is tempted to *deny* that influence altogether, drawing illegitimate historical conclusions from genuine theological disagreement. Both errors corrupt the history; they simply do so from opposite directions. And the reverse trap is, if anything, the more common error in our culture, and the more invisible to those who fall into it.

## The Shape of the Reverse Trap

The reverse trap runs like this. A person who does not believe Christianity is true — who regards its doctrines as myth and its God as fiction — concludes, *therefore*, that Christianity could not be the real source of our highest modern values. Those values, the reasoning goes, must come from somewhere respectable: from secular reason, from the Enlightenment's break with religion, from universal human nature, from plain common sense. Religion, on this picture, was the great obstacle that enlightened humanity had to overcome in order to arrive at human rights, equality, and compassion — not their source but their enemy. To credit Christianity with our moral inheritance feels, to the person caught in this trap, like a concession to a worldview they have rejected, and so they resist the historical claim because they reject the theological one.

This is precisely the same category error as the apologetics trap, simply reversed. The believer wrongly reasons: "Christianity gave us these goods, *therefore* Christianity is true." The skeptic wrongly reasons: "Christianity is not true, *therefore* Christianity did not give us these goods." Both let a metaphysical commitment dictate a historical conclusion. Both confuse the question of *where our values came from* (a matter for the historian) with the question of *whether the religion is true* (a matter for the philosopher and theologian). The truth or falsity of Christianity and the historical origins of Western values are independent questions — and the skeptic who cannot separate them is exactly as trapped as the apologist who cannot.

## The Forms It Takes

The reverse trap appears in several recognizable forms, and naming them helps us spot it. The first is the appeal to *obviousness*: the assumption that values like human dignity, equality, and universal compassion are simply self-evident — truths that any rational person, in any time or place, would naturally arrive at. But this is exactly the assumption the course's "strangeness test" was designed to dismantle. These values were *not* obvious to the ancient world. To a Roman aristocrat, the proposition that a slave possessed the same inherent worth as a senator, or that the weak deserved special compassion rather than contempt, would have seemed not self-evident but absurd, even repugnant. Values that strike us as universal common sense are, on inspection, the contingent inheritance of a particular and improbable history. To call them "obvious" is to mistake the water we swim in for the natural state of all water everywhere.

The second form is the appeal to the *Enlightenment*: the claim that our values are the gift of secular reason, won in the eighteenth century's revolt against religion. But this badly misreads the history. The Enlightenment did not invent its core moral commitments out of pure reason; it *inherited* them, overwhelmingly, from the Christian framework it was busy secularizing, and then re-described them as the deliverances of reason. The conviction that all human beings possess equal dignity and inviolable rights did not spring fully formed from the philosophers' brows; it was a Christian theological claim — that every person bears the image of God and was died for by Christ — translated into secular vocabulary, with the theology quietly removed but the moral content retained. The "secular" values of the Enlightenment are, to a remarkable degree, Christian values that have forgotten where they came from. The third form is the *conflict narrative*: the story in which religion is purely a force of oppression and superstition, and all moral progress is the story of humanity escaping it. This narrative ignores the awkward fact that the very moral standards by which we now condemn religion's historical failures — its cruelties, its complicity in oppression — are themselves, in large part, a Christian inheritance. We judge the sins of Christendom by a yardstick Christendom handed us.

## The Inheritance in Secular Dress

It sharpens the point to notice how often the most aggressively *secular* movements turn out to be carrying Christian moral cargo without the label. Consider the modern language of universal human rights — the conviction that every person, regardless of status, nation, or usefulness, possesses an inviolable dignity that no state may override. This is now framed as a secular, even anti-religious, achievement; yet its content is the Christian claim that every human being bears the image of God, translated into the vocabulary of rights and stripped of its theological premise. The premise was dropped; the conclusion was kept.

The same pattern appears in the secular humanitarian impulse — the special concern for the poor, the suffering, the marginalized, the victim — which the ancient world did not share and which the modern secularist treats as simply the natural shape of decency. And it appears, most ironically, in the moral fervor of the campaigns *against* Christianity itself, which routinely indict the faith for failing to live up to standards of compassion, equality, and the dignity of the oppressed that are themselves Christian in origin. The critic wields, against the church, a yardstick the church handed down. To recognize this is not to score a point for belief; it is simply to see the history clearly. The values feel secular because we have forgotten their address — but forgetting where something came from is not the same as its having come from nowhere.

## Holland as the Model, Again

The figure who best illustrates the escape from the reverse trap is, once more, Tom Holland — and his example is instructive precisely because he is not a believer.[^1] Holland is candid that he does not hold the Christian faith; he came to his thesis not as an apologist seeking to vindicate religion but as a historian following the evidence, and indeed somewhat to his own discomfort. As a young man steeped in the glamour of classical antiquity, he expected to find the roots of his values in Greece and Rome. What he found instead was that his deepest moral instincts — his horror at cruelty, his belief in the equal worth of every person, his sympathy for the weak and the victimized — were not classical at all but Christian, alien to the ancient world he admired and traceable to the religious revolution he did not share.

This is the model of intellectual honesty the course commends. Holland demonstrates that one can be a thoroughgoing non-believer and *still* recognize, as a matter of plain history, that one's values are a Christian inheritance — because the historical question is genuinely independent of the theological one. He refuses the reverse trap as firmly as he refuses the apologetics trap: he will not let his unbelief distort his history, just as he will not let the history be conscripted into a proof of belief. He holds the two questions apart, and in doing so he sees clearly what partisans on both sides tend to miss. The lesson for us is exact: you do not have to become a Christian to acknowledge Christianity's historical influence, and you should not let your rejection of Christianity blind you to it. To do so is to subordinate history to metaphysics — the very error, in reverse, that we warned the believer against.

## Why This Matters

The stakes here are not merely academic. A culture that misunderstands the origins of its own values is a culture poorly equipped to understand, defend, or even keep them. If we believe our commitment to human dignity and equality is simply the natural default of any reasonable mind — the spontaneous product of secular common sense — then we will assume it is secure, self-sustaining, and in no need of cultivation. But if these values are in fact the distilled inheritance of a particular and improbable historical tradition, then they may be far more fragile and contingent than we suppose, and their persistence may depend on resources we are busy forgetting. The reverse trap is not just a logical error; it is a form of cultural amnesia, and amnesia about one's own foundations is rarely harmless. To think clearly about where we are going, we must think honestly about where we came from — and that requires resisting the temptation to edit the history to fit our theology, whichever theology we happen to hold.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Having warned against the reverse trap, intellectual honesty requires that we not fall into its opposite — for the skeptic's error has a mirror of its own, and this course is as concerned to avoid overclaiming as underclaiming. It is entirely possible to overcorrect: to credit Christianity with *everything* good in the modern world, to ignore the genuine and independent contributions of other sources, and to airbrush out Christianity's real complicity in real evils. This overclaim problem is serious enough that the course devotes a later lesson to it, and we flag it here so that the reverse trap and the overclaim are kept in view together.

The honest position threads between them. The skeptic is right that Christianity was not the *sole* source of modern values: classical philosophy, Roman law, the distinctive dynamics of the Enlightenment itself, and various secular movements all made real contributions, and the causal story is genuinely complex and contested. The skeptic is right, too, that the Christian tradition often *opposed* the very values now traced to it — that Christians stood on both sides of slavery, that the churches frequently allied with hierarchy and oppression, that the moral revolution Holland describes was as often resisted by Christendom as advanced by it. To deny Christianity's historical influence is the reverse trap; but to make Christianity the explanation of all good, and to ignore the ways it betrayed its own moral logic, is the overclaim. The genuinely historical mind holds both at once: it traces the deep and underappreciated Christian roots of modern morality, against the secular amnesia of the reverse trap, while refusing to inflate that genuine influence into a tidy story in which one religion gets the credit for everything and the bill for nothing. Following the evidence wherever it leads — neither editing it down to protect our skepticism nor padding it out to flatter our faith — is the whole discipline this unit has been teaching. The next lessons turn from these cautions to the substance: whose story this is, and how the rest of the course will proceed.
