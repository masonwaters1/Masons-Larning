We have spent the last two lessons clearing away errors of inference — the apologetics trap, in which the believer leaps from history to theology, and the reverse trap, in which the skeptic denies the history because he rejects the theology. This lesson takes up a related question, one that bears directly on how much confidence the course's central thesis deserves: *who gets to tell this story?* When we hear the claim that Christianity made the modern moral world, we naturally ask who is making it and why — and the answer turns out to be illuminating. For the striking fact about this thesis is that it is affirmed not by one camp but by two opposed ones: by Christian scholars who would be glad to find it true, and by secular scholars who might prefer it false. That convergence is not a trivial detail. It is, properly understood, one of the strongest reasons to take the thesis seriously.

## The Suspicion of the Teller

Begin with the suspicion, because it is natural and worth respecting. When a Christian argues that Christianity gave us human dignity, equality, and compassion, a reasonable listener grows wary: this is exactly the conclusion a believer would *want* to reach, and motivated reasoning is real. The argument looks like special pleading — the apologetics trap dressed as history. Likewise, when a political conservative makes the claim, it can look like a weapon: Christian heritage invoked to argue for traditional authority, a piece of history pressed into the service of a present agenda. In both cases the suspicion is the same: that the conclusion is driven by the desires of the one who reaches it, not by the evidence.

This suspicion is a version of a familiar logical move — dismissing a claim because of who makes it or why, rather than on its merits. Taken to its limit, it becomes a fallacy: the truth of a historical claim does not depend on the identity, faith, or politics of the person advancing it. A claim made by an interested party may still be true, and must still be assessed on the evidence. But the suspicion is not *worthless*, either, for it rightly alerts us to the danger of motivated reasoning — and that is exactly where the question of the teller becomes interesting rather than merely fallacious.

## The Argument From Convergence

Here is the key move of this lesson. Motivated reasoning is a danger precisely when an argument is made only by those *motivated to reach its conclusion*. If only Christian apologists affirmed that Christianity shaped modern values, we would be right to discount the claim as the predictable output of wishful thinking. But that is not the situation. The thesis is affirmed, with equal or greater force, by scholars who have no such motive — by secular, agnostic, and atheist historians who came to it reluctantly, against their own expectations and even their own preferences. And *that* changes everything.

When observers biased in *opposite* directions converge on the same conclusion, the convergence is powerful evidence that the conclusion is not an artifact of anyone's bias. The believer's bias pulls toward affirming Christian influence; the skeptic's bias pulls toward denying it. If both nonetheless affirm it, the most economical explanation is that the evidence for it is strong enough to overcome the skeptic's contrary pull — that the thesis is being driven by the history rather than by the wishes of the historian. This is a general principle of sound reasoning: a claim endorsed by those inclined to reject it carries more weight than the same claim endorsed only by those inclined to accept it. The convergence of opposed observers is one of the surest signs we have that we are looking at something real and not merely at a projection of our hopes.

## Holland and the Reluctant Witness

The exemplary case is, once again, Tom Holland — and now we can see precisely why his standpoint matters.[^1] Holland is not a Christian apologist; he is a secular historian who set out, as a young man enchanted by classical antiquity, expecting to root his values in Greece and Rome. He arrived at the Christian thesis reluctantly, even uncomfortably, dragged there by the evidence against his own initial preference. He is, in the language of the courtroom, a *hostile witness* — one whose testimony counts for more precisely because he had no wish to give it. When such a witness affirms the thesis, the suspicion of special pleading evaporates, because special pleading requires a motive he does not have.

But we must be careful to draw the right lesson from Holland, and not the wrong one. His value is not that his unbelief makes him a *neutral* or *unbiased* observer — he has his own commitments and his own blind spots, as everyone does. His value is that his bias runs *opposite* to the believer's, so that his agreement with the believer cannot be explained by their sharing a motive. The thesis is not true *because Holland the skeptic affirms it* — that would be the same fallacy in reverse, accepting a claim because of who makes it. The thesis is true, if it is true, because the evidence supports it; Holland's reluctant agreement is simply strong evidence that the evidence is indeed there. We do not replace "believe it because a Christian said it" with "believe it because an atheist said it." We let the convergence tell us that the claim has survived scrutiny from both sides, and then we go look at the evidence ourselves.

## The Hostile Witness Elsewhere

The principle at work here is not peculiar to this debate; it is a general feature of sound reasoning, and seeing it operate in other fields confirms its force. In a courtroom, testimony that *favors* the side calling the witness is routinely discounted, for the witness has reason to shade it; but testimony *against* the interest of the party who offers it — an admission wrung from a reluctant or hostile witness — carries exceptional weight, precisely because the motive to lie runs the other way. Lawyers and juries have understood for centuries that a concession from the opposing side is worth more than a thousand assertions from one's own.

The same logic governs good science and good history. A finding is more trusted when it is confirmed by researchers who set out to disprove it, who had every professional incentive to find the opposite, and who report the result against their own expectations. Corroboration from a skeptic is worth more than enthusiasm from a partisan. This is why the convergence of believing and unbelieving scholars on the thesis of Christian influence is genuinely significant: it is the historical equivalent of the hostile witness and the disconfirming experiment. The thesis has been tested by people who would have been glad to refute it, and it has survived their scrutiny. That is not proof, but it is exactly the kind of evidence a careful mind should weight heavily — far more heavily than the agreement of those who wanted the conclusion all along.

## Beyond Identity

This reframes the contemporary anxiety about "who gets to tell this story" in a clarifying way. In some quarters, the question is asked as if history belonged to particular groups — as if the right to narrate the past were granted or withheld by identity, and a story could be accepted or rejected according to who is authorized to tell it. The convergence argument cuts against this entirely. History is not owned by believers or by skeptics, by insiders or by outsiders; it is assessed by evidence and argument, and it is open to anyone willing to engage the sources honestly. The story of Christianity's influence belongs to whoever can argue it from the record — and the fact that people of sharply opposed convictions, examining the same record, arrive at the same conclusion is precisely what tells us the conclusion is robust.

The deeper principle is that we must separate the argument from the arguer. The identity and motives of a teller are relevant to our *vigilance* — they tell us where to watch for motivated reasoning — but not to the *truth* of what is told, which depends on the evidence alone. The mature thinker neither dismisses a claim because a suspect party makes it nor accepts it because a sympathetic one does, but asks what the evidence shows, using the pattern of who affirms and who denies as a guide to where bias might lurk. On this thesis, the pattern is encouraging: those most inclined to doubt it have been among those most persuaded by it.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Several cautions keep the convergence argument from being overplayed. First, convergence is *evidence*, not proof. Scholars biased in opposite directions can still converge on an error — they may share a deeper assumption invisible to both, or be responding to the same fashion in their field, or the "secular" scholars affirming the thesis may be fewer or more qualified in their claims than a triumphant retelling suggests. Convergence raises our confidence; it does not settle the matter, and the evidence itself must still be examined. Second, we should not overstate the *unanimity*. Serious historians dispute the thesis, or accept it only in heavily qualified form, and the secular witnesses who affirm Christian influence often affirm something narrower and more careful than the sweeping version a partisan might wish — a point the course's later treatment of the overclaim problem will press.

Third, and most importantly, the convergence argument must not become a substitute for the evidence. It would be a subtle error to conclude, "believers and skeptics agree, therefore the thesis is true, and I need look no further." The convergence tells us the thesis is *worth taking seriously* — that it has cleared the hurdle of opposed scrutiny — but the actual case rests on the historical record: the strangeness of these values in the ancient world, their traceable emergence within the Christian revolution, their persistence in secular dress. Who tells the story helps us judge how much initial credence to extend; only the evidence can finally earn our assent. The convergence of the believer and the skeptic is a reason to look closely, not a reason to stop looking. With these disciplines of inference now in place, the course is ready to lay out the road ahead — to map the territory we will cross in tracing the long influence of the Christian revolution on the world we have made.
