We have nearly completed the foundational unit of this course. We have established the central image — the water we swim in, the inherited assumptions so pervasive we mistake them for the natural state of things. We have introduced the thesis, drawn from Holland and others, that the modern West's deepest moral commitments are a Christian inheritance. And we have spent several lessons installing the intellectual disciplines required to think about that thesis honestly: separating historical claims from theological ones, avoiding the apologetics trap and its skeptical reverse, and learning to weigh the convergence of opposed witnesses. Now, before we leave the foundations, it is worth pausing to survey the territory ahead — to preview the domains in which we will watch this inheritance show itself, and to explain why the course is built the way it is.

## From Argument to Evidence

The work so far has been largely *methodological* — clearing the ground, sharpening the tools, establishing how to reason about cultural inheritance without falling into the characteristic errors. This was necessary, because the thesis is easy to state and hard to handle: it invites overclaiming, special pleading, and motivated reasoning from every direction, and without the disciplines we have built, the inquiry would collapse into a contest of assertions. But methodology is not the point; it is the preparation. The point is the evidence — the actual tracing of specific modern values and institutions back to their Christian sources, domain by domain, with the strangeness test applied and the genealogy carefully drawn.

The structure of the course ahead therefore shifts from argument to demonstration. Having established *how* to think about the question, we turn to *what the evidence shows*, taking up one domain after another in which the Christian inheritance can be examined concretely. In each, the method will be the same: notice an assumption we treat as natural and universal, apply the strangeness test by asking how it would have struck the ancient world, and then trace its actual historical emergence to see whether the line runs back, as the thesis claims, to the Christian revolution. The disciplines of the opening unit are the instruments; the domains ahead are where we use them.

## The Domains of Inheritance

The course will examine the Christian inheritance across a series of distinct domains — distinct because the inheritance shows up differently in each, and because seeing it in many separate areas is itself part of the argument. A single example might be coincidence or special pleading; a consistent pattern across many independent domains is far harder to dismiss. The cumulative force of the case comes precisely from its breadth.

These domains span the moral, the political, the social, and the conceptual. We will examine the idea of *human dignity and equality* — the conviction that every person possesses inherent and equal worth, regardless of status, capacity, or usefulness. We will examine *human rights* — the notion of inviolable claims that belong to persons as persons and that no power may rightfully override. We will examine the moral status of the *weak and the suffering* — the revolution that made the victim, rather than the victor, the object of special moral concern. We will examine *compassion and charity* as social obligations rather than mere private sentiments. We will examine the development of *conscience and the individual*, of *sexual and family morality*, of attitudes toward *the body and toward work*, of conceptions of *history and progress*, of the very ideas of the *secular* and of *religious freedom*. And we will examine how the assumptions embedded in these domains continue to operate in a culture that has, in large part, forgotten their origin. Each domain is a separate test of the thesis; together they map the inheritance.

## Why So Many Domains

The reader might reasonably ask why the course must range so widely, and the answer goes to the heart of how the argument works. The thesis is not that Christianity influenced this or that isolated idea, which might be granted without disturbing one's larger picture of the world. The thesis is that the *whole moral framework* of the modern West — the deep grammar of how we think about persons, rights, suffering, equality, and obligation — is a Christian inheritance, so pervasive that it structures even the thought of those who reject Christianity entirely. A claim of that scope can only be supported by showing the pattern across many domains, demonstrating that it is not a few cherry-picked examples but a consistent and far-reaching influence.

This breadth is also the course's best defense against the charge of overreach. By examining each domain carefully and separately, applying the same disciplined method, the course can distinguish the cases where the Christian influence is strong and clear from those where it is partial, contested, or shared with other sources. Not every domain will yield the same verdict, and the honest tracing of inheritance must be willing to find Christian roots shallow in some areas even as they run deep in others. The many-domain structure is what allows this discrimination — what turns a sweeping slogan into a series of testable, examinable claims, each to be weighed on its own evidence. The map is detailed because the territory is, and because only a detailed map can be checked.

## A Contested Domain: The Modern Self

One domain deserves advance mention, because it differs in character from the others and will call for special care. Most of the domains ahead concern values the modern West embraces and takes for granted — dignity, rights, compassion — whose Christian descent the course will trace largely approvingly. But the inheritance also includes the very idea of the *self*: the modern conviction that a person's truest identity is an inner, authentic core of feeling and desire, to be expressed outwardly and recognized by others. This assumption, which the church historian Carl Trueman calls *expressive individualism*, is as pervasive and as unexamined as any we will study — another stretch of the water we swim in.

When the course reaches this domain, Trueman will be a principal interlocutor. He traces the modern self by the same genealogical method the other scholars use, back through Rousseau, the Romantics, and Freud, to its culmination in the sexual revolution and the contemporary politics of identity. But his is a more critical and contested voice than Holland's or Siedentop's: he writes as a confessional Christian and regards much of this development with concern, where others see in it hard-won gains in freedom and dignity. The course will engage his genealogy as serious intellectual history while presenting his evaluation as his own — one reading to be weighed against others, not a verdict to be adopted — the same even-handed discipline we apply to every contested claim. The point of flagging it now is simply that the self, too, has a history, and that tracing it honestly will require holding analysis and judgment carefully apart.

A related and still deeper domain awaits us as well: the *secular* itself. It is natural to assume that the secular is simply what remains when religion is subtracted — neutral ground, the absence of theology. But a powerful line of scholarship, associated with Charles Taylor and, in its most radical form, with the philosopher Michael Allen Gillespie, argues that the secular is itself a *theological* production: that modernity's foundations — the self, the sovereign state, even natural science — grew out of late-medieval theological revolutions, and that the modern age did not escape theology so much as rearrange it. When the course reaches this domain, Gillespie's account of the theological origins of modernity will be a principal interlocutor, and it will pose the strongest version of this whole course's thesis: that even the ground on which the secular believes itself to stand, apart from all religion, was laid by the theology it thinks it has left behind. Here too the claim is bold and contested, and here too the course will engage it as a powerful interpretation to be weighed rather than a settled result.

## What This Course Is Not

It will help to be clear about the course's limits, for much of the confusion surrounding this kind of argument comes from expecting it to do things it does not attempt. This is not a work of *apologetics*: it does not argue that Christianity is true, and nothing in the tracing of moral inheritance is meant to establish the truth of the creed — that, as we have insisted, is a separate question, settled by separate means. Nor is it a *history of Christianity* as such: it does not survey the doctrines, the councils, the schisms, or the institutions of the faith for their own sake, but attends only to the trail by which certain moral assumptions descended into the modern world.

Nor, finally, is it a work of *theology* or a defense of any particular Christian tradition. The course takes no position on the disputes that divide Christians among themselves; its interest is in the broad moral revolution that Christianity, in its long history, set loose in the West, not in the rights and wrongs of its internal quarrels. And it is emphatically not a brief for the *superiority* of Christian civilization, with its record of failures conveniently omitted; the failures, as we will repeatedly insist, are part of the honest account. The course attempts one thing, and means to do it carefully: to trace, domain by domain, the historical descent of the modern West's moral assumptions, crediting the Christian inheritance where the evidence supports it and qualifying it where it does not. Keeping that modest and specific aim in view is the best guard against both the believer's temptation to claim too much and the skeptic's temptation to hear a claim that was never made.

## The Order of the Domains

A word about the sequence in which the domains will be taken up, for it is not arbitrary. The course begins with the most fundamental moral assumption of all — the inherent and equal *dignity* of every human person — because it is the root from which so much else grows, and because its strangeness in the ancient world is the clearest demonstration of the course's whole method. From this root the inquiry moves outward: from dignity to the *rights* that are said to follow from it, from rights to the special moral status of the *weak and suffering* whom those rights protect, from there to the social obligations of *compassion and charity*, and onward to conscience, the individual, the family, the body, work, history, and the secular itself.

The order, in other words, moves roughly from the foundational to the derivative, from the moral core to its social and conceptual extensions, so that each domain can build on the ones before. The reader who follows the sequence will find the inheritance accumulating — each domain reinforcing the pattern established by the others, until the cumulative weight of the evidence becomes difficult to set aside. This is by design: a single domain proves little, but a consistent pattern across the whole moral architecture, traced carefully and honestly, is the real argument. The map is laid out so that the territory can be crossed in the order that makes its features clearest.

## How to Read What Follows

A word, finally, on how to approach the lessons ahead. The disciplines of the opening unit are not to be left behind once the evidence begins; they are to be carried into every domain. In each, the reader should keep separate the *historical* question of where a value came from and the *theological* question of whether Christianity is true, resisting both the apologetics trap and its reverse. In each, the reader should apply the *strangeness test* honestly, genuinely asking how an assumption would have struck a world that did not share it, rather than assuming its naturalness. And in each, the reader should remain alert to the *overclaim problem* — the temptation, which the very next lesson addresses directly, to credit Christianity with more than the evidence warrants, ignoring other sources and Christianity's own failures.

The aim throughout is neither apologetics nor its opposite, but historical honesty: to follow the evidence wherever it leads, crediting the Christian inheritance where it is real and qualifying it where it is partial, so that the reader emerges able to see the water clearly — to recognize the inherited assumptions that shape our moral world, to understand where they came from, and to judge for themselves what follows. That is the journey the rest of the course will take. Before we set out across the domains, however, one final piece of intellectual equipment remains to be fitted, and it is the most important of all: a direct reckoning with the ways this whole argument can be overstated. To the overclaim problem we turn next.
