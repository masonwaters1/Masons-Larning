In December 1948, in the shadow of a war that had revealed the depths of human cruelty, the newly formed United Nations adopted the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. It was a document of extraordinary ambition and moral grandeur, and it opened by proclaiming that all human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights, that they are endowed with reason and conscience, and that the recognition of the inherent dignity of every member of the human family is the foundation of freedom, justice, and peace. It has become, in the decades since, something close to the moral creed of the modern world — the nearest thing our fractured global civilization possesses to a shared confession of faith about the worth of the human person. And yet at its very heart lies a silence, a deliberate and revealing evasion, that this unit will spend its length examining. For the drafters of the Declaration could agree, with remarkable unanimity, on what rights human beings possess. What they could not agree on, and pointedly declined to specify, was why.

## An Agreement Without a Foundation

The committee that produced the Declaration was as ideologically and culturally diverse as any that has ever gathered.[^1] It included Eleanor Roosevelt, the American chair; René Cassin, a French Jewish jurist; Charles Malik, a Lebanese Orthodox Christian philosopher; Peng-chun Chang, a Chinese diplomat steeped in the Confucian tradition; John Humphrey, a Canadian legal scholar; and behind them a wider consultation of the world's philosophers and traditions. These were people who disagreed profoundly about the nature of the universe, the existence and character of God, the meaning of human life, and the ultimate source of morality. A Catholic natural-law thinker and a Marxist materialist, a Confucian and a secular rationalist, could not possibly share a common metaphysics. And so, in order to produce a declaration at all, they made a fateful decision: they would state the rights and leave the foundations unspoken.

The philosopher Jacques Maritain, who was involved in the surrounding deliberations, captured the situation in a remark that has become famous precisely because it is so revealing. The various parties, he observed, could agree about the rights, on the strict condition that no one asked them why. So long as the question of ultimate justification was left untouched, agreement was possible; the moment anyone demanded to know the ground on which these rights rested, the consensus would dissolve into the irreconcilable metaphysical disputes that divided the drafters. The Declaration, in other words, was built upon an agreement to not inquire into its own foundations — a shared assertion floating above an unshared, and perhaps unshareable, account of why the assertion was true.

## The Word "Inherent"

Look closely at the language, for it repays attention. The Declaration does not say that human beings are granted dignity and rights by the state, or that they possess them by convention, or that we have agreed to treat them as if they had such worth. It says that dignity is inherent — that it belongs to the human person as such, intrinsically, by nature, prior to and independent of any human decision. Article 1 proclaims that all are born free and equal in dignity and rights. This is a metaphysical claim of the strongest kind: it asserts that there exists, in every human being, a real property of inherent and equal worth, a dignity that is simply there, woven into what a human is, commanding respect and grounding rights whether anyone acknowledges it or not.

But where does this inherent dignity come from? What is it, exactly, that every human being possesses that makes them bearers of equal and inviolable worth? The Declaration does not say. It asserts the dignity and builds a magnificent structure of rights upon it, but it leaves the load-bearing concept unexplained, ungrounded, suspended in the air. It is as though the drafters had raised a great cathedral of human rights and left the foundation stone conspicuously absent — or rather, present but invisible, doing its work while no one is permitted to look at it. The most important moral document of the modern age rests its entire weight on a claim about inherent human worth that it declines to justify.

## The Ghost in the Machine

This is what this unit means by the ghost in the machine. The modern world operates on the conviction that every human being possesses inherent, equal, inviolable dignity — a conviction so deep that we experience it not as a contestable philosophical position but as the plainest of moral facts, the bedrock beneath all decent politics. We build our laws, our institutions, and our moral outrage upon it. And yet, when we ask what this dignity is and where it comes from, we find ourselves unable to give a clear answer that the modern secular picture of the world can actually support. The dignity is there, doing enormous work, but its foundation has become invisible, forgotten, or denied. A doctrine haunts the machine of modern morality, animating it from within, and we have largely lost the memory of what that doctrine is.

The argument of this unit, which follows the thread this whole course has traced, is that the ghost has a name and a lineage. The conviction that every human being possesses inherent and equal worth is not a self-evident deliverance of reason, nor a discovery of science, nor a natural feature of every culture. It is, in its specific form, the secularized descendant of a particular theological doctrine: the claim, rooted in the opening pages of the Hebrew Scriptures, that human beings are made in the image of God, and therefore possess a worth that derives from their relationship to the divine and belongs equally to every one of them regardless of their capacities or their station. The Universal Declaration's "inherent dignity" is, on this reading, the image of God, translated into secular language and detached from the God who once grounded it. The drafters could agree on the fruit precisely because it had grown, over long centuries, from a root that most of them no longer acknowledged and some of them explicitly rejected.

## The Question This Unit Puts

And this poses the question that will occupy the rest of the unit, a question that presses with particular force in a secular age. If the conviction of inherent human dignity is indeed the secularized inheritance of a theological doctrine, can the conviction survive the severing of its root? Can we go on affirming, rationally and stably, that every human being possesses inherent and equal worth, once we have abandoned the belief that grounded that worth in the image of God? Is "inherent human dignity," asserted without foundation, a permanent achievement of human reason that stands securely on its own — or is it a ghost, a theological doctrine still doing load-bearing work in a house whose foundations have been quietly removed, sustained for now by memory and habit but resting, ultimately, on nothing the modern world can any longer supply?

This is not a hostile question, nor a rhetorical one with a predetermined answer. It is a genuine and serious problem, and thoughtful people, including committed secular thinkers, have taken it with the utmost seriousness and reached a range of conclusions. Some hold that human dignity can be grounded on purely secular foundations, and this unit will examine their strongest attempts, and give them their due, in the dedicated lessons that close it. Others, including some notable atheists, have concluded that secular liberalism may be living on borrowed theological capital that it can no longer replenish. The dispute is live, and the stakes could hardly be higher, for what is at issue is nothing less than whether the moral creed of the modern world can justify itself. But before we can weigh the answers, we must feel the full force of the question — and to do that, we turn next to the philosophers' own struggle to say where rights come from.

## Where This Leads

We begin, then, with the strange foundations of the Declaration not to diminish it — it is one of the noblest documents humanity has produced — but to notice what it reveals. The near-universal modern agreement about human rights rests upon a deliberate silence about their basis, a silence that allowed people of irreconcilable worldviews to sign the same page. That silence was a diplomatic triumph. But it was also a confession: that the conviction of inherent human worth had come loose from any foundation the modern world could agree upon, and perhaps from any foundation the modern secular world could supply at all. The ghost was already in the machine in 1948, doing its indispensable work while no one was permitted to ask what it was. This unit asks.
