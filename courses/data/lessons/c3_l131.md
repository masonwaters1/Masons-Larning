The Universal Declaration asserted inherent human dignity and declined to say where it came from. But philosophy cannot rest in that diplomatic silence, and for two and a half centuries the modern secular mind has labored to answer the question the Declaration evaded: where do rights actually come from? What is it about a human being that generates a genuine, binding claim on the treatment of others — a right that exists whether or not any law recognizes it, whether or not any majority consents to it, whether or not it is convenient to anyone at all? This is one of the hardest questions in moral philosophy, and the difficulty of answering it, on purely secular premises, is the pressure point of this whole unit. To feel that difficulty honestly is not to settle the question against the secular view; it is to understand why the question refuses to go away.

## The Strangeness of a Right

Begin by noticing how strange a thing a right actually is.[^1] Consider any ordinary fact about a human being — the arrangement of their cells, the firing of their neurons, their capacity for language, their evolutionary history, their measurable behaviors. All of these are facts about what a human being physically is and does. Now consider the claim that this human being has a right not to be tortured — a claim that binds others, that stands as true even if the torturer disagrees, even if the society approves, even if no benefit follows from respecting it. This is a claim of an entirely different kind. It is not a description of what is; it is a prescription about what ought to be, a normative fact that seems to hover above the physical facts without being reducible to any of them. You may inventory every atom and every impulse of a person and never, among the physical facts, encounter a right. Rights are not the sort of thing a microscope can find.

This is the crux of the matter, and it was given its classic statement by the philosopher David Hume, who observed that one cannot logically derive a conclusion about what ought to be from premises about what merely is. From any set of purely factual statements — the world contains such and such, human beings are constituted thus — no statement about obligation or value follows by logic alone; an "ought" cannot be squeezed from an "is." There is a gap, often called Hume's gap or the is-ought gap, between the realm of fact and the realm of value, and it is not obvious how, on a picture of the world that contains only facts, one is ever to cross it. Science, however complete, describes what is the case; it does not and cannot tell us what we owe to one another, for obligation is not among the facts it describes. And if the modern secular worldview is, at bottom, the worldview of natural fact — of matter, energy, evolution, and physical law — then it faces the question of where, in a universe of facts, the binding force of a right could possibly come from.

## The Problem for a Naturalist

Press the difficulty further by taking seriously the naturalist's own account of what a human being is. On the standard secular and scientific picture, a human being is the product of a long, unguided process of evolution by natural selection — an organism, magnificently complex, but an organism, assembled by the blind sorting of variations according to their contribution to survival and reproduction. There is no design in it, no purpose, no intended end; there is only the vast, indifferent process that happened to produce us, as it happened to produce the beetle and the bacterium. This is not a caricature of the naturalist view; it is the view, stated by its own most eloquent defenders, who have insisted with admirable honesty that the universe we observe has, at bottom, no design, no purpose, no good and no evil, nothing but blind and pitiless indifference.

The question then becomes acute. If a human being is, in the last analysis, an arrangement of matter thrown up by an indifferent process — a collision, however intricate, of cells and atoms with no purpose behind them — from where does the claim arise that this arrangement of matter possesses inviolable dignity and inalienable rights? A rock has no rights; a river has no rights; the process that made us cared nothing for rights, being incapable of caring for anything. At what point, and by what alchemy, does the purposeless rearrangement of matter that is a human being acquire a sacred and inviolable worth that commands the respect of all other such arrangements? The naturalist can readily explain why we *believe* in rights — more on that in a later lesson — but explaining why we hold a belief is not the same as showing that the belief is true, that there really are rights out there in the world, grounded in something, binding in fact. And it is just here, at the demand for a ground, that the difficulty bites.

## The Answers on Offer

It would be unfair, and false, to suggest that secular philosophy has simply thrown up its hands. On the contrary, some of the finest minds of the modern age have offered serious and sophisticated answers, and this unit will examine the strongest of them with the seriousness they deserve. Some have sought to ground rights in reason itself, arguing that any rational agent is committed, on pain of contradiction, to respecting the rational agency of others. Some have grounded them in the capacity for suffering, holding that the ability to be harmed generates a claim not to be harmed. Some have grounded them in what free and equal persons would agree to under fair conditions, deriving rights from a hypothetical social contract. And some have argued that moral facts, including facts about rights, are simply real features of the world — brute, necessary truths, no more in need of a further ground than the truths of mathematics. These are not foolish positions; they are held by brilliant and honest thinkers, and the dedicated lessons at the close of this unit will give them a full and fair hearing.

But it is worth naming, at the outset, the recurring difficulty that each of these attempts must confront, for it is the same difficulty in different guises, and it is the difficulty that gives this unit its subject. Each secular grounding tends to locate human worth in some capacity or feature — rationality, the ability to suffer, the power to consent — and each therefore faces the problem that these capacities are unequal, that they come in degrees, that some human beings possess them fully, others partially, others not at all. A grounding of dignity in rationality struggles to account for the equal worth of the infant and the person with severe cognitive disability; a grounding in the capacity for consent struggles with those who cannot consent. The theological doctrine this unit examines had a ready answer to this: worth derives not from any capacity a human possesses but from the human's relation to God, and so belongs equally to all, whatever their capacities. The secular groundings must find some other way to secure the equality that the modern conscience insists upon — and whether they can is the question the coming lessons will pursue.

## The Question That Will Not Rest

So the question "where do rights come from?" is not idle, and it is not easily answered. It exposes a genuine difficulty at the foundations of the modern moral world: the conviction that human beings possess inherent, binding, equal rights is a conviction we hold with our whole hearts, and yet, when we ask what grounds it, we find ourselves reaching for something the purely secular picture of the world may not be able to provide. The gap between fact and value, the purposelessness of the process that made us, the inequality of every capacity in which we might try to root our worth — these are not tricks or sophistries but real problems, and the seriousness with which secular philosophers have wrestled with them is itself a measure of how real they are. This unit does not claim that the question is unanswerable on secular terms; it claims that the question is hard, that it will not rest, and that the answer the modern world has mostly relied upon, whether it knows it or not, is the theological one it inherited. To that inherited answer — the doctrine that has quietly borne the weight — we turn next.
