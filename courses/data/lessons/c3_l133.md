The claim that human dignity cannot be grounded without theological premises is easy to state and easy to dismiss as the special pleading of the pious. It becomes far harder to wave away when it is made not as a sermon but as a rigorous philosophical argument, worked out in careful detail by a first-rate philosopher who has taken the full measure of the secular alternatives and found them wanting. Nicholas Wolterstorff, one of the most respected philosophers of religion of his generation, has mounted exactly such an argument, most fully in his major work on the nature of justice and rights. His contention is precise: that the idea of inherent natural human rights — rights that belong to every human being simply as human, independent of any social recognition — is a genuine and precious thing, that it has a specific and largely religious history, and that the secular attempts to ground it fail, leaving theism as the most defensible, perhaps the only defensible, foundation. His argument deserves careful attention, because it presses the challenge of this unit with unusual clarity.

## Rights Require a Ground

Wolterstorff begins from a conviction he shares with the modern moral consensus: that human rights are real.[^1] He is no skeptic seeking to debunk rights; he affirms them wholeheartedly, holding that every human being really does possess inherent worth and the rights that flow from it, and that this is among the most important truths we know. But precisely because he takes rights seriously as real features of the moral world, he insists that they must have a ground — that there must be something about a human being in virtue of which they possess this inherent worth. A right, he argues, is not a free-floating abstraction; it is grounded in the worth of the being who bears it. To say that a person has a right not to be treated in a certain way is to say that they possess a worth that such treatment would violate. And so the question of the ground of rights becomes the question of the ground of human worth: what is it about human beings that gives them the inherent dignity from which their rights derive?

This is not a trick question or a rhetorical trap; it is the central question of any serious theory of rights, and Wolterstorff pursues it with rigor. He surveys the ways one might try to ground human worth, and he focuses his critique on the approach that dominates secular moral philosophy: the attempt to ground worth in some excellence or capacity of the human being — in rationality, in moral agency, in the capacity for a certain kind of life. This approach has a long and distinguished history, and it is the natural move for a secular thinker to make, locating human dignity in the remarkable capacities that distinguish human beings from the rest of nature. But Wolterstorff argues that it cannot do the work required of it, and his reason is the one this unit has already begun to identify.

## The Problem of the Excluded

The difficulty is that capacities vary, and any grounding of worth in a capacity will fail to secure the equal worth of those who lack it.[^1] If human dignity is grounded in the capacity for rational agency, then what of the human being who does not possess that capacity — the newborn infant, who cannot yet reason; the person in the grip of advanced dementia, whose rational agency has departed; the individual with profound cognitive disability, who may never exercise it at all? On a capacity-based grounding, these human beings would seem to possess diminished worth, or none — to fall outside the circle of full dignity, to be, in the coldest logic of the position, of lesser value than those whose capacities are intact. And yet the modern moral conscience insists, with its whole force, that these are precisely the human beings whose dignity we must most carefully protect, that the infant and the disabled and the demented possess exactly the same inviolable worth as the rational adult. A theory of human worth that cannot deliver this equal worth of the most vulnerable has, Wolterstorff argues, failed at the very point where it matters most.

This is the heart of his challenge, and its force should be felt. The secular groundings of dignity tend to locate worth in what is most impressive about human beings — their reason, their moral capacity, their higher faculties. But by doing so, they inevitably rank human beings according to the degree to which they possess these faculties, and they cannot avoid the implication that those who possess them less possess less worth. The commitment to equal human dignity — the commitment that every human being, however weak, however incapable, however useless by any worldly measure, possesses the same inviolable worth as every other — cannot be secured by any grounding that makes worth depend on a capacity, because capacities are not equal. And this is not a marginal worry; it is the challenge on which, Wolterstorff contends, the secular project of grounding human rights founders.

## Why Theism Answers

Against this, Wolterstorff sets the theological grounding, and shows why it does not face the same difficulty. If the worth of a human being is grounded not in any capacity they possess but in their being loved by God, or in their bearing God's image, then that worth belongs equally to every human being, whatever their capacities, because it does not depend on capacity at all. God's love does not track rational excellence; God loves the infant and the demented and the disabled as fully as the philosopher, and it is this love, and the image-bearing status it confers, that grounds their worth. The theological grounding secures the equal dignity of all precisely because it locates worth outside the realm of measurable human excellence, in a relationship to God that every human being possesses in full and none can lose. Where the capacity-based grounding excludes the incapable, the theological grounding embraces them, and does so not as an afterthought but as a direct consequence of its structure.

Wolterstorff's conclusion, then, is not a leap of faith but the terminus of an argument: that inherent natural human rights, if they are real — and he is convinced they are — are best grounded, and perhaps can only be securely grounded, in theological premises about the worth God confers on every human being. The secular alternatives, for all their sophistication, keep running aground on the equal worth of the vulnerable, and it is not clear that they can be rescued. This is a serious philosophical claim, advanced by a serious philosopher, and it cannot be dismissed as mere piety; it must be met, if it is to be met, by a secular grounding that can do what Wolterstorff says the secular groundings cannot — deliver the equal, inviolable worth of every human being, including those who possess no impressive capacity at all.

## The State of the Question

It would be false to present Wolterstorff's argument as the last word, and this unit will not do so. There are able secular philosophers who reject his conclusion and who have developed responses — arguing that worth can be grounded in the mere fact of being human rather than in any particular capacity, or that the capacities in question should be understood as belonging to the kind of being one is rather than to the individual's actual powers, or that moral status can be secured on other terms entirely. These replies are serious, and the unit's closing lessons will weigh them; the question of whether Wolterstorff's challenge can be answered on secular grounds is genuinely open, and honest thinkers disagree. But the challenge itself is real and sharp, and it names with precision the difficulty this unit exists to examine: that the equal worth of every human being, which the modern world holds as its deepest moral conviction, has proven extraordinarily resistant to any grounding that does not, in the end, appeal to something like the image of God. Wolterstorff has shown, at minimum, that the secular grounding of human dignity is not the easy or obvious matter it is often assumed to be — that there is real work here, unfinished, and a real possibility that the work cannot be completed without the premises the modern world has set aside. Whether the naturalistic alternatives can succeed where he says they fail is the question we take up next.
