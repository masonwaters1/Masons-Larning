We come now to the sharpest edge of the unit's challenge. Wolterstorff argued that grounding human worth in a capacity fails to secure the equal dignity of the vulnerable. This lesson generalizes and presses that argument across the whole field of secular options, examining in turn the three great naturalistic strategies for grounding human dignity — evolution, reason, and utility — and showing why each, on its own terms, struggles to deliver what the modern conscience demands: the inherent, equal, inviolable worth of every human being without exception. This is the lesson that puts the naturalistic worldview most directly on the cutting board. It should be read as what it is — a serious argument that has not been universally accepted, whose strongest rebuttals are gathered in the unit's closing lessons — but it should also be felt in its full force, because its force is real.

## Evolution Cannot Deliver Worth

Consider first the attempt to ground human worth in our evolutionary nature — to locate dignity in the remarkable capacities that natural selection has given our species.[^1] The difficulty here is twofold, and both halves are severe. The first is that evolution is a process supremely indifferent to worth. Natural selection sorts variations by their contribution to survival and reproduction; it knows nothing of dignity, sanctity, or rights, and it produced the tapeworm and the virus by exactly the same process that produced the human being. To say that we are the products of evolution is to say that we are, like every other organism, the outcome of an unguided process that had no aim and conferred no worth, for it is not the sort of process that can confer worth at all. Nothing that comes out of a value-blind process arrives bearing value stamped upon it; the value, if it is there, must come from somewhere else.

The second half of the difficulty is that evolution, far from grounding our moral convictions, tends to undermine their authority — a point developed more fully in the next lesson but essential here. If our sense that human beings possess inviolable worth is itself simply an evolved disposition, installed in us because groups whose members felt such things outcompeted groups whose members did not, then we have an explanation of why we *hold* the conviction that has nothing to do with the conviction being *true*. Our belief in human dignity would be, on this account, a useful adaptation, like our taste for sugar or our fear of heights — advantageous to our ancestors' genes, but no more a window onto moral reality than a sweet tooth is a window onto nutritional truth. Evolution can explain the feeling of worth without grounding the fact of worth, and an explanation that debunks the belief is no foundation for it. The evolutionary story, taken seriously, gives us the moral sentiments without the moral facts.

## Reason Cannot Secure Equality

Consider next the noblest of the secular attempts: the grounding of human dignity in reason, associated above all with the philosophy of Immanuel Kant. On this account, the human being possesses dignity in virtue of being a rational agent — a being capable of setting ends, following moral law, and acting from principle — and this rational nature is the source of an absolute worth that commands respect and forbids treating any person merely as a means. This is a magnificent construction, and it has done enormous work in modern moral philosophy; it is, in many ways, the most serious secular attempt to ground the dignity the theological tradition had grounded in the image of God.

But it faces the difficulty Wolterstorff named, and the difficulty is not easily escaped. If dignity is grounded in rational agency, then it attaches to human beings in proportion as they possess rational agency — and rational agency is not equally distributed, nor always present at all. The infant is not yet a rational agent; the person with advanced dementia is no longer one; the individual with profound cognitive disability may never be one. On a strict reading, the Kantian grounding would exclude these human beings from the full dignity it confers on rational agents, or grant it to them only by a courtesy the theory cannot quite justify. Defenders have labored to repair this — arguing that dignity belongs to the human as a member of a rational *kind*, whether or not the individual exercises reason — but this move has its own difficulties, for it must explain why membership in a kind confers worth on an individual who lacks the kind's defining capacity, and it risks either arbitrariness or a return to something very like the theological move of grounding worth in what a being *is* rather than what it can *do*. The grounding in reason, for all its grandeur, has never fully escaped the charge that it secures the dignity of the rational at the cost of the equal dignity of all.

## Utility Dissolves the Individual

Consider finally the attempt to ground morality in utility — in the maximization of well-being, the greatest happiness of the greatest number, the reduction of suffering across all who can suffer. This is a powerful and humane tradition, and it has motivated real moral progress, extending concern to all sentient beings and refusing to privilege the arbitrary boundaries of tribe or nation. But as a grounding for the inviolable dignity of the individual, it faces a difficulty that runs the opposite way from the others: it threatens to dissolve the very notion of individual worth into a calculation of aggregate benefit. If what matters, morally, is the sum of well-being, then the individual has worth only as a container of well-being, a site where utility is produced or consumed — and there is, in principle, no barrier to sacrificing the individual for the aggregate, no inviolable dignity that stands against the calculus of the greater good. The rights that were supposed to be inviolable become, on a strict utilitarian accounting, provisional, defeasible, subject to being overridden whenever the numbers favor it.

The most honest utilitarians have acknowledged this and followed the logic where it leads, and their willingness to do so is itself a powerful witness to the difficulty this unit describes. The philosopher Peter Singer, whose consistency the next lesson will examine more closely, has argued forthrightly that the traditional doctrine of the equal and sacred worth of every human life cannot be sustained on secular premises, that it is a residue of the religious framework the modern world has abandoned, and that a consistent secular ethic must replace it with an assessment of the actual capacities and interests of each being — a conclusion that leads him to positions on the moral status of infants and the severely disabled that many find deeply troubling. Singer is not a careless thinker; he is a rigorous one, and he has grasped, more clearly than most, that the equal inviolable dignity of every human being does not survive the move to a consistent secular utilitarianism. That a philosopher of his seriousness accepts this is not a point against him; it is a point in favor of the unit's thesis, that the dignity in question is harder to ground, on secular terms, than the modern world likes to suppose.

## Where This Leaves Us

Each of the three great naturalistic strategies, then, encounters a characteristic failure at the same crucial point — the point of equal, universal, inviolable worth. Evolution cannot confer worth and tends to debunk the belief in it; reason secures the dignity of the rational but strains to include the incapable; utility dissolves the individual into the aggregate and, in its most honest practitioners, openly abandons the equal sanctity of every human life. The convergence is striking: three very different attempts, arriving at three different versions of the same difficulty, each unable to deliver the full package of inherent, equal, inviolable dignity that the modern conscience demands and the theological tradition supplied. This is the pressure at the foundations that this unit exists to expose.

Honesty requires two things to be said clearly, and then the challenge can stand. The first is that these arguments are contested; able secular philosophers have offered responses to every one of them, and the claim that naturalistic grounding *fails* is a strong claim that many serious thinkers reject. The unit's closing lessons will give those responses a full and fair hearing, and the reader should suspend final judgment until then. The second is that the difficulty is nonetheless real, not manufactured — it is felt and acknowledged by secular philosophers themselves, some of whom, like Singer, follow it to conclusions that reveal just how much of traditional human dignity is at stake. The naturalistic grounding of equal human worth is not impossible to attempt, but it is genuinely hard, and the hardness is the point. Whether the difficulty is finally insuperable, or whether some secular grounding can bear the weight, is the question that hangs over the rest of this unit — and over the moral life of the secular age itself.
