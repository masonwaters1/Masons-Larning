There is one assumption so deep in the modern mind that we scarcely recognize it as an assumption at all: that human beings are, in some fundamental sense, equal. Not equal in strength or intelligence or virtue — no one believes that — but equal in worth, equal in dignity, equal in their basic claim to be treated as ends and not merely means. This conviction underlies our politics, our law, our morality, our instinctive sense of justice. We hold it as self-evident, in the ringing phrase of the American founders, as though it were a truth that any clear-eyed person would simply see. But it is not self-evident, and the belief that it is may be the clearest sign of how thoroughly we have forgotten where the conviction came from. Human equality is not an obvious fact about the world. It is one of the most historically anomalous ideas ever conceived, and it stands in flat contradiction to almost everything the unaided observation of human beings would suggest.

## What the Evidence Actually Shows

Set aside, for a moment, the conviction of equal worth, and simply observe human beings as they are.[^1] What the evidence shows is inequality — pervasive, undeniable, running along every measurable dimension. Human beings differ enormously in strength, in intelligence, in health, in beauty, in talent, in industry, in courage, in every capacity and quality one might name. Some are brilliant and some are dull; some are strong and some are frail; some are virtuous and some are vicious; some contribute much to those around them and some contribute nothing or worse. If one were to rank human beings by any capacity that might plausibly be thought to confer worth — reason, achievement, usefulness, moral character — one would find not equality but a vast and continuous spectrum, from the extraordinary to the negligible. The empirical reality of the human species is radical inequality in every respect that can be measured.

From this observed inequality, the ancient world drew what seemed the obvious conclusion, as the earlier units of this course showed at length: that human beings differ in worth as they differ in everything else, that the superior are worth more than the inferior, that the natural order is a hierarchy in which the excellent rightly stand above the base. Aristotle could hold that some men are natural slaves; the whole classical world took it for granted that the strong, the noble, and the capable possessed a worth that the weak, the low, and the incapable did not. This was not a moral failing peculiar to the ancients; it was the natural inference from the visible facts. Looking at the manifest inequality of human beings, they concluded that human beings were unequal in worth, and they built their civilization on that conclusion. The startling thing, from a historical vantage, is not that they believed this but that we do not.

## The Strangeness of Equal Worth

For our conviction runs directly against the evidence. We hold that human beings, manifestly unequal in every capacity, are nonetheless perfectly equal in worth — that the genius and the simpleton, the hero and the coward, the benefactor and the parasite, possess exactly the same inviolable dignity and the same fundamental rights. This is an extraordinary claim, and it is worth pausing to feel how extraordinary it is. It asserts an equality that is nowhere visible, that contradicts everything observation suggests, that cannot be derived from any measurable feature of human beings, since every measurable feature is unequal. The equal worth of all human beings is not read off the facts; it is asserted in defiance of them. It is a metaphysical conviction of the highest order, holding that beneath or beyond all the visible inequality there lies an invisible equality of worth that the eye cannot see and the measuring instrument cannot detect.

Where could such a conviction come from? Not from observation, which shows inequality. Not from reason alone, which, contemplating the spectrum of human capacity, would seem more likely to conclude with the ancients that worth tracks capacity. The conviction of equal human worth came, historically, from the doctrine this unit has already examined: that every human being, whatever their visible qualities, equally bears the image of God, and so possesses a worth that is grounded not in any capacity — for capacities are unequal — but in a relationship to God that all share alike. The invisible equality that the modern conscience insists upon is the equality of image-bearers before their Creator, the equality of souls equally loved by God. This is where the strange conviction was born, and it is difficult to see how it could have been born anywhere else, for only a ground outside the realm of visible capacity could sustain an equality that the realm of visible capacity so flatly contradicts.

## Nietzsche Saw It Clearly

No one saw this more clearly than the philosopher who most fiercely rejected it. Friedrich Nietzsche, whose challenge the earlier units of this course examined and to whom the final unit will return, understood with perfect lucidity that the modern belief in human equality was a Christian inheritance, and that it could not survive the death of the God who had grounded it. He regarded the notion of equal human worth as one of the great catastrophes of Western morality — a denial of the manifest order of rank among human beings, a leveling doctrine that exalted the weak and the botched to the same status as the strong and the noble, and he traced it directly to Christianity, to the doctrine that all souls are equal before God. And he drew the conclusion that the modern secular world has mostly refused to draw: that if God is dead, then the equality grounded in God is dead too, and the whole egalitarian morality that rests upon it is left hanging in the air, a magnificent structure whose foundation has been demolished, standing only by inertia until the recognition of its groundlessness catches up with it.

Whatever one makes of Nietzsche's own values — and this course has been sharply critical of them — his analysis on this point is difficult to fault, and its difficulty is precisely the difficulty this unit exists to expose. He saw that human equality is not obvious, not natural, not a deliverance of reason or observation, but a specific and anomalous conviction with a specific theological source; and he saw that abandoning the source raises, unavoidably, the question of whether the conviction can stand. That a thinker so hostile to Christianity should insist so emphatically that human equality is a Christian doctrine, unsustainable without its Christian ground, is powerful testimony — for he had every motive to deny the debt and none to affirm it. The atheist who wished to destroy Christian morality understood, better than the atheists who wish to keep it, exactly how much of that morality hangs upon the God they have set aside.

## The Anomaly and the Question

The belief in human equality, then, is not the natural condition of human thought but a rare and recent anomaly, achieved against the grain of observation and against the practice of most of human history, grounded originally in a theological doctrine that the modern secular world has largely abandoned. This does not prove that the belief is false, nor that it cannot be sustained on other grounds; that remains the open question this unit will pursue to its close, where the strongest secular defenses of equality will be weighed. But it does mean that the modern confidence in human equality as something self-evident is misplaced — that we are holding, as an obvious truth requiring no defense, a conviction that is in fact deeply strange, historically peculiar, and, on the evidence alone, quite unwarranted. We inherited the conviction of equal worth from a tradition that grounded it in God, and we have kept the conviction while discarding the ground. Whether it can survive that discarding is not obvious either. But the first step toward taking the question seriously is to stop pretending that the answer is easy — to recognize that equality is not obvious, and that we believe it, if we can still believe it, for reasons we have largely forgotten and may no longer be able to supply.
