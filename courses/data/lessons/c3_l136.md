If the challenge of this unit came only from believers, it would be easy to discount as the special pleading of those with a stake in the answer. Its force comes from the fact that some of the most serious secular thinkers of the modern age have looked hard at the foundations of their own moral world and concluded, uneasily, that those foundations may be religious after all. No one embodies this more strikingly than Jürgen Habermas — the towering philosopher of the Frankfurt School, the great theorist of secular reason and what he called post-metaphysical thinking, a lifelong defender of the rational, secular, democratic public sphere against every appeal to religious authority. If anyone might be expected to insist that human rights and equal dignity stand securely on purely secular ground, it is Habermas. And yet, late in his long career, he made a concession that has reverberated through the debate ever since.

## The Unexpected Admission

Habermas came to argue, with characteristic care, that the modern ideals of freedom, conscience, human rights, democracy, and the equal dignity of every person are not free-floating achievements of autonomous reason but the direct heirs of a specific religious inheritance.[^1] Egalitarian universalism — the conviction that every human being possesses equal moral worth and equal claims — he traced to the Judaic ethic of justice and the Christian ethic of love, and he insisted that this inheritance was not one source among many but the decisive one. Everything else, he suggested, in a phrase that startled his secular admirers, was so much postmodern chatter. The substance of modern moral universalism, on his account, flowed from the biblical tradition, and the secular Enlightenment had been its heir and its translator rather than its original author.

More unsettling still was his suggestion about the present and the future. Habermas raised the possibility that secular reason, for all its power, may not be able to generate from its own resources the moral motivations and solidarities that a decent society requires — that it may be drawing on a reservoir of meaning it did not create and cannot replenish. The modern secular state, he worried, may depend upon moral presuppositions that it is unable, by its own secular means, to guarantee or renew. This is a version of what has been called the dilemma of the liberal secular order: that it lives on ethical resources — the sense of the sacredness of the person, the reflex of solidarity with the suffering, the conviction of equal worth — which it inherited from a religious tradition and which it may not be able to reproduce once that tradition has faded. The great defender of secular reason had come to suspect that secular reason might be, in a certain sense, parasitic upon the faith it had left behind.

## The Dialogue with the Cardinal

This suspicion took public form in a famous exchange. In the years before he became Pope Benedict, Cardinal Joseph Ratzinger, one of the most formidable theological minds of his generation, met Habermas in a widely noted dialogue on the moral foundations of the secular state. It was a remarkable encounter — the leading philosopher of secular modernity and the leading defender of Catholic tradition, meeting not as antagonists but as interlocutors probing a shared worry. And what was remarkable was the degree of convergence. Habermas did not concede that Christianity was true, nor did he abandon his own secular standpoint. But he did concede that the secular, liberal, democratic order might rest on foundations it could not itself supply, and that secular reason would be wise to remain open to the religious sources of meaning it had too hastily dismissed, rather than sealing itself off from them in a posture of self-sufficiency it could not actually sustain.

The significance of this exchange, for the argument of this unit, is hard to overstate. Here was the modern world's most eminent secular philosopher acknowledging, in dialogue with a cardinal, that the moral capital of the secular age might be a religious inheritance in the process of being spent. This is precisely the borrowed-capital thesis that runs through this unit — the worry that the modern West is living on convictions it inherited from Christianity while cutting off the source that produced them — but now advanced not by an apologist seeking to vindicate the faith but by a secular thinker with every reason to resist the conclusion, and arriving at it only because the analysis compelled him.

## What the Concession Does and Does Not Grant

It is essential to be precise about what Habermas conceded, for the point is easily overstated in both directions. He did not concede that human rights are false without God, nor that the secular grounding of dignity is impossible, nor that one must be religious to be moral. He remained a secular philosopher, committed to the project of grounding morality in the structures of human reason and communication, and he continued to hope that secular reason could preserve the substance of the religious inheritance by translating it into terms available to believer and unbeliever alike. His concession was genealogical and motivational rather than metaphysical: that the *content* of modern moral universalism derives from the biblical tradition, and that the *motivational resources* to sustain it may not be securely available to a purely secular society. He thought this inheritance could, perhaps, be salvaged and carried forward by a secular reason humble enough to acknowledge its debts. He did not think it was doomed.

But even this bounded concession is momentous, and it sharpens the question this unit has been pursuing. For it grants the two things that matter most: that the modern conviction of equal human dignity has a specific religious genealogy, and that its survival in a secular age is a genuine problem rather than a foregone conclusion. Habermas's hope that the inheritance can be translated and preserved is exactly that — a hope, and a project, not an accomplished fact. Whether secular reason can in fact generate and renew the moral solidarities it inherited, or whether it is drawing down a reservoir it cannot refill, is the open question, and Habermas's honesty lies in his refusal to assume the comfortable answer. That the most serious secular philosopher of the age took the worry seriously is reason enough for the rest of us to take it seriously too. Whether the inheritance can be renewed, or is merely being spent, is a question this unit will keep before us — and one to which the image of a draining pool, examined shortly, gives its most vivid form.
