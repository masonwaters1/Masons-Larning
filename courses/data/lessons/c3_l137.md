The modern secular world tells a story about where it came from, and the story goes roughly like this: for long centuries, Europe lay under the darkness of religious authority, until the Renaissance rediscovered the human individual and the Enlightenment liberated reason, and from that double awakening emerged the modern ideals of individual liberty, equality, and rights, wrested from the church at last by the forces of secular progress. It is a stirring story, and it is the story most educated people half-consciously believe. The intellectual historian Larry Siedentop has argued, in a work of great learning, that it is almost exactly backward. The rights-bearing individual, he contends, was not the discovery of a secular age throwing off religion; it was the invention of Christianity itself, worked out over centuries and crystallized, of all places, in the law of the medieval church.

## The Invention of the Individual

Siedentop's central claim is that the moral intuition on which the whole edifice of individual rights rests — the intuition that each human being is a separate moral agent of equal standing, a person rather than merely a member of a family, tribe, or city — was a Christian creation.[^1] The ancient world, as the earlier units of this course showed, did not think in these terms. It thought in terms of the group: the family, the clan, the polis. The individual existed as a part of a larger whole, defined by their place in it, and the notion that each person was a separate moral unit of equal worth, standing in an unmediated relation to the ultimate source of value, was foreign to it. What introduced this notion, Siedentop argues, was the Christian conception of the soul — the conviction that every human being, whatever their social station, stands as an individual before God, equally the object of divine concern, equally responsible for their own moral choices, equally possessed of a conscience answerable to God alone.

This Christian conception of the moral equality of souls, first proclaimed in the letters of Paul with his declaration that in Christ there is neither slave nor free, contained within it, Siedentop argues, the seed of the modern individual. If every soul stands equal before God, then beneath every social hierarchy lies a deeper moral equality; and if each person is responsible for their own conscience, then each person is a moral agent in their own right, not merely a fragment of a group. It took many centuries for the implications of this conviction to be drawn out, but the conviction itself, planted at the origin of Christianity, was the source from which the rights-bearing individual would eventually grow. The individual was not discovered in opposition to Christianity; the individual was Christianity's own long-maturing invention.

## The Work of the Canon Lawyers

The most striking part of Siedentop's argument, and the reason his account matters so much for this unit, is where he locates the decisive crystallization of individual rights: not in the Renaissance, not in the Enlightenment, but in the canon law of the medieval church. In the great period of legal creativity that followed the eleventh and twelfth centuries, the canon lawyers of the church — the jurists who systematized and developed the law governing the Christian community — worked out, with increasing precision, the idea of subjective rights: rights inhering in the individual person as such, claims that a person could assert in virtue of their own moral standing. Grounding their work in the Christian conception of the person as a moral agent equal before God, endowed with conscience and free will, these medieval jurists developed the conceptual apparatus of the rights-bearing individual — the notion that persons possess rights simply as persons, which they can hold against others and even against authority.

This is a startling relocation of the origins of a central modern idea. The apparatus of individual subjective rights, which the modern world takes to be the very signature of its secular liberalism, was forged, on Siedentop's account, by churchmen, in the service of a thoroughly Christian conception of the human person, centuries before the Enlightenment was born. The rights are, in a precise double sense, canonical: they are authoritative and foundational, and they were born in canon law. When later thinkers came to articulate theories of natural rights, and later still when revolutionaries came to proclaim the rights of man, they were not inventing something new against Christianity; they were secularizing and universalizing a conception of the rights-bearing individual that Christianity had already created and the medieval church had already given legal form. The genealogy of rights runs not around Christianity but straight through its heart.

## Secularism as a Christian Achievement

Siedentop presses the point to a conclusion that is genuinely provocative and that connects to a theme this course pursues elsewhere: that secularism itself, properly understood, is not the negation of Christianity but one of its products. The separation of religious and secular authority, the idea of a space of individual conscience protected from coercion, the very notion of a neutral public order in which persons of different beliefs meet as equals — these, he argues, grew from Christian roots, from the church's own distinction between spiritual and temporal power and from the Christian conception of the individual conscience answerable to God. Liberalism, on this reading, is the political expression of Christian moral intuitions, and the secular order that liberalism built is a Christian achievement that has forgotten its own paternity. The house of modern rights, which its inhabitants believe they built themselves in defiance of the church, turns out to have been raised on foundations the church laid, and even framed with timber the church cut.

For the argument of this unit, Siedentop's contribution is to give the genealogy of rights a concrete institutional history. It is one thing to assert, in general terms, that human dignity descends from the image of God; it is another to trace the specific path by which the moral equality of souls became, through the labor of medieval jurists, the legal apparatus of individual rights that the modern world inherited. Siedentop supplies that path, and in doing so he strengthens the unit's central claim: that the rights the modern world holds as self-evidently secular have a traceable Christian lineage, running from Paul through the canon lawyers to the declarations of the modern age. Whether one finds his account of the causal chain fully persuasive — and historians do debate how much weight the medieval canonists can bear, and how much the Enlightenment genuinely added — the broad shape of his thesis is difficult to dismiss: the rights-bearing individual was not born secular. It was born Christian, and only later forgot where it came from. How that forgetting reached even the founding charter of the modern rights-republic, we see next.
