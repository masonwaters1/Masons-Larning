There is a sentence at the heart of the modern political world that nearly everyone in the West can quote, or half-quote, and that almost no one examines closely. It stands near the opening of the American Declaration of Independence, and it has become something like the charter of the modern rights-republic, echoed and invoked and appealed to across two and a half centuries of struggle for justice. It holds these truths to be self-evident: that all men are created equal, and that they are endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable rights. We tend to hear this as a ringing assertion of human equality and rights, and to pass quickly over the middle of it. But the middle of it is where the weight rests. The equality and the rights are not asserted as brute facts, nor as the conclusions of secular reason; they are asserted as grounded — endowed by their Creator. The founding document of modern secular democracy grounds its central claims in God.

## The Load-Bearing Phrase

Look at what the phrase is doing.[^1] The Declaration needs to assert that all men are equal and possess rights that cannot be taken away — unalienable rights, rights that inhere in the person and that no government may violate. But as the earlier lessons of this unit have shown, such a claim needs a ground; equal and unalienable rights do not simply hover in the air, and the manifest inequality of human beings in every visible respect gives the claim of equality nothing to stand on among the observable facts. So the Declaration reaches for a ground, and the ground it reaches for is the Creator. Human beings are equal because they are equally created; they possess unalienable rights because those rights are the endowment of God, and what God has given, no human authority may take away. The theological premise is not decorative. It is load-bearing. It is what makes the assertion of equal, unalienable rights something more than a bare declaration of preference — it is what gives the rights their unalienability, by grounding them in a giver higher than any human power.

Remove the Creator from the sentence, and notice what happens to it. "All men are created equal, and are endowed with certain unalienable rights" — endowed by whom? By what? If not by God, then by nature, perhaps; but nature, as the naturalist understands it, is the value-blind process examined earlier in this unit, which endows no one with anything and least of all with unalienable rights. Or endowed by society, by the state, by agreement — but then the rights are alienable after all, granted by human authority and revocable by it, and the whole force of the claim collapses. The unalienability of the rights depends on their coming from a source beyond human reach, and in the Declaration that source is explicitly the Creator. The sentence is built around a theological load-bearing member, and it is not clear that it stands without it.

## The Theology of the Founders

Here the history becomes genuinely interesting, and genuinely complicating. The men who wrote and signed the Declaration were not, for the most part, orthodox Christians of a simple kind. They were a mixed company — some devout, some conventionally pious, and some, including the principal author, Thomas Jefferson, distinctly heterodox, closer to Deism than to Christian orthodoxy, skeptical of miracles and dogmas, more comfortable speaking of Nature's God and Providence than of the Trinity. One might expect such men to have grounded their politics in secular reason alone, and in much of their thought they drew deeply on the secular and classical traditions of natural law and republican liberty. And yet, when it came to the decisive assertion — the equal and unalienable rights of man — even the skeptical Jefferson reached for the Creator. The theological grounding did more work than the founders' own sometimes-thin personal theology might have seemed to require, because the claim itself demanded it. Whatever their private doubts, the logic of unalienable rights drove them to a divine endowment, for there was nowhere else for unalienability to come from.

This is itself a striking confirmation of the unit's thesis. The point is not that the American founders were all fervent believers — several were not — but that the very structure of their central claim required a theological ground, and supplied one, even from the pen of a religious skeptic. The intellectual tradition they drew upon, the natural-rights philosophy that reached them above all through John Locke, was itself saturated with theological premises: Locke had grounded human equality in the fact that all human beings are the creatures and property of God, made in his image, and therefore not to be treated as the property of one another. The secular-seeming philosophy of natural rights was, at its foundation, a Christian philosophy, and when the founders built their republic upon it, they built upon its theological base whether they fully believed it or not. The Creator in the Declaration is doing the work the doctrine of the image of God had always done — grounding the equal, inviolable worth of every person in something higher than human power.

## The Self-Indicting Premise

There is a final dimension to the Declaration's theological grounding that must be faced honestly, and that gives it a peculiar power. The same founding that proclaimed all men created equal and endowed by their Creator with unalienable rights coexisted, notoriously, with the enslavement of hundreds of thousands of human beings — a contradiction so glaring that it has shadowed the American experiment from its birth, and which a later unit of this course examines directly. The principle and the practice stood in flat contradiction. But here is the crucial point: the theological premise, once asserted, became a standing indictment of the practice that violated it. If all men are created equal and endowed by their Creator with unalienable rights, then the slave is created equal too, and his rights are unalienable too, and the institution that denies this stands condemned by the nation's own founding creed. The Creator who grounds the rights does not grant them to some and withhold them from others; the premise is universal, and its universality became the lever by which the contradiction would eventually be attacked.

This is why the Declaration's theological grounding proved so consequential. The assertion that rights come from God, and therefore belong equally to all and cannot be taken away, was a promissory note that the nation had written against itself — a principle more expansive than the practice of those who proclaimed it, containing within it the condemnation of their own failures and the demand for its own fulfillment. When later generations sought to extend the rights the founding had proclaimed but denied, they did not have to import a new principle; they had only to hold the nation to the theological premise it had already written into its founding charter. The Creator-grounded rights of the Declaration were both the foundation of the republic and the indictment of its injustices — and the story of how that indictment was pressed, in the name of the very God who was said to have endowed the rights, is the story we turn to next.
