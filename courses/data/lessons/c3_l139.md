If the previous lessons have argued that the modern conviction of equal human dignity rests on a theological foundation, the American civil rights movement of the twentieth century offers the most powerful confirmation imaginable — for at the decisive moment, when the modern world made its greatest moral argument against its most entrenched injustice, it reached not for secular utility or social contract but for the image of God and the moral law of heaven. The movement that broke the back of legalized racial segregation in the United States was, at its core, a theological argument, conducted by preachers, grounded in Scripture, and drawing its language, its leaders, its institutions, and its overwhelming moral authority from the Christian tradition. To see this clearly is to see the load-bearing doctrine of this unit doing its load-bearing work at the hinge of modern history.

## The Argument from the Image of God

At the heart of Martin Luther King's case against segregation lay a theological claim about human worth.[^1] Segregation was wrong, King argued, not merely because it was inconvenient, or inefficient, or contrary to the national interest, but because it degraded the human personality — because it treated as inferior those whom God had made in his own image, and thereby assaulted the dignity that God had conferred. The wrongness of segregation was, for King, ultimately a theological wrongness: it was a sin against the image of God in the human person, a violation of the equal worth that every human being possesses as a child of God. This was not incidental to his argument but its very foundation. The equal dignity that segregation denied was grounded, in King's thought, in the imago Dei — in the conviction that every person, of every race, bears the image of God and therefore possesses an inviolable worth that no law and no custom may violate.

This grounding gave King's argument a force that no merely secular appeal could have matched. When he stood against the entrenched power of an unjust order, he stood not merely on his own authority, nor on the shifting ground of majority opinion, but on the authority of a moral law higher than any human law — the eternal and divine law against which all human statutes are to be measured. In his celebrated letter written from a jail cell in Birmingham, King drew on the deep tradition of Christian and natural-law thought, invoking the great theologians to argue that a law out of harmony with the moral law of God is no true law at all, and that the unjust law degrades the human person and therefore may, and must, be resisted. The distinction between just and unjust law, on which the whole moral logic of civil disobedience rested, was a theological distinction: the just law squares with the law of God, and the unjust law contradicts it, and it is by this divine standard that the laws of men are to be judged and, where necessary, defied.

## The Church as the Engine

The theological character of the movement was not confined to its arguments; it pervaded its very substance. The civil rights movement was, to an extraordinary degree, a movement of the Black church — led by ministers, organized in and through congregations, sustained by the prayer, the preaching, the hymns, and the communal life of Christian worship. Its mass meetings were church services; its anthems were spirituals and gospel songs; its discipline of nonviolence was rooted in the Christian ethic of love of enemy and the example of the cross; its vision of the goal was the beloved community, a profoundly theological conception of a reconciled humanity living in justice and love. The movement drew on the oldest resources of the Christian imagination — the Exodus story of liberation from bondage, the prophets' thunderous demands for justice, the cross's identification of God with the suffering and the oppressed, the promise of a redeemed creation. It was Christianity in action, the ethic of the earlier units of this course enacted in the streets and at the lunch counters and on the marches of the American South.

This matters for the unit's argument because it shows where the moral power actually came from. When the modern world confronted one of its gravest injustices and summoned the force to overcome it, that force was drawn, overwhelmingly, from the Christian tradition — from its doctrine of the image of God, its ethic of love, its prophetic demand for justice, its identification of the divine with the oppressed. The civil rights movement did not ground its case in a secular theory of utility or a social contract; it grounded it in God, and it drew its strength from the church. At the decisive moment, the load-bearing doctrine bore the load. And King explicitly cashed the promissory note of the founding examined in the previous lesson, holding the nation to its own declaration that all are created equal and endowed by their Creator with unalienable rights — invoking the theological premise of the founding against the nation's betrayal of it.

## The Honest Complication

Honesty requires acknowledging that the relationship between Christianity and the struggle over race is not a simple story of the faith arrayed on the side of justice, and King himself knew this better than anyone. The bitter truth is that Christianity was invoked on both sides of the conflict. The defenders of slavery had quoted Scripture, and the defenders of segregation quoted it too; the white churches of the South were, in many cases, bulwarks of the very order King was fighting, and some of his most anguished words were directed not at open enemies but at the white moderate and the silent white church that counseled patience and looked away. The same tradition that armed King with the image of God and the moral law also furnished his opponents with their proof-texts and their pulpits. Christianity, in other words, did not automatically deliver the just answer; it was a contested inheritance, and the question was always which reading of it would prevail.

But this complication, rightly understood, does not weaken the unit's thesis; it refines it. The point is not that Christianity mechanically produces justice — plainly it does not, or the segregationist churches would not have existed. The point is that the moral framework within which the argument was conducted, by both sides, was a Christian framework, and that the winning argument, the argument that carried the moral authority and finally prevailed, was the one that appealed to the deepest logic of that framework: the equal worth of every person made in God's image, the ethic of love, the prophetic demand for justice. King defeated the segregationists not by abandoning the Christian tradition for some secular alternative but by wielding the tradition's own central convictions against a corrupted reading of it. The civil rights movement was an argument within Christianity about the meaning of Christianity, and it was won by the side that grasped most deeply what the image of God required. That the modern world's greatest moral victory took the form of a theological argument, won on theological grounds, is the fact this lesson exists to record — and it is a fact that the secular self-understanding of that victory has largely forgotten.
