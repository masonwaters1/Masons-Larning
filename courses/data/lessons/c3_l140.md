The logic of dignity, once set loose in the world, has not stayed still. Having established the equal worth of every human being, the modern moral conscience has pressed relentlessly outward, extending its circle of concern to beings the tradition never contemplated: to animals, whose suffering we increasingly count as a moral claim upon us; to future generations, who do not yet exist and can offer us nothing; and, at the frontier of speculation, to artificial minds, whose possible moral status is now seriously debated. This expanding circle is one of the most distinctive features of modern ethics, and it presents a fascinating puzzle for the argument of this unit. For the expansion is driven, at bottom, by the very logic the earlier lessons have traced to Christianity — and yet, in one of its dimensions, it turns against the human exceptionalism that logic was born to establish.

## The Circle Keeps Expanding

Consider the trajectory.[^1] The earlier units of this course described how Christianity universalized moral concern, breaking the ancient boundaries of tribe, city, and status to embrace every human being — the slave, the stranger, the enemy — as a bearer of equal worth. The circle of moral concern, once drawn tightly around one's own group, was expanded to encompass all humanity. And in the modern world, that expansion has not stopped at the boundary of the human. Concern for the suffering of animals, once marginal, has become a serious and growing moral movement, insisting that the capacity to suffer generates a claim to consideration wherever it is found. Concern for future generations — for the people who will inherit the consequences of our choices about the climate, about resources, about the world we leave behind — has become a central theme of contemporary ethics, extending obligation across time to those who cannot yet make any claim. And at the speculative frontier, serious thinkers now ask whether artificial intelligences, should they attain consciousness, would possess moral status and deserve moral consideration. The circle keeps expanding, drawing ever more beings within the scope of moral concern.

What drives this expansion? The engine, this unit contends, is the same intuition that Christianity planted and the earlier lessons have traced: the conviction that the vulnerable, the voiceless, the ones who cannot reciprocate, nonetheless have a claim upon us — that worth, and the capacity to suffer, generate obligation regardless of whether the being in question can offer anything in return. This is the logic of the Good Samaritan, of love extended beyond the boundary of reciprocity, of the moral authority of the victim, examined at length in earlier units. It is a logic that, once accepted, has an inherent tendency to expand, for it grounds moral concern not in the usefulness or the reciprocal power of its object but in the object's capacity to be harmed and its intrinsic worth — and there is no obvious place for such a logic to stop. The modern extension of concern to animals, to the unborn future, to possible artificial minds, runs on this inherited software: the deep conviction that the weak and the voiceless matter, that their suffering makes a claim, that we owe consideration even to those who can do nothing for us.

## The Software Running Beneath the Secular Surface

The irony that this unit keeps encountering appears here in a new form. The expanding circle is, in our moment, largely the project of secular progressives — of people who would reject the religious framework entirely and who understand their concern for animals, for the climate, for future generations, as the deliverance of enlightened secular reason. And yet the structure of their moral concern is recognizably the Christian structure: the siding with the voiceless against the powerful, the extension of compassion past every boundary of reciprocity, the conviction that the vulnerable possess a claim that commands sacrifice. The activist who has never entered a church, campaigning for the suffering of factory-farmed animals or the welfare of generations not yet born, is enacting a moral logic whose deep grammar is the one Christianity wrote — the logic of love for the least, extended now beyond the human altogether. The circle expands on Christian fuel, even where the fuel is unrecognized and its source denied. Modern ethics, at its expanding frontier, is running Christian software on secular hardware.

## The Turn Against Human Exceptionalism

But here the story takes a genuinely complicating turn, one that honesty requires facing squarely. For the expansion of the circle beyond the human does not merely extend the logic of human dignity; in one of its most influential forms, it turns against the very human exceptionalism that the doctrine of the image of God had established. Recall that the imago Dei grounded a specifically human dignity — the unique worth of the human being as the bearer of God's image, set apart from the rest of creation. But the modern extension of concern to animals, in its most rigorous versions, explicitly rejects this human uniqueness as an unjustified prejudice. The philosopher who coined the phrase the expanding circle, Peter Singer, whose consistency this unit has already noted, argues that the special moral status of human beings is a form of unjustified favoritism toward our own species — a prejudice he names speciesism, on analogy with racism — and that a consistent ethic must weigh the interests of all sentient beings impartially, without according human beings any special standing simply because they are human.

Here, then, is a real and instructive tension at the heart of modern ethics. The expanding circle is driven by the Christian intuition that the vulnerable and voiceless have a sacred claim — and yet, carried to its conclusion, it dissolves the human exceptionalism that the Christian doctrine of the image of God had established, leveling the human being back into the order of nature as one sentient creature among others. The same movement of thought both depends upon the inherited conviction of the sacredness of the vulnerable and corrodes the ground on which the special sacredness of the human had rested. Modern ethics wants both to extend the dignity of the vulnerable ever outward and, in extending it, undermines the unique dignity of the human that was its original form. This is not a stable resting place; it is a live tension, and it reveals something important about the inheritance this unit describes. The Christian dignity-intuition is powerful enough to keep expanding long after its theological source has been abandoned — but as it expands beyond the human, it begins to consume the very human exceptionalism from which it sprang. Whether the modern conscience can extend concern to all that suffers while still securing the special, inviolable worth of the human person, or whether the one expansion must come at the cost of the other, is among the unresolved questions the draining of the inherited reservoir presses upon us — and that draining is our next subject.
