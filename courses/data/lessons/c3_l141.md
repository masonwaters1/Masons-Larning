We arrive at the worry that gives this unit its deepest urgency, and it is worth stating that it is a worry, not a prediction — a possibility raised, with characteristic honesty, by the very historian whose thesis has framed much of this course. Tom Holland, having argued at length that the modern West's moral convictions are the inheritance of Christianity, is too careful a thinker to claim certainty about the future, and he does not. But he raises a question that haunts the whole account, and it can be put in the form of an image: what if the Christian moral capital on which the secular West is living is not an inexhaustible endowment but a finite reservoir — a pool that is being drawn down, and no longer refilled? What if the water we swim in is slowly draining away?

## The Image of the Reservoir

The metaphor captures something the abstract argument can miss.[^1] Picture the moral convictions of the modern West — the sacredness of the person, the equal worth of all, the reflexive compassion for the suffering, the conviction that the weak have a claim on the strong — as a great reservoir of belief, filled over many centuries by the steady rainfall of Christian faith and practice. For generation after generation, the belief that grounded these convictions was held, taught, worshipped, and lived, and the reservoir was continually replenished from its source. Then, over the past few centuries, and with gathering speed in our own time, the source has been cut off: the belief has receded, the practice has thinned, and the West has increasingly ceased to hold the faith that filled the pool. And yet the convictions remain; the reservoir is still full, or nearly so; we still hold, with passionate conviction, that every human being possesses inviolable dignity and equal worth. We are drawing on the water still.

The question the image poses is simple and grave. If the source has been cut off, is the reservoir being drained? The convictions we live by were replenished, for centuries, by a faith that most of the West no longer holds. They persist now on the accumulated capital of that faith — on habit, on memory, on the deep formation of a civilization steeped for a thousand years in Christian assumptions. But capital that is spent and not renewed runs out. If the convictions of human dignity and equal worth were sustained by a belief that is now fading, and if nothing has replaced that belief as their source, then we may be living on a finite inheritance, drawing down a reservoir that will not be refilled, and the water may, over time, run low. The full pool of the present may be no guarantee of the future, if the rain has stopped.

## The Evidence Cuts Both Ways

It would be dishonest to present this worry as an established conclusion, for the evidence genuinely cuts both ways, and thoughtful people read it differently. On one side, there is real reason to think the convictions may be more robust and self-sustaining than the draining-pool image suggests. They have, after all, persisted powerfully for generations after the decline of belief; the most secular societies on earth remain, in many respects, deeply committed to human rights and human dignity, and show no obvious signs of reverting to the frank contempt for the weak that characterized the pre-Christian world. Perhaps the convictions, once established, have become genuinely autonomous — grounded now in secular reason, in human sympathy, in stable institutions and shared commitments that can sustain and replenish them without the original faith. Perhaps the pool has found new sources. The persistence of the values through decades of secularization is at least some evidence that they are not so fragile as the worry implies.

On the other side, there are signs of strain that the worry can point to. The very difficulty this unit has documented — the inability of secular philosophy to ground equal human dignity as securely as the theological tradition did — is itself a kind of evidence that the convictions rest on foundations that are eroding. There are, in various quarters, the stirrings of a frank return to older logics: the reassertion of hierarchy, the celebration of strength, the questioning of human equality as a sentimental prejudice, the reappearance of the view that some human lives are worth more than others. And there is the generational question, which the image of the reservoir sharpens: each generation raised further from the faith that filled the pool may hold the inherited convictions more weakly — as unexamined habit rather than grounded belief — until the habit, lacking any deep root, gradually loosens and gives way. Whether these are marginal countercurrents or the first signs of a genuine draining, no one can say with confidence. But they are not nothing, and they are what the worry points to.

## Holding the Question Open

The honest position, and the one this unit commends, is to hold the question genuinely open. The draining-pool worry is not a demonstration that the convictions of the secular age are doomed; it is a serious possibility, raised by a serious thinker, that deserves to be taken seriously rather than either dismissed as alarmism or embraced as certainty. It may be that secular modernity will prove able to ground and renew its moral convictions from its own resources, translating the inherited faith into secular terms robust enough to sustain it, as thinkers like Habermas hope. It may be that the convictions will persist indefinitely on the strength of habit, sympathy, and institution, needing no metaphysical foundation to endure. Or it may be that the worry is well founded — that the convictions are inherited capital being spent, that the reservoir is draining, and that the coming generations will hold ever more weakly the belief in human dignity that their ancestors held as an unquestioned truth. We do not know which of these is true, and pretending to know would be a betrayal of the honesty the question demands.

What we can say is that the question is real, and that it is the deepest form of the challenge this unit has been pressing. The whole preceding argument — the strange foundations of the Declaration, the is-ought gap, the load-bearing doctrine of the image of God, the failure of the naturalistic groundings, the anomaly of equality — converges here, in the worry that the modern West may be living on a moral inheritance it can no longer replenish. If the convictions of human dignity really were grounded in a faith the West has abandoned, then their future is genuinely uncertain, and the confident assumption that they will simply endure, self-evident and secure, may be the most dangerous assumption of all. The pool is full now. Whether it is draining, and how fast, and whether it can be refilled, are questions the secular age would do well to face rather than ignore.
