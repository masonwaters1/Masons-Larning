The worry that the West's moral convictions may be a draining inheritance can seem, stated in the abstract, like a speculation about the distant future. The philosopher Alasdair MacIntyre argued that it is not a speculation about the future at all, but a diagnosis of the present — that the draining has already happened, that we are already living amid the wreckage of a moral tradition whose foundations have collapsed, and that the disorder of modern moral life is the visible symptom of the loss. His book on the subject, published in 1981, is among the most influential works of moral philosophy of the past century, and its central image gives the challenge of this unit its sharpest and most disturbing form: that we go on using the language of morality while having lost the framework that gave it meaning, speaking a tongue whose grammar we have forgotten.

## The Parable of the Shattered Science

MacIntyre opens with a thought experiment that repays careful attention, for it captures his whole diagnosis in a single image.[^1] Imagine, he says, that a catastrophe befalls the natural sciences: a wave of hostility destroys the laboratories, burns the books, kills or scatters the scientists, and wipes out the practice of science entirely. Then imagine that, generations later, people attempt to revive science from the fragments that survive — scattered terms, isolated formulae, half-remembered procedures, pages torn from vanished books. They would use the words still — "mass," "atomic weight," "neutrino" — and they would argue with one another using these terms, and they would believe themselves to be doing science. But they would not be doing science, for the theoretical framework that gave the terms their meaning, that made them parts of a coherent and intelligible whole, would be gone. They would be manipulating fragments, uttering the vocabulary of a lost coherence, mistaking the shards of a shattered discipline for the discipline itself.

This, MacIntyre argues, is the actual condition of modern moral discourse. We have inherited fragments of older moral traditions — pieces of Aristotle, pieces of Christianity, pieces of various later systems — but we have lost the frameworks that gave those fragments their meaning and coherence. We still use the words: we speak of rights, of duties, of justice, of the good, of human dignity. But we have lost the context — the account of human nature and human purpose, the shared vision of what a human life is for — within which those words once formed a coherent and rationally intelligible whole. And so we are in the position of MacIntyre's imagined heirs of the shattered science: manipulating the fragments of a lost moral coherence, using a moral vocabulary whose grammar we have forgotten, and mistaking the shards for the thing itself.

## Why the Arguments Never End

From this diagnosis MacIntyre draws an explanation of one of the most striking features of modern moral life: the interminability of its debates. We notice, if we are honest, that our great moral and political arguments — over justice, over rights, over the most contested questions of life and death and freedom — never seem to reach resolution. They go on and on, each side invoking principles the other does not share, each argument valid in its own terms yet unable to persuade across the divide, until the disputes settle into permanent, unresolvable opposition. MacIntyre argues that this interminability is precisely what we should expect if his diagnosis is correct. The rival positions are fragments of different lost traditions, invoking incommensurable premises drawn from frameworks that no longer cohere; there is no shared framework within which the arguments could be rationally adjudicated, and so they cannot be adjudicated, and the debates grind on forever, each side unable to convince the other because they no longer speak a common moral language.

And there is a darker consequence still. If moral terms have lost the framework that made them rationally grounded, then they become, MacIntyre argues, mere instruments for expressing and imposing preference — masks for the will. When "this is unjust" can no longer be cashed out in a shared framework as a rational claim, it collapses into "I disapprove of this, and I want you to disapprove too"; moral language becomes a tool of manipulation, a way of dressing up preference as principle in order to move others to one's will. This is the condition the philosophers call emotivism, and MacIntyre's disturbing claim is that modern moral discourse, having lost its grounding, has largely become emotivist in practice, whatever it professes — that beneath the confident language of rights and justice lies, increasingly, the naked assertion of preference and the will to power. Here his analysis converges with Nietzsche's, and MacIntyre acknowledges the convergence: if the older moral traditions have truly collapsed, then Nietzsche may be right that what remains is only will and power wearing the mask of morality — and the real choice, MacIntyre famously argued, is between Nietzsche and a return to the tradition Nietzsche despised.

## The Grammar We Have Lost

For the argument of this unit, MacIntyre supplies the philosophical account of what the loss of grounding actually does, and why it matters even if the convictions have not yet visibly collapsed. The draining of the reservoir, in his diagnosis, does not announce itself with a sudden failure of moral conviction; it manifests, more subtly, as a growing incoherence — as the increasing inability of a civilization to give a rational account of the moral convictions it still holds, as the interminability of its moral disputes, as the gradual slide of its moral language from grounded claim toward mere expression of preference. We may go on affirming human dignity and human rights with undiminished passion; but if MacIntyre is right, we affirm them increasingly as fragments cut off from any framework that could ground them, and our inability to say, when pressed, why every human being possesses inviolable worth — the inability this whole unit has documented — is exactly the symptom his diagnosis predicts. We are speaking a moral language whose grammar we have lost, and the confidence with which we speak it may conceal, rather than refute, the loss.

MacIntyre's own response points beyond this unit toward the constructive question the course will take up in its final movement: whether the remedy lies in the recovery of tradition — in the rebuilding of communities and practices within which moral language might once again become coherent and grounded, a possibility he associated with a kind of new monasticism and a waiting for renewal. That prescription is contested, and MacIntyre's whole diagnosis is contested; many able philosophers reject the claim that modern moral discourse is genuinely incoherent, holding that we can reason about morality perfectly well amid pluralism, and that the interminability of moral debate reflects the difficulty of the questions rather than the collapse of a framework. Whether MacIntyre has diagnosed a fatal disorder or merely the ordinary difficulty of moral reasoning in a plural age is, like so much in this unit, a genuinely open question. But his diagnosis names, with unusual precision, what it would look like for the reservoir to be draining while the pool still appears full — and that is a possibility the secular age cannot responsibly ignore. Having pressed the challenge to its philosophical depth, we turn at last to the other side of the argument, and give the strongest secular case for grounding human dignity without God the full and fair hearing it deserves.
