The previous lesson left us at the threshold of the most fundamental assumption a modern person makes about morality — so fundamental that we scarcely recognize it as an assumption at all. Ask anyone raised in the modern West whether all human beings are equal in worth and dignity, and they will answer yes almost before the question is finished, as though it were a fact as plain as the sun. This conviction feels less like a belief than a perception, something we simply see. And yet it would have been very nearly unintelligible to the great civilizations of antiquity. The ancient world was built, from top to bottom, on the opposite assumption: that human beings are profoundly and naturally unequal, that hierarchy is woven into the very structure of the cosmos, and that this is not an injustice to be corrected but the good order of things. To understand the world before Christ, we must first inhabit this logic of hierarchy.

## A Ranked Universe

The ancient mind did not look upon a world of equal individuals; it looked upon a vast, ordered ranking of beings, a cosmos in the original sense of the Greek word — an *order*, a structured arrangement in which everything had its proper place.[^1] At the summit stood the gods; below them, human beings; below them, animals; and within the human order itself, ranks descended in a great chain: the free above the enslaved, the citizen above the foreigner, the Greek or Roman above the barbarian, the highborn above the lowborn, the male above the female, the adult above the child. This was not experienced as an arbitrary social arrangement that might have been otherwise. It was felt to be natural, cosmic, and right — the way reality itself was constituted. To be highborn and free was to occupy a higher rung of being; to be a slave or a barbarian was to occupy a lower one, and to possess correspondingly less claim upon the moral concern of others.

The consequence of this ranking was that human worth was not equal but graduated. A person's moral standing, the weight their life and suffering carried, depended on where they stood in the hierarchy. The life of a Roman senator and the life of a slave were not two instances of a single, equal human dignity; they were different in kind, as different as the eagle and the worm. The powerful mattered; the weak and the low mattered little or not at all. This was so obvious to the ancient world that it rarely needed stating, and when philosophers did address it, they did so not to question it but to explain and justify it.

## The Justification of the Strong

Nor was this merely the unreflective prejudice of the powerful. The most penetrating minds of antiquity articulated the logic of hierarchy as a principle of justice itself. In Plato's dialogues we meet figures who state the case with brutal clarity: Thrasymachus, who declares that justice is simply the advantage of the stronger, and Callicles, who argues that it is natural justice for the strong to rule the weak and to have more, that nature herself demonstrates in every sphere that the superior should dominate the inferior. Plato has Socrates argue against these figures, and the philosophical tradition would develop far more elevated conceptions of justice; but the very fact that such views could be stated as serious positions, requiring serious refutation, reveals how naturally the ancient mind reached for the idea that strength and superiority carried their own entitlement.

Beneath the philosophical debates lay a shared bedrock: the assumption that inequality was natural and that the ordering of superior over inferior was not a problem to be solved but a fact to be managed. The question the ancients asked was not "how do we honor the equal dignity of all?" — a question that would have struck them as based on a false premise — but "how do we maintain the proper order among naturally unequal beings?" Hierarchy was the answer to the deepest human questions about how to live together, and the good society was the well-ordered one, in which each rank kept its place and performed its proper function. Equality, far from being the foundation of justice, would have appeared as its subversion — a disorder, a violation of the natural arrangement of the world.

## The Absence of the Equal Individual

What was missing, then, was the very idea around which modern moral and political life revolves: the notion that beneath all the differences of rank, wealth, sex, and origin, there is a single, equal, inviolable human worth shared by every person simply in virtue of being human. The ancient world had no concept of the individual as such a bearer of equal dignity. It had persons defined by their place — this man's son, that city's citizen, this master's slave — but not the abstract, universal human person whose worth is independent of role and rank. The slave was not a human being who happened to be enslaved, a free person temporarily in chains whose essential dignity remained intact; the slave was, in the settled understanding of the age, a lower kind of being, whose subordination expressed rather than violated the natural order.

This absence is difficult for us to feel, precisely because the idea of equal individual worth has become the air we breathe. When the American founders wrote that all men are created equal, they were stating what they took to be a self-evident truth; but it would have been self-evidently *false* to Aristotle, to Cicero, to any thoughtful person of the classical world, who would have seen in it a plain contradiction of everything nature reveals about the ranking of human capacities and kinds. The proposition that the barbarian slave and the Roman aristocrat possess an identical and equal dignity would have seemed not merely wrong but absurd — a denial of the obvious. The revolution that made it seem obvious to us, rather than absurd, is the subject of this entire course, and its ultimate source, this course argues following the historian Tom Holland, lies not in Greece or Rome but in the strange conviction, inherited from Israel and radicalized by Christianity, that every human being is made in the image of God and stands equal before him — the claim, scandalous in its time, that in Christ there is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female.

## Where Equality Came From

If human equality is not natural and self-evident — if the most brilliant civilizations in history found it neither — then it requires an explanation, and the explanation cannot simply be that humanity gradually noticed a truth that had been there all along. Something had to introduce the idea into the world, against the grain of everything experience seemed to teach. The thesis of this course, drawn from Holland and from the political philosopher Larry Siedentop's account of the "invention of the individual," is that the decisive source was the biblical tradition: the Hebrew insistence that every person bears the divine image, and the Christian universalization of that claim into the astonishing assertion that the God of the universe had become a slave and died a slave's death, thereby investing the lowest of the low with infinite worth. From this seed, over many centuries, grew the conviction of equal human dignity that the modern West now treats as a first principle and imagines to be the natural property of all reasonable people.

This is a genealogical claim, not a claim that only Christians can believe in equality; countless people who reject Christianity hold the conviction of human equality with complete sincerity, and rightly so. The point is about *origins* — about where a idea so contrary to the evidence of nature came from, and why it took root when and where it did. The ancient world, for all its genius, did not generate it, and could not, because its entire moral universe rested on the opposite foundation. To see that clearly is to see how contingent, how hard-won, and how genuinely revolutionary the idea of human equality really is.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Several qualifications are needed to keep this argument honest. First, the ancient world was not wholly without intimations of a common humanity. Stoic philosophy, in particular, developed a genuine cosmopolitanism, teaching that all human beings share in the divine reason, the *logos*, and are therefore in some sense fellow citizens of a single world-city — a spark of universalism that pointed beyond the logic of hierarchy, even if the Stoics rarely drew from it the radical egalitarian conclusions that later ages would. Certain philosophers questioned whether particular slaves were truly slaves by nature, and the seeds of a more universal moral concern can be found scattered through the classical tradition. The contrast this lesson draws is real, but it is a contrast of dominant assumptions, not an absolute absence on one side and pure presence on the other.

Second, the genealogical claim that human equality descends chiefly from the biblical tradition is a serious historical argument, but it is contested, and thoughtful scholars locate the sources of modern egalitarianism variously in Stoicism, in the Enlightenment, in the rise of commercial society, or in some combination of forces — the causation of so vast a cultural shift can never be established with the certainty of a proof. Third, we must resist any triumphalism, for Christian civilization itself retained rigid hierarchies for the better part of two millennia, defended slavery, subordinated women, and honored the idea of equal dignity far more in profession than in practice; the introduction of a principle is not its realization, and the gap between the two is the substance of much that this course will later examine. The claim is not that Christianity instantly abolished hierarchy, but that it introduced, into a world built on the naturalness of inequality, a revolutionary and destabilizing principle of equal worth — a principle whose slow, uneven, and still-unfinished working-out has shaped the entire moral history of the West. Having seen how the ancient world assumed hierarchy, we turn next to how its greatest philosopher defended it.

[^1]: The moral and cosmic order of classical antiquity assumed natural, graduated inequality rather than equal human worth: a ranked universe (gods, humans, animals; and among humans, free over enslaved, citizen over foreigner, Greek/Roman over barbarian, male over female) experienced as the natural and good order of the cosmos. Figures in Plato's dialogues (Thrasymachus in the *Republic*; Callicles in the *Gorgias*) articulate the principle that justice favors the stronger and that it is natural for the superior to rule the inferior — positions Socrates opposes but which reveal the era's instinctive reach for hierarchy. The classical world lacked the concept of the individual as a bearer of equal, inviolable dignity independent of rank. This course argues, following Tom Holland's *Dominion* (2019) and Larry Siedentop's *Inventing the Individual* (2014), that the conviction of universal human equality descends principally from the biblical tradition — the Hebrew *imago Dei* and its Christian radicalization ("neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female," Galatians 3:28) — rather than from Greece or Rome, and would have struck an educated ancient as self-evidently false. The honest qualifications are that Stoic cosmopolitanism offered a genuine spark of universalism; that the genealogy of modern equality is contested (with rival claims for Stoicism, the Enlightenment, and commercial society); and that Christian civilization long retained hierarchy and slavery, honoring equal dignity more in principle than in practice.
