Ask a modern person what religion is fundamentally about, and somewhere near the center of the answer will be morality. We assume, almost without thinking, that a religion tells you how to be good — that its gods or its God care about how you treat other people, that piety and virtue belong together, that to be religious is, at least in aspiration, to be moral. This assumption is so basic to how we understand religion that we project it backward onto all religions everywhere, including those of the ancient world. And here we make a profound error. For the gods of Greece and Rome were, on the whole, not moral teachers at all. They did not issue commandments about how to live; they were not exemplars of goodness; and ancient religion was, for the most part, not about being good. To grasp this is to grasp one of the deepest differences between the pagan world and the world Christianity would make.

## Gods Behaving Badly

Anyone who has read the myths of Greece and Rome knows that the gods of Olympus were not paragons of virtue.[^1] Zeus, the king of the gods, was a serial adulterer and rapist, forever deceiving his jealous wife and forcing himself upon mortal women in a bewildering variety of disguises. The gods were vain, quarrelsome, vengeful, and partisan; they nursed grudges, played favorites, deceived one another, and visited terrible punishments on mortals who offended their pride or crossed their whims. They behaved, in short, like a family of enormously powerful, immortal aristocrats — possessed of superhuman strength and beauty, but not of superhuman goodness. Their power was infinite; their morality was, by any elevated standard, often appalling.

This was not felt by the ancients as a scandal or a problem to be explained away, because no one expected the gods to be morally good in the first place. The gods commanded honor because they were powerful, not because they were righteous. One feared them, flattered them, and sought their favor as one might a capricious and dangerous king, whose good will was worth cultivating and whose anger was to be avoided — not because he was virtuous, but because he was mighty. To look to Zeus or Aphrodite or Ares as a model of how to live a good life would have made no sense; they were forces to be reckoned with, not teachers to be followed. The idea that a god should be *good*, that divinity and moral perfection should coincide, was not the natural assumption of ancient religion. It was, again, an inheritance from elsewhere.

## Religion as Ritual, Not Righteousness

If ancient religion was not primarily about being good, what was it about? Overwhelmingly, it was about *cult* — about the correct performance of ritual, sacrifice, and observance, through which human beings maintained a proper relationship of reciprocity with the divine powers. The Roman formula captured the logic exactly: *do ut des*, "I give that you may give." One offered the gods their due — the sacrifices, the festivals, the rites performed correctly and at the proper times — and in return one hoped for their favor and protection: a good harvest, victory in war, safety at sea, the prosperity of the city. Religion was a system of exchange, an ongoing transaction between mortals and the powers that governed their fortunes, and its central concern was that the rituals be carried out properly, for an error in the rite could offend the god and bring disaster.

Morality, in this scheme, was a largely separate matter. The gods cared that you sacrificed correctly, that you did not neglect their honors, that you kept your oaths (for the gods witnessed oaths) and respected the sacred obligations of hospitality; but they did not, for the most part, concern themselves with whether you were kind to your slaves, generous to the poor, honest in your dealings, or pure in your heart. There was no divine commandment to love your neighbor, no sacred law binding the conscience to justice and mercy, no expectation that the gods would judge you morally. Ethics — the question of how to live a good human life — belonged not to the temple but to the philosophers, who pursued it as a matter of reason and human wisdom, largely independent of the cult of the gods. Religion and morality occupied different spheres, and the fusion of the two that we now take for granted had not yet occurred.

## The God Who Judges

Set against this background, the God of Israel, and the God proclaimed by the Christians who inherited Israel's faith, appears as something genuinely new — a deity for whom holiness and moral righteousness were not incidental but essential, who was himself the source and standard of goodness, and who cared, above all, about justice and mercy in human dealings. This God did not merely demand correct ritual; he demanded a righteous life, and he declared, through his prophets, that he despised the sacrifices of those who oppressed the poor and perverted justice. He commanded his people to care for the widow, the orphan, and the stranger; he identified himself with the cause of the weak and the wronged; and he promised to judge every human being, not for the correctness of their rites, but for the moral quality of their lives — for how they had treated the least among them.

Here was a fusion that the pagan world had not known: the union of religion and morality, of the divine and the ethical, in a single God who was both worshipped and obeyed, both feared and imitated, whose very nature was goodness and who required goodness of his creatures. This is the source of our now-instinctive assumption that religion is *about* morality — that a god worth worshipping must be good, and must want us to be good. We inherit this assumption so completely that we cannot easily imagine religion otherwise, and we are surprised to learn that the temples of antiquity were not, in our sense, schools of virtue. The moralization of the divine — the conviction that God is good and cares supremely about how we treat one another — was one of the most consequential of all the transformations the biblical tradition worked upon the world, and it has become so basic to our understanding of religion itself that we forget it was ever an innovation.

## What the Change Made Possible

The consequences of this fusion were immense, for once the divine and the moral were joined, morality acquired a weight and an authority it could not have had when it was merely the counsel of philosophers. To wrong the weak was no longer simply unwise or unseemly; it was to sin against God himself, who saw and judged and cared. The poor and the oppressed were no longer merely unfortunate; they were the special concern of the Lord of the universe, whose eye was upon them and whose judgment awaited those who despised them. The moral life was raised from a matter of human prudence and reputation to a matter of eternal significance, played out before the gaze of a God who was goodness itself and who would call every soul to account. This gave to conscience, to justice, and to compassion a foundation and an urgency they had never possessed in a world where the gods cared only for their honors.

It is worth pausing to feel how strange, and how contingent, this now-familiar arrangement really is. That religion should be about being good; that God should be the guarantor of morality; that the divine should stand on the side of the weak against the strong — none of this was obvious, none of it was universal, and none of it was the natural shape of humanity's religious life. It was the particular achievement of a particular tradition, and it reshaped the moral imagination of the world so thoroughly that its innovation now looks like the plainest common sense. To recover the pagan world, in which powerful and amoral gods demanded ritual rather than righteousness, is to recover a sense of just how much the biblical revolution changed.

## The Honest Counterpoint

This contrast, like the others in this unit, requires qualification lest it harden into caricature. Ancient religion was not entirely devoid of moral dimensions. In the Greek tradition, Zeus was in some contexts the guardian of justice and of the sacred bonds of hospitality and the oath; the poet Hesiod could speak of Justice as a divine daughter of Zeus who reported human wrongs to her father. Notions of ritual pollution attached to certain crimes, such as murder, giving them a religious as well as a social dimension. Egyptian religion, notably, envisioned a moral judgment of the dead, in which the heart was weighed against the principle of *Maat*, truth and justice — a striking anticipation of moral accountability before the divine. And the philosophers, especially Plato with his supreme Form of the Good and the Stoics with their providential divine Reason, did draw the divine and the moral together in sophisticated ways. The separation of religion and morality in antiquity was therefore a matter of dominant emphasis, not an absolute divorce.

Moreover, the fusion of ethical monotheism was the achievement of the Jewish tradition first, upon which Christianity built; this course will devote a later lesson to what Israel brought to the world, and the innovation described here should be credited to that longer lineage rather than to Christianity alone. Finally, one may fairly ask whether the moralization of religion was in every respect a gain, for the union of divine authority with moral demand also gave rise, in later ages, to forms of guilt, fear, and religious coercion whose costs were real — a complexity later units will not ignore. Yet with all these qualifications granted, the central point stands: the pagan gods were not, on the whole, moral teachers, and the assumption that they should have been is itself a legacy of the tradition that made God and goodness one. Having seen that the ancient heavens offered no moral law, we turn next to what they offered instead: a universe governed by fate and fortune, and strangely empty of hope.
