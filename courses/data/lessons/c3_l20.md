We have seen that the ancient gods offered no moral law. They offered something else instead, or rather they presided over a universe whose deepest structure was governed by forces even they could not finally control: Fate and Fortune. To live in the ancient world was to live under the shadow of powers that were blind, impersonal, and indifferent to human hopes — a cosmos that was not going anywhere, that promised no redemption, and that met human suffering not with the assurance of a future deliverance but with the counsel of dignified endurance. The ancient relationship to time, to destiny, and to the future was profoundly different from our own, and at its heart lay an absence that a modern person, heir to a tradition of hope, may find it hard even to perceive. The ancient world was, in a specific and consequential sense, a world without hope.

## Under the Rule of Fate

At the foundation of the ancient imagination lay the conviction that human life was subject to Fate — an impersonal, inexorable ordering of what must be, to which even the gods were ultimately bound.[^1] The Greeks personified it as the Moirai, the three Fates who spun, measured, and cut the thread of each mortal life; the Romans spoke of *fatum*, that which is spoken, decreed, fixed. Fate was not a moral order rewarding the good and punishing the wicked; it was simply the way things were destined to be, unalterable and often inscrutable, indifferent to human deserving. The greatest heroes met their appointed ends not because they had earned them but because they were fated, and the tragic grandeur of much ancient literature lies precisely in the spectacle of noble men and women struggling against a destiny they cannot escape. To be wise was, in part, to recognize the limits that Fate imposed and to meet one's appointed lot without the delusion that it might be otherwise.

Alongside Fate stood Fortune — Tyche to the Greeks, Fortuna to the Romans — the capricious power that governed the ups and downs of human life, raising some to prosperity and casting others down, turning her wheel without regard to justice or merit. Fortune was fickle, unpredictable, and impossible to secure; the man who stood high today might be cast down tomorrow, and nothing he possessed was truly safe from her turning. The image of Fortune's wheel, which would persist deep into the Christian Middle Ages, captured the ancient sense that human prosperity was inherently precarious, a loan that Fortune might recall at any moment. Between them, Fate and Fortune framed a universe in which the human being was a small and vulnerable creature, subject to vast forces beyond his control or comprehension, able to influence his lot only at the margins and never to master it.

## A Universe Going Nowhere

Just as consequential as the rule of Fate was the ancient sense of time itself, which differed fundamentally from the one most moderns unconsciously assume. We tend to imagine time as a line running from a beginning toward a goal, history as a story with a direction, the future as a place where things might be different and better. The ancient world, on the whole, did not think this way. Time was widely conceived as cyclical, turning in great recurring rounds like the seasons and the heavens, or as a process of decline from a lost golden age into progressively baser ages of silver, bronze, and iron — the myth of decline that the poet Hesiod gave classic form. In neither vision was history going anywhere new; it was either endlessly repeating or steadily deteriorating, and the notion that it might be moving toward some future consummation, some redemption of all that had gone before, was foreign to the ancient mind.

This absence of a forward-moving, redemptive history had profound consequences for how human beings related to their own suffering and to the future. If time is a wheel or a decline, then there is no arc bending toward justice, no promised future in which present wrongs will be set right, no consummation toward which one might hope and labor. There is only the recurring pattern, the turning wheel, the slow descent. The appropriate posture toward such a universe is not hope but acceptance — the endurance of what cannot be changed, the wisdom that aligns itself with the order of things rather than straining against it. The Stoics made this posture into a philosophy of great nobility, teaching that the wise man accepts his fate, wills what happens as though he had chosen it, and finds his freedom in the acceptance of necessity. It was a magnificent discipline, but it was a discipline of resignation, not of hope.

## The Shadow of Death

Nowhere was the absence of hope more stark than in what the ancient world expected beyond death. For most, the afterlife was not a place of promised joy but a cheerless, shadowy half-existence, a realm of pale ghosts drained of the vividness of life. Homer gave the classic expression of this bleak vision when he had the shade of the great Achilles declare, from among the dead, that he would rather be a living servant to a poor man than a king over all the perished dead — a stunning admission that even the most glorious of heroes found the underworld a thing of horror and diminishment, not reward. The dead endured a gray and joyless persistence, or, in some views, simply faded. There was no general expectation of a blessed immortality for the ordinary person, no promise of resurrection, no reunion in a better world.

Others took an even starker view. The Epicureans taught that death was simply the end, the dissolution of the atoms that composed us, after which there was nothing at all — and they offered this as a *consolation*, arguing that since death is annihilation, it is nothing to fear, for where death is, we are not. This was a genuine philosophy of tranquility, and for some it brought real peace; but it was peace through the extinction of hope for anything beyond, not through the promise of it. Even the ancient conception of hope itself was ambivalent. In the myth of Pandora, when all the evils escaped from the jar to plague mankind, hope alone remained inside — and it was never entirely clear whether hope was a comfort left to humanity or the final evil, a delusion that keeps the wretched clinging to false expectation. The ancient world, for all its wisdom and grandeur, did not know hope as we know it: the confident, forward-looking expectation of a good that is coming.

## The Revolution of Hope

Into this world Christianity introduced something that would transform the human relationship to time, suffering, and death: a linear, redemptive history and a hope grounded in it. The biblical vision saw time not as a wheel or a decline but as a story — creation, fall, redemption, and a promised consummation toward which all things were moving — history with a beginning, a turning point, and a goal. And at the center of this story stood a promise that shattered the ancient shadow of death: the promise of resurrection, of a life beyond death that was not a gray diminishment but a fullness surpassing this life, and of a future in which suffering would be redeemed, tears wiped away, and the whole creation made new. Hope, which the ancient world had regarded with such ambivalence, became a virtue — one of the three theological virtues, alongside faith and love — the confident expectation of a promised good, resting not on the turning of Fortune's wheel but on the faithfulness of a God who had entered history and pledged its redemption.

The effect of this was to reorient the entire human posture toward the future. Where the ancient wise man had counseled resignation to an unchangeable order, the Christian was summoned to hope for a coming deliverance; where suffering had been something to be endured with dignity because it could not be helped, it became something that could be borne in the confidence that it was not the final word. This hope would prove one of the most powerful forces in Western history, and — as a later unit will argue — even the modern secular faith in progress, the conviction that history moves forward toward a better future, is in large part this Christian hope transposed into a worldly key, a redemptive arc of time inherited from the faith and retained long after the faith itself was set aside. The idea that the future is open, that things can get better, that history is going somewhere — so natural to us, so foreign to the ancients — is among the deepest of our inheritances.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Several qualifications temper this picture. First, the ancient world was not uniformly gloomy or despairing; it knew great joy, festivity, and love of life, and its philosophies of acceptance brought many people a genuine serenity. The Stoic who aligned himself with the cosmic order and the Epicurean who freed himself from the fear of death each achieved a real and hard-won peace, and some thoughtful observers, ancient and modern, have judged this mature acceptance of finitude more honest and more dignified than what they regard as the wish-fulfillment of religious hope. To describe the ancient world as "without hope" is to speak of a specific kind of forward-looking, redemptive expectation, not to deny that the ancients found meaning, contentment, and courage in the lives they had.

Second, the contrast between cyclical pagan time and linear Christian time, though broadly valid, is a simplification; ancient thought was various, and elements of directional or providential thinking can be found here and there within it. Third, the mystery religions, to which the next lesson turns, did offer some initiates a hope of blessed immortality, complicating any claim that the pagan world knew no hope of an afterlife at all. And the deeper roots of redemptive, linear history lie, once again, in the Jewish tradition, with its covenant and its prophetic expectation, upon which Christianity built. Yet when these qualifications are weighed, the fundamental contrast remains illuminating: the dominant ancient posture was endurance under Fate and Fortune in a universe going nowhere, and the confident hope in a redemptive future that we now take for granted entered the world as part of the biblical revolution. Having surveyed the ancient world's fatalism, we turn next to one of its most striking moral features — the conviction that pity itself was a weakness to be overcome.
