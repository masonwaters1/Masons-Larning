We come now to what may be the single most shocking discovery a modern person can make about the moral world of antiquity — more shocking than the acceptance of slavery, more shocking than the exposure of infants, because it strikes at a value we hold so dear that we can scarcely conceive of anyone rejecting it. In much of the ancient world, and especially among its most serious moral philosophers, *pity* — compassion for the suffering, being moved by another's pain — was regarded not as a virtue but as a weakness, a failing, even a vice. The reflex we consider the very touchstone of a good heart, the welling of sympathy for someone in distress, the ancient sage was trained to suppress. To understand why, and to feel how alien this makes the ancient moral world, is to understand something essential about the revolution that Christianity would bring.

## The Sage Who Would Not Weep

The most rigorous case against pity was made by the Stoics, whose ethics dominated the moral thought of the late Roman world.[^1] For the Stoics, the goal of life was a serene rationality, an inner tranquility undisturbed by the passions — those turbulent emotions that cloud judgment and enslave the soul to things outside its control. Pity, in their analysis, was precisely such a passion: a disturbance of the mind, an irrational contraction of the soul at the sight of another's suffering, a kind of emotional infection that reason ought to master. The wise man, the Stoic sage, would not feel pity, because to be moved by another's misfortune was to be governed by emotion rather than reason, to let one's peace be shattered by things one could not control. Seneca, the great Roman Stoic, drew the distinction with care: the good man will *help* those in need, generously and rationally, but he will not *feel pity*, for pity is a vice of the small and womanish mind, a sickness of the soul. He would give aid without the disturbance of sympathy — do the compassionate thing, one might say, without the compassionate feeling.

This was not cruelty, and it is important not to mistake it for such. The Stoics advocated genuine beneficence; they held that the wise man would assist others and act rightly toward them. What they rejected was the *emotion* of pity, the being-moved, the sharing in the sufferer's pain — and they rejected it precisely because they prized an inner freedom that no external misfortune, one's own or another's, should be allowed to disturb. It was a philosophy of magnificent self-command, and there is something genuinely admirable in its refusal to be tossed about by every wave of feeling. But it was also a philosophy in which the tender movement of the heart toward the suffering, which we regard as the beginning of all goodness, was treated as a defect to be overcome. The ideal was the sage who helped without being moved, who remained serene and untouched while doing good — the very opposite of the compassion that trembles and weeps.

## The Contempt for Weakness

Beyond the philosophers, in the broader moral culture of the ancient world, the low valuation of pity took a harder form. In a world organized around honor and strength, as we have seen, the admired qualities were those of the powerful — courage, victory, command, the maintenance of standing — and the natural correlate of admiring strength was a certain contempt for weakness. The defeated, the poor, the enslaved, the sick, the failed: these occupied the low end of the human hierarchy, and to align oneself with them, to take their side, to be moved by their plight, was to associate oneself with weakness rather than strength. The instinct of the honor culture ran not toward the sufferer but away from him; his suffering was often taken as a sign of his low place in the order of things, and pity for him carried a whiff of the contemptible.

This helps explain a feature of the ancient world that can astonish a modern observer: the near-absence of organized charity for the needy as such. The ancients gave, sometimes lavishly — but their giving followed the logic of reciprocity and honor, not of compassion for need. The wealthy funded public works, games, and buildings, a practice modern scholars call euergetism, and they were honored for it; but this civic generosity was directed toward the community and its citizens, toward those who could confer honor in return, and it aimed at glory and standing. It was not charity to the destitute, the beggar, the stranger, the person whose only claim was need itself. One benefited one's friends, allies, and fellow citizens within a web of mutual obligation; one did not, as a recognized duty, care for the poor and the suffering simply because they were poor and suffering. The idea that the weak and the needy had a special claim on us, precisely in their weakness and need, was not part of the ancient moral vocabulary.

## The Revaluation of Mercy

Against all of this, the tradition that would remake the Western world proclaimed something that inverted the ancient valuation at its root: that mercy and compassion were not weaknesses but the highest of virtues, that the weak and the suffering had a special claim upon us, and that to care for the poor, the sick, the stranger, and the least was the very heart of the good life. Building on the Jewish tradition's insistence on care for the widow, the orphan, and the stranger, Christianity placed compassion at the center of morality in a manner without precedent. In the parable of the Good Samaritan, it was the one who was *moved* by the wounded stranger's suffering, who felt compassion and acted on it, who was held up as the model of neighbor-love. In the great scene of judgment, the criterion by which souls were divided was how they had treated the hungry, the sick, the imprisoned, the stranger — "whatever you did for the least of these," the King declares, "you did for me." The suffering of the least was identified with God himself.

This was a revaluation of shattering implications, for it took exactly what the ancient world had despised — the weak, the lowly, the suffering — and placed them at the center of moral concern, even identifying them with the divine. And it bore fruit not merely in sentiment but in institutions the ancient world had never produced: organized charity as a religious duty, the systematic care of the poor, and in time the hospital, the orphanage, the almshouse — structures dedicated to the care of the needy as such, founded on the conviction that the sufferer had a sacred claim. The witness of history here is remarkably vivid. When the emperor Julian, in the fourth century, sought to revive the old pagan religion against the rising tide of Christianity, he found himself frustrated and even shamed by the Christians' care for the poor — including the pagan poor — and urged the pagan priesthood to imitate it, for he saw that this compassion was winning the world. The pagan reviver himself testified that the Christians' mercy toward the weak was a power the old world could not match.

## A World Made Over

It is difficult for us to grasp how thoroughly this revaluation has shaped our moral instincts, precisely because we now take it for granted. That compassion is good; that we ought to be moved by suffering; that the weak and the poor have a claim on us; that a society is to be judged by how it treats its most vulnerable — these convictions feel to us like self-evident moral truths, the natural furniture of any decent conscience. The ancient world reveals that they are nothing of the kind. They are the deposit of a specific moral revolution, one that overturned the settled valuations of one of history's greatest civilizations and enthroned as supreme virtues the very dispositions that antiquity had regarded as weaknesses. When we instinctively side with the sufferer, when our hearts go out to the vulnerable, when we feel that a good person is a compassionate person, we are thinking thoughts that would have puzzled Seneca and appalled a Roman of the old school — thoughts we owe to the transformation this course exists to trace.

The measure of the change is this: in the ancient world, to be moved by pity was to reveal a weakness of soul; in the world Christianity made, to be *un*moved by suffering became the mark of a hardened and defective heart. The same human reflex was read, on one side of the revolution, as a vice, and on the other, as the beginning of virtue. Few reversals in the history of morality have been so complete, or so consequential.

## The Honest Counterpoint

This contrast, powerful as it is, needs careful qualification. First, the Stoic position was more subtle and more humane than a bare summary suggests; the Stoics did advocate genuine beneficence and even developed, in some thinkers, an ideal of humane fellow-feeling and cosmopolitan concern for all rational beings. Seneca could write movingly of treating slaves with kindness, and figures like Epictetus and Musonius Rufus voiced a real tenderness toward human dignity. The rejection of pity was the rejection of a passion, not necessarily a licence for callousness, and the ancient world was not simply devoid of kindness — ordinary human sympathy surely operated in countless lives regardless of what the philosophers taught. Second, the deeper root of the ethic of compassion lies in the Jewish tradition, with its ancient commands to care for the poor and the stranger, upon which Christianity built; a later lesson will give Israel its due.

Third, the modern reader should beware of a smug reversal that congratulates the present at the expense of the past, for our own age has its characteristic failures of compassion, and some thoughtful critics argue that an unbalanced or sentimentalized compassion can itself distort judgment and enfeeble the will — a debate this course will not pretend to settle. And it must be admitted that Christian civilization, for all its enthronement of mercy, was frequently and grievously cruel, honoring the ideal of compassion far more consistently in word than in deed. Yet with all this granted, the central historical point holds firm: the ancient world largely regarded pity as a weakness and produced little organized charity for the needy as such, while the tradition that overtook it enthroned mercy as the supreme virtue and made care for the weak a sacred duty. Having seen how the ancient world valued strength over compassion, we turn to the most tangible expression of that valuation — its treatment of the bodies of the weak, which it held to be, quite literally, disposable.
