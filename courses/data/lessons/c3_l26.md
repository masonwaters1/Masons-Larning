We have spent this unit reconstructing the moral world of antiquity — its hierarchy, its virtues of strength, its amoral gods, its fatalism, its contempt for pity, its disposable bodies, and also its genuine reaches toward the universal and the hopeful. Now let us perform an act of historical imagination that will draw the whole unit together and carry us to the threshold of the next. Let us sit, for a moment, on a Roman bench — let us inhabit the mind of a cultivated Roman of the first century, a man steeped in the values we have examined, and let us hear, as if for the first time, what the Christians were saying. For only by feeling how their message struck such a man — how absurd, how offensive, how contemptible it appeared from within the ancient moral world — can we grasp how genuinely revolutionary it was, and how astonishing it is that it conquered.

## A God on a Cross

Begin with the central claim, the one that would have struck our Roman as simply insane.[^1] The Christians worshipped, as a god, a man who had been crucified. We examined crucifixion in an earlier lesson: it was the punishment reserved for slaves, rebels, and the lowest of criminals, a torture designed for maximum degradation, so shameful that polite Roman society scarcely named it. To be crucified was to be unmade, stripped of all dignity, displayed as refuse. And these people took such a man — a provincial nobody executed in the most contemptible manner the empire knew — and proclaimed him not merely innocent, not merely a martyr, but *God*, the divine lord of the universe. To our Roman, this was not a bold religious claim; it was an obscenity, a category error so grotesque as to defy belief. The apostle Paul, who knew his audience, put the matter with perfect accuracy when he acknowledged that the crucified Christ was, to the Greeks, sheer foolishness — a stumbling block, an absurdity.

We are not left to imagine the Roman reaction; a piece of graffiti survives that captures it exactly. Scratched into a wall, it depicts a man raising his hand toward a crucified figure with the head of a donkey, and bears the mocking caption that a certain Alexamenos "worships his god." Here is the ancient world's verdict on the Christian claim, preserved in a sneer: to venerate a crucified figure as divine was fit only for ridicule, the mark of a fool or a slave. Everything in our Roman's moral formation — his reverence for strength, his contempt for the degraded, his sense of what divinity and greatness required — recoiled from the notion that God might be found on a cross. The most important claim Christianity made was, to the ancient world, its most ridiculous.

## A Religion of the Weak

Press further into what the Christians taught, and the offense only deepens. Their God, they said, cared especially for the weak, the poor, the sick, the enslaved, the least — sided with them, identified with them, and would judge the mighty by how they had treated them. Their moral heroes were not the strong and the glorious but the humble and the meek; they praised poverty of spirit, mercy, lowliness, the turning of the other cheek — the very dispositions our Roman had been raised to regard as weaknesses fit only for slaves and women. They exalted a virtue they called humility, which in the Roman moral vocabulary was scarcely distinguishable from the abjectness of the defeated. They taught that one should love one's enemies and forgive those who wronged one, when every honorable Roman knew that a real man defended his honor and repaid injury. To our Roman, this was not merely strange; it was a systematic inversion of everything virtue meant, a celebration of weakness masquerading as morality, an ethic that would, if taken seriously, unman the strong and dissolve the natural order of superior over inferior.

And they did take it seriously, which made it worse. These Christians actually welcomed slaves and women into their assemblies as full members, even as leaders; they seated the lowborn beside the wellborn as equals; they practiced charity toward strangers and the destitute, people who could offer them nothing in return and stood outside every proper network of reciprocal obligation. They refused to expose their unwanted infants and condemned the practice in others; some of them turned away from the games, unwilling to watch the spectacle that every normal Roman enjoyed. To our cultivated observer, all of this was not admirable but bizarre and faintly subversive — a willful overturning of the settled distinctions on which civilized life depended, an antisocial eccentricity that mistook the low for the high and the weak for the worthy.

## Atheists and Subversives

There was, too, a graver charge, and it explains why our Roman might regard the Christians not merely as fools but as a danger to be suppressed. They refused to honor the gods of the city and the divine spirit of the emperor. In the ancient world, as we have seen, religion was not primarily about private belief but about the ritual maintenance of right relations with the powers that protected the community; the proper performance of the civic cult secured the favor of the gods and the safety of the state, and to neglect it was to endanger everyone. The Christians' stubborn refusal to sacrifice — their insistence on their one exclusive God and their contemptuous dismissal of the others as no gods at all — struck our Roman not as a private religious preference, which Rome would have tolerated readily enough, but as *atheism*: a reckless impiety that threatened to provoke the anger of the neglected gods and bring disaster upon the whole community. This is why the Christians were persecuted — not for holding odd beliefs, for the tolerant empire cared little about that, but for their antisocial refusal of the civic religion that bound society together and kept it safe.

Add to this the sheer implausibility of what they claimed about the future. They preached that their crucified man had risen bodily from the dead, and that all the dead would one day rise — a notion our Roman found not just incredible but distasteful, for educated opinion held the body to be a thing to be shed at death, not raised. When Paul spoke of resurrection to the philosophers of Athens, we are told, they laughed and dismissed him. And the Christians preached a whole strange history moving toward a redemptive consummation, a hope of eternal life in a renewed world — the linear, hopeful time we examined earlier, so foreign to a mind formed on fate, fortune, and the turning wheel. From every angle, the Christian message affronted the ancient world: absurd in its crucified God, contemptible in its exaltation of the weak, subversive in its equality and its charity, impious in its exclusive worship, and incredible in its bodily resurrection and its hope.

## The Scandal That Conquered

And here is the astonishing thing, the fact that should leave us genuinely amazed: this message, which affronted the ancient world at every point, *conquered* it. The faith that our cultivated Roman dismissed as a superstition fit for slaves and fools spread through the empire, gathered in the lowly and eventually the mighty, outlasted the persecutions, and within a few centuries had remade the moral imagination of the very civilization that had mocked it. The values that made Christianity contemptible to our Roman — its crucified God, its siding with the weak, its insistence on the equal worth of every person, its enthronement of humility and mercy — became, over the long centuries, the moral foundations of the West, so deeply assumed that they now seem to us simply obvious, the natural furniture of any decent conscience. The scandal became the common sense.

This is why the act of sitting on a Roman bench is so valuable. It restores to us a sense of how strange, how offensive, and how improbable the Christian revolution really was — how far it ran against the grain of the ancient world, how little it resembled a plausible improvement that any reasonable person would naturally accept. We who inherit its results cannot easily feel its scandal; we have to reconstruct it by an effort of imagination. But unless we feel it, we cannot understand either the magnitude of the transformation or the genuine mystery of its success. The crucified God, whom the ancient world found most contemptible, is where the following unit begins — with the scandal of the Cross, and with the question of how the most degrading symbol the ancient world possessed became the sign under which that world was overthrown.

## The Honest Counterpoint

This imaginative reconstruction is a device, and like all such devices it simplifies. Not every Roman thought identically; the empire contained a vast range of temperaments and views, and our composite "cultivated Roman" is a useful fiction, not a portrait of everyone. More importantly, the emphasis on scandal and revulsion, though accurate, tells only half the story, for Christianity did not merely offend the ancient world — it also *attracted* it, and its attraction is what explains its growth. The very things that scandalized our imagined Roman drew others powerfully: the care for the poor and the sick that no pagan institution matched, the community that welcomed the lonely and the outcast, the hope of eternal life that answered the ancient dread of death, the moral seriousness that spoke to those weary of the cold civic cult, the dignity conferred on the humble. The contempt and the attraction coexisted, often in the same society and perhaps the same heart, and Christianity conquered not by overcoming pure hostility but by answering hungers the ancient world already felt — hungers this unit has traced in the mysteries and the universalisms as much as in the shadow of the cheerless grave.

We should also beware the distortion of hindsight, which makes the triumph of Christianity look inevitable when it was anything but; for centuries it remained a persecuted minority whose eventual success was in no way guaranteed, and to read that success backward as destiny is to misunderstand the contingency of history. And a fair-minded reader will note that this course frames the Christian revolution as a moral advance, a framing that is itself a value judgment; from other vantage points — Nietzsche's above all, whom a later lesson will confront — the very inversion celebrated here appears as a catastrophe, the triumph of a slave morality over the nobility of the strong. The scandal was real; so was the attraction; and whether the revolution was a liberation or a fall is a question this course poses honestly and will return to. With the ancient world's scandalized eyes now our own, we turn to reckon with a modern counter-story — the myth of a pagan golden age that Christianity is said to have destroyed.
