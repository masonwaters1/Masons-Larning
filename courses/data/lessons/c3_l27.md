Having spent this unit reconstructing the moral world of antiquity, we must now confront a powerful counter-narrative that runs in exactly the opposite direction — a story so influential that it shapes the assumptions of many educated people to this day. It holds that classical antiquity was a golden age of the human spirit: rational, free, humane, beautiful, and life-affirming — and that Christianity, when it came, extinguished this light, plunging the West into centuries of superstition, intolerance, and gloom, the so-called Dark Ages. On this telling, the Christian revolution was not an advance but a catastrophe, the smothering of a noble civilization by a life-denying creed, and the recovery of the modern world began only when the Renaissance and the Enlightenment threw off the Christian pall and returned to the wellsprings of classical wisdom. This is the myth of the pagan golden age, and it is important to examine it directly — both to grant what is true in it and to see clearly where it goes wrong.

## The Making of a Myth

The myth has a long and distinguished pedigree.[^1] It drew strength from the Renaissance rediscovery of classical learning, from the Enlightenment's often bitter hostility to organized Christianity, and above all from the great eighteenth-century historian Edward Gibbon, whose monumental account of Rome's decline and fall laid a substantial share of the blame at Christianity's door, portraying the new faith as a solvent that dissolved the martial vigor and civic virtue of the ancient world. In the nineteenth century, Friedrich Nietzsche gave the myth its most powerful philosophical form, contrasting the noble, life-affirming, aristocratic values of pagan antiquity with the resentful, life-denying "slave morality" of Christianity, and lamenting the triumph of the latter as a disaster for the human spirit. And the myth persists today in various forms — in the secular assumption that pre-Christian antiquity was simply more enlightened and free, in the romantic admiration of classical "virtue" against modern softness, and in certain contemporary movements that explicitly celebrate a revival of pagan strength, hierarchy, and vitality against what they see as the enfeebling legacy of Christian compassion.

The myth is not pure invention; it draws on real materials. Classical antiquity did achieve genuine greatness — in philosophy, art, literature, law, and the beginnings of science — and the Renaissance revival of that inheritance was a real good, as the next lesson will insist at length. Christianity did, at various times and places, suppress inquiry, persecute dissent, and cast suspicion on earthly joys, and these failures were real. The myth of the golden age takes these genuine facts and weaves them into a story of light extinguished by darkness. The trouble is not that its raw materials are false but that the story it builds from them is a profound distortion, and seeing why is deeply instructive.

## The Values We Forget to Notice

The deepest flaw in the myth is that it praises the ancient world for possessing values that the ancient world did not, in fact, possess — values that are, this course has argued, the very inheritance of the Christian revolution the myth condemns. When admirers of classical antiquity celebrate it as humane, they are projecting backward a conception of humanity — the equal dignity of every person, the wrongness of cruelty to the weak, the claim of the suffering upon our compassion — that the ancient world, as this whole unit has shown, did not share. The "humane" classical civilization was built upon mass slavery, the routine exposure of unwanted infants, the slaughter of human beings as public entertainment in the arena, the torture of the powerless, and a settled contempt for the weak and the defeated. To admire antiquity for its humanity is to admire it for something it largely lacked, and to overlook the very features of it that a modern conscience, formed by Christian values, finds most appalling.

The same confusion runs through the myth's praise of ancient "freedom" and "reason." The freedom of the classical city was the freedom of a small class of free men resting on the unfreedom of the many; the reason so admired coexisted, in the greatest philosophers, with the rational justification of slavery and the subordination of women. And when the myth's proponents denounce Christianity in the name of tolerance, compassion, human dignity, and the equal worth of persons, they are wielding, without noticing it, the very weapons that Christianity forged. This is the sharp and central point that the historian framing this course drives home: even the fiercest critics of Christianity tend to attack it using values that are themselves a Christian inheritance — condemning the faith's historical cruelties by a standard of universal human dignity that the faith itself introduced into the world. The myth of the pagan golden age is, in this sense, self-refuting: it praises antiquity for Christian virtues and condemns Christianity by Christian standards.

## The Dark Ages That Weren't

The myth's other great pillar — that Christianity extinguished classical civilization and plunged the West into a long night of ignorance — has been steadily dismantled by historians, to the point where serious scholarship no longer speaks of the "Dark Ages" without heavy qualification. The fall of Rome had many causes, of which Christianity was at most a minor one, and the notion that the Church destroyed classical learning inverts a good deal of the truth: it was in significant part the monasteries and the Church that preserved, copied, and transmitted the texts of classical antiquity through the centuries when the political order of the West collapsed, and that later fostered the universities in which learning was institutionalized. The medieval period, far from being a pit of superstition, produced formidable intellectual achievements, recovered and built upon Aristotle, and laid foundations — as this course's sixth unit argues at length — for the eventual rise of modern science. The tidy story of classical light, Christian darkness, and Renaissance dawn is a piece of Enlightenment polemic, not sober history, and it does a serious injustice to a millennium of genuine cultural and intellectual life.

There is a further, revealing test of the myth. Those few who admire antiquity honestly — who embrace not a sanitized, projected classical world but its actual values, its hierarchy, its exaltation of strength, its contempt for the weak, its disposable bodies — reveal, precisely by their honesty, what a genuine return to paganism would mean. The contemporary movements that explicitly reject Christian compassion in favor of pagan vitality and strength are, at least, consistent; they want the real thing. And the real thing is a world most admirers of the "golden age" would recoil from in horror, for it is a world without the equal dignity, the universal compassion, and the sacredness of every human life that they themselves take for granted. The myth survives only by not being taken to its logical conclusion; those who take it there show us the cost.

## The Uses of the Myth

Why, then, does the myth persist, if it is so distorted? Partly because it serves real purposes. For the Enlightenment, it was a weapon against the power of the Church; for later secularism, it offers a comforting genealogy in which modern liberal values descend from noble pagan reason rather than from the Christianity that secular thought has rejected; for contemporary critics of a "therapeutic" or "victimhood" culture, the honor-world of antiquity offers an image of strength and self-reliance against which to measure modern softness. The myth flatters the modern self-image by allowing it to claim the achievements of the classical world while disowning the Christian tradition, to enjoy the fruits of the moral revolution while attributing them to a different and more congenial source. It is, in this sense, a genealogical evasion — a way of holding Christian values while denying their origin.

To dispel the myth is not to deny the greatness of antiquity, which the next and final lesson of this unit will honor fully and gladly. It is only to insist on historical accuracy: that the ancient world was not a golden age of the values we cherish, that Christianity did not extinguish a humane civilization, and that the modern conscience which recoils from ancient cruelty is itself a product of the revolution the myth condemns. The classical world was magnificent in many ways — but not in the ways the myth claims, and not for the reasons the myth supposes. To see it clearly, in both its grandeur and its harshness, is worth more than any comforting story of light and darkness.

## The Honest Counterpoint

This lesson has been sharply critical of the golden-age myth, and fairness now requires turning the criticism back upon itself, for the anti-myth can overcorrect into a Christian triumphalism just as distorting as the story it refutes. Gibbon and the Enlightenment critics were not simply wrong; Christian intolerance, the persecution of heretics, the suppression of certain lines of inquiry, and a real strain of hostility to earthly life and joy were genuine features of Christian history, and a mature account must grant them rather than explain them away. The claim that runs through this whole course — that the values of the modern West descend chiefly from Christianity — is a serious and powerful thesis, but it is a thesis, contested by able scholars, not a settled fact; the sources of modern liberalism, equality, and human rights are plausibly located by others in Stoicism, in the Enlightenment's own innovations, in the rise of commerce and law, or in some tangled combination, and the genealogical argument can be pressed too hard.

We should also be wary of the rhetorical move by which every good value is claimed as "really Christian," a move that can become unfalsifiable and imperialistic, absorbing all human moral achievement into a single tradition and leaving other sources — Greek, Roman, secular, or belonging to entirely different civilizations — no independent standing. The truth is surely that the modern moral world has multiple headwaters, of which the Christian is arguably the deepest but not the only one. The myth of the pagan golden age deserves the criticism this lesson has given it; but the corrective is not a triumphalist counter-myth in which Christianity is the sole author of everything good, but a clear-eyed recognition of a great and harsh antiquity, a genuine Christian revolution, and a modern world indebted to both, along with much else besides. Having dismantled the myth that denigrates the ancient world, we turn, in fairness, to honor what that world genuinely achieved.
