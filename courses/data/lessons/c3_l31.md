If the cross was, as the previous lessons have shown, the ancient world's supreme symbol of degradation — the death of slaves, the mark of maximal shame, the sign of a man accursed — then the earliest Christians faced a problem of the first magnitude. Their movement rested on the claim that a crucified man was the Lord of the universe, and this claim was, on its face, an embarrassment so acute that it threatened to strangle the movement in its cradle. How could the earliest followers of Jesus possibly persuade anyone that their crucified teacher was the Messiah, the Son of God, when everything the ancient world knew declared that a crucified man was the opposite of everything divine and honorable? The story of how the first Christians confronted this embarrassment — and what they did with it — is one of the most remarkable in the history of religion, and it turns on a decision that defied every rule of persuasion.

## A Fact Too Awkward to Invent

Begin with a point that historians of early Christianity have long recognized, one that bears on the credibility of the whole account.[^1] The crucifixion of Jesus is among the most securely established facts about him, and one of the reasons is precisely its embarrassment. Scholars speak of a "criterion of embarrassment" — the principle that details which were awkward, shameful, or counterproductive for a movement are unlikely to have been invented by that movement, and are therefore probably historical. And no detail was more embarrassing to the early Christians than the manner of their founder's death. A movement fabricating a messiah from scratch, seeking to win adherents in the ancient world, would never have invented a crucified one; it would have imagined a triumphant deliverer, a glorious king, a figure of power and honor. To claim a crucified Messiah was to saddle oneself with the least persuasive possible story, and the fact that the early Christians nonetheless proclaimed it strongly suggests that they did so because it was, inconveniently, what had actually happened. Even a hostile Roman source, the historian Tacitus, matter-of-factly records that the founder of the movement had been executed under Pontius Pilate — the awkward fact confirmed from outside.

The embarrassment was not an abstraction; it was a lived and constant obstacle. The cross was, as the apostle Paul acknowledged, a stumbling block to Jews, for whom a crucified messiah was a contradiction and a scandal, and foolishness to Greeks, for whom the worship of a crucified criminal was an absurdity fit only for mockery. We have already met, in the previous unit, the scrawled graffito that captured the pagan verdict — a crude drawing ridiculing a Christian for worshipping a crucified figure with the head of a donkey. This was the everyday reality the early Christians faced: their central proclamation was, to their neighbors, either blasphemous or laughable, and the cross that stood at the heart of their message was a perpetual invitation to contempt.

## The Temptation to Look Away

Faced with so acute an embarrassment, a movement might be expected to minimize it — to soften the scandal, to explain it away, to shift attention from the shameful cross toward more palatable claims. And there were, in the early centuries, tendencies that did exactly this. Among the various currents that scholars group loosely under the label of Gnosticism, some held a view known as docetism, from the Greek word meaning "to seem." The docetists taught that Christ had not really suffered and died at all, but had only *appeared* to do so — that the divine, being beyond suffering and degradation, could not truly have undergone the horror of the cross, and that the crucifixion was therefore a kind of illusion, a phantom suffering that left the divine untouched. The appeal of this view is easy to understand: it relieved the embarrassment at a stroke, preserving the dignity of the divine by denying that God had really descended into the shame and agony of crucifixion. It was, among other things, a way of looking away from the offense of the cross.

Mainstream Christianity, however, rejected this escape decisively and even fiercely. The emerging orthodox tradition insisted, against the docetists, that Christ had really suffered and really died — that the crucifixion was no illusion, that God had genuinely entered into the depths of human agony and degradation, flesh and blood and nails and death. This insistence would harden into doctrine, and the reality of Christ's suffering and death became a non-negotiable of Christian faith. The significance of this choice is easy to miss but immense: at the very point where the movement's greatest embarrassment lay, and where an easy exit was available, Christianity refused the exit and clung to the scandal. It would not purchase respectability by denying that its God had suffered the slave's death. The reality of the cross, in all its horror, was retained.

## The Boast in the Cross

But the early Christians did something far more astonishing than merely retaining the embarrassing fact. They did not simply accept the cross as an unfortunate necessity to be acknowledged and then hurried past; they placed it at the very center of everything, embraced it, proclaimed it, and gloried in it. Paul, the movement's greatest theologian, declared that he had resolved to know nothing except Christ crucified — that the cross, the very source of the embarrassment, was the heart of his message. He announced that he would boast in nothing except the cross of Christ. The Christians took their single greatest liability, the fact most calculated to repel Jew and Greek alike, and made it their central symbol, their boast, the emblem they would eventually raise above their gathering places and inscribe upon their bodies. They turned the instrument of their shame into the sign of their glory.

This is, when one considers it, a staggering reversal of every principle of persuasion and self-presentation. Movements seeking to grow do not ordinarily take their most embarrassing feature and thrust it into the foreground; they conceal their weaknesses and advertise their strengths. The early Christians did the opposite: they seized upon the one thing most likely to provoke contempt and ridicule, and made it the center of their proclamation. To preach Christ crucified was to lead with the scandal, to force every hearer to confront, at the very threshold, the offense that the movement might have been expected to hide. That they did this, and that the movement not only survived but conquered, is among the great puzzles and wonders of religious history.

## Why the Embarrassment Became the Boast

Why did they do it? The answer the early Christians themselves gave was that they had become convinced — by the resurrection, they claimed, by their conviction that the crucified man had been raised and vindicated by God — that the shameful cross was not, in truth, a defeat at all, but the site of God's supreme victory and act of love. What the world saw as degradation, they had come to see as glory; what looked like the ultimate proof that Jesus could not be divine, they had come to understand as the ultimate revelation of who God is. The embarrassment, in other words, was transformed by a reinterpretation so radical that it turned the meaning of the cross inside out: the place of maximal shame became the place of maximal glory, the sign of defeat became the sign of triumph, the instrument of degradation became the throne of God. The scandal was not denied but transvalued — and it is that transvaluation, worked out above all in the writings of Paul, that constitutes the theological revolution at the heart of this unit.

The first Christians, then, faced their embarrassment not by fleeing it, minimizing it, or explaining it away, but by embracing it so completely that it ceased to be an embarrassment and became the center of a new vision of God, power, and worth. This was the decisive move, the hinge on which everything turned. To understand what they made of the cross — how the death of a slave became the wisdom and power of God, and how this reinterpretation overturned the values of the ancient world — we turn now to the mind of the man who did more than any other to forge it.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Several qualifications keep this account honest. First, the criterion of embarrassment, though a useful historical tool, is not infallible, and scholars rightly caution against leaning on it too heavily; embarrassing details can sometimes serve unexpected rhetorical or theological functions, and the argument that "no one would invent this" has real but limited force. The crucifixion's historicity rests on more than embarrassment alone, but the criterion should be wielded with care rather than as a knockdown proof. Second, the currents labeled Gnostic and docetic were more varied, sophisticated, and internally motivated than a simple picture of "fleeing embarrassment" conveys; docetism arose from serious convictions about the nature of the divine and matter, not merely from a wish to escape scandal, and to reduce it to evasion is unfair to its complexity.

Third, and most importantly, the claim that the resurrection explains the transformation of embarrassment into boast is a report of what the early Christians believed, not a historical demonstration that the resurrection occurred; the historicity of the resurrection is a distinct and far more contested question that this course, concerned with the shape and consequences of the Christian revolution rather than the adjudication of its supernatural claims, does not attempt to settle. What can be established historically is that the cross was a genuine and severe embarrassment, that its centrality was profoundly counterintuitive, and that the early movement, against every rule of persuasion, made its scandal its boast. The reinterpretation that accomplished this — whatever its ultimate ground — is a historical fact of the first importance, and its author is our next subject.
