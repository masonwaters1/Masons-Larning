Having traced the scandal of the cross and the inversion it produced, we turn now to consequences — to what this strange reversal actually did in the world. And there is no domain where its effect was more profound, or more enduring, than in the human relationship to power. Before Christianity, power was, on the whole, simply admirable: the strong ruled by right, the powerful were the favored, and might, if not quite making right, at least commanded the respect and deference that were its due. The cross introduced into this world a permanent and unsettling ambivalence about power — a suspicion, a critique, a haunting — that would never afterward be fully dispelled. For at the center of the new faith stood not the emperor who wielded power but the victim whom power had destroyed, and this simple fact would trouble the powerful of the West for two thousand years.

## God on the Wrong Side of Power

Consider the sheer strangeness of where the cross placed God.[^1] In the ancient world, the divine belonged with the powerful; the gods were the mightiest of beings, and earthly power, especially the power of the emperor, participated in and reflected the divine order. To find God, one looked up — to the heights of power, glory, and dominion. But the cross placed God at the opposite pole: not with the emperor who ordered the execution, but with the criminal who was executed; not with the soldiers who drove the nails, but with the victim who received them; not with power, but with power's casualty. God was discovered on the wrong side of power — among its victims rather than its wielders, identified with the crucified rather than the crucifier.

This relocation of the divine had consequences that unfolded across centuries. If God was to be found with the victim of power rather than its possessor, then power could no longer be simply celebrated and revered; it stood, now, under suspicion. The powerful could no longer assume that their power was self-justifying, a sign of divine favor and natural right; they now faced, embedded at the very center of their civilization's faith, the memory of a God who had been power's victim, and who had thereby declared himself on the side of all power's victims. The cross planted, in the heart of the West, a permanent question mark over power — a suspicion that the mighty might be in the wrong and their victims in the right, that the executed might be holier than the executioner, that power itself required justification before a standard that sided with the weak.

## The Ruler as Servant

From this flowed a transformation in the very ideal of rulership. In the ancient world, as we have seen, the ruler was the dominator, the great man whose greatness consisted in his power to command, to conquer, and to impose his will. But the teaching that flowed from the cross reversed this: whoever wished to be great, Jesus had taught, must become the servant of all, and whoever wished to be first must become the slave of all — for the Son of Man himself had come not to be served but to serve. The model of true greatness became not the master but the servant, not the one who is served but the one who serves, not the dominator but the one who gives himself for others. The ruler was now to understand himself, at least in principle, as a servant of those he ruled, accountable to a standard that measured greatness by service rather than dominion.

This was, of course, an ideal honored far more in the breach than in the observance, and we will come to Christianity's massive complicity with worldly power shortly. But the ideal itself was revolutionary, and its mere existence changed the moral situation of the powerful. The king who wielded absolute power now had to reckon, however hypocritically, with a faith that told him true greatness lay in service and that his Lord had been a crucified servant; the tyrant could now be named as a tyrant, judged against a standard that exalted the servant and condemned the dominator. The idea that power exists to serve, that the ruler is accountable to those he rules, that domination for its own sake is not glory but sin — this idea, however imperfectly realized, entered the moral vocabulary of the West through the cross, and it would prove to have a very long life.

## The Subversive Memory

Most profound of all was what we might call the subversive memory that the cross lodged permanently at the heart of Western civilization. The central story of the faith was the story of an innocent man, condemned by the authorities of his day — by the religious establishment and the imperial state together — and executed as a criminal, who turned out to be God. This meant that the highest authorities, religious and political, had committed the supreme crime; that the state's judgment had been not merely mistaken but catastrophically wrong; that the executed criminal had been in the right and his powerful judges in the wrong. And this story was told and retold, enacted in ritual, depicted in art, proclaimed from every altar, week after week, century after century, at the very center of the civilization's life.

The effect of this endlessly repeated memory was to relativize all earthly power under a higher judgment. If the mightiest authorities of the ancient world had condemned God himself as a criminal, then no earthly authority could claim to be beyond question; the state could be wrong, the establishment could be wrong, the powerful could be wrong, and the condemned victim might be the one in the right. The cross was a permanent reminder that power does not equal justice, that the judgment of the mighty is not final, that the victim of the state may be vindicated by God against the state. This is why, as the historian who frames this course observes, the cross keeps detonating throughout Western history — why every movement that has risen to defend the oppressed against the powerful, to vindicate the victim against the authorities, to insist that the executed or the enslaved or the despised might be in the right, has drawn, knowingly or not, on the subversive memory the cross implanted. The powerful of the West have never been entirely secure, because at the center of their own civilization stands the memory of a God whom power destroyed and who stands with all power's victims.

## The Long Consequences

The long-term consequences of this were immense, and they run through the whole subsequent history of the West. The martyrs who defied the empire, choosing death rather than submission to a power they judged unjust, embodied the conviction that earthly power could be wrong and must yield to a higher authority. The church, as it developed, became a rival center of authority that could, at least in principle, judge and rebuke kings, insisting that the ruler too stood under God's law. And in the long run, the conviction that power must be justified, that it must serve rather than merely dominate, that the ruled possess a dignity that limits what may be done to them, and that the victim of power may be in the right — these convictions, planted by the cross, would grow into the characteristic political achievements of the West: the accountability of rulers, the limitation of power, the rights of the ruled, the moral standing of the oppressed against their oppressors. The revolutions and reforms that repeatedly sided with the weak against the strong were, in this sense, the cross continuing to do its work.

None of this was inevitable or automatic, and much of it took many centuries and bitter struggle to unfold. But the seed was planted at the beginning, in the scandal of a God whom power had crucified: the permanent suspicion of power, the ideal of the ruler as servant, the subversive memory that the victim may be in the right and the mighty in the wrong. The cross did not abolish power, but it forever complicated the West's relationship to it, placing at the center of the civilization a standing critique of domination that no wielder of power could finally silence.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The account just given is, however, shadowed by a massive and painful irony that honesty cannot evade: the very faith that planted a permanent critique of power at the heart of the West also became, for most of its history, one of the greatest powers the world has ever known, and was again and again complicit in the domination it supposedly condemned. As the next lesson will explore, Christianity was transformed within a few centuries from a persecuted movement of the crucified into the religion of the Roman Empire, and thereafter it wielded power on a colossal scale — blessing kings, waging wars, launching crusades, conducting inquisitions, persecuting heretics and dissenters, and allying itself with the mighty against the weak. The church that preached the crucified servant became a fabulously wealthy and powerful institution; the faith that told rulers to serve produced popes and prelates who ruled like princes. The critique of power that this lesson has celebrated was, throughout the Christian centuries, contradicted and betrayed by the actual conduct of Christian power, and any honest account must hold this contradiction squarely in view.

Two further qualifications are needed. First, the critique of tyranny and the ideal of just rule were not unknown before or outside Christianity; the classical world debated the difference between the king and the tyrant, celebrated tyrannicide, and developed ideals of the ruler's duty, while the Hebrew prophets had already thundered against unjust kings. The cross radicalized and universalized a suspicion of power that had older roots. Second, the claim that the cross "keeps detonating" against power, while illuminating, can be pressed too far into a triumphalist story in which every good political development is credited to Christianity; the accountability of rulers and the rights of the ruled have complex causes, including secular and classical ones, and the genealogy should be held with appropriate humility. Yet when all this is granted, the central point endures: by placing God with the victim of power rather than its wielder, the cross introduced into Western civilization a permanent and destabilizing critique of power — one that Christian power itself could never escape, and that judged the church's own betrayals as surely as it judged the tyrannies of the world. That deepest irony, of a faith of the crucified becoming the faith of the throne, is the subject to which we now turn.
