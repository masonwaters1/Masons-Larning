The previous lesson ended on an irony, and this lesson is devoted to the single event in which that irony was born. For three centuries, Christianity had been the faith of the crucified — a persecuted movement gathered around the memory of a God whom Roman power had executed, spreading among slaves and the marginal, its adherents periodically hunted and killed by the imperial state. And then, in the early fourth century, something happened that reversed the entire situation and set a paradox at the heart of Christian history that has never been resolved. A Roman emperor took the cross as his battle standard, marched under it to victory, and made the faith of the crucified into the faith of the empire. The symbol of the executed slave became the emblem of imperial conquest. It is impossible to overstate the significance, or the strangeness, of this turn.

## In This Sign Conquer

In the year 312, the Roman emperor Constantine faced a rival for control of the empire at the Milvian Bridge outside Rome.[^1] According to the accounts that came down to us — accounts written afterward, and varying in their details — Constantine received, before the battle, a heavenly sign: a vision or a dream in which he was shown the sign of Christ and told that under this sign he would conquer. He ordered the sign marked upon his soldiers' shields, went into battle, and won a decisive victory that made him master of the western empire. Attributing his triumph to the God of the Christians, Constantine became the patron of the once-persecuted faith. The following year, the Edict of Milan extended toleration to Christianity throughout the empire, ending the era of persecution; and over the following decades, Christianity rose from a hunted minority to the favored religion of the Roman state, until, by the end of the fourth century, it had become the empire's official faith.

Pause to consider what had happened. The cross — the instrument by which Rome had tortured and executed the lowest of its subjects, the very apparatus on which Christ himself had been killed by Roman power — had become the standard under which a Roman emperor marched his legions to victory. The sign of the crucified victim had become the emblem of imperial military conquest. The faith that had begun among slaves and the crucified, that had defined itself against the power of Rome, that had cherished the memory of a God whom the empire had killed, was now the faith of the emperor, the establishment, the throne. The persecuted had become the powerful; the religion of the victims had become the religion of the state that had made them victims. Few reversals in all of history are so complete, or so laden with paradox.

## Triumph or Betrayal?

How should we understand this turn? Two readings have contended with each other ever since, and both capture something true. The first reading sees the conversion of the empire as the triumph and vindication of the cross — even as providence. On this view, the crucified God had conquered the world that crucified him; Christ was vindicated before the powers that had condemned him; the faith of the martyrs had outlasted and overcome its persecutors. And the practical fruits were real and immense: with the empire's power now behind it rather than against it, Christianity could begin to reshape society in accordance with its convictions. Over the following centuries, the gladiatorial games would be brought to an end, crucifixion abolished, the exposure of infants outlawed, and the great Christian institutions of charity — hospitals, orphanages, poor relief — established and spread. The alliance with imperial power, on this reading, was what allowed the moral revolution of the cross to move from the margins into the center and begin transforming the world.

The second reading sees the same event as a betrayal, a fall, the corruption and domestication of the faith. On this view, Christianity was co-opted by the very power it had once judged; the subversive movement of the crucified was tamed, absorbed, and turned into an instrument of empire. The cross, the sign of the victim, was inverted back into a sign of the conqueror; the faith of those whom power had killed became the faith of those who wielded power and would soon kill in its name. The church, once poor and persecuted, grew wealthy, powerful, and worldly, entangled with thrones and armies, and would before long be persecuting dissenters as it had once been persecuted. This critique — often called the critique of "Constantinianism" — has been made across the centuries by those who saw in the imperial church a departure from the way of the crucified: by radical reformers and Anabaptists, by the fierce solitary Kierkegaard, and by modern theologians who have argued that the church's fall began the day it took up the sword and the crown. On this reading, the cross on Constantine's battlefield marks not the triumph of the faith but the beginning of its long compromise with the very power it was born to judge.

## The Unresolved Tension

The deepest truth is that both readings capture something real, and that Constantine's turn set at the heart of Christian history a tension that would never be resolved. From that day forward, Christianity would carry within itself two contradictory possibilities, symbolized by the double meaning the cross now bore. The cross was the sign of the suffering victim, the crucified God who stood with the weak against the powerful — and it was also, now, the sign of imperial and ecclesiastical power, raised over armies and thrones, carried into battle and conquest. Christianity would forever be pulled between these two poles: between the crucified God who sides with the lowly and the triumphant church that wields the sword; between the memory of the victim and the reality of power. The Crusades would be waged under the cross; conquests would be blessed by it; inquisitions would be conducted in its name — and yet the same cross would perpetually generate, from within, the critique of that very power, the return of its original meaning to judge the church's own betrayals.

This last point is crucial and easy to miss. Even as Christianity became a great power, the memory of the crucified God remained embedded at its very center, an ineradicable seed of self-critique. The church that wielded power was always, whether it wished to be or not, haunted and judged by the crucified victim at the heart of its own faith — for how could an institution built around a God whom power had killed ever be entirely comfortable in the exercise of power? This is why the critique of Christian power so often arose from within Christianity itself, why reformers and prophets and dissenters repeatedly recalled the church to the way of the crucified, why the cross's original meaning kept breaking back through the imperial overlay. The tension Constantine introduced was permanent, but it was a living tension, and the crucified God never ceased to trouble the triumphant church that claimed him.

## The Weight of the Turn

The conversion of Constantine, then, was neither simply a triumph nor simply a betrayal but the beginning of a permanent and productive contradiction — the moment when the faith of the crucified became also the faith of the powerful, and had thereafter to live with both identities at once. It made possible the transformation of society along Christian lines, the ending of the games and the exposure of infants and the spread of charitable institutions; and it made possible, at the same time, the church's long entanglement with domination, wealth, and violence. It placed the cross simultaneously in the hand of the victim and the hand of the conqueror, and left Christianity to work out, across two thousand years, which it would finally be. That working-out is much of the subsequent history this course will trace. For now, we mark the paradox at its birth: the sign of the slave's execution, raised in triumph over a Roman army, and the faith of the crucified seated, for good and ill, upon the throne of the world.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Several qualifications are needed to keep this account from hardening into either a triumphalist or a declinist myth. First, the story of Constantine's vision is itself uncertain and probably embellished; the accounts we possess were written after the fact, differ in their details, and bear the marks of later theological interpretation, so the vivid image of the sign in the sky should be treated as tradition rather than secure history. Constantine's own religious convictions and motives are debated by historians to this day — whether his embrace of Christianity was sincere faith, shrewd political calculation, or some genuine mixture of both — and he retained pagan elements and delayed his own baptism until near death. The turn was messier and more gradual than the dramatic legend suggests.

Second, the "fall of the church" narrative, powerful as it is, is itself contested and can be sentimentalized. It tends to imagine a pure, pristine pre-Constantinian church that the alliance with power corrupted; but the earlier church had its own conflicts, compromises, and failings, and was not the flawless community of the declinist imagination. Moreover, the alliance with power, whatever its costs, did enable genuine goods that the powerless church could never have achieved — the legal reforms, the charitable institutions, the reshaping of a whole civilization — so a simple story of corruption fails to capture the real ambivalence. Third, the double meaning of the cross, as sign of both victim and conqueror, should not be resolved too quickly in either direction; the honest recognition is that Christianity became genuinely both, and that its subsequent history is the story of the unresolved struggle between them. With that paradox established, we turn from power to beauty — to the astonishing fact that this civilization made its supreme art out of the image of a tortured victim.
