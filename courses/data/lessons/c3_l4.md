In our first lesson we noted, in passing, that a remarkably mixed crowd of scholars has converged on this course's central thesis — believers and unbelievers, historians and philosophers and sociologists, arriving from very different starting points at the same conclusion. This lesson slows down and looks hard at three of them, because the *way* they converge is itself the strongest argument in the thesis's favor. Three authors, working in three different disciplines, entering through three different doors, with three different temperaments and degrees of religious sympathy — and all three walk into the same room. Understanding why that pattern is so persuasive, and where it can still mislead, is the work of this lesson.

## Why Convergence Is Evidence

First, the principle, because it governs everything that follows. When several independent lines of inquiry, using different methods and beginning from different assumptions, arrive at the same conclusion, that conclusion is far more credible than it would be on the strength of any single argument. This is **triangulation**, and it is how knowledge is secured across the sciences and the courtroom alike. A single witness can be mistaken or biased; when three unrelated witnesses who have never spoken to one another describe the same event, the odds that they are all wrong in the same way drop sharply. The same holds for scholarship. If only sympathetic historians argued that Christianity made the modern moral world, we might suspect special pleading. The interesting situation — the one we are in — is that the argument is made, in different forms, by people who have no shared motive to make it, using methods that have nothing in common. Let us meet them.

## The First Door: Tom Holland and the Historian's Sweep

We have already met Tom Holland, so we can be brief about his biography and focus on his method. Holland is a popular historian — a teller of grand narratives — and he enters through the door of broad civilizational history. His book *Dominion* sweeps across two and a half thousand years, from ancient Persia and Greece to the present day, to show how thoroughly the moral assumptions of the West were transformed by the Christian revolution and how completely that transformation has been forgotten.[^1]

Holland's distinctive contribution is his eye for *strangeness* and *inversion*. He dwells on how alien the pre-Christian world really was — its casual cruelty, its worship of strength, its contempt for the weak — and then on the sheer audacity of the Christian reversal: a faith whose central symbol was a man dying the death of a tortured slave, and which dared to proclaim that this humiliated victim was God, that the last would be first, that the meek would inherit the earth. To the ancient mind this was not merely false but obscene, a glorification of everything a serious person despised. And yet this inversion of values, Holland argues, slowly conquered the Western imagination so completely that its descendants — including its fiercest secular critics — now take its conclusions for granted as simple human decency. His method is to make the water visible by showing us, vividly, the dry land that came before. His power is the panorama.

## The Second Door: Bart Ehrman and the Skeptic's Rigor

The second author could hardly be more different in temperament, and that is exactly why he matters. Bart Ehrman is one of the most prominent New Testament scholars in the world, a master of the historical-critical method, and — crucially — an agnostic. A former evangelical who lost his faith, he has spent his career applying cold, rigorous scholarship to the origins of Christianity, often reaching conclusions that discomfort believers, and he has no apologetic axe to grind. When Ehrman credits Christianity with anything, it is not because he wishes to.

He enters through the door of meticulous historical scholarship on the early church. In *The Triumph of Christianity*, he asks a hard empirical question: how did a tiny, obscure sect of Jewish followers of an executed teacher become, within a few centuries, the dominant religion of the Roman Empire and then the West?[^2] Part of his answer concerns the ethic the movement carried. Ehrman argues that early Christianity introduced into the ancient world a genuinely novel moral vision — that charity toward the poor and the sick was not optional generosity but a binding duty; that every human being, including the slave, the infant, the destitute, the stranger, possessed real worth; that love was owed even to enemies. The pagan world had nothing quite like this organized, universal ethic of care, and its spread changed the moral sensibility of an entire civilization. And here is the line that lands with special weight precisely because a skeptic is saying it: Ehrman concludes, as a non-believer, that without this transformation the modern Western world would be unrecognizable, and in important moral respects far harsher — that the very values secular people now hold most dear came to them, historically, through the religion many of them have rejected. His contribution is the rigor and the source: an unsentimental historian, following the evidence against his own sympathies.

## The Third Door: Larry Siedentop and the Genealogy of Liberalism

The third author enters through yet another door — not history's sweep, nor the early church, but the genealogy of political ideas. Larry Siedentop is a scholar of the history of Western political thought, an authority on liberalism, and in *Inventing the Individual* he advances the most philosophically startling version of the thesis.[^3] His claim is that the foundations of modern secular liberalism itself — the things we are most sure we invented *against* religion — are in fact Christian creations.

Siedentop traces a long conceptual genealogy. It begins with the Apostle Paul's revolutionary proclamation of moral equality before God — that in Christ there is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female — which planted the radical idea that every soul has equal standing regardless of status, ethnicity, or sex. He follows this idea through the centuries as it was developed, with painstaking technicality, by medieval theologians and above all by the canon lawyers of the church, who built up the concepts of the individual person, of natural rights, of moral equality, and even of a distinct secular sphere. The separation of spiritual and temporal authority, the sovereignty of the individual conscience, the notion of a society of equal individuals rather than fixed ranks and castes — these, Siedentop argues, were not the achievements of the Enlightenment overthrowing Christianity. They were achievements *of* Christianity, slowly worked out over a thousand years, which the Enlightenment then inherited, secularized, and turned against its own source. Secular liberalism, on this reading, is not Christianity's conqueror but its child. His contribution is the precise tracing of concepts through law and theology — the demonstration that even our political categories carry a Christian genealogy.

## One Room, Three Doors

Now stand back and see the shape of it. Three authors. Holland enters through grand civilizational narrative; Ehrman through the historical-critical study of the early church; Siedentop through the genealogy of political and legal concepts. One is a romantic popular historian whose sympathies began with the pagans; one is an agnostic skeptic with no wish to flatter religion; one is a philosopher of liberalism. Their methods share almost nothing. Their starting dispositions point in different directions. And they emphasize different facets of the same object: Holland the broad inversion of moral values, Ehrman the specific ethic of care and the mechanism of its spread, Siedentop the birth of the individual and the political concepts of modernity. Yet all three arrive at one conclusion — that the modern Western moral and political world, including its secular forms, is a Christian creation that has forgotten its own origins. The facets are different; the object is the same. That is what convergence from independent doors looks like, and it is why this thesis has become so difficult for serious people to wave away.

## A Concrete Convergence: The Case of the Poor

The convergence becomes most vivid when all three authors are turned on a single concrete transformation. Take one: the moral status of the poor and the weak. In the classical world, as we have seen, poverty carried no special moral claim; the destitute were unfortunate, sometimes contemptible, and giving was largely a matter of public benefaction by the rich to enhance their own honor, directed at the citizen body rather than at the helpless as such. Watch how each author illuminates the same shift from a different angle.

Holland, through his civilizational lens, frames it as a stunning *inversion* of values — a world that had exalted strength learning instead to see the face of God in the beggar, the leper, and the prisoner, until caring for the lowest became the highest virtue rather than a sign of weakness. Ehrman, through his historical-critical lens, traces the *mechanism*: he shows how the early Christian communities built organized systems of charity — feeding the hungry, burying the destitute, nursing the sick even during plagues when others fled — and how this practical ethic of care, unprecedented in its scope and its sense of obligation, drew converts and reshaped expectations across the empire. Siedentop, through his conceptual lens, reveals the *idea* underneath: the principle of moral equality, the conviction that the pauper's soul is worth precisely as much as the emperor's, which over centuries hardened into the very notion of equal individuals that secular politics now assumes. Same transformation, three beams of light, three different instruments — and a far fuller picture than any one of them could produce alone. This is triangulation doing its work on a single case.

## Three Doors Among Many

It is worth stressing, finally, that these three are not the whole of the convergence but representatives of a much larger one. Other serious scholars, from still other disciplines, have pushed open still other doors onto the same room. The sociologist Rodney Stark applied the cold tools of his discipline to the numerical growth of the early church and concluded that its distinctive ethic of care was central to its success. The classical historian Kyle Harper has documented how Christianity overturned the entrenched sexual norms of the ancient world, transforming assumptions about the body, consent, and the worth of every person. The philosopher Charles Taylor has written the most ambitious account of how the secular age itself is a Christian production. The evolutionary anthropologist Joseph Henrich, approaching from data and cross-cultural psychology, has argued that the peculiar psychology of Western people was shaped by the medieval church's reforms of family and kinship.

The point is not that any of these scholars is beyond criticism — each has able critics, as we will note. The point is the *pattern*: door after door, opened by very different hands using very different keys, all swinging onto the same conclusion. When the convergence is this broad and this methodologically diverse, dismissing it as the special pleading of believers becomes very difficult, since so many of the witnesses are not believers and had no wish to reach this verdict. The three authors of this lesson are simply the clearest individual examples of a convergence that has, over the last two decades, quietly become one of the more striking developments in the humanities.

## What the Convergence Does Not Claim

Because the convergence of these three authors is genuinely persuasive, it is especially important to be precise about what it does and does not establish — and here the disciplined framing of this whole course must be applied to them as firmly as to anyone. The convergence does *not* establish that Christianity is true. Holland, Ehrman, and Siedentop are making a historical and conceptual claim about cultural influence, not a theological claim about the reality of God or the resurrection; one can accept every word of their argument and remain an atheist, as Ehrman himself does. To slide from "Christianity shaped our moral world" to "therefore Christianity is true" is the precise confusion this course has warned against from the start.

Nor does the convergence claim that Christianity is the *sole* source of the modern world. All three authors would acknowledge that Greek reason, Roman law, the Enlightenment, economic transformation, and sheer historical accident also did their part; their claim is the more specific one that a particular and distinctive cluster of moral and political convictions traces to a Christian origin. And it does not claim that these inherited values are *good because* they are Christian — their value, if they have value, stands on its own merits, exactly as their truth would. The genealogy tells us where they came from; it does not certify them.

What the convergence does establish is narrower and sturdier: that the modern Western moral and political imagination, including its secular forms, carries a Christian genealogy that has been largely forgotten, and that this is the considered conclusion of serious scholars approaching from independent directions. That is a substantial claim, well supported, and worth taking seriously — and it is also, precisely, the limit of what three converging authors can prove. Holding the claim at exactly that size, neither inflating it into apologetics nor deflating it into triviality, is what it means to think clearly about the water we swim in.

## The Honest Counterpoint

But triangulation, however powerful, is not proof, and intellectual honesty requires marking its limits squarely. Three independent witnesses are more reliable than one — *unless* they share a common blind spot or a common source of error, in which case their agreement merely multiplies the same mistake. And there are reasons for caution here. All three scholars work within a modern Western academy that is itself partly shaped by the Christian inheritance they describe, which could make the conclusion feel more natural to them than the evidence warrants. The thesis has, moreover, become somewhat fashionable in recent years, and fashions in scholarship can produce a false sense of consensus, with each author drawing confidence from the others rather than from independent confirmation.

It is also essential to remember that this is a live debate, not a closed case. There remains a serious and venerable opposing tradition — the older account, still held by many capable historians, in which modernity represents a genuine *break* from Christianity rather than its fulfillment: a story in which the decisive sources of human rights, equality, and tolerance lie in the Enlightenment's revolt against religious authority, in Greek rationalism recovered, in the hard lessons of religious war that taught Europe to set faith aside. On that view, our three authors are reading the present too generously back into the Christian past, mistaking influence for parentage. Each of our three has able critics within his own field — Holland charged with the overreach of the popularizer, Siedentop with reading later liberalism teleologically into medieval texts, Ehrman's particular synthesis contested by other historians of the period.

So the honest verdict is this: the convergence of three such different scholars through three such different doors is genuinely weighty evidence, and it has shifted the burden of argument in their favor more than many secular readers realize. But it raises the probability of the thesis; it does not settle it. We hold the conclusion as strong and seriously argued, not as proven — which is exactly the posture this course has promised to maintain throughout.

With the diagnostic tools now complete — the strangeness of our values, the channels of inheritance, and the convergence of independent scholarship — we are ready to leave the question of *method* behind and turn to the history itself. In the next unit we go back to the ancient world to watch the transformation actually begin: to see, in detail, how a small and unlikely movement started to remake the moral imagination of the West.
