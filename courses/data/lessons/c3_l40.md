Throughout this unit, a single name has recurred as a warning and a promise — the name of the thinker who understood the Christian revolution as clearly as anyone who ever lived, agreed that it was real and world-historical, and pronounced upon it the opposite verdict from the one this course has so far advanced. It is time to give Friedrich Nietzsche his full hearing. For Nietzsche did not deny the inversion; he affirmed it with ferocious clarity. He saw exactly what the cross had done — the exaltation of the weak, the enthronement of pity, the overturning of the values of strength — and he called it not a liberation but a catastrophe: the greatest disaster in the moral history of humanity, the poisoning of the human spirit by the resentment of the weak. To understand his verdict is to confront the most powerful challenge to everything this unit has argued, and intellectual honesty demands that we confront it at full strength.

## The Two Moralities

Nietzsche's analysis begins with a distinction between two fundamentally opposed systems of value, which he traced to two opposed types of human being.[^1] Originally, he argued, "good" meant simply what the strong, noble, and powerful were and did — the qualities of the master: strength, courage, vitality, pride, the confident affirmation of life and of oneself. This was master morality, the spontaneous self-affirmation of the powerful, for whom "good" meant noble and "bad" meant merely low, weak, and contemptible. It was the morality of the ancient aristocratic world, the world of the Roman virtues examined earlier in this course, in which the strong esteemed themselves and their strength as good, and regarded the weak with a kind of pitying contempt.

But then, Nietzsche argued, came a revolt — the "slave revolt in morality." The weak, the oppressed, the impotent, unable to prevail against the strong by strength, took their revenge in the only realm available to them: the realm of values. Consumed by *ressentiment* — the festering, impotent resentment of those who cannot act, cannot prevail, and cannot discharge their hostility except inwardly — they performed an inversion. They declared the strong, the powerful, the proud, to be *evil*; and they declared themselves — the weak, the humble, the meek, the suffering — to be *good*. Weakness became "virtue," their impotence became "goodness of heart," their inability to take revenge became "forgiveness," their cowardice became "humility," their submission became "obedience to God." The values of the strong were condemned as sin, and the condition of the weak was exalted as holiness. This, Nietzsche held, was the origin of the morality that had conquered the West — and its authors were the priests, and above all, he said, the Jews, and then the Christians who inherited and universalized their revolt.

## God on the Cross

At the center of Nietzsche's account of the slave revolt stood the very symbol at the center of this unit: the cross. Nietzsche saw with perfect clarity what an earlier lesson described as the location of God at the bottom of the hierarchy of worth, and he identified it as the supreme and most dangerous expression of the slave revolt. "God on the cross" was, for Nietzsche, the most seductive and most poisonous formula ever devised — the deification of weakness, suffering, and lowliness, the enthronement of everything the noble spirit had rightly despised. In the crucified God, the values of the weak were not merely asserted but made divine; the slave revolt was given its most powerful and enduring symbol. And Nietzsche identified the apostle Paul, whom an earlier lesson called the great engine of the inversion, as the arch-architect of this catastrophe — the man of ressentiment who had taken the death of Jesus and forged from it the weapon by which the weak would conquer the strong and remake the world in the image of their own weakness. That Nietzsche fixed upon Paul and the cross as his supreme enemies is a striking confirmation of how accurately he perceived the very thing this unit has traced.

Here we must pause to register the remarkable fact that Nietzsche and this course agree almost entirely about the *facts* and disagree totally about their *value*. Both hold that the cross accomplished a genuine and world-historical inversion of values; both hold that it exalted the weak, the humble, and the suffering over the strong, the proud, and the powerful; both hold that this inversion became the moral foundation of the West and persists, secularized, into the modern world. Where this course has called that inversion a moral revolution and a liberation, Nietzsche called it a slave revolt and a poisoning. The disagreement is not about what happened but about whether what happened was good — and that disagreement is as deep as any in the history of thought.

## The Charge of Life-Denial

Why did Nietzsche regard the triumph of Christian morality as a catastrophe? His central charge was that it is *life-denying* — that it turns against strength, health, vitality, beauty, nobility, and the affirmation of life itself, and makes a virtue of weakness, sickness, and self-denial. The morality of pity, in particular, Nietzsche regarded as a poison: by preserving and coddling the weak, the sick, and the botched, and by teaching the strong to feel guilt for their strength, it drags down the healthy toward the level of the sick, spreads weakness through the whole of humanity, and prevents the emergence of the higher, nobler types in which life reaches its fullest expression. Where this course has celebrated the enthronement of compassion, Nietzsche saw a morality of the herd, hostile to excellence, resentful of greatness, dedicated to the leveling of all distinction and the triumph of mediocrity — a morality that would culminate, he warned, in the "last man," the comfortable, contemptible creature who has no aspiration beyond his own small comfort and safety, and who represents the extinction of everything great in humanity.

For Nietzsche, then, the whole trajectory this unit has celebrated appeared as a descent: the exaltation of the weak, the enthronement of pity, the deification of the victim, the leveling of the noble — all of it the revenge of the resentful many against the excellent few, the poisoning of the wellsprings of human greatness, the slow triumph of weakness dressed as virtue. His alternative was the affirmation of life in its fullness, including its strength, its hierarchy, and even its cruelty; the overcoming of the morality of pity; the emergence of higher human beings who would create new values beyond the slave morality that had ruled the West for two thousand years. He is the great dissenter, and his dissent cannot be waved away, for it forces upon us the question this unit has kept open: was the inversion of the cross a moral triumph or a moral disaster? Was the exaltation of the weak a liberation of humanity or a poisoning of it?

## Why Nietzsche Must Be Faced

It would be easy, and dishonest, to dismiss Nietzsche as a mere celebrant of cruelty, a proto-fascist whose views were refuted by the horrors his admirers later committed. This course will not take that easy path. Nietzsche must be faced, and faced at his strongest, because he perceived the truth about the Christian revolution more clearly than most of its defenders, and because his challenge exposes the fact that the framing of the inversion as a "liberation" is a value judgment, not a neutral description. This entire unit has presented the cross's exaltation of the weak as a moral advance; Nietzsche reminds us that this presentation rests on a prior commitment to the values — compassion, equality, the dignity of the lowly — that are themselves the fruit of the revolution being evaluated. We judge the cross to be good by standards the cross gave us; a consistent Nietzschean, standing outside those standards, judges it to be a catastrophe. There is no wholly neutral ground from which to settle the dispute, and honesty requires admitting this.

The question Nietzsche poses, therefore, is not a historical one that further research could resolve but a fundamental one about value itself: whether the exaltation of the weak and the enthronement of compassion represent the moral progress of humanity or its decline. This course has taken, and continues to take, the former view; but it does not pretend that the question is closed, or that the alternative can be dismissed without argument. Nietzsche's verdict stands as the permanent shadow over the celebration of the cross — the reminder that what looks like liberation from one vantage looks like poisoning from another, and that the choice between these visions is among the deepest a human being can make.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Having presented Nietzsche at his strongest, honesty now requires the strongest replies, for his verdict, powerful as it is, is far from unanswerable. First, Nietzsche's historical genealogy — the neat story of an original master morality overturned by a slave revolt of the resentful — is a brilliant hypothesis, not established history; scholars have questioned whether the tidy sequence ever occurred as he described, and the genealogy functions more as a suggestive myth than as a demonstrated account of moral origins. Second, and more importantly, the reduction of Christian morality to mere *ressentiment* is a hostile caricature that ignores what is most evident in the tradition: not resentment but love. The Christian exaltation of the weak flows, in its own self-understanding and often in its practice, not from the spite of the impotent but from a genuine compassion, a real love of neighbor and even of enemy, that Nietzsche's diagnosis cannot account for and simply refuses to see. To explain the care of the sick, the rescue of the abandoned, and the love of the outcast as disguised revenge is to be blinded by a theory.

Third, the charge that Christian morality is simply "life-denying" is contestable, for Christianity also affirms life, creation, embodiment, and joy, and has inspired not only asceticism but also profound celebrations of the goodness of the world; the picture of it as uniformly hostile to vitality is a polemical distortion. Fourth, and most gravely, Nietzsche's alternative has its own horrors. The affirmation of strength, hierarchy, and cruelty against the morality of compassion points toward a world most people, confronted with it honestly, recoil from — the world of the disposable body and the contempt for the weak that the previous unit described. The catastrophic appropriation of Nietzsche's ideas in the twentieth century, though a genuine distortion of his subtle thought, was not wholly accidental, and it revealed something about where the honest rejection of compassion can lead. Nietzsche must be faced; but facing him does not mean conceding to him, and the strongest reply to his verdict may simply be the love that his theory of resentment cannot explain, and the recoil that most of humanity feels from the world his critique would restore. The question he poses remains genuinely open; but the answer this course gives — that the exaltation of the weak was a liberation and not a poisoning — is not thereby refuted, and stands as a considered judgment against his. How that judgment fares in the face of a very modern development — a culture organized around trauma and victimhood — is the question to which we turn next.
