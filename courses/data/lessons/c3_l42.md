Throughout this unit we have described *what* the cross did — how it inverted the values of the ancient world, exalted the victim, and remade the moral imagination of the West. But we have not yet asked, in any systematic way, *why* — what it was about the cross that gave it this world-transforming power, what mechanism it disrupted. One thinker offered an answer of such scope and originality that, whether or not one accepts it, it illuminates the entire argument of this unit from a new angle. René Girard, a French literary critic and anthropologist who spent his career in America, developed a grand theory of human violence, religion, and culture, at the center of which stood a startling claim: that the cross exposed and disarmed a mechanism of scapegoating violence that had structured human society from its very foundations. To understand Girard is to understand a powerful account of why the cross had the effect this unit has traced.

## Desire, Rivalry, and Violence

Girard's theory begins with an account of human desire that overturns our usual assumptions.[^1] We tend to imagine that we desire things because of their intrinsic worth, or because of our own spontaneous preferences. Girard argued that this is largely an illusion — that human desire is fundamentally *mimetic*, that is, imitative. We desire things because others desire them; we learn what to want by watching what others want, and we come to want it because they do. This mimetic quality of desire, Girard argued, has a fateful consequence: it breeds rivalry. If I desire what you desire precisely because you desire it, then we become rivals for the same object, and as our imitation intensifies, so does our conflict. Mimetic desire, spreading through a community, generates escalating rivalry and, eventually, a crisis of all-against-all violence that threatens to tear the community apart.

This mimetic crisis — the war of each against each, driven by imitative desire and reciprocal violence — posed, Girard argued, a mortal threat to early human communities. And it was resolved, again and again, by a single mechanism, which Girard placed at the very origin of human culture and religion: the scapegoat mechanism.

## The Scapegoat Mechanism

When the crisis of all-against-all violence reached its peak, Girard argued, the community would, unconsciously and spontaneously, converge upon a single victim — a scapegoat — who would be blamed for the whole crisis and collectively killed or expelled. The choice of victim was essentially arbitrary, often falling on the marginal, the different, or the vulnerable, but the community became convinced of the victim's guilt, believed that this one was truly the cause of all their troubles. And the mechanism *worked*: the collective violence against the single victim discharged the accumulated violence of all against all, and the community, its tensions released, found itself suddenly at peace. The war of each against each was replaced by the unity of all against one, and out of the murder came reconciliation and calm.

This astonishing transformation — from murderous chaos to sudden peace, accomplished through the killing of a single victim — was so powerful and so mysterious to the communities that experienced it that they came to regard the victim as sacred. The scapegoat, blamed and killed as the cause of the crisis, was then revered as the source of the peace that followed — cursed and divine at once, a figure of terrible and holy power. Here, Girard argued, lay the origin of archaic religion and sacrifice: the founding murder, endlessly repeated in ritual sacrifice, which reenacted the killing of the victim to renew the peace it had first produced. And here too lay the origin of myth, which told the story of the founding murder from the perspective of the persecutors — presenting the victim as truly guilty, the violence as justified, the killing as sacred and necessary. The scapegoat mechanism worked precisely because it was hidden; the community had to believe in the victim's guilt, had to be blind to what it was really doing, for the mechanism to function. Myth and archaic religion, on Girard's account, are the veils that concealed the innocent victim behind the mask of the guilty one.

## The Cross as Exposure

Now comes Girard's central and most controversial claim, the one that bears directly on this unit. The biblical tradition, he argued, and supremely the cross, did something unprecedented in human history: it *exposed* the scapegoat mechanism that all previous myth and religion had concealed. The Gospels tell the story of a scapegoat — a single victim, blamed by the crowd and the authorities, collectively condemned and killed to restore order. But they tell it from the perspective of the *victim*, and they declare the victim *innocent*. Where myth had always sided with the persecutors and presented the victim as guilty, the Gospels side with the victim and reveal the persecutors' violence for what it is: the murder of an innocent. The crowd cries for his death, the authorities condemn him to preserve the peace, and the narrative insists, against them all, that he has done nothing to deserve it — that the mechanism has claimed an innocent victim, as it always had, but that this time the truth is told.

In doing this, Girard argued, the cross unveiled and thereby disarmed the scapegoat mechanism. Once the mechanism is exposed — once we can see the innocent victim behind the crowd's conviction of guilt — it loses its power to work its ancient magic. We can no longer participate in scapegoating with a clear conscience, no longer believe unquestioningly in the guilt of the accused, no longer find the sacred peace that came from the hidden murder of the innocent. The cross places God himself on the side of the victim, and in doing so it makes the victim visible forever, tearing away the veil that myth had drawn over the founding violence. This, Girard argued, is why the cross had the world-transforming effect this unit has described: it revealed the innocent victim, and having once seen the innocent victim, the world could never fully unsee him.

## Why the Modern World Cannot Stop Seeing Victims

Girard's theory offers, then, a powerful explanation for the phenomena the previous lessons have traced. The modern world's peculiar and unprecedented concern for victims — its inability to accept the persecutors' story, its reflexive suspicion of scapegoating, its compulsion to take the side of the accused and the marginalized — is, on Girard's account, the long-term working-out of the cross's exposure of the scapegoat mechanism. The Gospel taught the world to see the innocent victim, and the modern conscience, shaped over centuries by that revelation, can no longer look away. This is why, as an earlier lesson argued, modern compassion and the moral authority of the victim are inheritances of the cross: the cross made the victim visible, and victim-consciousness is the enduring result.

But Girard's theory also illuminates the distortions examined in the previous lesson. For the concern for victims, once unleashed, can itself become a new form of scapegoating — the persecution of the "persecutors," the identification and casting-out of new victims in the name of defending the old. A world taught to side with victims can turn that very impulse into a weapon, hunting for oppressors to blame and expel, reproducing the scapegoating mechanism under the banner of opposing it. Girard saw this danger clearly: the modern concern for victims is the fruit of the Gospel, but detached from the forgiveness at the heart of the cross, it can curdle back into the very violence it was meant to expose. His theory thus ties together the whole arc of this unit — the exaltation of the victim, its modern flowering, and its contemporary distortions — within a single, sweeping account of human violence and its unveiling.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Girard's theory is magnificent in its scope and genuinely illuminating, but its very grandeur is also its greatest vulnerability, and honesty requires substantial qualification. First, the theory is highly contested and remains a bold hypothesis rather than established knowledge. Anthropologists and historians of religion have questioned nearly every element of it: whether mimetic desire is truly the fundamental structure of human wanting, whether the scapegoat mechanism is genuinely universal, whether the "founding murder" ever occurred as Girard described, and whether archaic religion and sacrifice can really be explained by a single mechanism. The theory is sweeping, elegant, and difficult to test, and its critics charge that it is reductive — explaining the vast diversity of human religion and culture by one master key — and close to unfalsifiable, able to absorb any apparent counterexample.

Second, Girard's reading of the Bible is avowedly theological; he was a convert to Catholicism who came to see the Gospel as anthropologically revelatory, disclosing the truth about human violence that myth had concealed. This gives his interpretation great power and coherence, but it also means that his account of the cross's uniqueness is shaped by prior faith commitments, and a skeptic may reasonably suspect that the theory was built to arrive at the conclusion it reaches. Third, the claim that the cross *uniquely* reveals and sides with the innocent victim is contestable; other traditions have shown compassion for victims and have questioned the justice of persecution, and the sharp contrast between the Gospel and all previous myth may be overdrawn. These are serious qualifications, and they mean that Girard's theory should be received as a profound and suggestive interpretation rather than a proven account. Yet even held at this distance, it offers something valuable: a coherent and powerful explanation of *why* the cross might have had the world-transforming effect this unit has traced, and a framework that ties together the exaltation of the victim, its modern inheritance, and its potential to curdle into new persecution. With Girard's theory before us, we are ready for the unit's final reckoning — an honest examination of whether the inversion of the cross was really as unique as this unit has claimed.
