Having examined, in the previous unit, the scandal of the cross and the inversion of values it worked, we now trace one particular stream of that revolution as it flowed into the world and reshaped it. This unit concerns the most radical of all the new commandments — the command to love, and specifically to love the stranger, the enemy, the poor, the one who can give you nothing in return. It is a commandment we take so utterly for granted that we can scarcely imagine a moral world without it; and yet, as we shall see, it was a genuine novelty, and its consequences remade the West. We begin, fittingly, not with a believer's testimony but with a skeptic's, for the thesis of this unit is defended most strikingly by scholars who have no religious stake in it at all.

## A Skeptic's Testimony

Bart Ehrman is one of the most prominent scholars of the New Testament and early Christianity in the world, and he is emphatically not a Christian.[^1] Raised an evangelical, he lost his faith through his own scholarship and now describes himself as an agnostic; he has written a shelf of books challenging traditional Christian claims about the reliability of the Gospels, the divinity of Jesus, and the origins of the faith. He is, in short, no apologist, and no friend of the church's doctrines. And this is precisely what makes his argument so worth attending to. For in his study of how a tiny, persecuted Jewish sect grew to conquer the Roman Empire and remake Western civilization, Ehrman arrives at a conclusion that has nothing to do with the truth of Christian belief and everything to do with the transformation of moral values: that Christianity genuinely revolutionized the ethical assumptions of the West, and that we are, whether we know it or not, its moral heirs.

Ehrman's claim is that before Christianity, the dominant values of the ancient Mediterranean world were, as the previous units have shown, values of dominance, power, and status — the celebration of the strong, the honoring of the victorious, the contempt for the weak. The idea that one should love one's neighbor as oneself, that one should care for the poor and the sick, that every human life possesses worth including the lowest and most degraded, that mercy and humility are virtues rather than weaknesses — these were not the common assumptions of the classical world, and they became the common assumptions of the West because Christianity triumphed. Had it not triumphed, Ehrman argues, our moral world would look profoundly different — more like the world of Rome than the world we inhabit. That a secular, skeptical scholar should reach this conclusion, purely as a historian, lends the thesis a weight that no work of Christian apologetics could supply.

## The Revolution of Love

At the center of the ethical revolution Ehrman describes stands the commandment to love — and it is essential to see precisely what was new about it. Every human society has known love: the love of family, of friends, of one's own people. The ancient world was full of such love, and full of the reciprocal generosity that bound together kin, allies, patrons, and fellow citizens, as the next lesson will explore. What was new in the Christian ethic was not love as such but the *extension* of love beyond every natural boundary — to the neighbor who is a stranger, to the enemy who has wronged you, to the poor who can offer nothing in return, to the least and the lowest and the outcast. The command was not merely to love those who love you, which even the ancient world understood, but to love those who cannot or will not love you back — to extend the circle of moral concern past the limits of kinship, reciprocity, and self-interest, to embrace the whole of humanity and especially its most despised members.

This universal and non-reciprocal love, which the Greek of the New Testament calls agape, was the revolutionary core. It transformed love from a bond within a group into a universal ethical demand, owed to every human being simply as a human being, and owed most urgently to those with the least claim by the ordinary logic of the world. And bound up with this was a second transformation that Ehrman emphasizes: Christianity made active charity — the concrete care of the poor and the sick — into a central religious duty, on a scale and with an intensity the ancient world had never known. Religion, which in the pagan world had been largely a matter of ritual and reciprocity with the gods, became, in the Christian understanding, inseparable from the love and care of one's fellow human beings, especially the suffering. To be religious was now to be charitable; to worship God was to serve the poor. This fusion of piety and compassion, of religion and the love of the neighbor, was among the most consequential of all the changes the new faith worked upon the world.

## The Counterfactual

The force of Ehrman's argument comes into sharpest focus in a thought experiment that he and other scholars, including the historian whose work frames this course, have pressed: the counterfactual. What if Christianity had not triumphed? What if the small Jesus movement had remained an obscure sect, or died out entirely, and the classical values had continued unbroken into the modern world? The answer these scholars offer is sobering. Our characteristic modern convictions — that the poor and the sick have a claim on our care, that every human life is precious regardless of status, that compassion is a virtue and cruelty a vice, that we owe something to the stranger and even the enemy — are not, on this view, the inevitable deliverances of human reason or the natural endowment of any decent society. They are the contingent inheritance of a particular history, and had that history gone differently, our moral world might resemble the world of the previous unit — a world of hierarchy, of contempt for the weak, of disposable bodies and reciprocal generosity that fell silent beyond the circle of the useful.

This counterfactual is, of course, unprovable; we cannot run the experiment. But it serves to make vivid the central claim: that the moral assumptions we take for granted are historically strange, that they had to be introduced into the world against the grain of what came before, and that the agent of their introduction was, in significant part, the Christian ethic of love. To recognize this is not to be compelled toward belief; Ehrman himself is proof that one may accept the historical thesis while rejecting the theology. But it is to understand that our compassion has a history, that it was once a scandalous novelty, and that we are, in our deepest moral instincts, the heirs of a revolution we have largely forgotten. The rest of this unit will trace that revolution as it moved from a teaching into a lived practice, and from a lived practice into the enduring institutions that still shape our world.

## Why the Witness Matters

It is worth dwelling, before we proceed, on why the testimony of a skeptic like Ehrman carries such particular weight for the argument of this course. The claim that Christianity revolutionized Western morality is one that a believer might be expected to make, and a skeptic might be expected to dismiss as pious self-congratulation. When it is instead advanced by scholars who reject the faith — by an agnostic New Testament critic, by a sociologist analyzing conversion rates, by a historian who describes himself as no longer a Christian — it can no longer be waved away as apologetics. These scholars have no interest in flattering Christianity; several have devoted their careers to challenging its claims. Their agreement that the Christian ethic of love genuinely transformed the moral world is therefore a datum of a different kind, an independent convergence on a historical conclusion from those with every incentive to reach a different one.

This does not settle the matter, for historical theses about vast cultural transformations are always contestable, and we will weigh the objections in due course. But it establishes that the argument of this unit is not a work of special pleading. The claim that the command to love the stranger revolutionized the West is a serious historical proposition, held by serious scholars across the spectrum of belief and unbelief, and it deserves to be examined on its merits rather than dismissed as devotion in disguise. With that established, we turn to the world the revolution overturned — the world of ancient generosity, and its silence beyond the circle.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Ehrman's thesis, skeptical credentials notwithstanding, remains a historical interpretation, and honesty requires marking its limits. First, the counterfactual at its heart is by nature unprovable; we cannot know what the moral world would look like had Christianity not triumphed, and the confident assertion that it would resemble ancient Rome involves a large speculative leap. Human moral development is driven by many forces, and it is possible that some of the values we credit to Christianity would have emerged by other paths — through the Stoic universalism examined earlier in this course, through the natural human capacities for sympathy, through social and economic developments that had nothing to do with religion. The genealogical claim, here as throughout the course, has serious rivals and cannot be established with certainty.

Second, even a skeptical scholar is not a neutral instrument. As the historian framing this course has provocatively observed, the very values by which a modern secular person judges the ancient world to be cruel and the Christian ethic to be admirable may themselves be the inheritance of that ethic — so that Ehrman's evaluation of the moral revolution as a genuine advance may quietly presuppose the outcome of the revolution he describes. This does not invalidate his historical analysis, but it complicates the neat picture of a disinterested observer. Third, Ehrman's argument is historical and sociological, not an endorsement of Christian truth, and it should not be pressed into apologetic service beyond what it claims; that Christianity transformed Western values is compatible with every possible view of whether its theology is true. With these qualifications noted, the thesis stands as a serious and independently attested proposition: that the command to love the stranger was a genuine moral revolution, and that the modern West is its heir. To measure that revolution, we must first understand what it replaced.
