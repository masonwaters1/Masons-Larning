There is no better place to feel the force of the command to love the stranger than in the story Jesus told to illustrate it — a story so famous that its very title has entered our language as a synonym for kindness, and so domesticated that we have almost entirely lost its scandal. The parable of the Good Samaritan is not, as we tend to hear it, a gentle tale about being nice to people in trouble. It was a deliberate provocation, engineered to offend and unsettle its original hearers, to overturn the boundaries they took for granted and to force upon them a demand they did not want to accept. To recover the parable's original edge is to recover the radicalism of the command it dramatizes, and to feel, once more, how the ethic of love broke the ancient boundary of reciprocity and answered the silence beyond it.

## The Question Behind the Story

The parable arises, in its setting, from a question — and the question matters, because Jesus's answer subverts it.[^1] A legal expert, testing Jesus, asks what he must do to inherit eternal life, and when directed to the command to love God and to love his neighbor as himself, he presses further: "And who is my neighbor?" The question is not idle. It assumes that "neighbor" is a bounded category, that some people count as one's neighbor and others do not, and it seeks to know where the boundary lies — whom one is obligated to love, and, by implication, whom one may exclude. This was the natural way to hear the command in a world of bounded obligation, the world of the previous lesson: love was owed within a circle, and the sensible question was where the circle ended. The lawyer wanted a definition, a limit, a line beyond which his obligation ceased.

Jesus answers not with a definition but with a story, and the story dismantles the very assumption behind the question. A man travels the dangerous road from Jerusalem to Jericho and is set upon by robbers, who beat him, strip him, and leave him half-dead. Three travelers come upon him in turn. First a priest — a figure of the highest religious respectability, a servant of the Temple — who sees the wounded man and passes by on the other side. Then a Levite, another member of the religious establishment, who likewise sees and passes by. And then a third traveler, who stops, is moved with compassion, tends the man's wounds, carries him to an inn, and pays for his care. The question was "who is my neighbor?" — who falls within the circle of my obligation? — and the story, as we shall see, refuses the question and replaces it with another.

## The Scandal of the Samaritan

The provocation lies in the identity of the third traveler. He is a Samaritan — and to grasp the force of this, we must understand what a Samaritan was to the parable's original Jewish audience. The Samaritans were not merely foreigners; they were a despised and hated people, regarded by Jews as religious heretics and ethnic half-breeds, the objects of a deep and mutual contempt that ran back centuries. To a Jewish audience, the Samaritan was the enemy, the unclean outsider, the last person from whom one would expect righteousness, and very nearly the last person one would wish to be indebted to. And Jesus makes this figure — the hated enemy, the religious outsider — the hero of the story, the one who does what is right, while the respectable insiders, the priest and the Levite, the very embodiments of Jewish religious propriety, pass by and fail. The moral exemplar is the despised enemy; the moral failures are the religious establishment.

This was calculated to shock, and shock it did. It elevated the hated outsider over the honored insider; it made the enemy the model of virtue and the religious authorities the model of failure; it forced the hearer to admire, and to learn from, the very people they had been raised to despise. Imagine the discomfort of a first-century Jew being told that the good and righteous one in the story was a Samaritan, while the priest and the Levite — his own people, his own religious leaders — walked past a dying man. The parable did not flatter its audience; it indicted them, overturning their assumptions about who was good and who was bad, who was inside and who was outside, and demanding that they find their moral teacher in the person they most despised.

## The Question Reversed

Then comes the final turn, the reversal of the original question, and it is here that the whole meaning crystallizes. Jesus asks the lawyer which of the three proved to be a neighbor to the man who fell among robbers. The lawyer, unable even to say the word "Samaritan," answers that it was the one who showed him mercy. And Jesus says: go and do likewise. The question the lawyer had asked was "who is my neighbor?" — who qualifies for my love, where is the boundary of my obligation? But Jesus has reversed it. The question is not "who is my neighbor?" but "to whom will I be a neighbor?" — not "whom am I obligated to love?" but "will I act with love toward whoever lies in need before me?" The lawyer wanted to know the limit of the circle; Jesus abolishes the circle. The neighbor is not a category to be defined and bounded; the neighbor is anyone in need whom I encounter, and my task is not to determine who qualifies for my love but to become one who loves without qualification.

This is the destruction of the boundary that the previous lesson described. The ancient world, and the lawyer's question, assumed that love was owed within a circle and that the crucial task was to define its edge. The parable obliterates the edge: love is owed to the stranger, the outsider, even the enemy, wherever need is found, without regard to kinship, reciprocity, religion, or tribe. It commands precisely the love that crosses the boundary into the silence beyond — the love of the one who can give nothing back, the one who is not one's own, the one whom one has been taught to despise. In a single story, the command to love the stranger is dramatized in its full and offensive radicalism, and the whole logic of bounded, reciprocal generosity is overturned.

## The Provocation That Endures

The parable's power to provoke has not died; it has only been obscured by familiarity. We have made "Good Samaritan" a synonym for a nice person who helps in an emergency, and in doing so we have tamed the story into a comfortable lesson about kindness. But its original edge cuts as sharply today as it did then, if we let it. For the parable demands love across precisely the lines we most fiercely draw — the lines of ethnicity, religion, politics, and nation, the lines between "us" and "them," between our own people and the strangers we distrust or despise. To feel the parable's true force in our own moment, we need only imagine the hero as a member of whatever group we most fear or hate — the political enemy, the religious other, the foreign outsider — and to hear Jesus say that this person, and not the respectable members of our own tribe, is the one who did what was right, and the one we must learn to imitate and to love. The parable resists domestication because it will not let us define our neighbors narrowly; it insists, forever, that the stranger in need is the neighbor, and that love is owed across every boundary we would draw.

This is the seed from which the rest of this unit grows. The command to love the stranger, dramatized in the Good Samaritan, would not remain a mere teaching or a moving story. It would be enacted, at terrible cost, in the plague-stricken cities of the empire; it would be built into institutions that cared for strangers, widows, orphans, and the sick; and it would echo down the centuries into the modern conscience's concern for the suffering of people it will never meet. But it began here, on the road to Jericho, in a story designed to offend — the story of a hated enemy who stopped for a dying stranger, and of a command to go and do likewise.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The parable's radicalism should not be overstated or divorced from its context, and several qualifications keep the reading honest. First, the meaning of the parable is genuinely debated among scholars. Some read it primarily as a redefinition of "neighbor," others as a call to *become* a neighbor, others as a pointed critique of the priesthood and the Temple establishment, and still others as making a narrower point than the sweeping universalism often drawn from it; the interpretation offered here, while well grounded, is one reading among several. Second, the depiction of Samaritans as utterly despised enemies, though broadly accurate, is sometimes overstated for rhetorical effect, and the historical relations between Jews and Samaritans were more complex than a simple picture of pure mutual hatred.

Third, and most importantly, the ethic the parable dramatizes was not a Christian invention but a radicalization of a Jewish inheritance. The very command that frames the parable — to love one's neighbor as oneself — is drawn directly from the Torah, from the book of Leviticus, which also, in the same context, commands love for the stranger and the resident alien, grounded in Israel's memory of its own sojourn in Egypt. Jesus was intensifying and dramatizing a commandment already present in the tradition he inherited, not conjuring it from nothing, and the roots of "love thy stranger" run back into Judaism, as this course has repeatedly insisted. Finally, the parable's radical demand has been honored, throughout Christian history, as much in the breach as in the observance; Christians have drawn boundaries around their love as narrowly as anyone, and the parable stands as a perpetual judgment on that failure rather than a description of Christian practice. With these qualifications granted, the central point remains: the Good Samaritan dramatized, in a story built to provoke, the command to love the stranger across every boundary of reciprocity and tribe — a command whose enactment would remake the world. How it was enacted, at the cost of life itself, in the plague-stricken cities of the empire, is our next subject.
