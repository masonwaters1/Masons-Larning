A commandment is one thing; its enactment, at the cost of one's life, is another. The command to love the stranger might have remained a beautiful teaching and a provocative parable, admired and ignored, had it not been put to the hardest possible test — and passed. That test came in the form of catastrophe: the great plagues that periodically swept the Roman Empire, killing on a scale that shattered communities and revealed, with terrible clarity, what people actually believed and how they actually behaved. In those plagues, the sociologist Rodney Stark has argued, the Christian ethic of love was not merely professed but enacted, at enormous cost, in a way that both embodied the revolution and propelled the astonishing growth of the church. The plague was the test of "love thy stranger," and the Christian response to it may have changed the course of history.

## When the Cities Died

Twice during the early centuries of the Christian era, devastating epidemics ravaged the Roman world.[^1] The first, the so-called Antonine Plague, struck in the later second century and killed, by some estimates, a quarter or more of the population in affected areas. The second, the Plague of Cyprian, named for the bishop who described it, struck in the middle of the third century and was, if anything, more terrible still, reportedly killing thousands a day in the great cities at its height. These were catastrophes almost beyond our imagining — diseases that spread invisibly and lethally through crowded cities, striking down the healthy without warning, filling the streets with the dead and dying, and dissolving the ordinary bonds of society in a spasm of terror.

The normal human response to such a catastrophe was flight and abandonment. As the plague raged, those who could fled the cities for the countryside, hoping to escape the contagion; and, more tellingly, the sick were abandoned, often by their own families, cast out or left to die alone, because to remain near the afflicted was to risk death oneself. This was not mere cruelty; it was the rational response of terrified people in a world without medicine or understanding of disease, and it was widespread. The ancient sources describe the dying being thrown into the streets, the sick deserted even by those who loved them, the whole fabric of mutual care unraveling under the pressure of mortal fear. In the face of the plague, ordinary human solidarity, bounded as it already was, collapsed.

## The People Who Would Not Flee

Into this collapse, according to Stark and the ancient testimony he draws upon, the Christians did something extraordinary: they stayed. Motivated by the command to love the neighbor and the stranger, and sustained by a faith that this life was not the final one — so that death, while still fearful, had lost its ultimate terror — Christians remained in the plague-stricken cities and nursed the sick. And they nursed not only their own but pagans too, strangers, the abandoned, the very people who had cast out their own sick relatives. They brought food and water to the afflicted, cared for them in their extremity, and buried the dead, exposing themselves again and again to the contagion, and dying in large numbers as a result. The bishop Dionysius of Alexandria, writing of the Plague of Cyprian, described how the Christians of his city tended the sick and dying without regard for the danger to themselves, many of them contracting the disease and perishing in the act of caring for others — and contrasted this with the flight and abandonment he observed among the pagans. Cyprian himself urged Christians to care even for their persecutors.

This was the Good Samaritan enacted not once, on a single road, but en masse, across the dying cities of an empire. It was the command to love the stranger carried to its ultimate conclusion: the care of the sick stranger, at the risk and often the cost of one's own life, for no possible reciprocal benefit, extended even to enemies and persecutors. Whatever else one makes of it, it was a demonstration, in the most extreme circumstances imaginable, that the ethic of love was not mere words — that there were people prepared to die rather than abandon the suffering stranger. In a world where the sick were thrown into the streets, the sight of people who would hold the dying in their arms was not merely moving; it was revolutionary.

## The Sociology of Survival

Rodney Stark's contribution, in his influential study of the rise of Christianity, was to show how this ethic of care, beyond its moral grandeur, had concrete and world-historical consequences for the growth of the church. His argument turns on several mechanisms. The first is a matter of simple medical fact: even in the absence of any effective medicine, basic nursing — the provision of food, water, warmth, and care — dramatically improves survival rates in an epidemic, sustaining the sick through the crisis when abandonment would mean certain death. Christians who fell ill and were nursed by their community therefore survived at markedly higher rates than pagans who were abandoned; and pagans who were cared for by Christians, rather than deserted by their own, also survived at higher rates and were bound to their rescuers by gratitude. Over the course of an epidemic that killed a large fraction of the population, these differential survival rates alone would have shifted the composition of the surviving population toward Christians and those attached to them.

The second mechanism is the power of the demonstration itself. Pagans witnessed the Christians doing what no one else would do — risking and losing their lives to care for strangers, even enemies — and they were astonished. This visible, costly, inexplicable love was the most powerful possible advertisement for the faith, a living argument that no words could match, and it won admiration and, in time, conversions. The third mechanism is the strength of Christian community and its networks of mutual support, which held together under the pressure of catastrophe while the frayed bonds of pagan society dissolved, offering to the frightened and bereaved a solidarity and a hope that the surrounding world could not. Together, Stark argues, these mechanisms made the plagues, paradoxically, an engine of Christian growth — moments when the ethic of love, enacted at terrible cost, both demonstrated the faith and multiplied its adherents, helping to drive the movement's remarkable expansion from a tiny sect toward the domination of the empire.

## The Hostile Witness

We possess a striking confirmation of the phenomenon from a source that had every reason to deny it: the emperor Julian, called the Apostate, who in the fourth century sought to revive paganism and roll back the Christian tide. Julian was frustrated and even embittered by the Christians' charitable practices, and he acknowledged, in effect, that their care for the poor and the sick — including their care for pagans, for those outside their own community — was winning them admiration and converts and putting the old religion to shame. He urged the pagan priesthood to imitate the Christian example, to establish their own charities and care for the needy, in order to compete. That a hostile pagan emperor, laboring to defeat Christianity, should complain that its charity was a chief source of its success, and should try to copy it, is powerful independent testimony to the reality and the impact of the Christian ethic of love. The enemy of the faith confirmed what its adherents claimed: that the care of the stranger was both real and world-changing.

The plague test, then, reveals the command to love the stranger passing from teaching into costly practice, and shows that practice reshaping history. The Christians who stayed to nurse the dying enacted the Good Samaritan on the scale of an empire, and in doing so they both embodied the revolution of love and drove it forward. But the ethic did not remain a matter of heroic individual sacrifice in moments of crisis; it was built, over the following centuries, into enduring institutions — structures of care for the stranger, the widow, the orphan, and the sick that would outlast the plagues and remake the social world. To those institutions we now turn.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Stark's thesis is compelling and has been enormously influential, but it is also contested, and honesty requires marking its uncertainties. First, the ancient sources for the Christian response to the plagues are limited, and they are partly the Christians' own testimony about their own virtue — the account of Dionysius, for instance, comes from within the community it praises — so there is an inevitable risk of idealization and self-congratulation. We should be cautious about accepting a picture that flatters its authors, and we cannot rule out that the contrast between heroic Christians and fleeing pagans was sharpened in the telling. Second, Stark's quantitative arguments — the estimates of mortality, differential survival rates, and their effect on the growth curve of Christianity — are models and reconstructions built on fragmentary data, not measurements, and other scholars have questioned whether the effect was as large as he claims, or whether Christianity's growth is better explained by other factors entirely.

Third, the sharp dichotomy of "Christians cared, pagans fled" is surely idealized. Pagans, too, sometimes cared for their sick out of natural love and duty, and not all Christians, we may be sure, lived up to the heroic ideal; human beings of every creed both rise to and fall short of their principles under the pressure of catastrophe. The reality was doubtless messier and more mixed than the edifying contrast suggests. And Christianity's remarkable growth had many causes — its universalism, its community, its promise of eternal life, its appeal to the marginalized, examined in earlier lessons — of which care for the sick was only one. With these qualifications granted, the core of the argument stands on solid ground: the Christian ethic of love was genuinely enacted, at real cost, in the plague-stricken cities; this care was distinctive enough to draw the notice even of hostile witnesses; and it contributed, plausibly and perhaps substantially, to the faith's growth. The command to love the stranger was tested by catastrophe and was not found to be empty — and that is a fact of the first importance, however carefully we must hold the details. From this costly practice grew the institutions to which we turn next.
