The heroism of individual Christians nursing the plague-stricken was extraordinary, but heroism is fragile; it flares in a crisis and fades when the crisis passes. What made the Christian ethic of love a permanent force in history was not merely the courage of individuals but the transformation of that love into institutions — durable structures that carried the command to love the stranger beyond the reach of any single person's virtue and embedded it in the ongoing life of the community. And at the root of this institutionalization lay a theological conviction of remarkable power: that to welcome the stranger was to welcome God himself. Hospitality, in the Christian understanding, was not mere kindness; it was theology enacted, an encounter with the divine in the person of the needy. To grasp this is to understand how the ethic of love became not a passing heroism but a permanent institution.

## Christ in the Stranger

The theological engine of Christian hospitality was a conviction rooted in the teaching of Jesus himself, and it gave the care of the stranger a weight no merely humanitarian impulse could match.[^1] In a saying that would echo through the centuries, Jesus identified himself with the hungry, the thirsty, the stranger, the naked, the sick, and the imprisoned, declaring that whatever was done to the least of these was done to him — that to feed the hungry, welcome the stranger, and visit the prisoner was to feed and welcome and visit Christ himself, and to neglect them was to neglect him. This astonishing identification of the divine with the least and the neediest transformed the meaning of charity. To care for the suffering stranger was no longer merely to do a good deed; it was to serve God in disguise, to encounter Christ in the person of the destitute, to stand before the divine in the face of the beggar at the door. The word the early Christians used for this hospitality was philoxenia — literally, love of the stranger, the exact opposite of xenophobia, the fear of the stranger. And they were reminded that in showing such love, they might, as the Scripture said, entertain angels unaware.

This conviction did something that no calculation of reciprocity or honor could do: it made the care of the stranger the very criterion of one's standing before God. In the vision of judgment that Jesus offered, people were separated not by their beliefs, their rituals, or their status, but by whether they had fed the hungry, welcomed the stranger, and cared for the sick — by whether, in short, they had loved the least. Charity was not an optional adornment of the religious life; it was the substance of it, the thing on which everything turned. To be a Christian was to care for the suffering stranger, because in that stranger one met Christ, and one's eternal destiny hung upon the encounter. This fusion of the love of neighbor with the love and service of God, examined in an earlier lesson as one of Ehrman's central themes, is what gave Christian charity its extraordinary motive force and drove its transformation into lasting institutions.

## The Structures of Care

From this theological conviction grew a remarkable apparatus of organized care, unprecedented in the ancient world, that made the love of the stranger a systematic practice rather than a sporadic virtue. From the earliest days, the care of widows and orphans stood at the center of Christian communal life. The letter of James defined pure and undefiled religion as the care of orphans and widows in their distress, and the early church appointed officers — deacons — specifically to oversee the distribution of aid and the care of the needy, so that no one would be neglected. Churches maintained rolls of widows to be supported, and the community took responsibility for those who had no one else, the very people who fell into the silence beyond the ancient web of obligation. The orphan, the widow, the destitute — the categories the classical world had largely left to fend for themselves — became the special charges of the Christian community, systematically supported by its common resources.

Alongside the care of widows and orphans came the organized relief of the poor more broadly: common funds into which the community contributed, deacons who distributed food and money to the needy, and a settled expectation that the church would provide for those who could not provide for themselves. It came to embrace the care of prisoners, whom Christians visited and supported; of travelers and strangers, who were offered hospitality and shelter; and of the sick, who were tended and nursed. By the middle of the third century, the church at Rome is reported to have been supporting well over a thousand widows and needy persons — a systematic operation of poor relief on a scale that had no pagan precedent, sustained not by the state or by the honor-seeking benefactions of the wealthy but by the ordinary contributions of a religious community that regarded such care as the service of God. What had been, in the ancient world, the silence beyond the circle of reciprocity was now filled with an organized structure of care for exactly those the old order had ignored.

## The Bishop as Lover of the Poor

The historian Peter Brown has illuminated, with particular subtlety, the transformation this worked upon the very meaning of wealth and leadership in the late ancient world. In the classical model, examined in an earlier lesson, the wealthy man displayed his greatness by benefacting the *city* — the citizen community — and reaped honor in return. But in the Christian model that now emerged, a new ideal took hold: the leader, and above all the bishop, was to be the "lover of the poor," and his greatness was measured not by his gifts to the honorable citizenry but by his care for the destitute. The wealth of the church came to be understood as the "patrimony of the poor" — resources held in trust for the needy, to be distributed to them as their due rather than hoarded or spent on display. The poor, who had been invisible to classical benefaction, became the privileged objects of Christian giving and the very measure of Christian leadership.

This was a profound reorientation of the social imagination. It shifted the focus of generosity from the citizen to the poor person, from the honorable to the needy, from those who could confer honor to those who could offer nothing but their need. And it made the care of the poor not the private virtue of exceptional individuals but the institutional responsibility of the church and the defining duty of its leaders. The bishop who neglected the poor was failing in the essence of his office; the bishop who cared for them was fulfilling it. Wealth itself was redefined — no longer a means of purchasing civic honor but a trust to be administered for the needy, a danger to the soul if hoarded and a means of grace if given away. The whole apparatus of ancient generosity, oriented toward reciprocity and honor, was being replaced by an apparatus of charity, oriented toward need and grounded in the conviction that in the poor one served God.

## From Conviction to Institution

We can now see how the command to love the stranger became a permanent force in history rather than a passing heroism. The theological conviction that Christ was present in the least, that to welcome the stranger was to welcome God, that charity was the criterion of salvation, gave the care of the needy a motive force of extraordinary power and permanence. And that motive force was channeled into institutions — the care of widows and orphans, the relief of the poor, the support of prisoners and travelers and the sick, the redefinition of wealth as the patrimony of the poor and of leadership as the love of the poor. These structures embodied the ethic of love in durable form, carrying it across generations and embedding it in the ongoing life of the community, so that it no longer depended on the virtue of exceptional individuals but was built into the very institution of the church. From these foundations would grow the more specialized institutions that later lessons will trace — the hospital, the orphanage, the almshouse, the whole apparatus of organized care that the West would inherit. But it began with a theological conviction: that in the face of the suffering stranger, one met the face of God.

## The Honest Counterpoint

This account of Christian charitable institutions requires its qualifications. First, and as always in this course, the institutions were not created from nothing; they had a crucial Jewish precedent. Jewish communities had long practiced organized charity, with systems for supporting the poor, the widow, and the orphan grounded in the commandments of the Torah, and the early church — itself Jewish in origin — built upon and adapted this inheritance rather than inventing communal charity from scratch. The Christian achievement was to universalize and intensify a practice with deep Jewish roots, and to carry it into the wider Gentile world, but it was a development of an existing tradition, not a pure novelty.

Second, Christian charity was not always as pure or as boundless as its ideals proclaimed. It often favored fellow Christians over outsiders, drawing its own boundaries around the love it professed to extend to all; it could serve the church's power and recruitment, binding recipients in gratitude and dependence and advancing the institution's growth; and it could, like all charity, foster dependency or function as a means of social control, tying aid to conformity and good behavior. The gap between the ideal of boundless love and the reality of institutional charity, with its favoritisms and its self-interest, was real, and honesty requires acknowledging it. Third, the redefinition of wealth as the patrimony of the poor coexisted, uncomfortably, with the church's own growing wealth, power, and hierarchy, and the institution that preached the love of the poor would become, over the centuries, a great and sometimes rapacious power in its own right. With these qualifications granted, the central point endures: the Christian conviction that Christ was met in the stranger transformed the love of the needy from a sporadic virtue into an organized, theological, institutional practice, unprecedented in scale, that filled the ancient silence with structures of care — and laid the foundation for institutions that would outlast the empire itself. The first and greatest of those institutions, the hospital, is our next subject.
