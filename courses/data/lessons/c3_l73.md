We come now to a domain where the water we swim in is so total that we scarcely think of it as water at all. Everything in the modern world — the device you are reading this on, the medicine that will extend your life, the food on your table, the very expectation that tomorrow's world will be transformed by discovery — is the product of science. We are immersed in it as completely as a fish in the sea. And the standard story of where this all-encompassing thing came from is a story of *liberation*: science, we are told, was born when humanity finally threw off the shackles of religion, when reason defeated superstition, when the light of inquiry broke through the long darkness of faith. Science and religion, on this telling, are natural enemies, and science is what we got by winning the war. This unit will argue that this story is almost exactly backwards. But before we can tell the true story, we must dismantle the false one — and the false one has a name.

## The Warfare Narrative

The idea that science and religion are locked in perpetual, inevitable conflict is called the *conflict thesis* or the *warfare thesis*, and most people absorb it without ever examining it. It runs through popular culture as a background assumption: the Church burned scientists, suppressed inquiry, insisted the earth was flat, and fought every advance of knowledge tooth and nail, until the brave heroes of science — Galileo above all — finally broke its grip. Religion is cast as the eternal obstacle, science as the eternal liberator, and the history of the modern mind as the story of the second defeating the first.

What almost no one realizes is that this narrative is not ancient or self-evident. It has a specific and surprisingly recent origin. It was manufactured, largely, by two American books in the late nineteenth century: John William Draper's *History of the Conflict Between Religion and Science*, published in 1874, and Andrew Dickson White's *A History of the Warfare of Science with Theology in Christendom*, published in 1896.[^1] These two polemical works, written in a particular moment of cultural battle over Darwinism and the authority of the churches, invented and popularized the "warfare" framing that has dominated the popular imagination ever since. The conflict thesis is not the deposit of centuries of experience. It is a piece of Victorian propaganda that hardened into common sense.

## What the Historians Actually Say

Here is the fact that should stop us short: among the professional historians who actually study the relationship between science and religion, the conflict thesis is dead. It has been abandoned, thoroughly and nearly unanimously, as bad history — a distortion built on myth, exaggeration, and outright fabrication. This is not a fringe revisionism or a religious apologetic; it is the settled consensus of secular and religious historians of science alike, who regard the Draper-White warfare narrative as a polemical construction that does not survive contact with the evidence.

And the evidence dismantles it piece by piece. Consider the myths the conflict thesis rests upon. The claim that medieval Christians believed the earth was flat, and that Columbus proved them wrong against clerical opposition — pure fiction; educated medievals knew perfectly well the earth was a sphere, as had been known since antiquity. The claim that the Church banned human dissection and thereby crippled anatomy — false. The claim that Galileo was tortured and imprisoned in a dungeon — false. The claim that the Church suppressed the study of nature as a threat to faith — the opposite of the truth, as this unit will show. A landmark collection of essays by historians of science, surveying the popular myths one by one, found that the dramatic stories of religion crushing science are, again and again, either grossly exaggerated or entirely invented. The warfare narrative is a house built on fables.

## Entangled, Not at War

If not warfare, then what? The most illuminating recent account comes from Nicholas Spencer, whose 2023 history *Magisteria: The Entangled Histories of Science and Religion* offers a word that captures the real relationship far better than "conflict": *entanglement*.[^1] The true history of science and religion, Spencer argues, is not a story of two combatants but of two things woven together — intertwined for centuries, sometimes in tension, often in active cooperation, always entangled. Religious institutions funded and housed the study of nature; religious convictions motivated it; religious people did most of it. There were frictions and there were genuine conflicts, but they were episodes within a long relationship of profound entanglement, not battles in a perpetual war.

Spencer's title is a deliberate play on the biologist Stephen Jay Gould's proposal of "non-overlapping magisteria" — the idea that science and religion occupy neatly separate domains that need never touch. Spencer's history shows that this tidy separation is as false as the warfare story: the two have never been sealed off from each other, but have been messily, humanly, productively entangled throughout. The relationship was not war, and it was not cold peace across a fixed border. It was a centuries-long entanglement in which, for most of the story, religion was not science's enemy but its matrix — the soil in which it grew. And that reframing opens the door to the genuinely surprising claim this unit exists to make.

## The Reverse Trap, Applied to Science

Readers who have come this far in the course will recognize what the conflict thesis really is: it is the *reverse trap* from an earlier lesson, applied to the history of science. The reverse trap is the reflexive assumption that Christianity could only ever have obstructed the good things of the modern world — that wherever we find something valuable, religion must have been its enemy. Applied to science, this reflex produces the warfare narrative automatically: since science is good and modern, and religion is (in this reflex) backward and obstructive, the two must have been at war, and science must have won by defeating religion. The conclusion is reached not by examining the evidence but by applying the prejudice.

Clearing away this reflex is the necessary first step, because as long as we assume Christianity was science's enemy, we cannot even see the possibility that it was science's *source*. The warfare narrative functions as a kind of blindfold: it tells us in advance what we will find, so we never look. Remove it, and a strange and initially counterintuitive landscape comes into view — one in which the fundamental convictions that made modern science possible turn out to be Christian theological convictions, in which the founders of science were overwhelmingly devout believers who understood their work as worship, and in which the very expectation that nature is orderly and intelligible descended from a doctrine of creation. That is the landscape the rest of this unit will explore. But we could not enter it while the warfare story stood guard at the gate, and so we have spent this lesson tearing the story down.

## Why the Myth Persists

If the conflict thesis is such bad history, and if the professional historians abandoned it long ago, why does it remain the default assumption of educated people? The answer is that the myth serves purposes that have nothing to do with its accuracy. It persists, first, because it is the indispensable backbone of a particular story modern culture loves to tell about itself: the story of *progress* as liberation, in which humanity climbs out of the darkness of religious superstition into the light of secular reason, with science as the torch. In that grand narrative, religion must play the role of the darkness that science dispels; the warfare thesis supplies exactly that role, and so it is retold not because the evidence supports it but because the story requires it.

It persists, second, because it is *dramatic*. A tale of brave scientists defying a cruel and obscurantist Church — Galileo before the Inquisition, the lone truth-teller against the massed forces of dogma — is far more thrilling than the messy truth of centuries of entanglement, patronage, and mutual influence. Drama travels; nuance does not. And it persists, third, because it is *taught* — absorbed early and repeated everywhere, in textbooks, documentaries, and casual reference, until it becomes part of the mental furniture that no one thinks to question. Most people who "know" that religion warred against science have never examined the claim; they have simply inherited it, the way one inherits an accent. This is the mechanism the whole course has been tracing, now operating in reverse: an assumption so pervasive and so unexamined that it feels like plain fact, when it is in truth a particular and questionable inheritance. To see the Christian roots of science, one must first become conscious of the warfare myth *as* a myth — must notice the water — and that is exactly what makes this the hardest domain of all to see clearly.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Intellectual honesty requires immediately guarding against an overcorrection, because to say the conflict thesis is "mostly fiction" is not to say the relationship was one of perfect harmony. That would be its own myth, and just as false. There *were* real conflicts. The Galileo affair, whatever its complexities — and it has many, which a later lesson will examine honestly — was a genuine clash in which Church authority did suppress a scientific claim. There was real Christian resistance to Darwin, real friction over the age of the earth, real moments when religious authorities obstructed inquiry or punished those who pursued it. Christianity has not been an unbroken friend to science, and a triumphalist account that pretended otherwise would deserve the same skepticism we have aimed at the warfare narrative.

The honest claim is more precise, and it is the claim historians actually make. It is not that conflict never occurred, but that *perpetual, essential warfare* is a gross distortion of a relationship whose dominant mode was entanglement and, often, active support. The conflicts were real but episodic, particular, and frequently more about authority, politics, and biblical interpretation than about "science versus religion" as such. To replace the warfare myth with a harmony myth would be to swap one falsehood for another; the truth is the messier, entangled middle, in which Christianity was sometimes an obstacle to inquiry and, far more often and more fundamentally, its patron and its wellspring. With the warfare narrative cleared away and the entanglement acknowledged, we can now turn to the first and deepest of the Christian convictions that made science possible: the belief that behind the world stands a rational mind, and that nature is therefore something the human mind can read.
