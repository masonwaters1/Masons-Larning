Modern science rests on an assumption so fundamental that we almost never notice we are making it — the assumption that nature is *orderly*, that it is governed by consistent laws, and that those laws are *intelligible* to the human mind. Every experiment ever performed assumes that the universe behaves regularly, that the same causes will produce the same effects tomorrow as today, and that a human being can, by investigation, come to understand the hidden order of things. This assumption is the precondition of all science. And here is what should stop us: it is not self-evident, it cannot be proved, and it did not have to be believed. It is, in the strictest sense, an article of faith — and the faith from which it descended was Christian.

## The Unprovable Foundation

Ask why the universe should be orderly, and you will find no answer that science itself can supply, because science must *assume* the order before it can investigate anything at all. That nature is law-governed rather than chaotic; that its regularities are consistent across time and space; that the human mind is capable of grasping them — none of this can be established by experiment, because experiment already depends on all of it being true. These are the foundations beneath the foundation, the beliefs that make the whole enterprise possible and that the enterprise itself can never justify. The scientist takes them on trust, and could not take a single step without doing so.

This is not a criticism of science; it is simply an honest account of what science stands on. Every practicing scientist lives by the conviction that the universe is a *cosmos* — an ordered, rational whole — and not a chaos, and that this order is accessible to reason. But where did this conviction come from? Why should anyone have expected the universe to be orderly and comprehensible in the first place? It is a very particular expectation, and most human cultures across most of history did not hold it in the form that science requires. The expectation had to come from somewhere, and its historical source is a specific theological doctrine: the doctrine of a single, rational, faithful Creator.

## The God Behind the Order

Consider what follows from the Christian doctrine of creation. If the universe was made by a *single* God, then it is one unified system, not a battleground of competing divine powers, and so it can be expected to have a single coherent order. If that God is *rational* — if he creates by wisdom, by *logos*, by reason — then his creation will bear the marks of rationality, will be structured intelligibly, will make sense. If that God is *faithful* and *consistent* rather than capricious — if he does not change his mind or act arbitrarily — then the order he has established will be dependable, its regularities holding firm as reliable laws rather than shifting with divine whim. And if human beings are made *in the image* of this rational God, then their minds are fitted to grasp the rational order he has made. A rational Creator, a rational creation, and a rational creature to read it: the whole framework of scientific expectation is present, in embryo, in the doctrine of creation.

This is why the conviction that made science possible was, historically, a Christian conviction. The belief that nature is an ordered, law-governed, intelligible cosmos was not a neutral, universal human assumption; it was the natural expectation of people who believed the world was the deliberate creation of a single rational and faithful God. They expected to find order in nature *because* they believed a rational God had put it there, and they expected to be able to understand that order *because* they believed their minds were made in his image to do so. The scientific expectation is a theological inheritance, and the founders of science knew it.

## Whitehead's Argument

The most famous statement of this claim comes from an unexpected source: the mathematician and philosopher Alfred North Whitehead, no orthodox believer, who argued in the 1920s that the very possibility of science had a medieval and theological origin.[^1] Science, Whitehead observed, requires an unshakable faith that every event can be connected to its antecedents in a perfectly definite way, that nature is thoroughly and reliably ordered. Where did this faith come from? He answered: from the medieval insistence on the *rationality of God*. Medieval theology conceived God as combining the personal energy of the God of Israel with the rationality of a Greek philosopher, and this fusion produced the conviction that every detail of the world was supervised and ordered by a rational mind — a conviction that, once it had soaked deep into the European imagination, generated the confident expectation that nature could be rationally searched and understood.

Whitehead's point was that this faith in the order of nature had to precede science and make it possible; it could not be a *product* of science, since science already presupposes it. And that prior faith, he argued, was the long unconscious deposit of medieval theology — the centuries-long insistence that behind the world stood a rational God whose creation must therefore be rationally ordered. The scientists of the seventeenth century inherited this expectation as a settled instinct, so deeply held that they no longer noticed it as a belief at all. It had become, precisely, the water they swam in — an assumption about the world's intelligibility so total that it felt like simple common sense, though it was in fact the residue of a particular theology.

## The Theology of "Laws of Nature"

Nothing shows this inheritance more clearly than the phrase we use without a second thought: the *laws of nature*. We speak of natural laws as if the metaphor were transparent, but stop and consider it. A *law* implies a *lawgiver* — a legislator who decrees it, an authority who imposes it. The very concept of nature as governed by laws is a theological metaphor, and it is no accident that it emerged, in its modern scientific form, in Christian Europe. When Descartes and Newton spoke of the laws of nature, they meant, quite explicitly, the decrees that God had imposed upon his creation — the rules the divine legislator had ordained for the behavior of matter. The idea that the universe runs according to universal, mathematical laws was, at its origin, the idea that a rational God had written those laws into the fabric of creation.

Other cultures observed regularities in nature; every advanced civilization noticed that the heavens moved predictably and that events followed patterns. But the specific and powerful concept of *universal laws of nature* — precise, mathematical rules ordained by a rational legislator and governing all of matter everywhere — is distinctively a product of Christian monotheism, and it did the heavy lifting in the birth of modern physics. To look for the laws of nature is already to assume that nature is the kind of thing that *has* laws — that behind it stands a lawgiver who has ordered it rationally. The scientists who launched the modern investigation of nature were, in the most literal sense, searching for the legislation of God.

## The Founders Knew It

This was not, for the early scientists, a vague background sentiment; it was the explicit motivation and framework of their work. Johannes Kepler described his discovery of the mathematical order of the heavens as *thinking God's thoughts after him* — tracing the geometric harmonies that the divine mind had used in designing creation. Isaac Newton presented the magnificent order of the cosmos revealed by his physics as direct evidence of an intelligent and powerful God, and he meant it as the crown of his work, not an afterthought. Robert Boyle, Kepler, Newton, and their contemporaries pursued the study of nature *as* a theological enterprise, uncovering the rational order that a rational God had established, and reading in the lawfulness of the world the signature of its rational Author.

To these men, the intelligibility of nature was not a lucky accident or a brute fact to be shrugged at; it was exactly what one should expect if the world was the creation of a rational God, and its investigation was a way of honoring him. Their science was continuous with their faith, and their faith supplied the confidence that the search would succeed — that nature *would* prove orderly and intelligible, because the God who made it was rational and had made human minds to understand it. The expectation that made science possible and the theology that grounded it were, for the founders, one and the same, and they would have found the modern separation of the two not merely wrong but unintelligible.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Two important qualifications keep this argument honest. The first is that the conviction of a rational, ordered cosmos was not *exclusively* Christian. The ancient Greeks, above all, believed in a rational cosmos governed by *logos*, and they contributed enormously to the intellectual preconditions of science — the confidence in reason, the ideal of rational explanation, the mathematical study of nature. Christianity did not invent the idea of a rational order from nothing; rather, it *fused* the Greek conviction of a rational cosmos with the biblical doctrine of a personal, free, faithful Creator, and it was this particular fusion — Greek rationality married to Hebrew creation — that produced the distinctive expectation modern science required. The debt is real, but it is a debt to a synthesis, and the Greek half of it must be credited alongside the Christian.

The second qualification is that belief in an orderly cosmos, while *necessary* for science, was evidently not *sufficient* for it — because the Greeks held it and did not produce modern science, and neither did other cultures with strong convictions of natural order. Something more was needed, and the following lessons will supply it: the specific ideas of the world's contingency, the sanctifying of empirical investigation, the institutions that housed inquiry. The rational-Creator conviction was the deepest precondition, the assumption beneath all the others, but it was one ingredient in a larger recipe, and the mere presence of an orderly cosmos in a culture's imagination did not by itself yield science. We should therefore say, carefully, that the Christian doctrine of a rational Creator supplied the foundational expectation on which science was built — not that it single-handedly caused science, and not that it did so alone. With the foundation of order established, we turn to the ingredient that turned the expectation of order into the practice of experiment: the strange and fertile idea that the world did not have to be the way it is.
