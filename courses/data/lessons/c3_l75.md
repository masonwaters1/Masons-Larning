The previous lesson explained why the founders of science expected nature to be orderly. But order alone does not tell you *how* to study nature — and here the history takes a subtle and fascinating turn. For there are two very different ways one might investigate an orderly world, and the difference between them is the difference between ancient natural philosophy and modern experimental science. You might believe that the order of nature can be worked out by *pure reason*, deduced from first principles the way one proves a theorem in geometry. Or you might believe that the order of nature must be *discovered by looking* — by observation and experiment, because reason alone cannot tell you which of the many possible worlds God actually chose to make. Modern science took the second path, and it took it, this lesson argues, because of a Christian doctrine about the freedom of God.

## Two Ways to Study an Ordered World

The ancient Greeks, for all their genius, largely believed that nature could be understood by reason working from first principles. If the cosmos is rational, the thinking went, then a sufficiently rational mind should be able to deduce how it must be — to reason out, from self-evident starting points, the necessary structure of reality. Aristotle's physics, which dominated Western thought for nearly two millennia, proceeded largely in this way: it established what *must* be true of motion, matter, and the heavens by rational argument, and it treated the results as necessary truths, the way the world *had* to be. Experiment, in the modern sense of deliberately interrogating nature to see what it actually does, played a relatively minor role, because if you can deduce how nature must behave, you do not need to go and check.

This is a coherent and powerful approach, and it produced magnificent intellectual systems. But it has a fatal limitation for science: it assumes that the world is the way it is by *necessity* — that reality could not have been otherwise, and can therefore be worked out by pure thought. And this assumption, modern science discovered, is false. Nature is full of features that could perfectly well have been different, that no amount of armchair reasoning could ever have predicted, and that can be known only by observation. To do science, one has to abandon the confidence that nature can be deduced, and accept the humbler, more laborious task of finding out what nature actually does. That shift — from deducing the necessary to discovering the contingent — is the birth of the experimental method. And it required a particular belief about the world: that it is *contingent*, one possibility among many that might have been.

## The Freedom of God

Where did the belief in the world's contingency come from? From the Christian doctrine of God's *freedom* and *omnipotence*. If God created the world by a free act of will — if he was not compelled by necessity to make it any particular way, but *chose* to make it thus when he could have made it otherwise — then the world is contingent. It is one of many possible worlds God might have created, and there is no way to know which one he actually chose except by examining it. You cannot deduce a free choice; you can only discover it. And so a world made by a free God is a world that must be investigated empirically, because reason alone cannot tell you what a free Creator freely decided.

This is the crucial theological move, and its logic is precise. Against the idea that the world had to be as it is — that even God was constrained by rational necessity to create in a particular way — Christian theologians increasingly insisted on the *absolute power and freedom* of God: God could have made the world with different laws, different arrangements, different natures, and it is sheer presumption to claim that human reason can dictate to the Almighty what he must have done. This doctrine, which the historian of the previous course-unit's Gillespie material would recognize as theological *voluntarism*, had a momentous and unintended consequence. By insisting that God was free to create any world he chose, it made *this* world radically contingent — and thereby made empirical investigation not just useful but *necessary*. If you cannot presume to know what God must have done, you have no choice but to go and see what he actually did.

## The Condemnation That Freed Inquiry

This is not an abstract reconstruction; it played out in a concrete and pivotal episode. In the year 1277, the Bishop of Paris issued a sweeping condemnation of a long list of propositions being taught in the university — many of them Aristotelian claims about what was and was not possible.[^1] Among the condemned propositions were assertions that limited God's power: that God could not create a vacuum, that God could not make more than one world, that certain things were impossible even for God because they violated Aristotelian principles. The condemnation declared these limits on divine power to be errors. God, it insisted, *could* create a vacuum if he wished, *could* make multiple worlds, *could* do anything not involving outright contradiction, regardless of what Aristotle's philosophy said was possible.

The effect of this assertion of God's absolute power was, paradoxically, to *liberate* natural philosophy. By insisting that God could have made the world in ways Aristotle deemed impossible, the condemnation licensed thinkers to *imagine alternatives* to the Aristotelian system — to ask, "But what if the world were otherwise? What if there could be a vacuum, or multiple worlds, or motion of a kind Aristotle forbade?" It broke the grip of Aristotelian necessity and opened a space of possibility that could be explored. Some medieval natural philosophers, freed to consider what Aristotle had ruled out, began to develop ideas — about motion, about the possibility of the earth's rotation, about the void — that pointed beyond Aristotle toward later science. The early historian of medieval science Pierre Duhem was so struck by this that he provocatively dated the birth of modern science to 1277. That claim is surely overstated. But the underlying point is sound: the theological insistence on God's freedom to make the world otherwise cracked open the closed world of rational necessity and pointed toward a world that had to be *investigated* rather than merely deduced.

## Interrogating Nature

By the seventeenth century, this had matured into the explicit program of the experimental method. Francis Bacon, the great herald of the new science, framed the investigation of nature in exactly these terms: because we cannot know in advance what God has done, we must *interrogate* nature, put it to the question, extract its secrets by deliberate experiment rather than spin theories from our own heads. The early members of the Royal Society understood their experimental work as the reading of God's contingent creation — a creation whose actual character could be learned only by patient observation, because a free God might have made it in any number of ways, and had to be studied to discover which.

The contrast with the older approach is the whole point. The armchair philosopher, confident that nature could be deduced, had little reason to get his hands dirty with experiment. The Christian natural philosopher, convinced that nature was the contingent product of a free divine will, *had* to experiment, because that was the only way to discover what a free God had actually chosen to do. Humility before the freedom of God translated directly into empirical humility before nature: since we cannot presume to know what God must have made, we must go and find out what he did make. The experimental method — the defining practice of modern science — grew, on this account, out of a theological conviction about the freedom and power of the Creator. What looks like the most secular of activities, the laboratory experiment, has a lineage that runs back to a doctrine about the will of God.

## The Honest Counterpoint

This is one of the most elegant arguments in the whole case for the Christian roots of science, and precisely because it is elegant, it must be handled with care, for the "voluntarism thesis" is genuinely contested among historians. Some object that it is too neat — that it draws a straight causal line from a subtle theological doctrine to a complex historical development that had many other sources. They point out that not all the key figures of the Scientific Revolution were strict theological voluntarists, that some held quite different views of God and nature, and that the rise of experiment also owed much to craft traditions, technological advance, and the practical arts, which had nothing to do with debates about divine omnipotence. The experimental method, on this view, had many parents, and voluntarist theology was at most one of them.

These are fair cautions, and they should temper any claim that voluntarist theology *by itself* produced experimental science. The honest position is that the doctrine of a free God and a contingent creation was a real and significant *contributor* to the emergence of the experimental outlook — a genuine strand in the tangle of causes, and a strand that helps explain why empirical investigation came to seem necessary — without being the sole or sufficient cause. And, as with the previous lesson, the contingency argument depends on the order argument beneath it: a world must be both *ordered* (so that investigation can find stable laws) and *contingent* (so that investigation is necessary to find them), and it was the combination — a rational God who created freely — that supplied both conditions at once. That combination, distinctive to Christian theology, is the deep source of the peculiar modern conviction that nature is both lawful and surprising, both intelligible and unpredictable in advance: exactly the conviction that makes experimental science both possible and necessary. We turn next to the idea that made the whole enterprise not merely permissible but a form of devotion — the vision of nature as a book that God had written.
