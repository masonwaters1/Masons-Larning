We have seen why the founders of science expected nature to be orderly, and why they believed it had to be investigated rather than deduced. But a question remains that is easy to overlook from our vantage, where the value of studying nature is simply assumed: *why bother?* Why should serious, devout people, in a culture oriented toward the salvation of the soul and the worship of God, have poured their lives into the painstaking examination of rocks, stars, insects, and the motion of falling bodies? What made the study of nature seem not a distraction from the things that mattered, but one of the most important things a person could do? The answer lies in a single, powerful metaphor that pervaded Christian thought for centuries: the idea that God has written *two books*.

## God's Two Books

The metaphor runs like this. God reveals himself to humanity in two books. The first is the Book of Scripture — the Bible, God's revelation in words. The second is the Book of Nature — creation itself, God's revelation in things. Both books have the same divine Author; both are means by which God discloses himself; and both, therefore, are worthy of the most careful and reverent study. To read the Book of Scripture is to learn of God through his words. To read the Book of Nature is to learn of God through his works. The naturalist bent over a specimen and the theologian bent over a text are engaged in the same fundamental activity: reading what God has written, seeking to know their Creator through what he has disclosed.

This metaphor, which appears in Augustine and becomes pervasive in medieval and early modern Christianity, did something of enormous consequence: it made the study of nature a *religious act*. To investigate creation was not to turn away from God toward mere matter; it was to read God's other book, to trace the wisdom and power of the Creator in the intricate order of his works, to worship him by understanding what he had made. Far from being a distraction from piety, the study of nature became an expression of it — a way of glorifying God by attending to the magnificent text he had written in the language of creation. The metaphor sanctified science before science existed, giving the investigation of nature the highest possible motivation and the deepest possible legitimacy.

## Nature as a Text to Be Read

Notice what the metaphor implies about nature itself. A book is not a random heap of marks; it is *authored*, *meaningful*, *ordered*, and *legible*. To describe nature as a book is to say that it has an author (God), that it is meaningful (it says something), that it is ordered (books have structure), and that it can be read (it is intelligible to a properly trained reader). The two-books metaphor thus reinforced everything we established in the previous lessons: it framed nature as the intelligible, ordered composition of a rational Author, waiting to be deciphered by minds capable of reading it. Investigation became a kind of reading, and discovery a kind of understanding — the grasping of what the divine Author had written.

This framing gave scientific work a shape and a confidence it might otherwise have lacked. If nature is a book, then it *can* be read, and the reading will yield meaning rather than confusion — because a wise Author does not write nonsense. The naturalist approached the world expecting it to make sense, expecting his labor to be rewarded with intelligible order, because he believed he was reading a text composed by infinite wisdom. And he approached it with reverence, because the text he was reading was divine. The metaphor supplied both the confidence that nature would prove legible and the devotion that made the long labor of reading it feel worthwhile. It is hard to overstate how much motivational and intellectual work this single image did in the centuries when modern science was being born.

## Science as Worship

For the early scientists, this was not decorative language but the actual engine of their work. They studied nature *to glorify God* — to display his wisdom, his power, his goodness, as revealed in the order and intricacy of creation. Robert Boyle, one of the founders of modern chemistry, considered the study of nature a religious duty and himself a "priest" of nature, offering up the knowledge of creation as an act of worship. The naturalist John Ray wrote a hugely influential work titled *The Wisdom of God Manifested in the Works of Creation*, in which the detailed study of plants and animals served to reveal the wisdom of their Creator. For these men and countless others, the more deeply one understood nature, the more clearly one saw the mind of God behind it, and so the study of nature was a form of praise.

This doxological motivation — science as worship — was a tremendous spur to actual scientific labor. It is worth remembering how much painstaking, tedious work the early science required: the endless observations, the careful measurements, the meticulous cataloguing of species, the long nights of astronomy. What sustained such labor? For many of its practitioners, the conviction that in doing it they were reading God's book and glorifying its Author. The religious motivation did not merely permit science; it *drove* it, supplying the devotion and the sense of ultimate purpose that carried men through decades of exacting work. The founders of modern science were, in large part, worshippers, and their science was their worship.

## Natural Theology

Out of the two-books metaphor grew a whole tradition called *natural theology* — the project of arguing from the order and design of nature to the existence and character of God. If nature is God's book, then reading it should reveal its Author; and natural theologians set out to show precisely that, arguing from the exquisite adaptation of living things, the order of the heavens, and the intricate contrivances of the natural world to the wisdom and power of the Creator who made them. The most famous version is William Paley's image of the watch found on a heath: just as a watch, with its evident design, implies a watchmaker, so the far greater design of nature implies a divine Designer.

Natural theology, whatever one makes of its arguments, was an extraordinary motivator of scientific investigation. Because the design argument required detailed knowledge of nature's contrivances to make its case, it spurred enormous labor in natural history, anatomy, and astronomy: every newly discovered adaptation, every intricate structure, every elegant natural law became fresh evidence of the Creator's wisdom, and so the study of nature was pursued with religious zeal as a means of displaying God's glory. For roughly two centuries, much of the detailed empirical study of the living world was carried out under the banner of natural theology, by people convinced that in cataloguing the wonders of creation they were reading the mind of God. The tradition would later be complicated, and its central argument shaken, by Darwin — a story this unit will take up in its own place. But for the crucial formative centuries, natural theology harnessed religious devotion to the patient study of nature, and the harnessing was immensely productive.

## The Naturalist in Holy Orders

Nothing illustrates the two-books vision more concretely than the sheer number of clergymen among the founders and practitioners of natural science. For centuries, the study of nature was, to a remarkable degree, a *clerical vocation* — pursued by monks, priests, canons, and country parsons who saw no tension between their holy orders and their investigation of creation, because the investigation of creation was, for them, part of the vocation. John Ray, whose natural history laid foundations for modern biology and taxonomy, was an ordained clergyman who understood his cataloguing of species as the display of God's wisdom. Nicolaus Steno, a pioneer of geology and anatomy, became a bishop. Gregor Mendel, who worked out the laws of heredity, was an Augustinian friar conducting his experiments in a monastery garden. Nicolaus Copernicus was a cathedral canon.

The pattern extended far down into the ordinary texture of scientific life. The English country parson, with his living secured, his leisure, his education, and his conviction that studying nature glorified its Author, became one of the great engines of natural history — the parson-naturalist collecting beetles, cataloguing plants, recording the weather and the birds, contributing a vast body of careful observation to the growing store of natural knowledge. These were not men doing science in defiance of their faith or in some compartment sealed off from it. They were doing science *because* of their faith, as an expression of it, reading God's second book as naturally as they read his first. The clerical naturalist is not an anomaly to be explained away but the characteristic figure of the age in which modern science was born — living proof that the study of nature grew, for centuries, directly out of Christian devotion, and was carried on in large part by the Church's own servants.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Two honest qualifications are needed. The first is that the two-books metaphor, for all its power to motivate and legitimate science, could also *constrain* it — because when the two books seemed to *conflict*, the question arose of which took priority, and the answer was not always favorable to nature. When a scientific reading of the Book of Nature appeared to contradict a traditional reading of the Book of Scripture, some authorities insisted that Scripture must govern, and used the Bible to limit or reject the conclusions of natural investigation. The Galileo affair, which a later lesson examines, turned in part on exactly this question of how to handle an apparent conflict between the two books. So the metaphor was double-edged: it sanctified the study of nature, but it also raised the possibility of subordinating nature's book to a particular interpretation of Scripture's, and that subordination could and sometimes did obstruct inquiry.

The second qualification is that the religious motivation of the early scientists, real and productive as it was, does not by itself prove that science *requires* religion, or that only religious people can do science. It shows that, historically, religious devotion was a powerful driver of scientific work in the era when science was born — that the two-books vision supplied motivation and legitimacy at a crucial formative moment. It does not follow that the motivation cannot be replaced, or that science, once established, could not continue on other foundations; manifestly it has continued in the hands of many who do not share the founders' faith. The honest claim is historical: that in the centuries when modern science was taking shape, the vision of nature as God's second book gave the enterprise a motivation and a dignity that helped bring it into being — not that science is forever dependent on that vision. With the motivation for studying nature established, we turn to the institution that gave the study of nature a home, a method, and a continuous tradition: the medieval university.
