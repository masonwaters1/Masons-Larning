Ask most educated people what happened to science during the thousand years between the fall of Rome and the Scientific Revolution, and you will get a one-word answer: nothing. The Middle Ages, in the popular imagination, were the "Dark Ages" — a long night of superstition and stagnation, in which the Church smothered inquiry, learning was lost, and the human mind slept, until the Renaissance and then the Scientific Revolution finally reawakened it. This picture is one of the most durable myths in our culture, and it is almost entirely false. The demolition of it has been the life's work of a number of historians of medieval science, and one book in particular has carried the argument to a wide readership. It is James Hannam's *God's Philosophers* — published in the United States under the fitting title *The Genesis of Science* — and it tells a story that overturns the myth of the medieval dark.

## The Myth of the Dark Ages

The "Dark Ages" narrative is a close cousin of the warfare thesis, and it serves the same function: it casts the Christian centuries as the enemy of knowledge, so that the eventual triumph of science can be told as a liberation from religion. On this telling, medieval people were credulous simpletons who thought the earth was flat, who burned anyone who questioned dogma, who made no scientific progress because the Church forbade it, and whose thousand years of darkness were simply a gap to be skipped over on the way from the glory of Greece to the dawn of modern science. It is a story of a civilization that knew nothing and discovered nothing, held in intellectual bondage by a fearful and obscurantist faith.

Hannam's central argument, following a tradition of medieval-science scholarship going back a century, is that this picture is a fabrication — that the Middle Ages were not a scientific wasteland but a period of real and cumulative progress that laid the essential foundations for the Scientific Revolution.[^1] The medieval natural philosophers, far from sleeping, were hard at work: studying, translating, debating, and genuinely advancing the understanding of nature. The Scientific Revolution, when it came, did not spring from nowhere after a thousand-year void; it built directly on medieval foundations, using tools, concepts, and results that medieval scholars had developed. To skip over the Middle Ages is to skip over the very period in which the groundwork of modern science was laid.

## The Medieval Scientists

Consider the people the myth erases. Robert Grosseteste, a churchman and eventually a bishop, in the early thirteenth century developed ideas about the role of mathematics and experiment in the study of nature, and did important work in optics. His successor Roger Bacon championed observation and experiment and pursued optics further still. In the fourteenth century, a remarkable group of scholars at Merton College, Oxford — the "Oxford Calculators" — brought mathematics to bear on the study of motion, and formulated what is known as the *mean speed theorem*, a genuine result in kinematics describing uniformly accelerated motion.

On the continent, Jean Buridan developed the theory of *impetus* — the idea that a thrown object continues moving because it has been given an internal impetus that carries it along — a concept that broke with Aristotle's physics and pointed toward the later idea of inertia. Buridan's pupil Nicole Oresme was a figure of astonishing range: he developed graphical methods for representing changing quantities, worked with the mean speed theorem, seriously entertained the possibility that the earth rotates on its axis and argued that the available evidence could not decisively refute it, and mounted a sharp critique of astrology. These were not superstitious simpletons; they were sophisticated natural philosophers making real advances in physics, astronomy, and mathematics — advances that fed directly into the work of later figures. When Galileo analyzed accelerated motion, he was using the mean speed theorem that the medieval Oxford Calculators had formulated centuries before.

## Continuity, Not Rupture

This points to the heart of Hannam's argument: the relationship between medieval science and the Scientific Revolution is one of *continuity*, not rupture. The revolution of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries did not repudiate a dark medieval past and start fresh; it grew out of medieval developments, extending and transforming a tradition of natural philosophy that had been advancing for centuries. The medieval critique of Aristotle — the questioning of his physics, the exploration of alternatives licensed by the theological developments we examined earlier — prepared the ground for the more radical departures to come. The impetus theory pointed toward inertia; the mathematical analysis of motion pointed toward modern kinematics; the questioning of the earth's fixity pointed toward Copernicus. The seeds of the revolution were sown in the medieval universities.

This continuity thesis reframes the whole story of how science began. Instead of a sudden miraculous awakening after a thousand-year sleep, we see a long, cumulative development, stretching from the medieval recovery of Greek learning, through centuries of natural-philosophical work in the universities, to the flowering of the Scientific Revolution — a continuous tradition, carried on largely by Christian scholars in Christian institutions, building steadily toward the transformation that would eventually come. The "genesis" of science, in Hannam's title, lies not in a sudden seventeenth-century rejection of religion but in the long medieval labor that made the seventeenth century possible.

## Debunking the Fables

Hannam devotes considerable energy to dismantling the specific fables on which the Dark Ages myth rests, and the demolition is bracing. The claim that medieval people believed the earth was flat: false — educated medievals universally knew the earth was a sphere, and taught it in the universities. The claim that the Church banned human dissection and thereby crippled medicine: false — dissection was practiced in medieval universities. The claim that Galileo was tortured in the Inquisition's dungeons: false. The claim that the Church systematically suppressed science as a threat to faith: the opposite of the truth, as the Church's patronage of the universities and natural philosophy demonstrates. One by one, the dramatic stories of religion crushing medieval science turn out, on examination, to be either wild exaggerations or complete inventions, many of them manufactured in the same nineteenth-century polemical moment that produced the warfare thesis itself.

The cumulative effect of this debunking is to reveal how much of our received picture of the medieval mind is not history but propaganda — a story told by people with an interest in casting the Christian past as the enemy of reason, repeated so often that it became common sense. When the fables are cleared away and the actual record examined, what remains is a medieval civilization that preserved and extended ancient learning, built the institutions of higher study, made real advances in the sciences, and handed to the modern world the foundations on which the Scientific Revolution was built. The Dark Ages, in the sense the myth intends, never happened.

## The Church That Funded Astronomy

One concrete example dismantles the warfare myth more effectively than any general argument: the Catholic Church was, for centuries, one of the most generous patrons of astronomy in the world — and it funded that astronomy for a religious reason. The Church needed to know the precise date of Easter, which depends on the motions of the sun and moon, and the drift of the old Roman calendar had thrown the reckoning into disorder. Solving the problem required serious astronomical work, and the Church paid for it. The reform that produced the Gregorian calendar we use today, promulgated in 1582, was the fruit of decades of ecclesiastically sponsored astronomical research.

To carry out this work, the Church did something extraordinary: it turned some of its greatest cathedrals into astronomical instruments. Precise lines called meridians were laid into the floors of cathedrals, with a small aperture high in the structure admitting a beam of sunlight, so that the building itself became a giant solar observatory for tracking the sun's motion across the year with great accuracy. The historian who studies these "meridian cathedrals" finds, quite literally, astronomy being done inside churches, funded by the Church, in the service of the Church's own liturgical needs. Here is the warfare thesis reduced to rubble by a single fact: the institution supposedly at war with science built astronomical observatories into its holiest buildings and paid for generations of research into the heavens. The medieval and early modern Church was not science's enemy but, in astronomy as in so much else, its patron — and the cathedral floor, marked with the track of the sun, is a monument to the entanglement that the true history reveals.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Balance requires two significant qualifications, and the first is that Hannam, a popular historian with a case to make, can be read as leaning too far in the corrective direction — as overstating both the extent of medieval scientific achievement and the degree of its continuity with the Scientific Revolution. Some professional historians, while entirely agreeing that the "Dark Ages" myth is false, caution that the enthusiasm to rehabilitate the Middle Ages can obscure the genuine novelty and magnitude of the seventeenth-century transformation. The Scientific Revolution *was* a revolution — a real and profound change in methods, institutions, and the scale of achievement, not merely medieval science continued by other means. Medieval natural philosophy, real and valuable as it was, remained in crucial respects bounded by its Aristotelian framework and fell well short of what the seventeenth century accomplished. The truth lies between the myth of a dark medieval void and any suggestion that the revolution added little the Middle Ages had not already begun.

The second qualification is the recurring one: the "medieval origins" thesis, in its stronger forms, has been overstated before — Pierre Duhem's dating of modern science to 1277 being the classic example — and we should resist the temptation to collapse the genuine achievement of the Scientific Revolution into its medieval antecedents. The honest position is that Hannam and the scholarship he popularizes have decisively demolished the "Dark Ages" myth and established that medieval Christendom laid essential foundations for modern science — the institutions, the recovered learning, the critical engagement with Aristotle, the real advances in physics and mathematics — while the Scientific Revolution remained a genuine and momentous transformation that those foundations made possible but did not simply contain. Medieval Christian civilization did not produce modern science; it produced the soil, the seed, and the long cultivation from which modern science eventually grew. Having traced that long preparation, we are ready, in the lessons to come, to meet the revolution itself — and to see that the men who made it were, almost to a person, the devout heirs of everything this unit has described.
