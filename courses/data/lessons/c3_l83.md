The warfare thesis has a second great exhibit, and if anything it looms larger in the modern mind than Galileo: Charles Darwin. The story, as everyone knows it, is that Darwin's theory of evolution by natural selection disproved the biblical account of creation, exposed the design argument as an illusion, and provoked religion into a doomed rear-guard war against the plain truth of science — a war supposedly climaxing in the famous debate where science humiliated a bishop, and continuing to this day in the trenches of creationism. Evolution is taken to be the moment when science and religion could no longer coexist, when the choice between them became stark. And once again, the actual history is far messier, far more interesting, and far less obliging to the warfare thesis than the legend allows.

## Christians Who Embraced Evolution

The first fact the myth erases is that a great many Christians — including devout, orthodox, and even conservative ones — accepted evolution, often readily, and integrated it into their faith. In America, Darwin's most important early champion was Asa Gray, the Harvard botanist, who was both a committed Darwinian and a sincere and orthodox Christian, and who argued vigorously that evolution was compatible with belief in a purposeful Creator. In England, the Anglican clergyman and novelist Charles Kingsley responded to Darwin with something close to enthusiasm, writing that it was a still grander conception of God to believe he had made a world that could make itself — that a Deity who created organisms with the power to develop was more magnificent, not less, than one who had to fashion each separately. Even among theological conservatives, evolution found acceptance: the great Princeton theologian B. B. Warfield, a defender of biblical inerrancy, was open to evolution as the method of God's creating.

These were not marginal figures or desperate compromisers; they were serious Christians who saw no necessary contradiction between evolution and their faith, and who worked out ways of holding both. Their existence is fatal to the simple story of religion uniformly resisting evolution, because a substantial and respectable body of religious opinion did the opposite: it welcomed evolution and folded it into a theology of a God who creates through natural processes. The reception of Darwin among Christians was not a wall of rejection; it was varied, thoughtful, and often accommodating.

## The Resistance That Came from Science

The second fact the myth erases is that much of the serious early resistance to Darwin came not from religion but from *science itself*. Darwin's theory, when it appeared, faced substantial and legitimate scientific objections that had nothing to do with Genesis. The mechanism of heredity was unknown — Darwin had no idea how traits were actually passed on or how variation was preserved rather than blended away, and this was a genuine and serious gap that would not be filled until the rediscovery of genetics decades later. The physicist Lord Kelvin calculated, from the presumed cooling of the earth and sun, that the planet was far too young to allow the vast stretches of time evolution required — a formidable scientific objection, resolved only later by the discovery of radioactivity, which revealed that the earth was much older than Kelvin's physics allowed. The fossil record showed frustrating gaps. These were real scientific problems, raised by scientists on scientific grounds, and they meant that skepticism about Darwin's theory in its early decades was often a matter of scientific caution, not religious dogma.

This scrambles the warfare narrative badly. If resistance to evolution came significantly from within science, on scientific grounds, then "religion versus science" cannot be the frame. The early debate over Darwinism was not a simple contest between believers who rejected it and scientists who embraced it; it was a complex scientific and religious controversy in which the lines ran every which way — with religious people on multiple sides and scientific objections coming from serious researchers who were not defending Scripture but doing their jobs.

## The Debate That Wasn't

Consider the single most famous emblem of the supposed war: the 1860 encounter at Oxford between Thomas Huxley, "Darwin's bulldog," and Bishop Samuel Wilberforce, remembered as the moment when science publicly humiliated religion. In the legend, Wilberforce sneeringly asked Huxley whether he was descended from an ape on his grandfather's or grandmother's side, and Huxley crushed him with a devastating reply, and the room — and history — went to science. It is a wonderful story, and it is largely a later construction. The accounts that made it famous were written years afterward, mostly by the victors, and contemporary evidence suggests the encounter was far more ambiguous than the legend claims, with observers at the time not at all sure that Huxley had won or that anything decisive had occurred. Wilberforce, moreover, had raised genuine scientific objections to Darwin in a review that Darwin himself took seriously. The great symbolic battle of science against religion turns out to be, in significant part, a myth polished into legend by the side that wished to claim the victory.

## Conflict Imposed After the Fact

Step back, and a pattern emerges that recurs throughout this unit: the "warfare" framing of evolution was substantially *imposed after the fact*, not inherent in the science. The interpretation of Darwinism as a decisive defeat of religion by science was pressed hardest, in the very same decades, by the authors of the warfare thesis itself — the polemicists who were busy constructing the whole narrative of science's war with theology, and who seized on evolution as their prime exhibit. Later, the conflict was hardened further by specific historical developments, above all the rise of American fundamentalism in the early twentieth century and its dramatic public collision with evolution in the Scopes "Monkey Trial" of 1925, which fixed in the popular mind the image of religion as the implacable enemy of evolutionary science. The warfare between evolution and faith, in other words, was to a significant degree *made* — constructed by partisans and hardened by particular later conflicts — rather than being the natural and inevitable meaning of Darwin's discovery.

The actual reception of Darwin, when examined without the warfare lens, was a complex and varied affair: many Christians accepting evolution, serious scientists resisting it on scientific grounds, religious opinion running in every direction, and the simple "science versus religion" story assembled afterward from selected episodes. Like Galileo, Darwin turns out to be a far weaker exhibit for the warfare thesis than the legend claims — not because there was no tension, but because the tension was partial, mixed, and nothing like the total war the story requires.

## The Grander God

It is worth dwelling on *how* the Christians who accepted evolution actually integrated it, because their reasoning reveals that the supposed knock-out blow to religion was, for many thoughtful believers, nothing of the kind. The move they made, again and again, was to reconceive evolution not as a rival to divine creation but as its *method* — the means by which God created. On this view, natural selection is simply the process through which the Creator brought forth the living world, no more a threat to God than gravitation or chemistry. Charles Kingsley's formulation captured the reframing perfectly: a God who fashioned each creature separately was impressive, but a God who made a world with the power to bring forth creatures of itself, who wrote the potential for all that diversity into the very laws of nature at the beginning, was grander still. Evolution, far from shrinking God, enlarged him.

Even the design argument, which evolution genuinely wounded in its classic Paleyan form, was not so much destroyed as *relocated*. Where Paley had pointed to the individual contrivance — the eye, the wing — as direct evidence of a Designer, thoughtful theists after Darwin pointed instead to the deeper level: to the existence of a universe with laws capable of generating such contrivances through evolution, to the fine-tuned order that made life's self-development possible at all. The design inference moved from the particular organism to the lawful cosmos that could produce it. Asa Gray argued for exactly this kind of teleological reading of Darwin, in which natural selection was the instrument of a purposeful Creator. Whether or not one finds these integrations finally convincing, their existence proves the essential historical point: for a great many serious Christians, evolution was not the end of faith but a new and in some ways more magnificent way of understanding how God creates. The warfare story requires that evolution forced a choice; the actual history shows how many thoughtful believers declined to make it.

## The Honest Counterpoint

And yet — the honest counterpoint here is substantial, and it must not be soft-pedaled, because evolution posed real and lasting challenges to Christian belief that the accommodating story can understate. Darwin's theory *did* undermine the design argument in its classic form: the intricate adaptations that natural theology had cited as direct evidence of a divine Designer could now be explained by natural selection, and the watchmaker analogy lost much of its force. Evolution *did* challenge literal readings of Genesis, and the challenge was real for those committed to such readings. It *did* provoke genuine and serious religious opposition, and that opposition did not simply evaporate: young-earth creationism, the ongoing battles over the teaching of evolution, and the persistent unease of many believers are not myths but a real and continuing site of friction between certain religious commitments and evolutionary science. To pretend evolution slid frictionlessly into Christian faith would be its own falsification.

So the honest picture is, once again, the tangled middle. The reception of Darwin was far more varied and far less warlike than the legend claims — many Christians embraced evolution, much resistance was scientific, the famous "battles" were mythologized, and the warfare framing was imposed after the fact. *And* evolution genuinely challenged important religious commitments — the design argument, literal Genesis — and provoked real, lasting opposition that continues today. Both are true. The warfare thesis errs by universalizing and inflating a real but partial tension into total war; but a complacent harmony thesis would err by denying that evolution posed, and still poses for some, a genuine challenge. The relationship, here as everywhere in this unit, is one of complex entanglement — cooperation and tension together — not the simple story of either war or peace. Having examined the warfare thesis's two great exhibits and found both far more tangled than the legend, we are ready to state the mature reframe that should replace the warfare story altogether.
