We have now dismantled the warfare thesis from every angle. We have seen its recent and polemical origin, examined the theological preconditions of science, traced its Christian institutions and devout founders, and looked honestly at its two great supposed proofs, Galileo and Darwin, finding both far more tangled than the legend allows. It is time to gather all of this into a positive account — to say clearly what should replace the warfare story now that we have torn it down. For it is not enough to reject a bad framework; we need a better one. And the better one, developed by the finest recent historians of the subject, replaces the image of *war* with the image of *entanglement*, and the false clarity of the conflict thesis with the truer complexity of the actual history.

## From Warfare to Complexity

The professional historians of science and religion have a name for the mature view that has replaced the warfare thesis: they call it the *complexity thesis*. Its classic statement, by the historian John Hedley Brooke, is deceptively simple: the historical relationship between science and religion is *complex* — so varied, so dependent on time, place, person, and circumstance, that no single formula can capture it.[^1] It has been, at different times and in different hands, a relationship of conflict, of cooperation, of mutual indifference, of deep mutual influence. Sometimes religion obstructed science; far more often it motivated, funded, and housed it. Sometimes scientific findings challenged religious beliefs; sometimes they reinforced them. The one thing the relationship has never been is *simple*, and the warfare thesis fails precisely because it imposes a simple, single story — perpetual conflict — on a history that refuses to be reduced to any single story at all.

The complexity thesis can sound like an evasion — as though "it's complicated" were a way of dodging the question. But it is not an evasion; it is the hard-won conclusion of historians who went looking for the warfare and found, instead, something far richer and more entangled. It is a *substantive* claim: that the specific, particular, human relationships between scientific and religious ideas and people, examined case by case, do not line up into two opposing armies but into a dense weave of interaction that runs in every direction. The complexity is the finding, not the failure to find.

## Spencer's Entanglement

The most illuminating recent expression of this mature view is the one this unit opened with: Nicholas Spencer's *Magisteria*, and its governing metaphor of *entanglement*. Spencer's history sweeps across the whole long relationship of science and religion and argues that the two have been, throughout, irreducibly intertwined — not two separate things that occasionally collide, but two strands wound together through the same institutions, the same people, the same questions. The study of nature was done by religious people, in religious institutions, funded by religious patronage, motivated by religious conviction, and understood within a religious framework, for most of its history. Religion and science were not neighbors across a border, sometimes at peace and sometimes at war; they were *entangled*, woven into each other so thoroughly that the very attempt to separate them into two clean territories distorts the history.

Spencer's title, as we noted at the unit's start, plays deliberately against the biologist Stephen Jay Gould's proposal of "non-overlapping magisteria" — the tidy suggestion that science and religion occupy entirely separate domains, science handling facts and religion handling values, so that the two need never touch. Spencer's history shows this neat separation to be as false as the warfare it was meant to replace. The magisteria are not non-overlapping; they are *entangled*. Science and religion have overlapped, interpenetrated, and shaped each other continuously, sometimes in productive cooperation and sometimes in genuine friction, but always in contact, never sealed apart. The real history is neither war nor cold peace across a fixed frontier; it is entanglement — messy, human, and profound.

## The Categories Are Modern

There is a deeper move within the reframe, one that will occupy the next lesson fully but must be introduced here, because it transforms how we should even *ask* the question. The categories we are using — "science" and "religion," conceived as two distinct things that could be at war or at peace — are themselves relatively *recent inventions*. For most of the history we have been examining, there was no such thing as "science" set over against "religion." There was *natural philosophy*, pursued by religious people, within a religious understanding of the world, as part of a unified pursuit of truth about God and his creation. The idea of two separate domains, "science" and "religion," with a boundary between them and a potential quarrel across it, took its modern shape only in the seventeenth through nineteenth centuries.

This means that the warfare question is, in a strict sense, *anachronistic* when applied to earlier periods. To ask whether "science" and "religion" were at war in the Middle Ages is like asking whether the Democrats and Republicans were at war in ancient Rome: the categories did not yet exist in the form the question assumes. Natural philosophy was not a separate territory that could go to war with religion; it was part of a seamless whole. The very framing of "science versus religion" projects a modern division backward onto a past that did not contain it — and this projection is a large part of how the warfare myth was manufactured. Recognizing that the categories themselves have a history, and a recent one, is the deepest solvent of the warfare thesis, and it is the achievement of the historian to whom we turn next.

## What the Reframe Means for Us

Why does all this matter beyond the seminar room? Because the warfare thesis is not merely bad history; it is a bad *framework for the present*, and correcting it changes how we understand our own world. As long as we believe science and religion are natural enemies, we will misunderstand both — imagining that science has triumphed over and superseded religion, that the two occupy opposed camps, that to be scientific is to have left religion behind. The entangled history tells a different and stranger story: that science grew *out of* a religious matrix, that it remains entangled with metaphysical and even theological assumptions it cannot itself justify, and that the questions religion addresses — of meaning, purpose, value, and the grounds of reason itself — are not questions science has answered or abolished, but questions it has left exactly where it found them.

And here we arrive at the payoff for the whole course, the reason this unit sits where it does. We swim in science. Everything we touch, use, and experience is its product, and we take it to be the most secular thing in the world — self-grounding, self-evident, the natural successor and enemy of religion. The entangled history reveals this to be an illusion. The most secular-seeming feature of the modern world was born from Christian soil: from the conviction that a rational God had made a rational, contingent, legible creation; in institutions the Church built; by devout believers who understood their work as worship; on assumptions about the order and intelligibility of nature that science still relies upon and still cannot prove. Science is not the water that washed religion away. Science is, in significant part, water of the same source — and to see that is to see, with unusual clarity, exactly what this course exists to reveal.

## Where the Two Still Meet

The entangled history is not merely a fact about the past; it points to a permanent feature of the present, which is why the reframe matters so much. The warfare thesis implies that science, having won, has *answered* the questions religion used to address, rendering it obsolete. But this is precisely what science has not done. Science tells us, with unprecedented power, *how* the world works — the mechanisms, the laws, the causes. It does not tell us *why* there is a world at all, why it is intelligible, why anything matters, what a human life is for, or on what grounds we should trust reason itself. These questions are not gaps in current science that further research will close; they are questions of a *different kind*, lying outside the reach of the scientific method by its very nature, and they are exactly the questions that religion, philosophy, and metaphysics have always addressed.

This is why science and religion remain entangled even now, and cannot be neatly separated into Gould's non-overlapping domains. Science rests on assumptions it cannot justify — the order and intelligibility of nature, the reliability of reason, the reality of an external world — and it opens onto questions it cannot answer, about meaning and purpose and value. Far from having abolished the territory of religion and metaphysics, science depends on that territory beneath it and borders it on every side. The person who believes science has made the ultimate questions disappear has simply stopped noticing the assumptions science stands on and the questions it leaves untouched. The entanglement is not a relic of a superstitious past that modernity has outgrown; it is a permanent condition, and a later unit of this course will press directly into the assumptions that even the most rigorous science cannot prove and cannot do without.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Two cautions keep the reframe honest. The first is that "complexity" and "entanglement" must not become a way of *dodging real conflicts*. To say the relationship is complex and entangled rather than simple warfare is not to say that science and religion never genuinely conflict on substance — because sometimes they do. Specific empirical claims can and do collide: a literal six-day creation a few thousand years ago is not compatible with what geology and biology have established, and no amount of talk about entanglement dissolves that particular conflict. The complexity thesis is a claim about the *overall shape* of the relationship, not a denial that particular claims from religious and scientific sources have contradicted each other and will again. Entanglement includes friction; it does not abolish it, and an honest reframe keeps the real conflicts in view rather than smoothing them all into complexity.

The second caution is that the historical claim — that science has Christian roots and grew from a religious matrix — does not by itself settle the *philosophical* question of whether science today *needs* religion, or whether the theological framing was *essential* rather than merely the historical *scaffolding* that got the enterprise started. It is entirely possible that Christianity was the indispensable cradle of science and yet that science, once born, can stand on its own, its original theological supports removed like scaffolding from a finished building. The origins argument is powerful and, this unit has argued, largely correct; but origins are not destiny, and to show that science *came from* Christian convictions is not to show that it *depends* on them still. The honest reframe claims the history — the entanglement, the Christian roots, the debt — without overclaiming the philosophy. With the mature view of complexity and entanglement before us, we turn to the historian who has done the most to expose the deepest root of the warfare myth: the modern invention of the very categories "science" and "religion."
