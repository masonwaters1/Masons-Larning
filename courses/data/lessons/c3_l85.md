The previous lesson introduced a claim so fundamental that it deserves a lesson of its own: that the very categories "science" and "religion," which the warfare thesis treats as two eternal combatants, are in fact modern inventions — that they took their present shape only in recent centuries, and that projecting them back onto earlier ages distorts everything. This is the great contribution of the historian Peter Harrison, whose book *The Territories of Science and Religion* is among the most important works on the subject written in our time. Harrison's argument is subtle, and it is worth grasping carefully, because it dissolves the warfare thesis at a deeper level than any mere correction of the facts. It shows that the warfare question rests on a category error — that it asks whether two things fought each other, when those two things, as we conceive them, did not yet exist.

## Two Words That Changed Their Meaning

Harrison begins with a piece of intellectual archaeology, examining what the words "science" and "religion" actually *meant* before the modern period — and the answer is startling.[^1] The Latin *religio*, from which "religion" descends, did not originally mean what we mean by religion: a system of beliefs and practices, an institution, a body of doctrine that one might set over against science. It meant something more like inner *piety* or *devotion* — a personal virtue, a disposition of the soul, a quality of reverence in the individual. Religion was something you *had* as a trait of character, not a thing "out there" with doctrines and institutions and boundaries.

Likewise the Latin *scientia*, the ancestor of "science," did not mean an institution, a profession, or a body of knowledge about nature. It meant a *habit of mind* — an intellectual virtue, a kind of demonstrative knowledge possessed by a person, the quality of knowing something thoroughly and with certainty. Science, too, was a personal quality, a virtue of the intellect, not an external territory of laboratories and theories and disciplines. Both terms, in other words, originally named *inner qualities of persons* — piety of soul and rigor of mind — not the two great external domains, with a border between them, that the modern world takes for granted.

## The Making of Two Territories

The transformation Harrison traces is the shift from these inner virtues to the modern *territories* — the two great external domains we now call "science" and "religion," each with its institutions, its body of knowledge or doctrine, its practitioners, and crucially its *boundary* with the other. Over the seventeenth through nineteenth centuries, "religion" was gradually externalized into a set of propositions and institutions — a system of beliefs that could be compared, evaluated, and set against other systems — and "science" was externalized into the organized, professional, institutional enterprise of investigating nature. Only once both had been remade into territories, into external domains with a frontier between them, did it become possible to imagine them as two powers that might dispute that frontier — that might cooperate, or coexist, or go to *war*.

This is the crux of Harrison's argument, and its implications are profound. The warfare thesis assumes two combatants, "science" and "religion," and asks about the history of their conflict. But for most of the history it purports to describe, those two combatants *did not exist* — there were only pious and learned individuals pursuing a unified understanding of God and his creation, in whom *scientia* and *religio* were personal virtues, not opposing territories. To ask whether "science" warred with "religion" in the thirteenth century is to project modern categories onto a world that lacked them, as anachronistic as asking about the foreign policy of a nation not yet founded. The warfare question is not merely answered wrongly by the myth; it is *malformed*, built on categories that had not yet come into being.

## Why This Dissolves the Myth

See what this does to the warfare thesis. The conflict thesis needs two stable, trans-historical entities — Science and Religion — that have existed all along and can be tracked through history as they clash. Harrison shows there are no such entities. What we call "science" and "religion" are recent constructions, assembled in the modern period out of older materials that were configured quite differently. And this means the warfare narrative commits a fundamental error at the root: it reifies two modern categories, treats them as eternal, and then writes a history of their supposed perennial conflict — a history of a war between two things that, for most of the story, were not two things at all. The deepest reason the warfare thesis is bad history is that its very subject, "the conflict between science and religion," presupposes a division that history did not contain until yesterday.

This is why Harrison's contribution goes beyond the corrections of earlier lessons. Debunking the flat-earth myth, complicating Galileo, and untangling Darwin correct the *facts* the warfare thesis gets wrong. Harrison corrects something deeper: the *framework* itself, the assumption of two eternal territories, which no accumulation of individual facts could fix because the error is in the categories, not the details. Once you see that "science" and "religion" are modern constructions, the whole warfare narrative loses its footing, because it was a story about the relationship between two things that had to be *invented* before they could have a relationship at all.

## The Protestant Path to Nature

Harrison's other major work reveals a further, more constructive dimension of his thought, and it bears directly on this unit's central thesis. In *The Bible, Protestantism, and the Rise of Natural Science*, he advances a striking argument about *how* religious change fed the emergence of science. In the medieval period, Harrison argues, nature was read *symbolically* — every creature, every stone, every star was a web of spiritual meanings, an allegory pointing beyond itself to theological truths, much as Scripture was read for its layered spiritual senses. The lion signified Christ, the pelican signified sacrifice; the natural world was a text of symbols to be interpreted for its hidden religious significance.

The Protestant Reformation, with its insistence on the *literal* sense of Scripture against the layered allegorical readings of medieval interpretation, changed how people read the Bible — and, Harrison argues, changed how they read the book of nature too. As the allegorical reading of Scripture gave way to the literal, so the symbolic reading of nature gave way to a new, "literal" attention to natural objects as *things in themselves* — with straightforward physical properties to be observed and catalogued, rather than symbols to be decoded for spiritual meaning. Stripped of its allegorical significance, nature became available for empirical study as a domain of plain facts, and this Harrison sees as a significant spur to the empirical, observational science of the early modern period. On this account, a specific religious development — Protestant literalism — helped clear the way for the literal, empirical study of the natural world.

## Why the Reframe Matters Now

Harrison's archaeology of the categories is not merely an antiquarian correction; it changes how we should conduct the argument in our own moment. Every time someone declares that "science and religion have always been at war," or that "science has replaced religion," they are treating two modern constructions as if they were eternal, opposed powers — and building an entire worldview on a category error. Recognizing that "science" and "religion" are recent inventions, assembled out of older materials that were configured quite differently, dissolves the sense of inevitability that the warfare framing carries. The war was never written into the nature of things; it was written into a particular nineteenth-century way of carving up the intellectual world into two territories that could be set against each other.

This has a liberating effect on present debate. It means we are not condemned to choose sides in a perennial conflict, because the conflict was manufactured along with the categories that made it thinkable. It invites us to ask better questions than "which side are you on?" — questions about what specific claims are actually being made, on what evidence, and whether they genuinely conflict or merely appear to across a boundary we have drawn too sharply. And it reveals the warfare framing itself as a historical artifact, a piece of the water we swim in, mistaken for the permanent shape of reality. To see that the very terms of the debate have a history — and a recent, contingent, constructed one — is to be freed from the debate's false necessity, and to be able to look at the actual relationship of natural inquiry and religious conviction with clearer eyes. This is the practical gift of Harrison's scholarship: not a new side to take, but release from the compulsion to take sides at all.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Harrison is himself a model of the honesty this unit aspires to, and his own theses come with real qualifications that must be respected. First, the claim that "science" and "religion" are wholly modern categories, illuminating as it is, can be pushed too far. Even in the premodern world there were *some* distinctions between the investigation of nature and the study of theology, between natural philosophy and revealed doctrine; the boundary was configured very differently and was far more porous than ours, but it is an overstatement to say no distinction of any kind existed. And the point can be misused — deployed to suggest that because the *words* were different, the *substance* of what we now call scientific and religious claims never actually conflicted, which does not follow; whatever the vocabulary, particular claims about the natural world and particular religious commitments have sometimes genuinely contradicted each other.

Second, Harrison's Protestant-literalism thesis, provocative and influential, is itself contested. Critics note that Catholic contributions to early modern science were immense — Galileo, Descartes, and many of the greatest figures were Catholics, and the Jesuits were formidable scientists — which complicates any claim that Protestantism was the distinctive religious engine of science. The causal link between literal biblical interpretation and empirical natural science is suggestive but debated, one serious scholarly interpretation among others rather than an established result. So we take from Harrison two things held with appropriate care: the deep and largely convincing insight that the warfare thesis rests on anachronistic modern categories, which dissolves the myth at its root; and the more contested but illuminating suggestion that specific religious developments shaped the emergence of empirical science. Harrison represents the rigorous middle this whole unit has sought — resisting the warfare myth without lapsing into triumphalist apologetics, and holding every claim, including his own, to the standard of the evidence. Having reached the deepest reframe of the science-and-religion question, we turn finally to the honest reckoning the unit owes: the cases where Christianity genuinely did slow inquiry, and the calibration of the whole argument.
