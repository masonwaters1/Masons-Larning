This unit has made a bold and, I have argued, largely persuasive case: that Christianity did not obstruct science but birthed it — supplying the convictions, the institutions, the motivations, and the people from which modern science grew. But this course has a discipline it applies to every strong claim, and it must apply it here, to its own argument, most rigorously of all. That discipline is the honest counterpoint: the deliberate turning against one's own thesis to face the strongest evidence on the other side. For if we have spent an entire unit dismantling the warfare myth, we must not fall into the opposite error — a triumphalist story in which Christianity was the pure and unbroken friend of inquiry. The truth is more mixed than that, and honesty requires reckoning, plainly, with the cases where Christianity genuinely did slow, obstruct, or suppress the investigation of nature.

## The Real Record of Obstruction

Let us set them out without flinching. The Galileo affair, however complicated by unsettled evidence and Counter-Reformation politics, remains at bottom a case in which religious authority suppressed a true scientific claim and punished the man who made it. That is real obstruction, and no amount of context erases it. There were sustained conflicts over the age of the earth, in which literal readings of Scripture were pressed against the findings of geology. There was, and remains, religious resistance to evolution — from the controversies of Darwin's own day through the rise of young-earth creationism and the long battles over the teaching of evolution in schools, a genuine and continuing friction between certain religious commitments and established science. The Catholic Church maintained an Index of Forbidden Books that at times restricted the circulation of scientific works, including, for a period, those advocating Copernicanism.

Beyond these famous cases, there were subtler forms of obstruction. When the two books of God seemed to conflict, some authorities insisted that a particular interpretation of Scripture must govern, and used the Bible to limit or reject conclusions drawn from nature — subordinating inquiry to a fixed reading of the sacred text. And there have always been anti-intellectual strands within Christianity — a suspicion of "worldly" learning, a fideism hostile to reason, a biblicism that treats curiosity about nature as a distraction from salvation or a presumption against revealed truth. These strands were never the whole of Christianity, and this unit has shown they were far from dominant; but they were real, and at times and in places they discouraged and obstructed the investigation of the world. An honest ledger records all of this on the debit side, and does not pretend it away.

## Correcting Even the Counter-Myths

Intellectual honesty cuts in both directions, however, and even as we acknowledge real obstruction, we must correct the *exaggerations* of obstruction that the warfare thesis has spread — because false examples of persecution are as much a distortion as denied ones. The most instructive case is Giordano Bruno, frequently invoked as a martyr of science burned by the Church for teaching that the earth moves and the cosmos is infinite. The truth is that Bruno was executed in 1600 for *theological* heresies — he denied the divinity of Christ, the Trinity, and other core doctrines, and embraced a pantheistic mysticism — not for his cosmological speculations, which were not the substance of the charges against him. Bruno was no scientist and his cosmology was more mystical intuition than science; his death was a genuine horror of religious intolerance, but it was not the martyrdom of science that the legend claims. To count Bruno as a scientific martyr is to accept the warfare thesis's own mythmaking even while cataloguing real obstruction.

This is the discipline in miniature: we neither deny the real cases of obstruction nor accept the fabricated ones. The honest ledger includes Galileo and the age-of-the-earth conflicts and the resistance to evolution, because those are real; it excludes the flat-earth myth and the tortured-Galileo legend and the scientific martyrdom of Bruno, because those are false. Getting the ledger right — neither inflating nor denying — is exactly what separates history from myth, in both directions.

## Calibrating the Claim

With the real obstruction acknowledged and the false obstruction corrected, we can state the calibrated claim this unit actually defends — echoing, deliberately, the discipline of the earlier lesson on overclaiming. The strong and defensible thesis is this: Christianity provided crucial and underappreciated preconditions and support for the emergence of modern science — the theological conviction that a rational God had made a rational, contingent, intelligible creation worth investigating; the institution of the university; the motivating vision of nature as God's second book; and the devout personnel who launched the enterprise — without which modern science would very likely not have emerged when and where it did. That claim is real, well-evidenced, and largely overlooked, and establishing it against the warfare myth has been the work of this unit.

But there are overclaims to refuse, and refusing them is part of the same honesty. It would be an overclaim to say Christianity *alone* created science, or that it *never* obstructed inquiry, or that the relationship was one of pure harmony, or — most tempting of all — that the birth of science somehow *proves* Christianity true. None of these follows, and the unit has argued none of them. Science had many parents besides Christian theology: the rational inheritance of the Greeks, the immense contributions of the Islamic world, the recovery of ancient learning, the printing press, economic and technological developments, the political fragmentation of Europe that kept any single authority from suppressing inquiry everywhere at once, and a great deal of sheer historical contingency. Christianity was a crucial strand — arguably the crucial strand for the deepest preconditions — in a thick and tangled rope of causes, not the single thread from which science hangs.

## The Same Standard, Both Ways

The deepest expression of the discipline is this: the *same standard* must be applied to credit and to blame. If Christianity is to receive credit for fostering the convictions and institutions from which science grew, then it must also bear responsibility for the cases in which it obstructed particular inquiries. We cannot claim the credit and disown the blame; the honest ledger has both a credit and a debit side, and intellectual integrity requires reading both. This is the very discipline the course applied to itself in its lesson on the overclaim problem, now turned upon the unit's own strong thesis: the refusal to let a compelling argument become a triumphalist one, the insistence on facing the counter-evidence to one's own case as squarely as the evidence for it.

And the result of applying that standard fairly is not to weaken the unit's thesis but to *strengthen* it, by making it defensible. A calibrated claim that acknowledges Christianity's real obstruction and its multiple co-parents in the birth of science is far more credible, and far harder to dismiss, than a triumphalist claim that ignores them. The mature conclusion is this: Christianity was, on balance and in the deepest respects, the *matrix* from which modern science emerged — the single most important source of its foundational preconditions, its institutions, and its early motivation — while also, at particular times and in particular cases, an obstacle to particular inquiries. Both halves of that sentence are true, and the honest reader holds them together, refusing both the warfare myth and the triumphalist overclaim.

## The Fragmentation That Saved Inquiry

One of the non-theological causes deserves special attention, because it explains why even the real cases of obstruction did not stop Western science — and it shows how the honest ledger actually works. Europe, unlike the great unified empires of China or the Islamic caliphates at their height, was politically *fragmented*: a patchwork of competing kingdoms, city-states, and jurisdictions, divided further after the Reformation into Catholic and Protestant realms. This fragmentation was, in one respect, a weakness — a source of endless war. But for the freedom of inquiry it was a decisive strength, because *no single authority could suppress an idea everywhere at once*.

Consider the Galileo affair in this light. The Catholic Church condemned Copernicanism and silenced Galileo in Italy — but it had no power over Protestant northern Europe, where Copernican and then Newtonian astronomy flourished freely. A book banned in Rome could be printed in Amsterdam or Leiden and smuggled back across the borders. A scholar persecuted in one state could find refuge and a printing press in another. The very disunity of Europe meant that suppression in one place was escape in another, and the competition among states and confessions created spaces where inquiry could continue no matter who tried to shut it down. Where a unified empire could impose a single orthodoxy across its whole domain, fragmented Europe could not — and so its science proved uncannily resistant to obstruction. This is a genuinely non-theological factor, a matter of political geography and contingency, and it belongs squarely in the honest account. It reminds us that the freedom which let Western science survive its own obstructions owed as much to Europe's political disunity as to any theological conviction — one more strand in the thick rope of causes, and a caution against attributing the whole story to Christianity alone.

## The Water We Swim In

Return, at the last, to where the unit began, and to the reason it sits in this course. We swim in science. Everything we use, everything we experience, the very shape of the modern world and the modern mind, is science's product, and we take it to be the most secular thing there is — self-grounding, self-evident, the natural successor and the ancient enemy of religion. This unit has argued that this is an illusion, and one of the most complete illusions we live inside. The most secular-seeming feature of our world grew from Christian soil: from the conviction that a rational God had made a rational, contingent, legible creation; in institutions the Church built and sustained; by devout believers who understood their work as the reading of God's book and the tracing of his design; and it still rests, even now, on assumptions about the order and intelligibility of nature that it inherited from Christian theology and cannot itself prove. The disenchanted, mechanical, law-governed cosmos of modern science is not the water that washed Christianity away. It is, in large part, water of the same source.

To see this is to see the water — to become conscious, for a moment, of an inheritance so total and so invisible that we had mistaken it for the plain nature of things. That is the whole aim of this course, and there is no domain in which the seeing is harder, or more revealing, than this one. Having traced the Christian roots of the science we swim in, we turn next to another institution that seems the very definition of a universal human evil finally overcome by moral progress — slavery — and ask the same disorienting question: where did the conviction that it must be abolished actually come from?
