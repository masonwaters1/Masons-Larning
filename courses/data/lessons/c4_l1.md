A reasonable person might assume that a course in American history should begin with America — with the first permanent English settlement at Jamestown in 1607, perhaps, or with the Declaration of Independence in 1776. These are the obvious starting lines, the moments when something recognizably American steps onto the stage. Yet we are going to begin earlier, much earlier, in ancient Jerusalem and classical Athens, in medieval England and Reformation Geneva. We are going to spend our entire first unit on events that occurred centuries and even millennia before any European set foot on the North American continent.

This is not a detour. It is the necessary foundation, and the reason why goes to the heart of how this course understands its subject. America was not invented from scratch by clever men in the eighteenth century. It was the inheritor of a long and specific tradition — a particular stream of ideas about God, law, liberty, human nature, and the right ordering of society that had been flowing through Western civilization for thousands of years before it reached the shores of the New World. The men who founded the United States did not think of themselves primarily as inventors. They thought of themselves as *heirs* — as men entrusted with a precious inheritance, charged with conserving and applying it under new conditions. To understand them, we have to understand what they inherited.

## The Self-Understanding of the Founders

Consider how the founding generation actually talked about what they were doing. They did not speak the language of novelty and rupture that would characterize later revolutions. The French revolutionaries would soon try to abolish the past, rename the months, and reset the calendar to Year One, declaring that history itself was beginning anew. The American founders did almost the opposite. They reached constantly backward, draping their arguments in the authority of ancient precedent.

When they wanted to defend their liberties against the British Crown, they did not claim to be creating new rights. They claimed they were defending the ancient rights of Englishmen — rights stretching back to Magna Carta and beyond, into the mists of Anglo-Saxon custom. When they designed their new government, they pored over the histories of the Roman Republic and the Greek city-states, studying why those experiments in self-government had risen and why they had fallen. They signed their pseudonymous political essays with Roman names — Publius, Cato, Brutus, the Federal Farmer — because they understood themselves to be participants in a conversation that the Romans had begun. Their speeches and writings were saturated with the language and imagery of the Bible, which was the one book that nearly every American, educated or not, knew intimately. They quoted Cicero and they quoted Scripture, often in the same breath, and they expected their audience to catch every reference.

This was not decoration. It reflected a genuine conviction about the nature of political wisdom: that the deepest truths about human beings and human societies had been discovered long ago, refined over centuries of hard experience, and handed down to be guarded rather than reinvented in each generation. The founders were steeped in a sense that they stood at the end of a long chain of transmission, and that their task was to prove worthy of it. You cannot understand a people who thought this way without first understanding the inheritance they believed they were carrying.

## How Ideas Travel

There is a deeper point here about the nature of history itself, one worth pausing over because it shapes everything that follows in this course. Ideas are not confined to the moment of their birth. They travel — across centuries, across oceans, across the boundaries of dead empires — carried in books, in institutions, in laws, in habits of mind, in the deep grammar of a language, and above all in religious traditions that preserve and transmit them from one generation to the next.

A conviction first articulated by a Hebrew prophet three thousand years ago — that there is a moral law higher than the king, to which even the king must answer — does not stay in ancient Israel. It passes into the Christian church, which carries it through the collapse of Rome and the long centuries that followed. It shapes the medieval idea that the ruler is under God and under the law. It arms the Protestant reformers who defy popes and emperors. It crosses the Atlantic in the minds of Puritan settlers. And eventually it stands behind a colonial lawyer's claim that there are truths so fundamental that no Parliament and no king may violate them. The idea has traveled three thousand years and six thousand miles, changing its dress at every stage, but carrying its essential cargo intact.

This is why a serious history of America cannot begin in America. The ideas that made America were already ancient when America began. To start the story in 1607 or 1776 would be like walking into a great river miles from its source and imagining that the water began at the point where you happened to start watching. The water has been flowing for a very long time, gathering volume and force from many tributaries, and we need to walk back upstream to find the springs.

There is a further reason the upstream journey matters, beyond mere accuracy about origins. Ideas carry consequences that unfold across centuries, often in ways their originators never imagined and would not have endorsed. The Hebrew prophet who declared that the king stands under God's judgment was not thinking about constitutional government, but he had planted something that would one day help justify resistance to tyranny. The medieval canon lawyer refining the concept of the person before God was not a democrat, but he was building a category that democracy would later require. When we trace an idea to its source, we are not merely satisfying curiosity about the past; we are learning to see the hidden logic by which a civilization develops — the way a seed planted in one age bears fruit, sometimes bitter and sometimes sweet, in an age that has forgotten the planting. To understand America's possibilities and its pathologies alike, we have to know what seeds were in the soil.

## The Four Springs

This course will identify four great sources that fed the American river, and our first unit takes them up in turn. A useful way to name them, borrowed from the conservative historian Russell Kirk, is by the cities that symbolize them: Jerusalem, Athens, Rome, and London — with the Reformation running like a charge of electricity through the later stages.[^1]

From **Jerusalem** came the Hebrew inheritance: the revolutionary idea of one God who is righteous and who demands righteousness; the concept of a covenant, a binding mutual agreement between a people and their God and, by extension, among themselves; the conviction that there is a moral law that stands above all human authority; the prophetic tradition of speaking truth to power, of the lone voice calling kings and nations back to justice; and the radical notion that every human being, however lowly, bears the image of God and therefore possesses a dignity that no earthly power may erase. These ideas would echo through American history in ways we will trace again and again — in the covenant theology of the Puritans, in the abolitionists' insistence that the slave was made in God's image, in the prophetic cadences of Martin Luther King Jr.

From **Athens** came the gift of reason and the first great experiments in self-government: philosophy, the disciplined use of the mind to inquire into the nature of things; the idea of the citizen who governs and is governed in turn; democracy and its discontents; and a sober analysis, in thinkers from Aristotle to Polybius, of how governments are structured, how they decay, and how their decay might be slowed. The founders studied Athens not as a model to copy — they were sharply aware of how Athenian democracy had degenerated into mob rule and demagoguery — but as a warning and a source of hard-won political science.

From **Rome** came the towering achievement of law, and the ideal of the republic: the conviction that a polity should be governed by settled laws rather than by the whims of men; the architecture of mixed and balanced government that the founders would consciously adapt; the figure of the virtuous citizen who places the common good above private interest, embodied for them in heroes like Cincinnatus, the farmer-general who took up power only to lay it down again — the model George Washington consciously imitated; and the long, instructive tragedy of how the Roman Republic, through corruption and the concentration of power, gave way to empire.

From **London** — standing for the whole English constitutional tradition — came the practical machinery of ordered liberty: Magna Carta and the principle that the king is under the law; the common law, that vast accumulation of judicial wisdom built case by case over centuries; trial by jury; the rights of Englishmen; representative government in Parliament; and the deep Anglo-Saxon instinct for local self-rule that the colonists carried across the ocean and planted in their town meetings and colonial assemblies.

And cutting through the later centuries came the **Reformation**, the great religious upheaval of the sixteenth century, which radicalized everything it touched: the priesthood of all believers, which dignified the individual conscience and undercut hierarchy; the spread of literacy as ordinary people learned to read Scripture for themselves; new theories of the right to resist tyrannical rulers; and the Calvinist seriousness about sin and human depravity that would give the American founders their characteristic suspicion of concentrated power. We will see that this last inheritance is the hidden key to the Constitution's elaborate system of checks and balances — a system that makes sense only if you assume, as the founders did, that men are not angels and that power corrupts those who hold it.

## The Honest Angle

A word about the spirit in which we will conduct this study, because this course makes no pretense of being written from nowhere. Every history is told from some vantage point, by someone with commitments that shape what gets emphasized. The pretense of pure neutrality is just that — a pretense, and usually a dishonest one. The honest move is to be transparent about the angle of vision and then to work hard at being truthful within it.

The angle of this course is openly conservative, pro-American, and Christian — but engaged rather than insulated, and committed to honesty above flattery. This means we will take the American achievement seriously as one of the genuine high points of political history, and we will give Christianity its due as the dominant moral and intellectual force in American life for most of the nation's existence. You cannot understand America without understanding the faith that shaped its institutions, its reformers, and its conscience, and many textbooks badly distort the past by minimizing it.

But taking that angle does not mean telling a fairy tale. The same inheritance that produced the Declaration of Independence also coexisted, for far too long, with the slave auction. The same Bible was quoted to defend slavery and to destroy it. American Christians acquiesced in terrible things — in human bondage, in the dispossession of native peoples, in segregation — and frequently failed the very moral tests their own faith laid before them. This course will not flinch from those failures. But it will also show something that the standard secular account usually misses: that the most powerful critics of Christian failure were, over and over, *other Christians*, drawing on the same Scriptures to call the nation back to its better self. That internal prophetic tradition — the capacity of the inheritance to generate its own correction — is one of the most important things about the American story, and we will watch it operate again and again.

## The Quarrel With This Approach

Honesty requires that we put the strongest objection to this whole framing on the table at the outset, because it is a serious one and it will shadow the entire course. A skeptical historian might say: this story of noble inheritance is exactly the kind of selective, self-flattering narrative that nations tell about themselves. Of course the founders *claimed* the mantle of Jerusalem, Athens, and Rome — appeals to ancient authority are how the powerful have always dressed up their interests. To take their self-understanding at face value, the critic argues, is to mistake a society's official story for its actual operating logic. And to organize American history around a tradition of "ordered liberty under God" is to quietly write the dispossessed and enslaved into the footnotes — to tell the story of the inheritance from the vantage point of the heirs while the people who were crushed by it appear only as exceptions to an otherwise glorious arc.

This objection deserves respect rather than dismissal, and parts of it are simply correct. Appeals to tradition *can* be cover for power; official stories *do* obscure as much as they reveal; and a great deal of conventional patriotic history has indeed treated slavery and conquest as awkward asides rather than central facts. A serious course cannot pretend otherwise.

But the objection proves less than it claims. That a self-understanding can be self-serving does not mean it is false or unimportant; the founders' constant reaching backward into Scripture and classical history was not merely rhetorical decoration but genuinely shaped what they built, as the structure of the Constitution itself will show. And the charge of writing off the dispossessed cuts both ways. This course will insist that the inheritance contained, from the beginning, the very resources used to indict its betrayals — that the prophet calling the nation back to its creed was drawing on the same Jerusalem the founders claimed. The slave who appealed to the image of God, the abolitionist who quoted the prophets, the civil rights preacher who invoked the moral law above the unjust statute — these were not standing outside the tradition and attacking it. They were standing *inside* it and wielding it. A history that flattened the inheritance into pure self-congratulation would miss this; but so would a history that dismissed the inheritance as mere ideology. The truth is more interesting than either, and holding that tension without collapsing it to one side is the discipline this course demands.

## A Word on Sources and Method

A final practical note on how we will proceed. This course rests on a serious body of scholarship, and you should know the company we are keeping. We will draw on historians of early America like Thomas Kidd and Mark Noll, who have done the careful archival work of recovering the religious texture of the colonial and revolutionary periods; on Lincoln scholars like Allen Guelzo and Harry Jaffa, who have wrestled with the theological depth of the Civil War; on George Marsden and Nathan Hatch, who mapped the democratization of American religion; on political thinkers like Russell Kirk and Wilfred McClay, who have articulated the conservative reading of the American order; and on the indispensable outside observer Alexis de Tocqueville, the young Frenchman whose visit in the 1830s produced the single most penetrating account of American democracy ever written.

Wherever possible, we will go to the primary sources and let the figures speak for themselves rather than filtering them through summary. You will read Jonathan Edwards's own sermons, not just descriptions of them. You will work through the actual arguments of the *Federalist Papers*, Lincoln's Second Inaugural Address, Frederick Douglass's searing Fourth of July oration, and King's Letter from Birmingham Jail. The aim is not to hand you conclusions but to make you *literate* — historically, theologically, and constitutionally literate — so that you can weigh the evidence and the arguments yourself and arrive at your own judgments about the country and its tradition. Reverence without sentimentality, criticism without contempt: that is the posture throughout.

So we begin before the beginning. We start upstream, at the springs, because the water that would one day fill the American river was already flowing in Jerusalem and Athens and Rome and London long before anyone imagined a nation on the far side of the Atlantic. The founders knew they were heirs. To understand them — to understand ourselves — we have to learn what they inherited. That is the work of our first unit, and it is where the American story truly begins.
