Beneath the common law of Coke and Blackstone, beneath even Magna Carta, lies an older and dimmer layer of the American inheritance — a set of political instincts so deep that the founders themselves reached back to it for inspiration, and so woven into Anglo-American life that it can be hard to see as an inheritance at all. This is the layer of the *Anglo-Saxon imagination*: the tradition, real and mythologized in equal measure, of free men governing themselves through local assemblies, settling disputes among their neighbors, and recognizing a law older than any king. The American instinct for local self-government, for the jury, for the township and the county, for suspicion of distant central power — all of this was understood by many of the founders to descend from the Anglo-Saxons. Whether that descent is sober history or potent myth is a question we will have to weigh carefully. But the imagination was real, and it shaped a nation.

## The Germanic Root

The roots of the tradition reach back before England itself, to the Germanic peoples of the European forests, among them the tribes that would later cross the sea to become the Anglo-Saxons. Our earliest substantial witness to their political life is the Roman historian Tacitus, whose *Germania*, written near the end of the first century, described the customs of the Germanic tribes with evident admiration — and, some have suspected, with one eye on the contrast they offered to the despotism of imperial Rome.[^1] Tacitus depicted peoples among whom important matters were decided not by a sovereign's command but by *assemblies of free men*, who gathered, deliberated, and signified their assent or dissent; among whom kings and chiefs led by influence and example rather than absolute power; and among whom a fierce attachment to personal freedom and customary right prevailed.

Whatever the precise accuracy of Tacitus's portrait — and it was certainly idealized — it planted in the Western imagination a powerful image: that of a free, self-governing people whose liberty was native and ancient, rooted in the assembly of free men rather than granted from above. This image would be drawn upon again and again across the centuries, by people seeking to anchor their claims to liberty in a deep and venerable past. It is the distant headwater of the Anglo-Saxon imagination.

## The Assemblies of the Free

When the Anglo-Saxons settled in Britain, from the fifth century onward, they brought these instincts with them and gave them institutional form. Anglo-Saxon England developed a layered system of *local self-government* that would prove remarkably consequential. At the base were local assemblies: the *moot* of the hundred (a subdivision of the shire) and the *moot* of the shire itself — gatherings of free men who met regularly to settle disputes, administer customary law, and manage the affairs of their locality. Justice and governance, in this system, were not handed down from a remote capital but transacted face to face, among neighbors, in open assembly.

Above these local bodies stood the *witan*, or *witenagemot* — the assembly of "wise men," the leading nobles and churchmen who counseled the king, and whose consent lent weight to his rule. The witan was aristocratic, not democratic, and we should not romanticize it into a parliament; but it embodied a principle of enduring importance — that even the king governed in consultation, that authority was not the king's alone but was shared with a council representing the realm. Together, the local moots and the national witan expressed a political culture in which power flowed substantially *upward* from communities of free men and was exercised through deliberation and consent, rather than flowing only downward from an absolute sovereign. This was the Anglo-Saxon constitution as later generations would imagine it: government rooted in the self-governing locality and the counseled king.

## The Roots of the Jury

Among the most cherished of Anglo-American institutions is trial by jury, and here too the imagination reached back to Anglo-Saxon soil. The precise historical origins of the jury are tangled and debated — the institution as we know it took shape gradually, with significant developments after the Norman Conquest — but its deep roots draw on the Anglo-Saxon practice of involving the community in the doing of justice. In the local courts, justice was a communal affair: free men of the neighborhood participated in resolving disputes, and the guilt or innocence of the accused was a matter for the community rather than for a royal official alone.

The animating principle — that a person should be judged by a body of his *neighbors*, by members of his own community applying the community's sense of justice, rather than by the unchecked decree of a distant power — is the seed from which the jury grew, and it harmonized perfectly with the broader Anglo-Saxon instinct for local, participatory self-government. By the time the jury matured into its classic form, it carried this ancient resonance: the free community standing between the individual and the power of the state, insisting that judgment belong to one's peers. The American devotion to the jury, enshrined in the Constitution and the Bill of Rights, inherited not just a legal mechanism but this whole imaginative tradition of community justice.

## The Norman Yoke and the Dream of Lost Liberty

In 1066 the Norman Conquest fell upon Anglo-Saxon England, and with it — according to a powerful and enduring interpretation — a catastrophe for native liberty. The Normans imposed a centralized, feudal, hierarchical order, subordinating the free institutions of the Anglo-Saxons to the will of a foreign king and a foreign aristocracy. Out of this reading grew one of the most politically potent myths in English and American history: the theory of the *Norman Yoke*.

According to this theory, the free, self-governing Anglo-Saxons had enjoyed an "ancient constitution" of native liberty, which the Norman Conquest violently overthrew, fastening upon the English a foreign tyranny. The whole subsequent history of English liberty could then be told as a long struggle to *throw off the Norman Yoke* and *recover* the lost freedoms of the Anglo-Saxon past — to restore the birthright that 1066 had stolen. This narrative proved extraordinarily useful to those seeking to justify resistance to royal power: from the radicals of the English Civil War, who invoked Anglo-Saxon liberty against the Stuart kings, to the Whig tradition that followed. To demand liberty was framed not as innovation but as *restoration* — the recovery of an ancient inheritance unjustly suppressed. Liberty, once again, was conceived as a patrimony to be reclaimed rather than a theory to be invented.

## Jefferson and the Saxon Romance

This Anglo-Saxon romance crossed the Atlantic and took deep hold among the American founders, and in none more vividly than Thomas Jefferson. Jefferson was fascinated by the Anglo-Saxons to a degree that can seem eccentric. He studied the Anglo-Saxon language and urged its place in education; he held up the pre-Norman Saxons as a model of free self-government for the new republic; and when a committee was formed to design a seal for the United States, Jefferson proposed that one side depict Hengist and Horsa, the legendary Saxon brothers said to have led the first Germanic settlers into Britain — the mythic founders of English liberty — as the political ancestors of the American people.

For Jefferson and others, the American cause was, in part, a fresh chapter in the long story of recovering Anglo-Saxon freedom. The colonists were the heirs of the free Saxons; their local institutions — the New England town meeting, the county court, the elected assembly — were the Saxon moots reborn on American soil; their resistance to the distant power of king and Parliament was another casting-off of a yoke imposed from above. When later observers sought to explain the vitality of American democracy, they too pointed to this substratum: Alexis de Tocqueville, surveying the young republic, located the secret of its liberty in the self-governing *township*, the small local community managing its own affairs — the very pattern the Anglo-Saxon imagination had bequeathed. The American instinct that power should rest as close to the people as possible, in the locality and the community, drew strength from this deep and venerable source.

## The Honest Counterpoint: History and Myth

Intellectual honesty compels us to weigh how much of this is sober history and how much is inspiring myth — and the answer is humbling. The romantic picture of free, equal Anglo-Saxons governing themselves in idyllic liberty before the Norman tyranny is, in large part, a *construction* — a backward projection that flattens a far harsher reality. Anglo-Saxon society was steeply *hierarchical*, divided into ranks of nobles, ordinary freemen, and the unfree; it practiced *slavery*, with thralls who enjoyed no liberty at all; it was frequently violent, and its "free men" were a limited class, not the whole population. The "ancient constitution" of unbroken native liberty, like the Norman Yoke that supposedly shattered it, owes much to the political needs of later generations who found the story useful, and historians are rightly skeptical of any clean institutional descent running from the folk-moot to the American town meeting. The continuity is more imagined than documented.

There is a darker turn to acknowledge as well. In the nineteenth century, the romance of "Anglo-Saxon liberty" curdled, in some hands, into *Anglo-Saxonism* — a racial ideology that recast the love of Saxon freedom as a claim of racial superiority, used to justify conquest, exclusion, and empire. The language of a uniquely free "Anglo-Saxon race" became a tool of nationalism and racism quite at odds with the universal liberty the founders had reached for. A faithful account cannot omit this; the same myth that inspired resistance to tyranny was later bent to ignoble ends.

And yet — as with the "ancient constitution" of the previous lesson — the myth did real and lasting work, and a defender of the tradition may fairly insist on it. The *belief* in an Anglo-Saxon heritage of self-government helped produce and sustain actual self-government; the imagined inheritance shaped real institutions and real liberties. Some genuine continuities do exist beneath the romance: the long English tradition of local courts, of customary law, of community participation in justice, of the king bound by counsel, is not pure invention, and it did flow into Anglo-American practice. The truth, then, is layered: a core of genuine institutional inheritance, wrapped in a romantic and partly false narrative, which nonetheless inspired the building of free institutions — and was, in other hands, perverted into racial myth. To understand the American imagination, we must hold all of this together: the real roots, the inspiring myth, and the dangerous distortion alike.

With this we complete our descent into the deepest layer of the American inheritance — the instincts and institutions, real and imagined, that the founders carried with them from the Old World. We have traced the long pre-history of America from Sinai and Athens through Rome and Christendom, through Magna Carta, the Reformation, the common law, and now the Anglo-Saxon imagination. The inheritance is assembled. In the next unit we cross the Atlantic at last and turn to its first transplanting on American soil: the founding of the colonies, and the strange and consequential settlement of a new world by people carrying this ancient freight.
