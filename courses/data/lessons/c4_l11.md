With this lesson the American story proper begins. The previous unit traced the deep inheritances the colonists carried with them across the Atlantic — the common law, the rights of Englishmen, the Anglo-Saxon imagination of self-government, the long preparation of ideas. Now we arrive at the founding itself, and we find that it was not one founding but several, with different motives and different meanings. The two that loom largest in the national memory stand in instructive contrast: Jamestown in Virginia, founded in 1607, and Plymouth in Massachusetts, founded in 1620. Plymouth was a religious founding, which we take up next. Jamestown was something else — a *commercial* founding, born not of piety but of the pursuit of profit. And it was at Jamestown that two of the most consequential features of American life, one glorious and one terrible, first appeared.

## A Venture for Profit

Jamestown was established in 1607 by the Virginia Company of London, and the nature of that body tells us much about the settlement's character. The Virginia Company was a *joint-stock company* — a commercial enterprise in which investors pooled their capital in exchange for shares in the anticipated returns. Its purpose was not to build a refuge for the persecuted or a city on a hill, but to make money: to find gold, to discover a passage to the riches of Asia, to extract valuable commodities, to turn a profit for its shareholders back in England.[^1]

This is worth dwelling on, because it complicates any tidy account of American origins as purely religious or providential. The first permanent English settlement in what would become the United States was a business venture, and a speculative one at that. The men who came to Jamestown came, in large part, seeking quick wealth — many of them gentlemen-adventurers who expected to find gold lying on the ground and were unprepared, and disinclined, for the hard labor of survival. The commercial motive, the profit-seeking enterprise, the joint-stock company as the vehicle of colonization: here, at the very beginning, is the entrepreneurial and capitalist strand of the American character, present from the first landing. America was settled not only by saints but by merchants, and Jamestown is the merchant's founding.

## The Agony of Survival

The early years were a catalogue of suffering and near-total failure. The settlers chose a swampy, malarial site; they searched for gold instead of planting food; they quarreled and mismanaged; and they fell into desperate conflict with the powerful Powhatan confederacy, the Native American polity whose land they had occupied. Disease, starvation, and violence reduced the colony again and again to the edge of extinction. The winter of 1609–1610, remembered as the "Starving Time," was the worst: of several hundred settlers, only a few dozen survived, and the survivors were driven to eating their animals, their horses, and in the extremity of famine, the dead. Twice the colony came within a hair of being abandoned altogether.

That Jamestown survived at all owed much to the harsh discipline imposed by leaders such as Captain John Smith, whose blunt rule — that he who would not work would not eat — forced the gentlemen-adventurers to labor for their bread. But discipline alone could not make the colony viable. What Virginia lacked was an *economic foundation*, a reason to exist that could draw investment and settlers and sustain them. Gold never materialized; the passage to Asia was a fantasy. The colony needed a product the world would buy. And it found one.

## Tobacco

The salvation of Virginia was a weed. Around 1612, a settler named John Rolfe — later remembered also for his marriage to Pocahontas, the daughter of the Powhatan chief — began experimenting with a sweeter strain of tobacco than the harsh native variety, cultivating a leaf that suited European tastes. It was a triumph. Tobacco proved to be the cash crop Virginia had been desperate for: it grew well in the Chesapeake soil, it commanded high prices in England, where the smoking habit was spreading rapidly, and it transformed the struggling outpost into a profitable enterprise almost overnight.

The consequences of "the tobacco economy" radiated through every dimension of Virginia's development, and many of them would prove fateful. Tobacco was enormously labor-intensive and quickly exhausted the soil, which created an insatiable hunger for two things: *land* and *labor*. The hunger for land drove relentless expansion into the interior, intensifying conflict with the Native peoples. The hunger for labor drove the colony first toward *indentured servitude* — the importation of poor English laborers who worked for a term of years in exchange for passage — and then, as we shall see, toward something far darker. Tobacco gave Virginia a reason to exist and a path to prosperity; it also set in motion the forces that would shape the plantation South, with all its promise and all its tragedy. The colony was saved, but the manner of its saving planted seeds that would bear bitter fruit.

## The Rights of Englishmen Cross the Sea

The emergence of representative government at Jamestown was not a bolt from the blue; it grew from a seed carried across the Atlantic in the colony's founding documents. The First Charter of Virginia, granted in 1606, contained a provision of lasting importance: it declared that the colonists and their descendants would possess all the liberties, franchises, and immunities of natural-born subjects of England, as if they had been born and dwelt within the realm itself. In other words, the settlers did not shed the *rights of Englishmen* by sailing west; they carried those rights with them, and the charter guaranteed it.

This connects Jamestown directly to the deep inheritances traced in the previous unit — the common law, Magna Carta, the long tradition of self-government and consent. The House of Burgesses was, in this light, not an invention but an *application*: the natural extension to Virginia of the Englishman's ancient claim to be governed with the consent of his representatives. The colonists understood themselves to be exercising rights that were theirs by birthright, not privileges newly granted. Here is a pattern that will recur throughout the colonial story and culminate in the Revolution itself: Americans claiming, against distant authority, the liberties they believed were already theirs as the inheritance of England. The charter's promise planted that expectation at the very founding, and the assembly of 1619 was its first fruit.

## The Year 1619

No year in early Virginia carries more weight than 1619, for in that single year two developments appeared that would define the American story ever after — the one a seed of the nation's highest ideals, the other of its deepest sin.

The first was the convening of the *House of Burgesses* — the first representative legislative assembly in English America. The Virginia Company, seeking to attract settlers and give them a stake in the colony's success, authorized the planters to elect representatives, called burgesses, to a legislative body that would help govern the colony's affairs. It was a modest beginning, born as much of commercial calculation as of high principle: representative government was, in part, an inducement to immigration, a way of telling prospective settlers that they would have a voice. But modest beginnings can have mighty consequences. Here, in 1619, the tradition of representative self-government — the inheritance of the Anglo-Saxon assembly and the rights of Englishmen, examined in the previous unit — took root in American soil and began to grow. The seed of American democracy was planted in Virginia, and it was planted in the same decade as the Pilgrims' more famous compact.

The second development of 1619 was the arrival, aboard an English privateer, of the first documented Africans brought to English Virginia — the beginning of the long and terrible history of slavery in what would become the United States. The legal status of these first arrivals was ambiguous, and the full apparatus of racial slavery would harden only over subsequent decades, a story we will follow in a later lesson. But the arrival marks the entry of human bondage into the English colonies, and its coincidence with the birth of representative government is one of the most haunting facts in American history. In the same year, in the same colony, the nation's founding contradiction was inscribed: the institutions of liberty and the institution of slavery took root together, side by side, intertwined from the very beginning.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Several cautions guard against oversimplifying this founding. The first concerns the very framing of Jamestown and Plymouth as rival "foundings." Neither was *the* founding of America in any exclusive sense; the American story has multiple and tangled origins, and we should resist the temptation to tidy it into a single tale. Spanish settlement at St. Augustine predated Jamestown by decades; the French were establishing themselves to the north; and long before any European arrived, the land was home to populous and sophisticated Native nations whose presence and dispossession are part of the story, not a backdrop to it. To begin with Jamestown is to begin the *English* colonial story, not the human story of the continent, and honesty requires keeping that distinction in view.

The second caution cuts against an overly pious or providential reading of American origins. Jamestown is a useful corrective to the Pilgrim-centered narrative, for it reminds us that the first permanent English colony was a profit-seeking business venture, driven by the hope of gold and the cultivation of a cash crop, not by the quest for religious purity. The commercial, secular, entrepreneurial strand of the American character is not a later corruption of a godly founding; it was there from the start, at Jamestown, alongside the religious strand at Plymouth. Both are authentically American. And finally, the darkest caution: the same colony that gave America its first representative assembly also gave it the first cargo of enslaved Africans, in the same year. A faithful account of the founding cannot celebrate the one while suppressing the other. The glory and the shame of the American beginning are not separate stories to be told to different audiences; they are a single story, and they began, quite literally, together. We hold them together because the truth requires it — and because the long struggle of American history, in large part, has been the struggle to make the promise of 1619's assembly prevail over the sin of 1619's bondage. With the commercial founding before us, we turn next to its great counterpart: the religious founding at Plymouth, and the Pilgrims who sought not profit but a covenant with God.
