If Jamestown was the commercial founding, Plymouth was the religious one — and the contrast between them frames the whole of early American history. Where the Virginia settlers came seeking gold and profit, the settlers who landed at Plymouth in 1620 came seeking something they valued more than wealth: the freedom to worship God as they believed Scripture required. And before they set foot on shore, they did something the Virginia adventurers had not: they drew up, in the cabin of their ship, a brief and momentous agreement to govern themselves by mutual consent. That document, the Mayflower Compact, planted at the very root of American political culture an idea drawn straight from the religious life of its signers — the idea of the covenant.

## The Pilgrims

The people we call the Pilgrims were English *Separatists* — radical Puritans who had concluded that the Church of England was so corrupted that it could not be reformed from within, and who had therefore *separated* from it entirely to form their own congregations. This was a dangerous and illegal step, for the English crown regarded religious separation as something close to sedition, and the Separatists suffered harassment and persecution for it. We must distinguish them from the much larger body of non-separating Puritans who would soon found Massachusetts Bay, and whom we meet in the next lessons; the Pilgrims were the smaller, more radical group who had already cut their ties to the national church.

Fleeing persecution, they first sought refuge in Holland, settling in Leiden, where they found freedom of worship but grew uneasy: they feared losing their English identity, and they worried that their children were being absorbed into Dutch society. And so they resolved on a more drastic course — to cross the Atlantic and build a community in America where they could preserve both their faith and their English character. After arranging financing and securing a patent, a portion of the Leiden congregation, joined by others, embarked for the New World aboard the *Mayflower* in 1620.

## A Covenant in the Cabin

The voyage was hard, and it ended off course. The Pilgrims had intended to settle within the territory of the Virginia Company, where their patent gave them legal standing, but storms and navigation carried them far to the north, to the coast of Cape Cod in present-day Massachusetts — outside the bounds of any established English government. This created a genuine crisis. Some of the passengers were not Pilgrims at all but ordinary settlers, called "Strangers," and certain of them, recognizing that the patent did not apply where they had landed, began to mutter that once ashore they would be bound by no law and could do as they pleased. The little company faced dissolution into anarchy before it had even disembarked.

The Pilgrim leaders responded with the instrument that came most naturally to them. Before going ashore, the adult men gathered in the cabin of the *Mayflower* and drew up a brief written agreement, which forty-one of them signed.[^1] In it they solemnly bound themselves together into what they called a "civil body politic," agreeing to frame and obey such just and equal laws as they would enact from time to time for the general good of the colony. It was a tiny document, a few sentences long, composed in an emergency. But in those few sentences a people had constituted a government over themselves, by their own mutual agreement and consent — and that act would echo through American history.

## The Covenant Idea

To grasp why the Mayflower Compact matters so much, we must see where it came from — and it came from the Pilgrims' religion. As Calvinists and congregationalists, the Pilgrims were thoroughly accustomed to forming their *churches* by *covenant*: a congregation, in their understanding, came into being when believers freely bound themselves together, before God and one another, in a solemn mutual agreement to walk together as a church. The covenant was the constitutive act of their religious community — a people voluntarily binding themselves under God to one another and to a shared way of life.

When the Pilgrims faced the problem of civil order outside any existing jurisdiction, they reached instinctively for this same covenantal logic and applied it to *government*. The Mayflower Compact is, in form and spirit, a church covenant turned to civil ends: a people covenanting together, before God, to form a body politic and submit to laws of their own making for the common good. This is the deep significance the course wishes to draw out. The American tradition of self-government did not spring solely from secular social-contract philosophy; it grew, in substantial part, from Protestant *covenant theology* — from the practice of free people binding themselves together under God by mutual consent. The church covenant became the model for the civil compact, and the habit of constituting communities by agreement, learned in the gathering of congregations, became a habit of constituting governments. Here, in the cabin of the *Mayflower*, the religious root of American self-government is visible in its purest form. We will trace this covenantal thread further in the lessons on covenant theology and self-government that follow.

## Survival and Memory

The story that followed has become central to American memory, though memory has reshaped it. The first winter was brutal, and roughly half the small company died of cold, hunger, and disease. The survivors owed their continuance in part to the help of Native Americans — most famously Tisquantum, remembered as Squanto, who had learned English through an earlier and tragic captivity, and Massasoit, the Wampanoag leader who entered into an alliance with the struggling colony. The harvest celebration of 1621, shared between the Pilgrims and the Wampanoag, became enshrined in national tradition as the "first Thanksgiving." The colony's governor, William Bradford, recorded its history in his chronicle *Of Plymouth Plantation*, which preserved the Pilgrims' own understanding of themselves: a people led by Providence across the sea, a kind of new Israel, sustained by God through trial in a wilderness. That providential self-understanding, as much as the Compact itself, would become part of the American inheritance — the conviction of a people with a divine errand.

## Two Foundings, One Nation

With Plymouth before us alongside Jamestown, we can see the double character of the American beginning, and it is worth pausing over, for the contrast is not incidental but formative. The two foundings embody two different impulses, two different reasons to cross an ocean and build a new world. Jamestown was the *commercial* founding: a profit-seeking venture, organized as a joint-stock company, driven by the hope of wealth, made viable by a cash crop, and developing self-government partly as an inducement to investment. Plymouth was the *religious* founding: a community of believers fleeing persecution, bound together by covenant, governing themselves by mutual consent before God, and understanding their errand as a divine calling.

Neither founding is the whole story, and the American character would be drawn from both. From Jamestown's lineage came the entrepreneurial energy, the appetite for enterprise and expansion, the practical and acquisitive spirit that built a continental economy. From Plymouth's came the covenantal habit of self-government, the moral seriousness, the sense of providential mission, the conviction that a people might bind themselves together under God to pursue a higher purpose. The merchant and the Pilgrim are both authentic American ancestors, and the nation that grew from these beginnings would carry the tension between them ever after — between commerce and covenant, between the pursuit of prosperity and the pursuit of righteousness. To tell the American story as purely one or the other — a tale of pious founders or of grasping adventurers — is to lose half of it. The truth is that America had two foundings, and it has been arguing between them ever since.

## The Honest Counterpoint

A faithful account must temper the cherished story with several cautions. The first concerns *mythologizing*. The Pilgrim narrative, and especially the image of the first Thanksgiving, has been heavily romanticized in American memory, smoothed into a sentimental tableau that obscures the grimness of the reality and the complexity of the events. Plymouth itself was small and struggling, and would soon be overshadowed and absorbed by the far larger Massachusetts Bay colony; its outsized place in the national story owes much to later myth-making. The second caution concerns the *Compact's significance*, which can be overstated. It was, in its immediate context, a practical expedient for a specific emergency — a way to hold a company together when its legal patent did not apply — not a considered theory of democracy. It was not democratic in the modern sense: it was signed by the adult men of a particular religious community, not by all, and it established a government of the godly, not a liberal pluralist state. The line from the Compact to American democracy is real, but it should be drawn as a genuine root, not a straight and simple highway.

The third and weightiest caution concerns the *Native peoples*. The land the Pilgrims settled was not empty; it had been depopulated shortly before their arrival by epidemic diseases, introduced by earlier European contact, that had swept through the Wampanoag and their neighbors with catastrophic force — a grim fact that made the Pilgrims' survival possible and that they themselves read as providential. Squanto's ability to help them was the fruit of his own tragedy: kidnapped years earlier and carried to Europe, he returned to find his entire village dead of disease. And the early peace between the colonists and the Wampanoag, real as it was, would give way within two generations to the devastating violence of King Philip's War. The Pilgrim story is genuine and worth honoring, but it unfolded amid a Native catastrophe that honesty cannot omit. Finally, the course's own thesis — that American self-government grew from Protestant covenant theology — should be held as a strong and defensible interpretation rather than the only possible reading; secular historians may emphasize other roots, and the covenantal thread, real as it is, runs alongside others. With the religious founding now before us, alongside the commercial founding at Jamestown, we turn next to the larger and more influential Puritan migration, and to the political theology that would shape New England and, through it, the nation.
