No phrase from the American founding era is more quoted, or more misunderstood, than John Winthrop's image of a "city upon a hill." It has been pressed into the service of American exceptionalism by presidents of both parties, made to mean that America is a shining example to the world, a beacon of freedom and prosperity. Winthrop meant something at once grander and graver, and recovering what he actually meant opens a window onto the Puritan mind — the most influential single force in the shaping of early American culture. For if Plymouth was small, the Puritan migration that founded Massachusetts Bay was vast, and the political theology the Puritans carried with them would mark the American character far more deeply than the Pilgrims' covenant in the cabin of a ship.

## The Great Migration

The Puritans who founded Massachusetts Bay must be distinguished from the Pilgrims of Plymouth. The Pilgrims were Separatists, who had abandoned the Church of England as hopelessly corrupt. The Puritans were, for the most part, *non-separating* — they wished not to leave the Church of England but to *purify* it, to complete the Reformation they believed it had left half-finished. When persecution under the English crown made that hope seem impossible at home, a great body of them resolved to build a purified church and a godly society in America instead — not as a retreat from the Church of England, but as a demonstration of what a properly reformed Christian commonwealth could be.

The result was the Great Migration of the 1630s, in which thousands of Puritans crossed the Atlantic to New England — a movement vastly larger than the Pilgrim settlement, and incomparably more consequential. They came not as isolated adventurers seeking gold, like the Virginians, nor as a tiny band of refugees, like the Pilgrims, but as whole communities — families, congregations, ministers, magistrates — intent on founding a comprehensive Christian society. They were, by the standards of the age, highly educated, deeply literate, and intensely serious about both their theology and their social order. And they brought with them a distinctive understanding of their purpose, which their leader gave classic expression at the very outset.

## "A City Upon a Hill"

In 1630, aboard the ship *Arbella* (or shortly before embarking), John Winthrop, the colony's first governor, delivered the lay sermon known as "A Model of Christian Charity," in which he set out the spiritual meaning of the enterprise the migrants were undertaking.[^1] Its most famous words declare that the new community would be as a "city upon a hill," with the eyes of all people upon it. To understand Winthrop, everything depends on grasping what he meant by this — and it was not the comfortable flattery the phrase has since become.

Winthrop's image came from the Sermon on the Mount, where Christ tells his disciples that they are a city set on a hill that cannot be hidden. The point of the image, in its original setting and in Winthrop's use, was not boastful celebration but solemn *visibility and accountability*. To be a city upon a hill is to be exposed to view, watched by all — and therefore to bear a heavy responsibility, for one's failures will be as visible as one's successes. Winthrop was not telling the Puritans that they were a glorious example destined to inspire the world; he was warning them. If they kept their covenant with God, they would be a praise and a glory, and others would say of later plantations, "may the Lord make it like that of New England." But if they failed — if they dealt falsely with God, embraced the world, and broke their covenant — then they would become a *byword* and a *curse*, a cautionary tale of a people who had professed much and betrayed it, and the watching world would see their shame. The city on the hill was a position of peril as much as of privilege.

## The Errand and the Covenant

This brings us to the heart of the Puritan self-understanding, which scholars have called the *errand into the wilderness*. The Puritans understood themselves to be a people with a divine commission — sent into the American wilderness to accomplish a specific task: to build a model Christian commonwealth, a reformed church and a godly society, that would stand as a demonstration to the world and, some hoped, hasten the further reformation of England and of Christendom itself. They were not merely emigrants improving their condition; they were a people under orders, charged with an errand by God.

And the framework that bound the whole enterprise together was, once again, the *covenant*. Like the Pilgrims, the Puritans were Calvinists steeped in covenant theology, and they understood their venture as a covenant with God — a solemn mutual agreement in which they bound themselves to obey God's law and build a godly society, and God, in turn, would bless and prosper them if they kept faith and judge them if they broke it. This *national covenant*, modeled on the covenant between God and Israel, gave the Puritan enterprise its peculiar intensity. Every hardship was read as a test or a chastisement; every success as a sign of God's favor; the whole life of the community was understood as the working-out of its covenant with God. The Puritans saw themselves, in effect, as a new Israel, brought through the sea into a wilderness, bound to God by covenant, charged with a holy errand, and answerable to him for their faithfulness. This covenantal and providential self-understanding — a people in covenant with God, charged with a mission, watched by the world, accountable for their faithfulness — would prove one of the most enduring and double-edged inheritances in all of American culture.

## The Godly Commonwealth

The errand was not an abstraction; the Puritans set out to build a concrete social and political order, and what they built left deep marks on American life. Massachusetts Bay was a *godly commonwealth*, in which church and civil government were closely intertwined, each supporting the other in the common task of maintaining a holy society. The franchise — the right to vote and hold office — was tied, in the early decades, to membership in the church, so that the godly governed the community; and the magistrates saw it as their duty to uphold not only civil order but true religion. This was not religious liberty as later Americans would understand it; it was a confessional society, intolerant of dissent, in which the purposes of church and state were one.

Yet within this order grew habits that would prove momentous. Puritan congregationalism organized each church as a self-governing body of members who had covenanted together, called their own ministers, and managed their own affairs — and this congregational self-government spilled naturally into civil life, in the New England *town meeting*, where the freemen of a community gathered to govern their local affairs by discussion and vote. Here, in the practice of the gathered congregation and the assembled town, was a school of self-government whose influence on American political culture would be incalculable. The Puritans prized education as well, for a people who must read Scripture for themselves needed to be literate, and a church that needed learned ministers needed schools to train them: within a few years of their arrival they founded Harvard College and established laws requiring towns to maintain schools. A society built to read the Bible became one of the most literate in the world — and literacy, congregational self-rule, and the town meeting together seeded the civic and intellectual soil from which the later American order would grow.

## The Long Shadow

The Puritan errand cast a shadow far longer than the colony that began it, and its influence on the American character is hard to overstate. From it descends the deep and persistent American sense of *national mission* — the conviction, recurring throughout American history in both religious and secularized forms, that America is a nation set apart, charged with a special purpose, a model and an example to the world. The idea of American exceptionalism, the rhetoric of America as a redeemer nation, the recurrent sense that the country is accountable to a higher standard than mere self-interest — all of these trace, in part, to the Puritan errand and Winthrop's city on a hill.

The inheritance is genuinely double-edged, and honesty requires seeing both edges. At its best, the covenantal sense of mission gave America a moral seriousness, a conviction that the nation stands under judgment and must answer for its faithfulness to its ideals — a conviction that would later fuel movements for reform and against injustice, as reformers held the nation to the high standard it professed. At its worst, the same sense of being a chosen people with a divine errand could breed self-righteousness, the conviction that American purposes were God's purposes, and a willingness to justify the dispossession of others as the work of providence. The city on a hill could inspire humility before a watching God or arrogance before a watching world, and American history shows both. To understand this inheritance is to understand something essential about the nation that grew from it — its idealism and its self-righteousness alike, rooted together in the Puritan covenant.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Several cautions guard against distortion. The first is the persistent *misreading* of Winthrop's phrase itself, which has been so thoroughly absorbed into the rhetoric of national celebration that its original meaning — solemn accountability under the threat of becoming a curse and a byword — is routinely lost. To quote the city on a hill as simple praise of American greatness is to invert Winthrop's actual warning. The second caution concerns *continuity*: the line from the Puritan errand to later American exceptionalism is real but was not simple or direct; it ran through long processes of secularization and reinterpretation, and we should not imagine a straight inheritance passed down unchanged. The Puritans themselves would scarcely recognize much of what has been claimed in their name.

The third and most important caution concerns the *darker side* of the Puritan venture, which a faithful account cannot omit. The Puritans built a society of genuine moral seriousness, remarkable literacy, and real self-government — but also one of considerable intolerance, which banished dissenters such as Roger Williams and Anne Hutchinson, persecuted Quakers, and would later convulse itself in the Salem witch trials. Their errand into the wilderness was also, for the Native peoples already there, an invasion, and the covenantal conviction of being a chosen people in a promised land could and did serve to justify dispossession and, in the Pequot War and King Philip's War, brutal violence. The Puritan inheritance is profound and, in important ways, admirable; it is also implicated in real intolerance and real injustice, and the course will not celebrate the one while suppressing the other. The city on a hill was a place of high ideals and hard failures, and the truth requires both. We turn next to the covenant theology itself, and to how this Puritan habit of binding communities together under God became, in time, a seedbed of American self-government.
