We have watched the covenant idea appear twice already in the founding of New England — in the Mayflower Compact, where a people bound themselves into a body politic, and in the Puritan national covenant, by which a whole society pledged itself to God. Now we examine the covenant at its source, in the theology and church life of the Puritans, and we discover something remarkable: that the Puritan churches were organized, in effect, as little republics — voluntary associations of equals, formed by consent, governing themselves, electing their own leaders. And we discover that this church polity was a school of self-government whose graduates would build a nation. The roots of American democracy, this lesson argues, run not only through Parliament and the common law but through the gathered congregation.

## The Covenant Idea

At the center of Puritan thought stood the idea of the *covenant* — a binding, mutual agreement, with obligations and conditions on both sides. The Puritans were Reformed Christians, heirs of a theology that understood God's dealings with humanity in covenantal terms: God relates to his people not by arbitrary decree alone but through covenants, structured relationships of promise and obligation. This theological framework saturated the Puritan imagination, and it did not stay confined to the relationship between God and the soul. The Puritans came to see covenant everywhere — as the bond that constituted a church, the tie that bound a community, the basis of a rightly ordered society, and, ultimately, the foundation of legitimate government.

The crucial feature of a covenant, for our purposes, is that it rests on *consent*. A covenant is entered into freely, by mutual agreement; it is not imposed from above but assented to by the parties who bind themselves. A people in covenant are a people who have agreed together, who have given their word, who are bound by terms to which they have consented. This single idea — that the most fundamental relationships are constituted by free, mutual consent — carried within it, as we will see, the seed of a whole theory of self-government.

## The Self-Governing Church

To see how the covenant shaped political habits, we must look at how the Puritans, and especially the Congregationalists among them, organized their churches — for the church was the institution in which ordinary people actually practiced the covenant, week by week, for generations. The Congregational church was constituted by covenant: it came into being when a body of believers freely agreed together, before God and one another, to walk together as a church. No bishop established it from above; the members covenanted it into existence from below, by their own consent.

And having been formed by consent, the church *governed itself* by consent. Each local congregation was independent, managing its own affairs without subjection to any external hierarchy of bishops or archbishops. The members *elected* their own officers — they called their minister, chose their elders and deacons — so that leadership rested on the choice of the congregation rather than appointment from above. The members collectively exercised *discipline*, deciding together the affairs of their community. A Congregational church was thus, in its very structure, a voluntary association of equals: formed by consent, self-governing, electing its leaders, deciding its affairs by the participation of its members. It was, in everything but name, a little republic — a self-governing commonwealth in miniature, embedded in the ordinary religious life of every Puritan town.[^1]

## A School of Self-Government

Here is the heart of the matter. A whole population accustomed, generation after generation, to *practicing* self-government in the church could hardly fail to carry those habits into civil life. The Congregational church was a school of self-government, and its lessons were learned by everyone, not by a political elite alone but by the ordinary farmers and tradesmen who gathered as members of the church.

Consider what they learned there. They learned to form communities by *covenant and consent* — to understand that legitimate association rests on the free agreement of its members. They learned to *govern their own affairs locally*, without dependence on a distant hierarchy. They learned to *elect their leaders* and to hold them accountable to the body that chose them. They learned to *deliberate and decide together*, conducting the business of a self-governing body by discussion and vote. These are precisely the habits and skills of democratic citizenship — and the Puritans practiced them every week, in the most important institution of their lives, long before they applied them to the state. When the time came to govern towns and colonies, the people of New England already knew how, because they had been doing it in church. The New England town meeting, where the freemen gathered to govern their local affairs by discussion and vote, was the civil mirror of the church meeting; the one flowed naturally from the other.

## From Covenant to Constitution

The covenant did more than train habits; it supplied *principles* — a whole implicit theory of legitimate government that the Puritans drew out and applied. If even one's relationship with God could be understood as a covenant resting on consent, then surely human government, too, should rest on the covenant and consent of the governed, rather than on mere force or imposition. Government by consent was not, for the Puritans, an alien Enlightenment notion; it flowed directly from the covenantal logic at the heart of their faith.

This principle was made explicit early and unmistakably. In 1639 the Puritan settlers of Connecticut, under the influence of the minister Thomas Hooker, drew up the Fundamental Orders of Connecticut, often called the first written constitution in the English colonies — a frame of government established by the people for themselves. Hooker preached, in a famous sermon, that the foundation of authority is laid in the free consent of the people, and that those who have the power to appoint their officers also have the power to set the bounds of the power they grant them. Here, in plain words, is the covenantal theory of government: authority arises from the consent of the governed, and it is limited by the terms of the grant. The same logic implied that authority is *conditional* and *accountable* — that rulers, like parties to any covenant, are bound by its terms, and that a ruler who breaks the covenant forfeits his claim to obedience, a seed of the right of resistance to tyranny. The covenant of the gathered church had become the covenant of the constituted commonwealth.

## The Religious Roots of American Liberty

We can now state the thesis of this lesson plainly. American self-government — with its insistence on consent, its practice of elections, its prizing of local autonomy, its conception of limited and accountable authority, its very habit of constituting communities and governments by written agreement — grew in substantial part from Puritan covenant theology and Congregational church polity. The roots of American liberty are not merely secular; they are, in significant measure, *religious*. The self-governing covenant church was a seedbed of the self-governing republic.

This is a claim that runs against a common assumption — that democracy and self-government are essentially secular achievements, products of the Enlightenment's break with religion. The American case complicates that story profoundly. Long before Locke's treatises crossed the Atlantic, the people of New England were governing themselves by covenant and consent in their churches and their towns, having learned the practice from their theology. The habits and principles of self-government were bred in the bone of a deeply religious people, in the most religious of their institutions. When later Americans built a nation on the consent of the governed, they were drawing, whether they knew it or not, on a tradition that ran back through the Congregational meetinghouse to the covenant theology of the Reformation. The American experiment in self-government had Protestant roots, and the gathered church was its nursery.

## The Honest Counterpoint

This thesis, powerful as it is, must be held with care, for it is easily overstated, and a faithful account requires several qualifications. First, the religious root was not the *only* root. American self-government drew on multiple sources — the English common-law and parliamentary tradition examined in the previous unit, the classical republican heritage, the Enlightenment social-contract theory of Locke and others, and the sheer practical experience of decades of colonial self-rule. The covenantal and Congregational root is real and underappreciated, but it is one strand among several, and to make it the whole story would be to commit, in the political domain, exactly the overclaim that honest history must avoid.

Second, there is a genuine tension to confront. The same Puritan society that practiced self-government within its churches was, toward outsiders, *intolerant and theocratic*: it tied civil rights to church membership, enforced religious conformity, and banished or persecuted dissenters such as Roger Williams and Anne Hutchinson. The "little republics" were republics of the godly, not liberal democracies; their internal self-government coexisted with a harsh intolerance toward those outside the covenant. There is a real irony here, and it points forward: some of the most important developments toward genuine religious liberty came not from the Puritan establishment but from the *dissenters it expelled* — from Roger Williams, whom we meet in the next lesson, and the colony of Rhode Island he founded as a refuge for the very liberty Massachusetts denied. The democratic legacy of New England is thus as much the work of those who rebelled against Puritan orthodoxy as of the orthodoxy itself. And finally, secular historians may weigh the Enlightenment and common-law roots more heavily than the religious one; the covenantal thesis is a strong and well-supported interpretation, but a contested one. Held with these qualifications, the claim stands: the self-governing covenant church was a profound and underappreciated source of the American habit of self-rule — one stream, but a deep one, in the river that became American liberty. To the most famous of the dissenters, and the surprising roots of religious freedom, we turn next.
