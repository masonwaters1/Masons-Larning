In the last lesson we saw how Puritan covenant theology seeded the American habit of self-government — but we also saw its shadow: the Puritan establishment was intolerant, banishing dissenters and tying citizenship to religious conformity. Now we follow one of those banished dissenters, and we discover something surprising. The American principle of religious liberty — the separation of church and state, the freedom of conscience, the wall that keeps government out of the soul — is often assumed to be a secular achievement, the gift of skeptical Enlightenment minds suspicious of religion. But its earliest and most radical American champion was not a skeptic at all. He was an intensely religious man, driven by passionate Christian conviction, who argued for religious liberty *because* of his faith, not in spite of it. His name was Roger Williams, and his story reveals the surprising religious roots of American freedom.

## The Troublesome Saint

Roger Williams arrived in Massachusetts in 1631, a brilliant and fervent young Puritan minister, welcomed at first as a promising addition to the godly commonwealth.[^1] But it quickly became clear that Williams's convictions were too radical for the Puritan establishment to tolerate. He was not less religious than the Puritan leaders but, in a sense, *more* so — more rigorous, more uncompromising, more willing to follow his principles wherever they led. And they led him to conclusions that struck at the foundations of the Massachusetts order.

Williams argued that the civil magistrate had no rightful authority over religious matters at all. The state, he insisted, could legitimately enforce only the "second table" of God's law — the commandments governing our duties to one another, against theft and murder and the like — but had no business enforcing the "first table," the commandments governing our duties to God. Religion was simply not the state's affair. From this followed his most explosive convictions: that no one should be compelled in matters of faith, that the church must be entirely separate from the state, and that liberty of conscience must be granted to all. To the leaders of Massachusetts, who had crossed an ocean to build a unified godly commonwealth in which church and state worked hand in hand, these were dangerous and subversive ideas.

## Soul Liberty

At the heart of Williams's thought was a principle he expressed with unforgettable force — what later generations would call "soul liberty," the absolute freedom of the individual conscience in matters of religion. Faith, Williams insisted, cannot be coerced. Genuine belief is a free act of the soul before God; it cannot be compelled by the magistrate's sword, and the attempt to force it produces not faith but hypocrisy. Forced worship, he declared in one of his most striking phrases, is a stench in God's nostrils — an offense to the very God it pretends to honor, because God desires the free worship of the heart, not the outward conformity extracted by fear.

This argument had a double edge that is essential to grasp. Williams opposed religious coercion not because he thought religion unimportant, but because he thought it *supremely* important — too sacred to be handled by the clumsy, corrupting instrument of state power. Compelled religion was both a *tyranny* against the conscience and a *blasphemy* against God, who would have only willing worshippers. The freedom of conscience, for Williams, was not a concession to doubt or indifference; it was a requirement of true faith, which by its nature must be voluntary. Here is the surprising core of his vision: religious liberty as a deeply *religious* demand, rooted in a high view of the sacredness and freedom of faith.

## The Wall of Separation

Williams gave the principle of church-state separation an image that would echo through American history. He wrote of a "wall of separation" between the "garden of the church" and the "wilderness of the world" — a wall whose purpose was to protect the garden of the church from being overrun and corrupted by the wilderness of worldly power. When the wall was broken down, he warned, and the church was entangled with the state, the result was not the sanctification of the state but the corruption of the church, which lost its purity by grasping at worldly power and entangling itself in worldly affairs.

This is a crucial point, and it distinguishes Williams's wall from the more famous one that Thomas Jefferson would invoke a century and a half later. Jefferson's wall of separation was designed largely to protect the *state and society from the church* — to keep religion from dominating public life. Williams's wall was designed to protect the *church from the state* — to keep worldly power from corrupting the purity of faith. The two men reached a similar institutional conclusion, the separation of church and state, but from opposite directions and opposite motives: Jefferson from an Enlightenment concern to free public life from religious control, Williams from a religious concern to free the church from worldly corruption. The American principle of separation, it turns out, has both a secular and a religious genealogy — and the religious one came first.

## Banishment and Refuge

Massachusetts could not abide such teachings, and in 1635 Williams was tried and sentenced to banishment. In the dead of winter he fled into the wilderness, where he was sheltered by the Narragansett people, whose language he had learned and whose rights he had defended — for Williams also argued, scandalously, that the colonists had no rightful claim to Native lands merely by royal charter, since the king had no authority to grant what was not his to give. In 1636 Williams founded a new settlement, which he named Providence, in gratitude for what he saw as God's merciful guidance.

Providence, and the colony of Rhode Island that grew around it, was established on a principle unprecedented in the modern world: complete religious liberty. There would be no established church, no religious test for citizenship, no compulsion in matters of faith. Liberty of conscience was extended to all — to Christians of every variety, to Jews, to Quakers, and in principle even to those of no religion at all. Rhode Island became a refuge for the persecuted, a haven for the dissenters and outcasts whom the other colonies drove out, and the first government in the modern world founded on the full separation of church and state and the freedom of conscience for everyone. Derided by its neighbors as a chaotic sink of heresy — "Rogue's Island," they called it — it was in fact a bold experiment in a freedom the world had not seen, and a living anticipation of the principles that would one day be written into the American Constitution.

## The Baptist Connection

Williams's journey carried him, for a time, into the Baptist fold; he helped establish what is often considered the first Baptist church in America, at Providence, around 1638, before moving on in his restless search for the true church. And this connection points to something larger and important: the Baptist tradition, and the broader family of radical, free-church Protestantism, was the great carrier of religious liberty in the English-speaking world.

The reason lies in Baptist conviction itself. Baptists held that the church is a *voluntary* community of true, believing Christians — a body that one joins by a free, adult profession of faith, sealed in believer's baptism, rather than a state institution into which one is born and baptized as an infant. But a voluntary church is, by its very logic, incompatible with a state church and with religious coercion. If the church is a free assembly of genuine believers, then the state cannot rightfully compel membership or enforce belief, and church and state must be separate. The Baptist principle of the gathered, voluntary church thus *required* religious liberty and church-state separation as its natural consequence. And because Baptists were themselves a persecuted minority — harried by Puritans in New England and Anglicans in the South — their commitment to liberty of conscience was sharpened by their own suffering. The line runs from Williams and the early Baptists forward through later Baptist champions of religious freedom, whose agitation would help secure the First Amendment. Religious liberty in America was, to a remarkable degree, a Baptist and radical-Protestant achievement before it was an Enlightenment one.

## Why This Matters

The story of Roger Williams overturns a common assumption about the origins of American religious liberty. The standard narrative treats the separation of church and state as a secular, Enlightenment idea — the work of skeptics and rationalists who wanted to free society from the grip of religion. Williams shows that this narrative is, at best, half the truth. The earliest and most radical American argument for religious liberty came not from a skeptic but from a passionate Christian believer, and it was grounded not in indifference to religion but in a profound conviction about religion's sacredness and the necessity of free, uncoerced faith.

This is the "surprising" thesis of the lesson, and it fits the larger argument of the course: that some of the deepest principles of American freedom have religious roots that the secular telling has obscured. The separation of church and state was, in its American origins, as much a religious idea as a secular one — born of piety as well as philosophy, of the conviction that faith must be free because it is holy. The wall of separation was first built by a believer, to protect the garden of the church from the wilderness of the world. To understand the American achievement of religious liberty fully, we must hold both genealogies together: the secular Enlightenment stream that would flow through Jefferson and Madison, and the religious, radical-Protestant stream that flowed first through Roger Williams and the Baptists.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Several qualifications keep this thesis honest. First, the religious genealogy is not the *only* genealogy, and to make it so would be its own overclaim. Enlightenment thought made a real and major contribution to religious liberty: John Locke's argument for toleration, the broader philosophical case for freedom of conscience, and the practical recognition that a religiously diverse society could not be governed by enforced uniformity all played essential roles. The later architects of the First Amendment, Jefferson and Madison, were motivated substantially by Enlightenment conviction, and Jefferson's wall, as we saw, faced in a different direction from Williams's. Religious liberty had two parents, religious and secular, and an honest account credits both. The course's point is that the religious root is real and underappreciated, not that it stands alone.

Second, we should not overstate Williams's direct influence. He was, in his own time, an eccentric and marginal figure, his colony small and derided, and the precise line from Williams to the First Amendment is debated; the causal path ran less through Williams personally than through the broader Baptist and free-church tradition he represented, which agitated for religious liberty down through the founding era. Most colonies maintained established churches well into the eighteenth century, and full religious liberty was a long, contested achievement rather than a straight line from Providence. And there is a deeper irony to register, connecting back to the previous lesson: the New England legacy of liberty came as much from the *dissenters against* the Puritan establishment, like Williams, as from the establishment itself. The orthodox Puritans gave America the covenant and the habit of self-government; the radical dissenters they expelled gave America soul liberty and the separation of church and state. Both streams — the establishment and its rebels — flow into the river of American freedom, and the fullest account honors both. We turn next to another colony founded on principles of conscience, William Penn's holy experiment in Pennsylvania.
