The previous lesson followed Roger Williams into the wilderness of Rhode Island, where he founded a colony on the then-startling principle that the state has no business coercing the conscience. Williams was a voice crying in the wilderness, and Rhode Island a small and often-scorned refuge. But the principle he championed would find, a generation later, a far grander and more deliberate expression, in a colony founded not by an exile fleeing persecution but by a wealthy and well-connected Englishman who set out, with a king's charter in hand, to build a society on liberty of conscience from the ground up. William Penn called it his "holy experiment," and it would become one of the most consequential experiments in the whole colonial enterprise — a test of whether a society built on religious freedom, fair dealing, and peace could actually flourish. The answer it gave would echo through the American founding.

## The Inner Light and Its Consequences

To understand Pennsylvania, one must first understand the faith of its founder, for the colony was the political expression of a theology.[^1] Penn was a Quaker, a member of the Religious Society of Friends, a movement that had arisen in England amid the ferment of the mid-seventeenth century and that held convictions radical enough to earn its members savage persecution. At the heart of Quaker faith lay the doctrine of the Inner Light: the belief that the light of God, the presence of Christ, dwelt within every human being, and that each person could encounter God directly, without the mediation of priest, sacrament, or established church. From this single conviction flowed a cascade of startling consequences. If God's light was in everyone, then all people were spiritually equal — and the Quakers refused, accordingly, to doff their hats to social superiors or to use the deferential forms of address that ranked one person above another, addressing everyone alike with the plain "thee" and "thou." If God's light was in everyone, then no one had the right to coerce another's conscience, and religious liberty followed. And if God's light was in everyone, then to do violence to another was to do violence to something sacred, and so the Quakers embraced pacifism, refusing to bear arms or to swear oaths.

These convictions made the Quakers intolerable to the authorities of their day, and they were fined, imprisoned, and abused on both sides of the Atlantic; even in Puritan Massachusetts, several Quakers were hanged for persistently returning to preach. Penn himself, though the son of a distinguished admiral, was imprisoned more than once for his faith. But Penn also possessed something few persecuted radicals possessed: a large inheritance, powerful connections at court, and a claim upon the crown, for the king owed his late father a substantial debt. In settlement of that debt, Penn secured, in 1681, a charter to a vast territory in America — and he resolved to use it to build a refuge not only for his fellow Quakers but for the persecuted of every faith, a commonwealth founded on the principles his religion had taught him.

## The Frame of Liberty

Penn set about his experiment with deliberate care, drafting a Frame of Government for his new colony that embodied his convictions in law. Its centerpiece was liberty of conscience: Pennsylvania would have no established church, would compel no one to attend or support any form of worship, and would extend toleration to all who professed faith in God. In an age when nearly every society, colonial and European alike, enforced religious uniformity and treated dissent as a crime, this was a remarkable provision — a whole colony organized on the principle that the conscience must be free. Penn's government was also notably representative, with an elected assembly and broad participation, and his penal code was humane by the brutal standards of the age, sharply limiting the death penalty and emphasizing reformation over mere punishment. He was building, quite consciously, a model society, a demonstration to the world that liberty and order were not enemies but could flourish together.

And flourish it did. Philadelphia, the "city of brotherly love," which Penn laid out as a planned city of broad streets and open squares, grew with astonishing speed into one of the largest and most prosperous cities in the colonies. Settlers poured in, drawn precisely by the promise of freedom and opportunity — not only English Quakers but Germans of many varieties, Mennonites and Pietists and Lutherans and Reformed, the people who would become known as the Pennsylvania Dutch, along with Scots-Irish Presbyterians and others. Pennsylvania became a patchwork of faiths and peoples living side by side, and it prospered mightily, its very diversity and freedom proving to be engines of growth rather than sources of chaos. The holy experiment was working: a society built on liberty of conscience was not descending into anarchy but rising into prosperity, and the lesson would not be lost on the founders of the nation that Pennsylvania would help to shape.

## Fair Dealing with the Lenape

Penn's principles extended, remarkably, to his dealings with the native peoples of his colony, the Lenape or Delaware. Where so much of the colonial encounter was marked by fraud, violence, and dispossession, Penn insisted on fair dealing. He took the trouble to learn something of the Lenape language, he treated their leaders with respect, and he insisted on purchasing land rather than simply seizing it, negotiating treaties that he intended to honor. The most famous of these, concluded beneath an elm at Shackamaxon, became legendary as a covenant of peace between the settlers and the Lenape — a treaty that, in the admiring phrase later attributed to Voltaire, was the only one never sworn to and never broken. The legend outran the history, as legends do, but the underlying reality was genuine: for as long as Penn's own principles governed the colony, relations with the Lenape were markedly more peaceful and just than in most of colonial America, a rare instance of the Christian ethic of fair dealing actually governing the encounter between settler and native.

## The Experiment and Its Limits

Pennsylvania's significance for the American story is hard to overstate. It stood as a working demonstration — prosperous, orderly, and free — that a society need not enforce a single faith to hold together, that liberty of conscience and civil peace could coexist, that diversity could be a strength. Alongside Puritan New England, with its covenant and its pursuit of religious uniformity, and the Anglican South, with its established church and social hierarchy, Pennsylvania offered a third model of colonial Christian society: pluralist, tolerant, commercial, and free. And of the three, it was Pennsylvania's model that would come closest to the settlement the nation would eventually reach — a republic with no established church, in which many faiths coexisted under a government that protected the conscience of all. Penn's holy experiment was, in a real sense, a rehearsal for the American experiment.

Yet honesty requires acknowledging that the experiment was imperfect and, in some measure, unsustained. Penn's toleration, broad as it was, was not unlimited: full political rights were reserved for Christians, and Catholics and Jews, though free to worship, faced restrictions on holding office. More troublingly, Penn himself owned slaves, and the colony that produced the first organized protests against slavery also participated in it — a contradiction we will confront directly in a later lesson. And the fair dealing with the Lenape did not survive Penn's own generation; his heirs, less scrupulous and more grasping, engineered the notorious Walking Purchase of 1737, a fraudulent land grab that betrayed the very peace Penn had built. The holy experiment, like every human experiment, fell short of its own ideals, and its noblest achievements proved fragile in the hands of lesser men. But the ideal itself — a society founded on liberty of conscience, fair dealing, and peace — was real, and it was planted in American soil at Philadelphia, where it would help, in time, to shape a nation. To the wider world of colonial pluralism from which it grew, we turn next.
