Pennsylvania was the most deliberate experiment in religious pluralism, but it was not an isolated one. It sat at the heart of a whole region — the middle colonies of New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware, together with the neighboring colony of Maryland — that constituted, taken as a whole, the most religiously diverse society anywhere in the Atlantic world. If New England was defined by the Puritan pursuit of a covenanted uniformity, and the South by the transplanted establishment of the Church of England, the middle colonies were defined by their sheer, unruly variety: a patchwork of faiths and peoples living side by side, jostling and trading and worshipping in a dozen different ways, held together not by a shared creed but by the practical necessity of coexistence. Here, more than anywhere, one can watch American religious pluralism emerging — not as a philosophy proclaimed in advance, but as a fact on the ground, worked out among neighbors who had no choice but to tolerate one another.

## The Polyglot Port

The diversity of the middle colonies had deep roots, and its oldest source was Dutch.[^1] Before the English seized it in 1664 and renamed it New York, the colony of New Netherland had been a Dutch commercial venture, and it had carried across the Atlantic something of the famous tolerance of the Dutch Republic — a tolerance born less of principle than of commerce, for a trading society learns quickly that profit flows more freely when it is not dammed up by religious quarrels. New Amsterdam, the little port at the tip of Manhattan, was a genuinely polyglot place; a visiting Jesuit reported that a remarkable number of different languages could be heard in its streets, spoken by settlers of many nations and faiths. The Dutch Reformed Church was the official establishment, and the colony's governors were not always tolerant by inclination — one of them tried to turn away a group of Jewish refugees who arrived in 1654 — but the commercial logic of the place, reinforced by the directors of the Dutch West India Company back home, pushed consistently toward accommodation. Those Jewish refugees were allowed to stay, founding the first Jewish community in what would become the United States, and the pattern of pragmatic tolerance survived the English takeover and deepened over the following century.

By the eighteenth century, the religious map of the middle colonies had become bewilderingly various. There were Dutch Reformed and English Anglicans, Scots-Irish and Scottish Presbyterians arriving in great waves, German Lutherans and German Reformed, the peace churches of the Mennonites and the Moravians and the Dunkers, Quakers in abundance, Catholics, Jews, and every shade of sectarian enthusiasm. No single church could dominate such a mixture, and none did. The result was a society in which religious diversity was simply the water people swam in — a normal condition of life rather than a scandal to be suppressed.

## Maryland and the Catholic Refuge

The most poignant experiment in toleration in the region, and one of the most instructive, unfolded in Maryland. The colony had been founded in the 1630s by the Calvert family, the Lords Baltimore, who were Catholic — and therein lay both the promise and the peril of their venture. In Protestant England, Catholics were a persecuted minority, barred from office, suspected of disloyalty, subject to harsh penal laws. The Calverts envisioned Maryland as a refuge where their fellow Catholics could worship in peace. But they faced a delicate problem: they could not build a purely Catholic colony, for they needed Protestant settlers to make it viable, and they governed under a Protestant crown that would never have tolerated an openly Catholic establishment. Their solution was toleration — and in 1649 the colony enacted the Maryland Toleration Act, which guaranteed liberty of conscience to all who professed faith in the Trinity, forbidding anyone to be troubled for their religion. It was one of the earliest laws of religious toleration in the English-speaking world, and it arose, tellingly, from the situation of a religious minority that needed toleration in order to survive.

The fate of the act is as instructive as its passing. Toleration in Maryland proved fragile, hostage to the shifting balance of power between Catholic and Protestant; when Protestants gained the upper hand, the act was repealed, and the Catholics who had founded the colony as a refuge found themselves, in the colony they had made, subjected to the very penal laws they had fled. The episode reveals both the promise and the limits of colonial toleration: it was real, it was significant, and it was often precarious, dependent on the fortunes of faction rather than secured by settled principle. But the Maryland experiment planted another seed of the American settlement, and it demonstrated a truth that would recur throughout the American story — that toleration often arises first not from lofty principle but from the practical needs of a minority, and that those who have been persecuted are frequently the first to grasp why the conscience must be free.

## Pluralism by Necessity

What the middle colonies demonstrated, taken together, was that religious pluralism could work — not because anyone had designed it, but because the facts of settlement had made it unavoidable, and coexistence had proven not only possible but prosperous. This was pluralism by necessity, toleration arrived at through the sheer impracticability of enforcing uniformity upon so various a people. And it mattered enormously for what was to come. When the founders of the nation eventually confronted the question of religion and the state, they did so as heirs not of a single model but of several, and the middle-colony model — of many faiths coexisting under a government that favored none — was the one that most closely anticipated the arrangement they would choose. The middle colonies were, in effect, a laboratory in which the American religious settlement was tested in advance and found to work, decades before it was written into the Constitution.

It would be a mistake, of course, to paint this world in colors too bright. The toleration of the middle colonies was limited and uneven, and it was, almost entirely, a toleration among Christians, and mostly among Protestants; Catholics were widely distrusted and periodically disabled, Jews remained a tiny and legally constrained minority, and the language of "toleration" itself implied a hierarchy in which some were graciously permitted to exist rather than recognized as equals. Full religious equality, as opposed to mere toleration, still lay in the future. But the future was being prepared here, in the crowded and various middle colonies, where people of many faiths had learned, out of necessity, to live as neighbors. That hard-won practical wisdom would prove to be one of the middle colonies' great gifts to the nation. To the very different religious world of the South, ordered not by pluralism but by establishment, we turn next.
