We have seen two models of colonial Christian society: the covenanted uniformity of Puritan New England, and the tolerant pluralism of the middle colonies. The southern colonies — Virginia above all, and the Carolinas, and eventually Georgia — offer a third, and in some ways it was the most familiar of the three to English eyes, for it was the most straightforward transplant of the old country. In the South, the Church of England was established by law, as it was at home, and religion was woven into a hierarchical social order of gentry and commoners, masters and servants, in a way that reproduced, on American soil, the settled arrangements of the English countryside. If New England was a departure from England in the direction of intense reform, and the middle colonies a departure in the direction of variety, the South was, at least in its intentions, England carried across the ocean largely intact — established church, social hierarchy, and all.

## The Transplanted Establishment

In Virginia, the oldest and most important of the southern colonies, the Church of England was the established church from the beginning, and its structure mirrored the English model.[^1] The colony was divided into parishes, each with its church and its minister supported by public taxes, the tithes levied on all inhabitants regardless of their private convictions. Local religious affairs were governed by the vestry, a body of leading parishioners — which in practice meant the local gentry, the great planters who dominated Virginia society. The worship followed the Book of Common Prayer, decorous and formal, and the whole arrangement bound church and social order tightly together: the same gentlemen who controlled the land, the county courts, and the militia also governed the parish, so that religion in Virginia was thoroughly interwoven with the hierarchy of a landed society. This was a Christianity of a very different temper from the searching, experiential piety of New England — more formal, more restrained, more a matter of decent observance and social cohesion than of the anxious examination of the soul's estate. It was the religion of a gentry class, ordered and dignified, and it served, among other things, to sanctify the social order over which that class presided.

Yet the southern establishment, imposing as it looked on paper, was in practice surprisingly weak, and its weakness would prove consequential. The parishes were often enormous, sprawling across vast distances in a thinly settled land, and there were never enough clergymen to serve them; ministers were frequently in short supply, sometimes of indifferent quality, and stretched impossibly thin. Church attendance and religious discipline were correspondingly lax in many places, the establishment more a legal framework than a living spiritual reality. And this weakness created openings. Into the religiously underserved spaces of the South, and especially into the growing backcountry where the establishment's grip was weakest, poured dissenting movements — Presbyterians among the Scots-Irish, and, with explosive effect, Baptists — offering a warmer, more demanding, more egalitarian faith than the formal Anglicanism of the tidewater gentry.

## The Dissenters and the Cracks in the Order

The growth of dissent in the South set up a collision that would have large consequences for the American story. The Baptists in particular, with their fervent preaching, their insistence on adult conversion, and their disregard for the social niceties of gentry religion, spread rapidly through the Virginia backcountry in the middle decades of the eighteenth century, drawing in the common folk and unsettling the established order. The gentry and the authorities responded, in places, with persecution: Baptist preachers were harassed, disrupted, and jailed for the crime of preaching without the license the establishment required. This persecution, and the resentment it bred, planted seeds that would flower dramatically in the revolutionary era, when the dissenters' demand for religious liberty would find powerful allies. It was no accident that Virginia, the colony with the most entrenched establishment and the sharpest conflict over it, would become the birthplace of the young nation's most influential arguments for religious freedom — that it was here that James Madison and Thomas Jefferson would take up the dissenters' cause and press, successfully, for the disestablishment of the Anglican church and the guarantee of liberty of conscience. The persecution of Baptists in the Virginia backcountry was, in a sense, the seedbed of the First Amendment.

Georgia, the last of the thirteen colonies to be founded, added its own distinctive note to the southern picture. It was established in the 1730s not as a commercial venture or a religious refuge but as a philanthropic experiment, the project of James Oglethorpe and his associates, who envisioned it as a place where the "worthy poor" of England, including those imprisoned for debt, might make a fresh start, and as a buffer protecting the older colonies from Spanish Florida. In its idealistic early years the colony banned both slavery and hard liquor, hoping to build a society of sturdy small farmers — restrictions that were later abandoned under economic pressure, with slavery in particular taking hold and transforming Georgia into another plantation society. Georgia also drew, in its early days, notable religious figures: the young John Wesley, later the founder of Methodism, served briefly and unhappily as a minister there, and the great revivalist George Whitefield, whom a later lesson will meet, made it the site of an orphanage and a base for his American labors.

## Three Models, One Tension

With the South, then, we complete the picture of colonial religious life, and we can see the whole. Three models of Christian society had taken root in British America: the covenanted commonwealth of Puritan New England, seeking a purified uniformity; the tolerant pluralism of the middle colonies, accommodating many faiths out of principle and necessity; and the established hierarchy of the Anglican South, transplanting the English order of church and gentry. These were not merely three regional variations but three genuinely different answers to the question of how Christianity and society should be joined — and the young nation, when it came, would have to reckon with all three, and would fashion from them something none of them had quite been.

Within the southern model in particular lay a tension that would grow into the central agony of the American story. For the established, hierarchical Christianity of the South was bound up, from an early date, with a social order resting increasingly upon the enslavement of human beings — a Christianity that blessed a hierarchy in which some men owned others. The decorous Anglicanism of the tidewater gentry sanctified a plantation world built on slavery, even as the egalitarian dissent stirring in the backcountry carried implications that would prove, in the long run, corrosive of that very order. The collision between a hierarchical Christianity that accommodated slavery and an egalitarian Christianity that could not finally abide it was being set up here, in the colonial South, and it would take a civil war to resolve. But before we can trace that collision, we must confront directly the institution at its heart — the taking-root of slavery in the colonies, which is the subject we can no longer defer.
