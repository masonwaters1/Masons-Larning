The parent lesson watched slavery take root in the English colonies. This cluster of four sub-lessons pulls the camera all the way back, because the American story of slavery cannot be truthfully told — in either direction — without the world story around it. Told without the world story, American slavery appears as a uniquely American invention, a singular national crime against a backdrop of general innocence; told only as world story, it dissolves into an everybody-did-it shrug. Both distortions are common, both serve present-day agendas, and the course's charter — providential and imperfect, refusing triumphalism and grievance alike — requires the actual picture, which is more interesting than either. The actual picture is this: slavery was among the most universal institutions in human history, practiced on every inhabited continent by virtually every civilization that left records; the New World's version added features that made it distinctive and, in some respects, worse; and the genuinely exceptional thing — the thing that had never happened anywhere, ever — was not that the Anglo-American world practiced slavery but that it turned against it: that from inside this civilization, and substantially from inside its churches, came the first movement in human history to declare slavery itself an abomination and destroy it, at staggering cost. This first sub-lesson establishes the universality; the second examines the African side of the Atlantic trade; the third and fourth follow the long Christian bell and the crusade that answered it.

## The Institution of the Ancient World

Begin where Western civilization begins, because slavery is there in the foundations.[^1] Athens at its democratic height was a slave society through and through — plausible estimates put the enslaved at a third or more of the population — and its greatest minds did not merely tolerate the fact but theorized it: Aristotle's Politics defends the doctrine of the natural slave, persons slavish by nature whom it benefits to be ruled. Rome industrialized the institution: the conquests poured millions of captives into Italy, the latifundia estates and the mines consumed them, the household of a great Roman might number hundreds, and the slave revolts — Spartacus's most famously — were crushed with mass crucifixions along the roads as advertisement. The Bible's world is saturated with the institution: Israel's law regulated slavery (while, remarkably for the age, commanding death for man-stealing and protection for the escaped slave — texts the abolitionists would one day wield); the New Testament's letters address slaves and masters as fixtures of every congregation. Northern and eastern Europe fed the Mediterranean's demand for a millennium — the very word slave descends from Slav, so many Slavic captives passed through the markets — and Viking wealth was substantially slave wealth, Dublin one of its great marts. Medieval Christendom gradually transmuted slavery into serfdom at home while the institution roared on around it; and the Islamic world ran, from the seventh century to the twentieth, one of history's largest and longest slave systems — the trans-Saharan, Red Sea, and Indian Ocean trades drawing, by the standard scholarly estimates, on the order of ten to twelve million Africans across twelve centuries, alongside the vast traffic in Slavs, Circassians, and others; the Ottoman devshirme levied Christian boys; and the Barbary corsairs of North Africa enslaved, by the most cited estimate, something like a million Europeans between the sixteenth and eighteenth centuries — coastal Italy and Spain depopulated in stretches, Icelandic villages raided, American sailors ransomed by the new republic. Add the Aztec and other indigenous American systems, the Korean nobi, the Russian serf-slaves, and the picture completes itself: slavery was not a Western aberration or a white invention. It was the human default — the near-universal fate of the war captive, the debtor, and the powerless in nearly every society that could afford to keep them.

## What the New World Changed

Universality established, honesty now requires the other half, because "everyone did it" is where the analysis begins, not where it ends, and the New World system had features that must be named. Scale and industry: the Atlantic plantation complex — sugar above all, then rice, tobacco, and finally cotton — consumed human beings as industrial fuel at a rate and with a commercial rationalization that few older systems matched; the sugar islands were death camps with balance sheets, where owners calculated that working slaves to death and buying replacements could be cheaper than sustaining families. Heredity and permanence: many old-world systems offered roads out — manumission was common in Rome and encouraged in Islamic law, the freedman a normal social type; the mature American system moved the other way, toward slavery as a permanent, heritable caste from which exit became progressively harder. And race: the New World fused slavery to blackness — constructing, over the colonial period the parent lesson described, a system in which African descent itself marked a person as enslaveable, and generating, to justify it, a racial ideology whose poison outlived the institution by centuries. These are real distinctives, and the course will not blur them: a Roman slave might be Greek, Gaul, or Syrian, and his grandson a citizen; the American system built a wall of color around bondage and made it eternal by birth. Whoever says only "slavery was universal" and stops has not yet described what was built in the Americas.

## The Real Exception

But now complete the frame, because the deepest truth in this subject cuts across both distortions. For all of recorded history, the moral debate about slavery — where one existed at all — was about treatment, never about the institution: be kind to your slaves, free the deserving, do not enslave the wrongly taken. That slavery as such might be an abomination to be abolished everywhere was a thought almost no civilization thought; the institution was as unquestioned as war or weather. The exception — the thing actually unprecedented — arose in one place: the Christian West, and overwhelmingly its Anglo-Protestant wing, where between roughly 1688 and 1833 a minority conviction became a mass movement that condemned slavery in principle, abolished the trade, emancipated an empire's slaves at enormous public cost, and sent a navy to hunt the traffic of other nations. The next two sub-lessons tell that story and give the bell-ringers their names. And America's place in it is exactly double, which is the whole point of this cluster. America inherited the universal institution and built one of its harshest versions — and America is also the only society in human history that fought a full-scale civil war substantially over slavery: six hundred thousand and more of its own sons dead in a conflict that its own president interpreted, in the greatest of American speeches, as the price of the bondsman's two hundred and fifty years of unrequited toil. Other nations abolished by statute, by decree, by compensation; the enslaved of Haiti freed themselves by revolution; only America paid for abolition in a war among its own people. That fact carries two readings, and the honest account holds both: it is a measure of how deeply slavery had entwined itself in American life that nothing less could remove it — the indictment; and it is a measure of the moral seriousness loose in American civilization that removal was pursued to that price rather than abandoned — the credit. The unique American thing, in short, was never the sin, which was mankind's; it was the reckoning. How the sin was supplied — the African side of the trade, its ports and its princes — is the next lesson; the reckoning occupies the two after that, and then this whole course through Gettysburg and beyond.
