The image most Americans carry of the slave trade's African end is of European raiders with nets and chains, hunting villagers in the interior. The image is almost entirely false, and correcting it is not a matter of shifting blame but of seeing the machine whole — because the Atlantic trade was a partnership, and it could not have operated for a single season without the African half of the partnership. Europeans on the West African coast were traders at the edge of powerful states that dwarfed their little forts; they rarely ventured inland, where disease killed them in months; and the roughly twelve and a half million people embarked into the Atlantic system were, overwhelmingly, captured, condemned, marched, and sold by other Africans — by raiding states, merchant networks, and coastal brokers, some of whom became as rich as any merchant in Liverpool. This lesson walks the African side honestly: the pre-existing world of African slavery into which the Atlantic demand plugged, the mechanics of capture and sale, the great ports — Anomabu, Ouidah, Bonny, Old Calabar, Luanda — and the men who ran them; and then, with equal honesty, the two calibrations the evidence requires: what this does and does not do to European and American culpability, and what the trade did to Africa itself.

## The World the Ships Found

When Portuguese caravels first felt their way down the West African coast in the mid-1400s, they did not introduce slavery to Africa; they connected to it.[^1] Slavery and the traffic in persons were ancient and widespread across the continent, as across every continent: war captives were the standard booty of African warfare; criminals and debtors were condemned to servitude by African courts; domestic slavery structured many societies, where — land being generally held in common — wealth and power were measured less in acres than in people, and dependents, clients, and slaves were the very form of riches. And Africa was already an exporter: the trans-Saharan caravans had been carrying enslaved Africans north to the Islamic Mediterranean for seven centuries before any Portuguese ship arrived, and the east-coast trade to Arabia and the Indian Ocean was older still. The first European purchases simply entered existing markets — indeed, early Portuguese traders sometimes bought slaves at one African port and sold them at another, participating in an intra-African commerce that long predated them. This is the foundation of the historians' consensus that John Thornton has stated most bluntly: European traders did not, and could not, seize their cargoes; they bought them, from African sellers, in transactions the African side controlled — setting prices, taxing the trade, dictating terms, and expelling or destroying Europeans who broke the rules. Whether the Atlantic demand then merely tapped this world or monstrously transformed it is the genuine scholarly debate — Thornton stressing African agency and the trade's continuity with existing institutions, Paul Lovejoy and others documenting a "transformation": wars increasingly fought for captives, slavery within Africa itself expanding and hardening as the export economy grew. The honest summary holds both: the supply system was African, ancient, and self-governing; and two centuries of insatiable Atlantic demand bent it toward something bigger and crueler than it had been.

## The Ports and the Princes

Now the machine at working scale, and the places the user of this course asked after by name. The captives came to the coast from as far as a thousand miles inland, taken principally in war — the great predator states of the era, Asante on the Gold Coast, Dahomey and Oyo in the Bight of Benin, made war a harvest, and the "gun-slave cycle" turned it self-feeding: guns were bought with slaves, and more guns took more slaves — supplemented by organized raiding, judicial condemnation (courts multiplying capital-and-sale offenses as prices rose), kidnapping, and famine sale. They were marched to the coast in coffles by African merchant networks and sold at entrepôts whose names deserve to be as familiar as Liverpool's. Anomabu, on the Gold Coast of modern Ghana — the port you asked about — was the busiest slave port on that coast in the eighteenth century, funneling hundreds of thousands into the ships; and Anomabu was a Fante town, run by Fante brokers who played European powers against each other for decades. Its great caboceer John Currantee (Eno Baisie Kurentsi) negotiated with Britain and France as a sovereign operator, sending one son to be educated in Paris and another, William Ansah Sessarakoo, to London — where, in the era's perfect irony, the young man was himself treacherously sold into Barbados slavery en route, redeemed by the Royal African Company for diplomacy's sake, and feted in London society as an African prince while his father's port shipped thousands. At Ouidah, the kingdom of Dahomey ran the trade as a royal monopoly; the king's annual customs revenue from it was estimated by contemporaries at a quarter of a million pounds in the mid-1700s — an income exceeding most English aristocrats. In the Bight of Biafra, the Efik merchant lords of Old Calabar — men who took English names like Duke Ephraim and kept trading diaries in English — grew so rich that "Great Duke Ephraim" in the early nineteenth century commanded wealth and canoe-fleets that awed European captains; Bonny's kings made their river the single greatest funnel of the trade's final decades. And south of the equator, Luanda in Portuguese Angola out-shipped them all — the largest single embarkation point of the entire Atlantic trade — through Afro-Portuguese merchant dynasties. The wealth was real, African, and immense: the trade's African beneficiaries were not dupes at the water's edge but kings, states, and merchant princes who grew mighty on it, resisted its abolition — Dahomey and others lobbied and fought to keep it going when Britain turned — and whose descendants' public reckonings with that history (Ghanaian and Beninese leaders have issued formal apologies in recent decades) are among the more remarkable moral events of our own time.

## The Two Calibrations

Now the discipline, because this material is weaponized daily in opposite directions, and the course exists to state what the evidence bears. First calibration: African supply does not dilute European and American guilt; it multiplies the guilty. The demand was European and American — no sugar islands, no cotton kingdom, no twelve million shipped; the ships, the capital, the insurance, the plantations, and the consuming markets were Western; the Middle Passage's horrors and the chattel system the survivors entered were Western constructions; and "Africans sold Africans" quietly imports a solidarity that did not exist — the Asante warrior selling a northern captive no more betrayed "his people" than an English privateer selling a Frenchman betrayed "Europeans"; the very category African is largely a product of the diaspora the trade created. A partnership in atrocity acquits no partner. Second calibration, cutting the other way: the African half is not a footnote to be mumbled, and the common picture that omits it — white raiders, passive victims, a continent without agency — is itself a distortion, and a condescending one, that erases African states, African commerce, African power, and African moral responsibility alike; the same scholarship that convicts Liverpool convicts Ouidah. And the ledger for Africa itself was catastrophe compounding: the trade drained the continent's people (with the demographic historians debating how deeply), militarized its politics, rewarded its predators, corrupted its justice, and — Walter Rodney's and later economists' argument — helped lock in the instability and low trust that would leave it prey to the colonizers of the next century. The honest sentence is long but it is one sentence: Africans, Europeans, and Americans built the Atlantic slave system together, each supplying what the others could not, and its profits enriched elites on three continents while its costs fell on the millions in the coffles and the holds — which is why the movement that finally attacked the system, the subject of the next two lessons, had to attack it whole: the ships at sea, the markets in the West, and the supply at its African source. The bell that began ringing against all of it had been ringing, faintly, for a very long time.
