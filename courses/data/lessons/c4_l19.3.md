Before there was a movement, there was a bell — solitary voices, centuries apart, declaring against the moral consensus of all mankind that the owning of human beings was an offense against God. This lesson collects those voices, because they are the buried genealogy of everything the next lesson narrates: abolitionism did not spring from nowhere in the 1780s; it was the sudden victory of an argument that Christians had been making, in isolated pulses, for fourteen hundred years. And the lesson must be built with the double honesty the subject demands. The bell-ringers were Christians, arguing from Scripture and the image of God — that is simply what the record shows, and the record deserves to be far better known than it is. And the church they rang the bell inside was, for most of those centuries, an accommodating church — baptizing the institution, holding slaves, supplying its theology on demand — which is precisely what makes the ringers remarkable: they were not the voice of Christendom; they were the conscience of Christendom arguing with its practice, until at last, in one corner of the Protestant world, the conscience won. Here are the names.

## The Ancient and Medieval Peals

The first known human being to attack slavery as an institution — not its abuses, the institution — was a Christian bishop.[^1] Gregory of Nyssa, preaching on Ecclesiastes in Cappadocia around 379 AD, turned on the slaveholding of his own congregation with an argument of astonishing finality: "I got me slaves and slave-girls," says the rich fool of the text — and Gregory answers: at what price? "How much does wisdom cost? What did you pay for the image of God?... God said, let us make man in our own image... tell me, who buys him? Who sells him?" Man is made free and sovereign by his Creator; to claim ownership of God's image is to "condemn to slavery one whose nature is free," a sentence against God's own decree. Nothing like it survives from any pagan philosopher; Aristotle had defended the natural slave, the Stoics had counseled kind mastery, and Gregory alone said the thing itself was impossible before God. The peal did not become a chorus — the church around him accommodated, as it would for centuries — but it was struck, and from the same theological metal every later ringer would use: the imago Dei. The medieval church slowly squeezed slavery toward serfdom within Christendom (forbidding, at least, the enslavement of fellow Christians) and occasionally produced a Gregory-like figure: Saint Wulfstan, bishop of Worcester, waged a preaching campaign in the 1080s and 1090s against the slave trade of Bristol — English dealers shipping English captives to Ireland — going down among the traders until, the chronicler says, they abandoned the traffic; the Council of London in 1102 decreed against "that nefarious business by which men were sold like brute animals." Bristol's trade died — and the reader should hold the irony that the same port would rise again, six centuries later, on the African trade, until another generation of Christians shut it a second time. When the Atlantic system opened, the bell rang against it almost at once: Bartolomé de las Casas spent fifty years assaulting the enslavement of the Indians (and repented publicly of his early, catastrophic suggestion to substitute Africans — becoming, by the end, an opponent of that too); a line of popes issued condemnations of Indian and unjust enslavement (Sublimis Deus, 1537, declaring the Indians true men not to be deprived of liberty) that colonists ignored and other papal acts undercut; and Spanish theologians of the School of Salamanca dissected the trade's legality while the ships sailed on. The pattern of the whole era was set: the arguments existed; the interests were stronger.

## Baxter and the Puritan Indictment

Now the figure this course's reader asked for by name, and he earns the place. Richard Baxter — the great Puritan pastor of Kidderminster, author of The Reformed Pastor and The Saints' Everlasting Rest, perhaps the most respected Protestant divine of his century — published in 1673, inside his enormous Christian Directory, a treatment of slaveholding that reads like a war-crimes indictment written a century early. Of the slave traders: "To go as pirates and catch up poor negroes... and to make them slaves and sell them, is one of the worst kinds of thievery in the world" — and those who buy them "are fitter to be called incarnate devils than Christians." He grounded the charge exactly where the Bible's own law put it: man-stealing, the crime the law of Moses punished with death and Paul listed among the works of the lawless; and he pressed the master's duty in terms that dissolved the master's claim — the slave is "as good as you... God is his absolute Lord as well as yours," and to use men as beasts for mere commodity is "a sin exceeding the robbing of them of their purses." Baxter still imagined a regulated, temporary, evangelizing servitude — the full abolitionist conclusion was a step he did not take — but the indictment of the trade was total, it stood in the era's most widely used manual of Protestant ethics, and the next century's abolitionists quoted it as ancestral authority. Nor was he alone in his generation: in Boston in 1700, the Puritan judge Samuel Sewall — the same Sewall who had publicly repented his role in the Salem trials — published The Selling of Joseph, the first antislavery tract printed in America, arguing from Genesis that "all men, as they are the sons of Adam... have equal right unto liberty," demolishing the curse-of-Ham excuse, and declaring "man-stealing" an atrocity "ranked among the most atrocious of capital crimes." One judge against a colony's practice; the tract sold poorly and drew a rebuttal; the bell rang anyway.

## The Quakers Turn the Bell Into an Alarm

The transformation from voices to a people began with the sect everyone else despised. In 1688, four Quakers of Germantown, Pennsylvania — Dutch- and German-speaking immigrants, led by Francis Daniel Pastorius — sent up to their monthly meeting a two-page protest that is the first corporate antislavery document in American history, and its argument is devastating in its plainness: we would not wish to be "sold or made a slave for all the time of our life"; therefore, by the rule of doing to others what we would have done to us, "here is liberty of conscience, which is right and reasonable; here ought to be likewise liberty of the body." The meeting tabled it — too weighty to decide — and the paper vanished into the files for a century and a half. But the leaven worked. Through the early 1700s Quaker radicals (some of them wild figures — Benjamin Lay, the four-foot hunchbacked prophet, once splattered slaveholding Friends with fake blood from a pierced Bible) harried the Society; then came the gentle ones who finished it: John Woolman, the New Jersey tailor whose Journal is an American spiritual classic, spent thirty years traveling among Friends, refusing to write wills that bequeathed slaves, paying wages to the enslaved who served him, quietly persuading meeting after meeting that a people of the Light could not hold people in the dark; and Anthony Benezet, the Huguenot-Quaker schoolmaster of Philadelphia, opened a school for Black children (and reported, against the age's entire assumption, that he found their capacities equal to whites'), and wrote the pamphlets — Some Historical Account of Guinea above all — that became the movement's ammunition. By 1776 the Philadelphia Yearly Meeting had done what no religious body in recorded history had done: made slaveholding a disowning offense, purging the institution from an entire communion. And Benezet's pamphlets crossed the sea and did their most consequential work in other men's hands: John Wesley's Thoughts Upon Slavery (1774) — which pronounced the trade "the execrable sum of all villanies" and American slavery "the vilest that ever saw the sun" — was drawn substantially from Benezet; and a Cambridge student named Thomas Clarkson, researching a Latin essay, found in Benezet the facts that broke his life open. The bell was now an alarm, and the alarm had reached England — where lived a small, sickly, silver-tongued member of Parliament whom the next lesson will meet, and whom Wesley, six days before his death, would charge in the last letter he ever wrote: unless God has raised you up for this very thing, you will be worn out by the opposition of men and devils — but "if God be for you, who can be against you?... Go on, in the name of God and in the power of His might."
