In the spring of 1787, under an oak at William Pitt's estate, the youngest and most charming member of Parliament decided to take up a cause his friends warned would ruin him. William Wilberforce was twenty-seven, rich, tiny — Boswell called him a shrimp who, speaking, "grew and grew until the shrimp became a whale" — recently and inconveniently converted to serious Christianity, and wondering whether a real Christian could remain in politics at all. John Newton, the slave-ship captain turned hymn-writing pastor, told him God had placed him in Parliament for a purpose; his diary that October recorded the conclusion: "God Almighty has set before me two great objects: the suppression of the Slave Trade and the reformation of manners." What followed — a twenty-year parliamentary siege that abolished the British slave trade in 1807, and a forty-six-year campaign that emancipated the empire's slaves as he lay dying in 1833 — was the first mass humanitarian movement in the history of the world: the first time any people organized itself to destroy, at its own expense, an institution as old as civilization, from which it profited enormously, on behalf of strangers it would never meet. This lesson tells that story, and then closes the whole four-lesson arc where the reader asked it to close: on what, exactly, was unique about America.

## The Evidence and the Machine

The crusade had two engines, and Wilberforce was only one of them.[^1] The other was Thomas Clarkson, the Cambridge prize-essayist whom Benezet's facts had broken open, who got down from his horse on the road at Wadesmill in 1785 with the settled conviction that "it was time some person should see these calamities to their end" — and became history's first professional human-rights investigator. Clarkson rode thirty-five thousand miles; interviewed twenty thousand sailors in the waterfront taverns of Bristol and Liverpool at real risk to his life (a Liverpool gang once tried to shove him off a pier); collected the instruments — shackles, thumbscrews, the speculum oris for force-feeding the hunger strikers — that he displayed from his traveling chest like relics of an anti-gospel; and procured the two most devastating documents of the age: the diagram of the slave ship Brookes, 482 human figures packed in their allotted inches, which the committee printed by the thousands and which still appears in every textbook on earth; and the muster rolls proving that the trade killed British sailors faster than the enslaved — demolishing the "nursery of seamen" defense. Around Clarkson's evidence and Wilberforce's parliamentary genius grew the machine: the London committee of 1787 (nine Quakers, three Anglicans — the bell-ringers' sects united); Josiah Wedgwood's cameo of the kneeling captive — "Am I Not a Man and a Brother?" — mass-produced into the first political logo, worn on bracelets and hairpins; petitions on a scale never seen (in 1792, five hundred and nineteen from England alone, signed by perhaps a fifth of the adult male population); a sugar boycott joined by some three hundred thousand families, in which women — barred from Parliament and largely from the petitions — made the kitchen a polling place; and the testimony of the formerly enslaved themselves, above all Olaudah Equiano, whose 1789 autobiography toured and sold like scripture. This was the invention, from nothing, of the entire modern repertoire of moral campaigning — investigation, imagery, boycott, petition, celebrity testimony — created by evangelical and Quaker Christians for the purpose, and every movement since, from every point of the political compass, has campaigned with Clarkson's tools.

## The Twenty Years and the Two Acts

The siege itself was long because the enemy was the economy. Wilberforce brought his first abolition motion in 1789, in a three-and-a-half-hour speech even his adversaries called sublime — "I mean not to accuse anyone, but to take the shame upon myself, in common indeed with the whole Parliament... we are all guilty" — and was crushed; and then crushed again, year upon year, through the 1790s, as the West India interest mobilized, the French Revolution made all reform smell of Jacobinism, and war shelved conscience. His allies in the fight were the remarkable colony of evangelicals at Clapham — Henry Thornton the banker whose home was headquarters, Granville Sharp the ancient agitator of the Somerset case, Hannah More writing the movement's popular literature, Zachary Macaulay the walking database whom the others called on for any fact — a fellowship nicknamed "the Saints," mocked and unbudging. Victory came in 1807, twenty years after the oak tree: the Abolition of the Slave Trade Act passed the Commons 283 to 16, the House rising to cheer Wilberforce as he sat weeping; and Britain then did the thing that still astonishes the economists: it enforced the ban against the world, stationing the Royal Navy's West Africa Squadron off the coast for sixty years — capturing on the order of sixteen hundred slavers, freeing about a hundred and fifty thousand Africans, losing thousands of its own sailors to fever and action — and paying, by the standard scholarly reckoning, costs amounting to a measurable share of national income for decades: the most expensive sustained international moral action in recorded history, undertaken after the profitable sin had been renounced. Emancipation itself took one siege more: the aged Wilberforce handed the cause to Thomas Fowell Buxton; the enslaved of the Caribbean pressed from their side (the Jamaican rebellion of 1831–32, led by the Baptist deacon Samuel Sharpe — "I would rather die upon yonder gallows than live in slavery" — and the planters' savage reprisals against Black Christians and missionary chapels swung British opinion decisively); and in July 1833 the Emancipation Act was assured of passage. The news was carried to Wilberforce's deathbed — "Thank God that I should have lived to witness a day in which England is willing to give twenty millions sterling for the abolition of slavery" — and he died three days later, buried in Westminster Abbey beside Pitt. The twenty millions was the compensation paid to the slaveholders — forty percent of the state's annual budget, a moral scandal to modern eyes and to many then, and also the price that bought eight hundred thousand people's freedom without a shot; both things are true, and the tension between them is the bridge to this lesson's end.

## The American Difference

For now set the two national stories side by side, and the arc of these four lessons closes on the point it opened with. Britain ended slavery by purchase and Parliament: petitions, acts, compensation, a navy — bitter, long, but bloodless at home. And it could, in part, because British slavery lived offshore: no MP's constituency was a sugar island; the institution could be amputated without cutting the body politic. America's slavery was in the body — a domestic institution, woven into the households, economies, politics, and self-understanding of half the states, constitutionally entrenched, and expanding; and so America's reckoning, when it could no longer be deferred, took the form nothing else in the history of slavery ever took: total civil war. Every other abolition in the world came by statute, decree, or — in Haiti — the revolution of the enslaved themselves; only in America did a slaveholding nation tear itself in two over the institution, and only there did the free half conquer the slave half at the cost of six hundred thousand and more of its own sons. Hold the double reading steady, as the first lesson of this cluster framed it, because each half corrects the other. The war is the great indictment: it measures how deep the sin had gone, that the world's leading republic of liberty could be brought to Gettysburg by it, three decades after Britain had simply written a check. And the war is the great, terrible credit: it is the one case in human history of a people paying blood on that scale substantially to destroy its own peculiar institution — a price its own president read, in the Second Inaugural this course will reach in Unit 5, as divine justice: every drop of blood drawn with the lash paid by another drawn with the sword. The unique American thing was never the owning of slaves, which was mankind's common crime; nor even the awakening against it, which Britain led. It was the price finally paid. And the men and women who forced the paying — the American heirs of Baxter and Benezet and the Clapham Saints, the evangelists and free Black churches and radical Christians whose agitation made compromise impossible — enter this story in Unit 4, where the American abolitionist movement rises.
