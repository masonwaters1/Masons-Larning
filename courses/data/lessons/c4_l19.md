We arrive at the wound. This course is subtitled a history of a providential and imperfect nation, and if the earlier lessons have dwelt on the providential — the covenants, the liberties, the experiments in conscience — this lesson confronts the imperfection at its deepest and most terrible. For woven into the fabric of colonial America from an early date, and hardening over the seventeenth century into one of the most brutal systems of human bondage the world has known, was the enslavement of Africans. No honest telling of the American story can pass over this, minimize it, or hurry past it toward more comfortable themes. The same colonies that produced the Mayflower Compact and the holy experiment, that founded schools to teach the reading of Scripture and colonies to shelter the persecuted conscience, also built and sustained a system that treated human beings as property. This is the contradiction at the heart of the American story, and we must look at it squarely.

## The Terrible Beginning

The beginning was, at first, ambiguous, and the ambiguity is part of the tragedy.[^1] In 1619, a ship brought some twenty Africans to the Virginia colony at Point Comfort, where they were sold into the labor-hungry tobacco economy. But the status of these first Africans, and of those who followed in the next few decades, was not yet the fixed, hereditary, race-based chattel slavery that would later develop. In the early years, the line between indentured servitude and slavery was blurry; some Africans served terms and were freed, acquired land, and even, in a few remarkable cases, themselves became property-owners and masters — the free black Virginian Anthony Johnson being the most famous example. For a brief window, it was not yet inevitable that African ancestry would mean perpetual bondage.

That window closed, and its closing was a series of deliberate human choices, written into law. Over the second half of the seventeenth century and into the early eighteenth, the colonial legislatures, especially Virginia's, constructed piece by piece the legal architecture of racial slavery. They decreed that slavery would follow the condition of the mother, so that the child of an enslaved woman was born a slave, making bondage hereditary and perpetual. They decreed, in a fateful and revealing ruling, that baptism into the Christian faith would not free a slave — deliberately severing the ancient link between Christian brotherhood and freedom, so that a master might Christianize his slaves without losing them. They drew ever sharper lines of race, until African ancestry itself became the marker of a status from which there was no escape. By the time the Virginia slave code was consolidated in 1705, hereditary, race-based, lifelong chattel slavery was fully established in law — human beings defined as property, to be bought, sold, worked, and inherited, their bondage passed to their children forever. What had begun in ambiguity had been hardened, by deliberate legislation, into one of history's most complete systems of dehumanization, driven by the relentless economic engine of the plantation — tobacco first, then rice, and in time the cotton that would bind the whole system tighter still.

## Christianity on Both Sides

Here the honest historian must say something uncomfortable and something hopeful in the same breath, for Christianity stood on both sides of slavery from the very beginning, and to tell only one side is to falsify the record. On the one side, Christianity was pressed into the service of slavery, made to justify and sanctify it. Slaveholders and their apologists reached for the curse of Ham, the passages in which the apostles instructed slaves to obey their masters, and the argument that enslavement was a providential means of bringing heathen Africans to the light of the Gospel — a "civilizing" and "Christianizing" rationale that cloaked brutal exploitation in the language of benevolence. The established churches of the plantation South, as the previous lesson noted, largely accommodated themselves to the slave order and blessed the society that rested upon it. It cannot be denied that Christianity, as actually practiced by many, was complicit in slavery, and worse than complicit — actively enlisted to defend it.

But that is not the whole of the record, and the other side must be told with equal clarity. From the very beginning, Christian voices rose against slavery, and they were among the earliest antislavery voices anywhere. In 1688, a group of Quakers and Mennonites in Germantown, Pennsylvania, drafted a protest against slavery — the first formal antislavery document in the colonies — arguing, with devastating simplicity, that the Golden Rule forbade it: that those who would not themselves be made slaves had no right to enslave others, whatever the color of their skin. In 1700, the Puritan judge Samuel Sewall of Boston — the same Sewall who had sat on the witchcraft court and come to repent of it — published The Selling of Joseph, the first antislavery tract in New England, arguing from Scripture that all men are the sons of Adam, that liberty is a fundamental right, and that the enslavement of Africans was a sin. These were, at first, lonely voices, easily ignored, and slavery only grew stronger in the decades that followed. But they were the first stirrings of what would become, in time, the greatest moral movement in American history, and they were, from the outset, overwhelmingly and explicitly Christian, grounded in the conviction that every human being bears the image of God and that to enslave a person is to sin against that image.

## The Contradiction We Inherit

How are we to hold these things together — a Christian people building a system of racial bondage, and a Christian conscience condemning it from the start? The temptation is to seize one half and discard the other: to indict Christianity as merely the ideology of the slaveholder, or to exonerate it by pointing only to the abolitionists. Both are evasions. The truth is that slavery in America was a human institution, built by human choices, and Christianity was the moral universe within which those choices were both made and contested. The slaveholder and the Germantown protester were reading the same Bible and arriving at opposite conclusions, and the long struggle over slavery would be, in large part, a struggle within Christianity over the meaning of Christianity — a struggle that would not be resolved for nearly two centuries, and only through a war that consumed hundreds of thousands of lives.

Two further truths must be held alongside this, in the interest of an honest reckoning that is neither triumphalist nor despairing. The first is that slavery was not an American invention or an American peculiarity but a near-universal institution of human history; virtually every civilization the world had ever known — ancient and modern, European, African, Asian, and American — had practiced some form of it, and the Atlantic slave trade itself depended upon African as well as European participation. America inherited an institution as old as civilization, and the horror of American slavery lies not in its novelty but in its brutality, its racial character, and its coexistence with a professed creed of liberty and equality. The second truth is the one that gives the story its arc and its hope: that America also generated, from within its own Christian and revolutionary traditions, the moral arguments that would eventually destroy slavery, and that the institution which existed nearly everywhere was abolished, in the end, by people quoting Scripture. Both the sin and the seeds of its undoing were present in the colonial soil from the beginning. The sin would grow monstrous before the seeds bore fruit, and the reckoning would be terrible. But the reckoning would come, and when it came, it would come in large part in the name of the same God whose word had been twisted to justify the sin. That is the deepest and most painful pattern in the American story, and we will return to it again and again. First, though, we turn to a brighter inheritance from the colonial world — the schools and colleges that a Scripture-reading people raised up, and the extraordinary mind that would emerge from them.
