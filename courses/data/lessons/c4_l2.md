We begin our walk upstream at the oldest of the four springs, and in some ways the most surprising. When the American founders reached for the deepest justification of their cause — for the claim that there are rights no king may violate, that rulers are accountable to a law above themselves, that the humble possess a dignity the mighty must respect — they were drawing, often without tracing it that far back, on ideas first articulated by a small, frequently conquered people on the eastern edge of the Mediterranean more than two and a half thousand years ago. The symbol of that inheritance is the city of Jerusalem, and the inheritance itself is contained in the Hebrew Scriptures. This lesson is about four revolutionary ideas that the ancient Israelites gave to the world — ideas so woven into our assumptions now that we can scarcely imagine a world without them, and which were, in their own time, genuinely without precedent.

To feel how radical they were, you have to first see the world they broke into.

## The World Before

The civilizations surrounding ancient Israel — Egypt, Mesopotamia, the empires of the Near East — shared a basic picture of reality, and it was not the one we inherited. The gods were many, and they were essentially personified forces of nature and of the state: the storm, the river, the sun, the fertility of the fields, the power of the throne. They were not especially moral; they were powerful, capricious, and to be appeased rather than obeyed in righteousness. And crucially, the political order was sacred and fixed. The king was often himself a god or the son of a god — Pharaoh was divine — and the social hierarchy, with the god-king at the top and the masses of laborers and slaves at the bottom, was simply how the cosmos was structured. To question it was to question reality itself. There was no vantage point from which to judge the king, because the king was the embodiment of the divine order. Power and the sacred were fused, and the fusion was unbreakable.

Against that backdrop, the ideas that emerged from Israel were not refinements. They were detonations.

## The First Idea: A God Above the World

The Hebrew revolution began with a new conception of God — and the novelty was not mainly in the number. Monotheism matters here less as arithmetic (one god instead of many) than as a moral and political transformation. The God of Israel was understood to be **transcendent**: not a force within nature but the maker of nature, standing outside and above the whole created order, including every kingdom and king within it. And this God was, above all, **righteous** — a God whose central demand was not sacrifice or flattery but justice and faithfulness, a God who cared how human beings treated one another.

The political consequence of this was seismic, and it is the hinge on which everything else turns. If God stands above the entire world, then no earthly power is ultimate. The king is not divine; he is a creature, under God, like everyone else. The state is not sacred; it can be judged. By lifting the highest authority clean out of the natural and political order, the Hebrew conception of God created, for the first time, a place to stand *outside* the system of earthly power from which that power could be measured and found wanting. Every later assertion that there is a higher law above human law, that conscience may be bound to obey God rather than men, that tyranny can be named as tyranny — all of it depends on this single move. A transcendent moral God relativizes every throne on earth.

## The Second Idea: Covenant

The second idea is the one that would echo most directly through American history, and it is the idea of **covenant**. The relationship between God and Israel was not conceived as the arbitrary dominion of a master over slaves, but as a *covenant* — a solemn, binding, mutual agreement, with obligations on both sides, entered into at Sinai. God would be their God and they would be his people; he bound himself to them and they bound themselves to him and, through him, to one another. A whole nation understood itself as constituted by a shared agreement and a shared law.

Sit with how strange this is against the Near Eastern background. A people defined not by mere blood or by submission to a god-king, but by common consent to a covenant and its law — this is the embryo of constitutional government. It contains the germ of ideas that would take millennia to mature: that legitimate authority rests on agreement, that a community can be founded by a binding compact, that a people can be a moral body bound to a common purpose and a common standard. It is no accident that the word *federal* descends from the Latin *foedus*, meaning covenant, or that the Puritans who settled New England were soaked in covenant theology and reproduced its logic in their founding compacts. The Mayflower Compact, in which a body of settlers covenant together to form a civil body and submit to laws of their own making, is covenant thinking transposed into politics. America was, in a real sense, founded by people who believed a society begins with a covenant.

## The Third Idea: The King Under the Law

From the transcendent God and the covenant flows the third revolutionary idea: that the ruler himself stands **under the law**, not above it. This is perhaps the single most important political inheritance from Jerusalem, and it could not be more opposed to the god-king model that surrounded Israel.

The Hebrew Scriptures contain something astonishing for the ancient world: a law for the king. When Israel is permitted a monarch, the king is explicitly placed under the same divine law as everyone else; he is required to keep a copy of the law for himself, to read it all his life, and to govern in submission to it rather than exalting himself above his brothers.[^1] The king is a subject of the law, not its source. Compare this to a Pharaoh who *was* the law, whose word was divine, and the contrast is total.

And this principle was not left as a paper ideal. The Hebrew narrative dramatizes it in a scene that has no parallel in the surrounding literature. King David, at the height of his power, commits adultery and arranges the death of a loyal man to cover it. In any normal ancient court, that would be the end of the story; the god-king does as he pleases. Instead, the prophet Nathan walks into the palace and confronts the most powerful man in the kingdom to his face, telling him a parable that traps David into condemning himself, and then declaring that David is the guilty man.[^2] And David — this is the staggering part — *submits*. He does not have the prophet killed; he confesses. The story enshrines a principle that would take the West thousands of years to institutionalize: that even the king is accountable, that there is a justice he cannot escape, that someone may speak truth to the throne and the throne must hear it. Magna Carta, centuries later, is this idea in legal form. So is the impeachment clause of the American Constitution.

## The Fourth Idea: The Prophetic Voice and the Cry for Justice

The figure of Nathan introduces the fourth great inheritance: the **prophet**, and the tradition of prophetic critique. The Hebrew prophets were not primarily fortune-tellers predicting the future. They were the conscience of the nation — men seized, as they understood it, by the word of God, who arose to denounce the powerful for their injustice and to call the whole people back to the demands of the covenant.

And the content of their denunciation is the thing to notice, because it set the moral agenda of the West. Over and over, the prophets thunder not against ritual failures but against *social injustice* — against the rich who trample the poor, the courts that can be bought, the powerful who seize the land of the weak. Their special concern, returned to relentlessly, is for the most vulnerable members of society, named again and again as a kind of refrain: the widow, the orphan, the poor, and the stranger — the foreigner with no clan to protect him. The prophet Amos demands that justice roll down like waters; the tradition summarizes the whole duty of man as doing justice, loving mercy, and walking humbly.[^3] This is a moral revolution in itself: the claim that true religion is inseparable from how you treat the powerless, that God himself takes the side of the oppressed against the oppressor, and that no amount of piety or prosperity can substitute for justice.

This prophetic voice never left the West, and it is loudest in American history at exactly the moments the nation confronts its own injustices. The abolitionists who denounced slavery, the reformers who fought for the poor, and above all the preachers of the civil rights movement spoke in unmistakably prophetic cadences, indicting the powerful in the name of a higher law and calling the nation back to its covenant. When Martin Luther King Jr. quoted Amos on justice rolling down like waters, he was not decorating his speech; he was standing consciously in a tradition three thousand years deep.

## The Dignity of the Person, and the Shape of Time

Beneath all four ideas lies a foundational conviction about the human being. In the Hebrew account of creation, the human person — and not the king alone, but every human person — is made in the image of God.[^4] This is the deep root of the idea of universal human dignity. If every person bears the divine image, then every person possesses a worth that does not depend on strength, wealth, status, or usefulness, and that no other human may rightfully violate. The slave bears it; the foreigner bears it; the poor man bears it. Here is the seed of the proposition that all are created equal — a proposition that would have been unintelligible to Aristotle, who held that some are slaves by nature, and that flows instead from Jerusalem.

The Hebrew Scriptures gave the West one more gift worth naming: a new shape for time. The surrounding cultures largely understood time as cyclical — the endless wheel of seasons and ages, going nowhere. Israel understood time as **linear and purposeful**: history as a story moving from a beginning toward a goal, under the guidance of a God working out his purposes within it. This is the hidden source of the Western faith in progress, in hope, in the conviction that history is going somewhere and can be made better — a faith that survives, transposed, even in thoroughly secular form. The idea that the arc of history bends toward justice is Jerusalem's idea, whether or not those who voice it know its address.

## How Jerusalem Reached Philadelphia

It is worth pausing to see, concretely, how these ancient ideas actually traveled the long road into the American founding, because the connection is far more direct than most people realize. The colonists who settled New England did not regard the Hebrew Scriptures as distant history; they read them as their own story, and they understood themselves as a new Israel — a covenanted people who had fled bondage in the old world, crossed a wilderness of water, and entered a promised land to build a society under God's law. The Puritan settlers reproduced the logic of Sinai in their founding documents, binding themselves together by compact into self-governing communities. This covenantal habit of mind, repeated in town after town, accustomed Americans to the idea that legitimate government is something a people *constitute* together by agreement — the very idea that would later be written large in a written Constitution beginning with the words "We the People."

The biblical saturation of the founding era is easy to underestimate from a more secular age. The Bible was the one book in nearly every colonial home, and its cadences, stories, and moral assumptions shaped the common imagination as nothing else did. Hebrew was studied at Harvard and Yale; election sermons drew political lessons straight from the Hebrew prophets and the law of the king; and when the founders wanted an image for liberty, they reached for one inscribed on the Liberty Bell itself — the ancient command to proclaim liberty throughout the land. Their suspicion of concentrated power drew not only on classical history but on the Hebrew conviction that no ruler stands above the law and that even kings must answer to a higher justice. When Americans later confronted the deepest contradiction of their founding — human slavery in a land proclaiming liberty — the decisive moral language came overwhelmingly from this same source: the prophetic demand for justice, the insistence that the oppressed bear God's image, the memory of a God who hears the cry of the enslaved and leads them out. Jerusalem did not merely influence Philadelphia from a distance; it lived in the minds of the people who built the American order and in the conscience that would later judge it.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Intellectual honesty, which this course owes you in every unit, requires facing squarely what these same Scriptures also contain — and it is not all liberating. The Hebrew Bible records commands of conquest and the destruction of peoples; it regulates slavery rather than abolishing it; its social order is patriarchal; and the dignity it proclaims was, in its original setting, applied first and most fully within Israel itself rather than instantly to all humanity. The universalization of these ideas — the extension of covenant, dignity, and justice to *every* person without exception — was a long and incomplete process, advanced by the prophets, radicalized by Christianity, and still being worked out, often against fierce resistance, in the centuries that followed. The same texts that armed the abolitionist were quoted by the slaveholder.

So we should be precise about the claim. Jerusalem did not hand the world a finished doctrine of universal human rights. It planted *seeds* — a transcendent moral God who relativizes all earthly power, a covenant that grounds authority in agreement and law, a king bound under that law, a prophetic demand for justice toward the weak, and a human person stamped with the divine image. These seeds were radical beyond anything in the surrounding world, and they carried within them a logic that would, over millennia, push toward conclusions their first bearers did not fully draw. To trace that growth honestly means neither pretending the seeds were the full flower nor pretending the seeds were not really there. They were there, and they were revolutionary, and they were also embedded in a text and a world that fell far short of them. Both things are true.

That tension — between the radical principle and its incomplete application — is not a flaw in the story. It *is* the story, and we will watch it play out across the whole of American history: a nation founded on inherited ideals it did not yet live up to, containing within those very ideals the standard by which its failures would be judged and, slowly, corrected. The seed and the standard came, in large part, from here.

With Jerusalem's contribution in hand — the moral law, the covenant, the accountable ruler, the prophetic conscience, the dignity of the person — we turn next to a very different city, and a very different gift: Athens, and the birth of reason, philosophy, and the first experiments in governing oneself.
