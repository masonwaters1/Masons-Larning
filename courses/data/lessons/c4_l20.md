Turn now from the darkest inheritance of the colonial world to one of its brightest, and one of its most enduring. The same Protestant conviction that had driven the Puritans across the ocean — that every believer must be able to read the Word of God for themselves — produced, on American soil, an extraordinary commitment to education, to literacy, and to learning, that would shape the character of the nation and leave institutions still standing three and four centuries later. The great universities whose names now signify worldly prestige and secular sophistication — Harvard and Yale and Princeton and their peers — began as schools of the prophets, founded by churches to train ministers and to propagate the faith. To recover this origin is to see how deeply the American commitment to education was rooted in religious conviction, and how the intellectual seriousness of American life grew from the soil of Protestant Christianity.

## The Old Deluder Satan

The story begins not with the colleges but with the humbler and more radical commitment to universal literacy, and it begins in Puritan New England.[^1] The Puritans held, as a matter of first principle, that salvation came through the Word of God, and that every soul must therefore be able to read the Scriptures — for how could one heed a Word one could not read? This conviction had a democratizing implication of enormous consequence: it demanded not the literacy of a priestly elite but the literacy of the whole people, every farmer and craftsman and, in principle, every woman and child, so that all might read the Bible for themselves. And so the Puritans set about, with characteristic thoroughness, to make a whole society literate. In 1647 the Massachusetts legislature passed a law that captured the whole enterprise in its very preamble, declaring that it was "the old deluder, Satan," who sought to keep men from the knowledge of the Scriptures, and requiring, in order to foil him, that every town of sufficient size establish a school and hire a teacher. It was one of the first laws in the history of the world to mandate public education, and its explicit purpose was religious: to defeat Satan by teaching children to read the Word of God.

The result was that New England achieved rates of literacy extraordinary for the age, among the highest anywhere in the world — a whole society of Bible-readers, which is to say a whole society of readers. The printing press came early to the colony; among the first books printed in British America was the Bay Psalm Book, a metrical psalter for congregational singing. A Scripture-centered faith had produced a reading people, and a reading people would prove capable of things — of self-government, of argument, of revolution — that an illiterate people could not have attempted. The American commitment to widespread education, which would become one of the nation's defining characteristics, was born here, in the Puritan conviction that every soul must be able to read the Word.

## Schools of the Prophets

Above the common schools rose the colleges, and their origins were unmistakably religious. Harvard, the oldest, was founded in 1636, only a few years after the Puritans landed, and its founding purpose was stated with memorable clarity: the colonists, having provided for their livelihoods and their houses of worship, longed to advance learning and perpetuate it to posterity, dreading above all, as the famous phrase had it, to leave an illiterate ministry to the churches when their present ministers should lie in the dust. Harvard existed, first of all, to train ministers — to ensure that the churches of New England would never lack for learned pastors able to expound the Scriptures. William and Mary followed in Virginia in 1693, an Anglican foundation. Yale was established in 1701 by Puritan ministers who feared that Harvard was growing lax and liberal, and who wanted a college more firmly committed to orthodox faith. And the College of New Jersey, later Princeton, was founded in 1746 by Presbyterians associated with the Great Awakening, to train ministers in the fervent New Light tradition.

The pattern held across the colonies. Nearly every one of the colleges that would later constitute the American academic elite began as a religious foundation, established by a church or a religious movement to train clergy and educate a Christian citizenry: Brown by the Baptists, Rutgers by the Dutch Reformed, Dartmouth by Congregationalists with a mission to educate Native Americans, King's College — later Columbia — by Anglicans. The colleges were, in the phrase of the age, schools of the prophets, seminaries of learning and piety, and they gathered and cultivated the intellectual life of colonial America. It was no accident that the greatest mind the colonies produced, Jonathan Edwards, whom the next lesson will meet, was a product of this world — a graduate of Yale, a college president, a theologian and philosopher of the first rank, formed by the Scripture-centered learning that the Protestant conviction had raised up. The intellectual seriousness of American Protestantism, its capacity to produce not only fervent preachers but rigorous thinkers, grew directly from these institutions and the faith that founded them.

## Seedbeds of Revival and Revolution

The colleges would prove to be more than seminaries; they would be seedbeds of the movements that made the nation. The College of New Jersey, in particular, became an engine of the Great Awakening, training the New Light ministers who would carry revival across the colonies, and later, under the presidency of John Witherspoon — a Presbyterian minister who would himself sign the Declaration of Independence — it would educate a remarkable share of the revolutionary generation, including a future president and numerous members of the Continental Congress and the Constitutional Convention. The schools of the prophets, founded to train ministers of the Gospel, ended up training the founders of a republic, and the ideas that animated both the revival and the Revolution flowed through their halls. The religious commitment to learning had produced an educated leadership class, steeped in Scripture and in the classics, capable of the extraordinary intellectual labor that founding a nation would require.

The picture should not be idealized. Colonial education, for all its reach, was still bounded: the colleges were overwhelmingly the preserve of white men of some means, the higher learning largely closed to women, the poor, and the enslaved; and the missionary education directed at Native Americans, as at Dartmouth and in the earlier praying towns, was entangled with the effort to erase native cultures and remake native peoples in the colonizers' image. And the religious purpose of the colleges would, over the generations, gradually loosen and secularize, until the seminaries of the seventeenth century became the secular research universities of the twentieth, their pious origins half-forgotten by those who walked their quadrangles. But the origin was real, and it left a permanent mark. The American reverence for education, the density of colleges and schools across the land, the conviction that learning matters and that a free people must be an educated people — all of this grew from a religious root: the Protestant insistence that every soul must be able to read the Word of God, and that the churches must never be left with an illiterate ministry. From the schools that faith raised up came the mind we turn to next — Jonathan Edwards, the greatest thinker of colonial America, in whom the Scripture-centered learning of New England reached its height.
