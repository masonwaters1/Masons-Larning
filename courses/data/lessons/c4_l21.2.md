What did it actually feel like to be awakened? The previous lesson watched the pulpit; this one turns around and watches the pews — because the Great Awakening is, among everything else, the best-documented episode of mass religious experience in early American history, and Northampton is its laboratory. Edwards was not only the revival's preacher; he was its clinician. He interviewed his converts one by one, took notes, traced the stages of their experience, published case studies, and watched his own household pass through states he could only describe as heaven begun on earth. He also buried the revival's casualties — including his own uncle — and told the world about them, in print, when concealment would have served the cause better. Through his documents we can do what almost no other event of the eighteenth century allows: follow the Awakening off the page and into particular rooms, and watch what it did to a merchant's daughter, a four-year-old girl, a pastor's wife, and a despairing man at dawn. This is the Awakening at the scale where it was actually lived.

## The Town Turned Upside Down

Begin with Edwards's own wide-angle report of Northampton in the spring of 1735, because its details are the texture of the thing.[^1] The town — some two hundred families, all of whom attended the one meetinghouse — became, in his account, a place with one subject: "other discourse than of the things of religion would scarcely be tolerated in any company." The tavern emptied; the young people, whose "frolics" and night "company-keeping" Edwards had campaigned against for years, abandoned them without being told; work itself had to be defended — Edwards found himself counseling parishioners not to neglect their farms and shops for religious meetings. The meetinghouse changed acoustically: "the assembly in general were, from time to time, in tears while the word was preached; some weeping with sorrow and distress, others with joy and love." Conversions ran through every demographic the social order recognized — the old, the young, the respectable, the profligate — and Edwards counted more than three hundred in half a year, in a town whose entire adult population was not vastly larger. News of it ran down the Connecticut Valley and leapt the Atlantic; and the crucial point for everything that follows is that this was 1735, four years before Whitefield: the pattern of the awakened community — total preoccupation, public emotion, mass conversion, moral transformation — was set in a country town by a manuscript preacher, and the Faithful Narrative that described it became the script that a continent, and then two, would consciously follow.

## Four Souls

Edwards built his Narrative around case studies, and two of his choices tell us what he thought conversion was. Abigail Hutchinson was a young woman, frail and dying of a condition that was closing her throat, and Edwards traces her passage in stages he clearly regards as exemplary: first terror — days of such distress over her sin that she could not sleep; then the turn — not relief about herself but a new sight of Christ, "a constant sweetness in her soul"; then the transformation of her dying, so that she spoke of death "as of going to a wedding," and starved with a serenity that unnerved and converted observers. Phebe Bartlet was four years old — Edwards knew perfectly well how the choice would read, and made it anyway — a child who took to her "closet" for secret prayer several times a day, was found weeping over her state, announced her love of God above her family, and began admonishing her parents about the poor; Edwards documents her with the same gravity he gives adults, staking the claim that the work was supernatural precisely because it exceeded what nurture or imitation could produce in an infant. Alongside these published cases stands the experience of Sarah Edwards, his wife, in January 1742: days of ecstasies — overwhelming perceptions of the love of God, the body fainting under them, joy that made "the sweetest night I ever had in my life" out of hours awake in the dark — which Jonathan recorded in her own words and then presented (gender-anonymized) in print as his prime exhibit that the revival's heights were not hysteria: this was the most theologically literate woman in the valley, in perfect health of mind, and he essentially dared the critics to explain her away. And then there is the fourth soul, the one that darkens the file: Joseph Hawley II, Edwards's uncle by marriage, a leading merchant of the town, who in the revival's white heat of June 1735 fell into a despair about his own soul that would not lift — the "melancholy" the Puritans knew well — and cut his throat at dawn. Edwards reported it to his correspondents in grief and candor, noting that afterward a kind of suicidal whisper ran through the region, sober people suddenly urged, as if by a voice, to "cut your own throat, now is a good opportunity." He understood — and said — that Satan had been permitted to counterattack; a modern reader will say that mass psychological events have casualties at their edges. Both descriptions fit the record, and the course's honesty requires the record whole: the same wave that carried Abigail Hutchinson to a radiant death carried Joseph Hawley to a razor, and Edwards published both.

## The Anatomy of a Conversion

Out of hundreds of such interviews Edwards distilled the morphology his tradition had long taught and his data refined: conviction first — the terrors, as the awakened saw their state before the law; then humiliation — the collapse of every self-rescue, the discovery that one cannot repent or believe by trying; then, characteristically at the point of giving up, the turn — the "new sense" of the previous lessons, experienced not as a decision achieved but as a sight given: Christ, suddenly, appearing lovely. Then joy, often overwhelming; then — and here Edwards the clinician did his most enduring work — the long test. For the question the Awakening forced on every town was: which of these experiences are real? Edwards's answer, systematized in Religious Affections, was ruthless toward his own movement's favorite evidences: intensity proves nothing; visions, texts of Scripture flashing into the mind, bodily effects, fluent religious talk, assurance itself — none of it distinguishes grace from nature or from the devil. The signs that count are dispositional and slow: a new taste for holiness itself, humility that deepens rather than performs, and — the "chief of all the signs" — Christian practice: the whole tenor of a changed life, over time. He then applied the test to his own trophy case and published the results: within a few years, he wrote with astonishing candor, many of Northampton's revival converts looked no different from before, and the town that had been all love in 1735 was, by the mid-1740s, quarrelsome enough to fire him. No critic of the Awakening ever audited it more severely than its own chief defender — which is precisely why his defense of its genuine core has worn so well.

## What It Did to American Lives

Pull back, finally, to what the experience wrought at scale, because the inner event had outer consequences that compounded across the century. The Awakening put into wide circulation a new default shape for a human life: the biography with a hinge in it — before and after, the datable new birth — which became the organizing story of millions of American lives and remains, from testimony night to the recovery meeting, one of this country's native genres. It generated mass lay religious practice on a new scale: family devotions, private prayer, religious conversation, and above all religious reading — the demand that made colonial printers rich and made Edwards and Whitefield bestsellers — knitting ordinary people into an intense, self-directed piety that no parish structure administered. It changed who had standing to speak of the deepest things: the converted farmhand, the awakened servant, the four-year-old in her closet, the pastor's wife whose ecstasies out-credentialed the clergy — experience, not office, now authorized testimony, with consequences for American authority far beyond religion. And it planted the expectation that God acts now — that the extraordinary is available, that dead towns can blaze in a season — an expectation that made revival a permanent American institution and made American Christianity, for better and worse, an affair of waves. Whether the price of the wave — the casualties, the counterfeits, the crash that follows the crest — was inherent or accidental became the great fight of the era, and Edwards's disciples would carry his careful answer into the next century. But the man whose voice turned the local wave into a continental one had a very different instrument, and it is time to hear it properly: Whitefield, in full voice, next after the Edwards cluster closes.
