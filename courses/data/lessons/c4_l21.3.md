When Jonathan Edwards died of the smallpox inoculation in March 1758, the ledger of his life read as failure by every visible measure: fired by his own congregation, exiled eight years to a frontier mission, dead five weeks into the college presidency that was to be his restoration, his books admired by a scattered few and unread by the many. No American of his century would have believed what the ledger reads now: that the fired pastor became the most influential mind American Christianity has produced — fountainhead of its first theological school, grandfather of its missions movement, ancestor (by routes he did not choose and would not all have blessed) of its abolitionism, its colleges, and its revival tradition — and that in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries, long after his supposed obsolescence, he would be the one colonial American still read, straight through, by philosophers, historians, and millions of ordinary believers. This lesson traces the shadow — how it lengthened, through whom, and what it says about how ideas actually travel through American history.

## The School of Edwards

The first carriers were men he trained in his own house.[^1] Edwards took ministerial students as boarders — the colonial equivalent of graduate school — and two of them, Samuel Hopkins and Joseph Bellamy, became the founders of what was called the New Divinity: the first indigenous American school of theology, a network of Yale-trained country pastors (their opponents sneered at the "farmer metaphysicians") who spent the next two generations systematizing, debating, and extending Edwards's thought through New England's pulpits and parlors. The school's history carries a double lesson about intellectual inheritance. The New Divinity men were rigorists — where Edwards held mystery in tension, they drove distinctions to conclusions, and their scholastic edge could turn the master's living vision into machinery; every powerful thinker's first heirs tend to be his hardeners. But the same rigor produced the shadow's most astonishing branch: Hopkins, pastoring in Newport, Rhode Island — the North's busiest slave port, whose pews held slave-trading wealth — took Edwards's signature ethical concept, disinterested benevolence (true virtue as love to Being in general, without private bias), and aimed it at the harbor: if virtue is disinterested love to all being, the African is included, and slaveholding is sin. Hopkins became one of America's first public abolitionist theologians, preaching against the trade to the traders, publishing his Dialogue Concerning the Slavery of the Africans in 1776 and mailing it to the Continental Congress; Edwards's own son, Jonathan Edwards Jr., preached the same conclusion. The father, as the earlier lesson recorded, owned slaves to his death; the sons derived emancipation from his premises within a generation. Ideas, once loosed, out-travel their authors — and sometimes correct them.

## The Little Book That Circled the World

The shadow's second branch grew from the least likely soil: a dead missionary's diary. David Brainerd — the young man expelled from Yale for saying a tutor had no more grace than a chair — became a missionary to the Delaware Indians, kept a journal of his brief, tubercular, self-lacerating, occasionally luminous labors, and died at twenty-nine in Edwards's own house, nursed by Edwards's daughter Jerusha, who followed him into the grave within months. Edwards edited the diary and published The Life of David Brainerd (1749) — and the book became, by a wide margin, the most reprinted and most consequential thing he ever wrote. Its portrait of a life utterly spent for the gospel among the unreached detonated slowly and then everywhere: William Carey, the English cobbler whose 1792 mission to India begins the modern Protestant missions movement, carried Brainerd as a sacred text; Henry Martyn went to India and Persia "with Brainerd in hand"; generations of missionary candidates on every continent traced their call to the book. Through it, the fired pastor of Northampton became a grandfather of the worldwide missionary enterprise — a movement that would carry American religious energy, for better and sometimes for worse, to the ends of the earth — and the pattern deserves notice: Edwards's widest influence traveled not through his hardest arguments but through a story of a consecrated life. Americans, then and since, are converted by biographies.

## Buried and Raised

The shadow then did something instructive: it disappeared. Through the nineteenth century Edwards's reputation sank — the democratic, Arminian, self-improving temper of the age (the Second Great Awakening lessons will meet it) had no use for his God-centered Calvinism, and by the century's end "Edwardsean" was, in polite religion, a term for the outgrown; Oliver Wendell Holmes wondered aloud how so great a mind had served so dreadful a creed, and the anthologies embalmed him as the Sinners sermon and nothing else. The resurrection came from an unexpected quarter: secular scholarship. Perry Miller — Harvard's agnostic historian of Puritanism — published in 1949 an intellectual biography announcing Edwards as a thinker of world rank, "so much ahead of his time that our own can hardly catch up with him," and launched the Yale critical edition of the Works that would run to seventy-three volumes and six decades. The scholars recovered the philosopher (the idealist metaphysician, the moral theorist of True Virtue, the psychologist of the Affections); and on the scholars' heels came the churches: the late-twentieth-century evangelical and Reformed resurgence took Edwards as its patron saint — his God-entranced vision, in the phrase of his most effective modern popularizer, set against the therapeutic shallows of American religion — until the fired pastor stood, three centuries after his birth, as the shared property of the academy that doesn't believe him and the believers the academy studies. Few American afterlives are stranger, and none says more about the two audiences that have always contended for the country's soul.

## The Measure of the Shadow

Total the estate, then, and mark what kind of influence it is. Institutionally: Princeton, shaped in its founding era by his circle and presidency; the New Divinity network and, downstream, Andover Seminary — the nation's first — founded by his theological heirs; the missions movement seeded by Brainerd's Life; a strand of early abolitionism argued from his ethics. Intellectually: the only body of colonial American thought that the world's philosophy departments still read, and the theological grammar — God's absolute sovereignty joined to experiential, affectionate piety — that gave American evangelicalism its original spine. Culturally: the template of revival itself, exported from Northampton in the Faithful Narrative, plus the enduring standard for testing religious experience that every discerning generation of American Christians rediscovers when a new wave crests. And note what the estate lacks: no denomination bears his name, no movement he organized, no office he leveraged — he died holding, in worldly terms, almost nothing. His influence ran entirely through the two channels this course keeps finding at the bottom of American history: trained persons and printed pages.

One more channel deserves naming, because millions have entered the shadow through it without reading a page of the theology: the young Edwards's seventy Resolutions. Written mostly at nineteen and twenty, before Northampton, before fame — "Resolved, to live with all my might, while I do live"; "Resolved, never to do anything, which I should be afraid to do, if it were the last hour of my life"; resolved to examine every night wherein he had been negligent — the Resolutions, with the accompanying diary, became one of American Christianity's most reproduced devotional documents: copied into commonplace books for three centuries, printed on cards and posters, assigned to students, taken up each January by readers who know nothing else of the man. They transmit the thing beneath the system — the deliberate, total consecration of an ordinary day — and they explain something the institutional ledger cannot: why Edwards's influence has always run person to person, one serious soul at a time, as much as through schools and movements. The disciplined interior life he practiced at twenty became, through a few hundred words, a template thousands still measure their mornings against.

It is the providential-and-imperfect pattern in miniature — the greatest gifts carried in flawed vessels, the correction of the flaws generated by the gifts themselves — and it sets up the sharpest possible contrast with the man whose influence ran through neither students nor system but through sheer voice and presence, and who was, in his own lifetime, everything Edwards was not: famous, beloved, heard by millions, and organizationally childless. To Whitefield, in full voice.
