The colonial world the last ten lessons have built — covenanted towns, learned clergy, colleges founded to train them — produced, in its third generation, the greatest mind America had yet seen, and by many sober estimates the greatest it has ever produced in philosophy and theology. Jonathan Edwards is remembered by most Americans, if at all, for one sermon about a spider dangled over a flame, encountered in a high-school anthology and filed under Puritan gloom. The reduction is almost comically unjust. Edwards was a scientist who wrote pioneering observations of spiders as a boy; a philosopher engaging Newton and Locke at the frontier of European thought from a parsonage on the Massachusetts frontier; a psychologist of religion whose analysis of authentic and counterfeit spiritual experience has never been surpassed; a pastor who presided over the revivals that begin the next two lessons; a missionary to the Stockbridge Indians; and, at his death, president of Princeton. To understand the Great Awakening — and the distinctly American religious temperament it forged — you must first understand the man who was at once its instrument, its theologian, and its most searching critic.

## The Prodigy of the Connecticut Valley

Edwards was born in 1703 in East Windsor, Connecticut, the only son among eleven children of a pastor, grandson through his mother of Solomon Stoddard — the "Pope of the Connecticut Valley," whose Northampton pulpit was the most influential in western Massachusetts and which Jonathan would one day inherit.[^1] The boy's mind announced itself early. At about eleven he wrote a small masterpiece of natural observation on the "flying" of spiders — how they let out thread and ride the air — precise, delighted, and reverent, already displaying what would mark everything he wrote: the conviction that close attention to nature and rigorous reasoning were forms of attention to God. He entered Yale just before turning thirteen and graduated first in his class; there he encountered Newton's physics and Locke's Essay Concerning Human Understanding, and read the latter, he said, with more pleasure "than the most greedy miser finds when gathering up handfuls of silver and gold." The detail matters against the caricature: the arch-Puritan of American memory was formed on the newest science and philosophy of the European Enlightenment, and his life's project was not to hide from that new learning but to think it through to the glory of God — to show that the world Newton measured and the mind Locke anatomized belonged, all of it, to the God of the covenant.

In his early twenties, Edwards underwent the experience that organized everything after. He had been religious all his life, and had also long choked on the doctrine of God's absolute sovereignty — it had seemed to him, he confessed, "like a horrible doctrine." Then, reading 1 Timothy 1:17 — "Now unto the King eternal, immortal, invisible" — something turned. There came into his soul, he wrote, "a sense of the glory of the Divine Being; a new sense, quite different from any thing I ever experienced before." Sovereignty now appeared not horrible but "exceedingly pleasant, bright, and sweet." Walking in his father's pasture, looking at the sky and clouds, he perceived what he could only call the sweet conjunction of majesty and meekness. That phrase — a new sense — became the seed of his life's thought: true religion, Edwards would argue for thirty years, is not new information about God but a new perception of God, as different from mere doctrinal knowledge as tasting honey is from being told it is sweet.

## The Theology of Beauty and the Affections

From that seed grew a body of thought astonishing in range, and two of its pillars must be in hand before the revival lessons. The first: Edwards is the great theologian of beauty. Where the schoolmen had reached first for being or power, Edwards reached for excellency — God is glorious, and glory is beauty; the divine holiness is "the highest beauty and amiability, vastly above all other beauties"; and the universe exists as God's communication of that beauty, an emanation of glory meant to be perceived and enjoyed. Conversion, in this frame, is aesthetic before it is legal: the new sense given to the soul is precisely a taste for divine beauty, and the unconverted man's problem is not chiefly that he lacks arguments but that he lacks the taste — he may know everything about God and relish nothing. The second pillar follows: religion lives in the affections. In his masterpiece Religious Affections (1746), distilled from the revivals, Edwards argued that "true religion, in great part, consists in holy affections" — that the heart's loves, desires, and joys are not decoration on the real business of belief but the real business itself; a religion of the head alone is no religion, for "he that has doctrinal knowledge and speculation only, without affection, never is engaged in the business of religion." At the same time — and this double edge is Edwards's genius — not all affections are holy: heat is no proof of light. The book is a surgeon's manual for distinguishing true spiritual experience from its counterfeits, written by a man who had seen both erupt in his own parish, and it made him simultaneously the revival's greatest defender and the most penetrating critic of its excesses — a balance the next two lessons will watch him fight to hold.

## Northampton, the Spider, and the Surprising Work

Edwards succeeded his grandfather at Northampton in 1729, and in the winter of 1734–35 his preaching — on justification by faith alone — detonated something no one planned. The town, by his account, was "never so full of love, nor so full of joy"; conversions multiplied across every age and rank; taverns emptied; and Edwards's careful report, A Faithful Narrative of the Surprising Work of God (1737), became a transatlantic sensation that gave Protestants on two continents a template for what "revival" meant — the local rehearsal, as the next lessons will show, for the continental Awakening that Whitefield's arrival would ignite. It is against this background that the famous sermon must be placed. Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God, preached at Enfield in July 1741 at the Awakening's height, was not a typical Edwards performance — his manner in the pulpit was calm, his voice level, his usual register the beauty and sweetness described above — but a deliberate act of awakening aimed at a notoriously complacent town, and its terrifying imagery (the spider held over the fire, the bow of God's wrath bent) served a precise theological point: that the sinner's every breath is sheer mercy, that nothing but God's good pleasure — no health, no prudence, no church membership — holds anyone out of ruin, and that this mercy is presently, freely offered. The congregation's cries of "What shall I do to be saved?" drowned him out before he finished. To read the sermon as sadism is to miss that its engine is the same sovereignty Edwards had come to taste as sweet: the God whose hand alone upholds the sinner is the God whose hand is extended to him.

## Greatness and Clay

The course's commitment to a providential and imperfect story requires the whole man, and Edwards's story supplies both halves in painful proportion. The imperfection first, and squarely: Edwards owned slaves. He purchased at least several enslaved persons over his lifetime — a girl named Venus, bought at auction; others named in his estate — and he defended slaveholding when a fellow minister was criticized for it, even while condemning the Atlantic slave trade itself. The greatest theologian of divine beauty did not see, or did not let himself see, the deformity in his own household; his son Jonathan Edwards Jr. and his disciples Samuel Hopkins and Joseph Bellamy would draw the abolitionist conclusion from his own theology within a generation, which sharpens rather than softens the judgment on the father. The clay extended to his pastorate: in 1750, after twenty-three years, Northampton dismissed him — by a vote of some ninety percent of the members — in a bitter dispute over his reversal of his grandfather's open-communion policy, compounded by a botched church discipline case and long-simmering resentments. America's greatest theologian was fired by his own congregation, and took himself, his wife Sarah, and their children to the frontier mission town of Stockbridge to preach to the Mohicans and Mohawks — where, in exile, in a house full of children, he wrote the philosophical masterworks (Freedom of the Will, Original Sin, The End for Which God Created the World) on which his world reputation rests. Called in 1757 to the presidency of the College of New Jersey — Princeton — he arrived in January 1758, submitted to the new practice of smallpox inoculation to set an example of enlightened faith, and died of the inoculation on March 22, aged fifty-four. His last recorded words gave the family his summary of everything: "Trust in God, and ye need not fear." What he left — the theology of the new sense, the anatomy of the affections, a trained cohort of disciples, and a template for revival — was about to be carried across thirteen colonies by a very different man: a twenty-five-year-old English preacher with a voice like an organ and an instinct for publicity that would make him the first celebrity in American history. George Whitefield is next.
