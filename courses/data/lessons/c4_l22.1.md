Edwards read from manuscript in a level voice and stared at the bell rope. Now the other instrument. George Whitefield preached without a note, in the open air, to crowds the size of small cities, with a trained actor's total command of voice, face, and body — weeping freely, personating biblical characters, breaking off mid-sermon to address heaven aloud — and contemporaries on two continents, including the era's greatest actor and its most famous skeptic, testified that nothing in the theater or the pulpit had prepared them for it. This sub-lesson is about the instrument itself: where the craft came from, how it actually worked on the platform, what the open field did to the sermon as a form, and why Whitefield's method — far more than any doctrine he preached — became one of England's most consequential exports to America. For the American religious future did not inherit Edwards's manuscript. It inherited Whitefield's voice.

## The Craft

Whitefield's technique had a traceable pedigree: the stage.[^1] The innkeeper's son had been a boy actor of real promise — he later confessed he had sometimes skipped school for days to prepare parts, and had studied the manuals of elocution that taught aspiring performers the "passions": the codified gestures, facial configurations, and vocal colorings by which grief, terror, joy, and pity were represented to an audience. Converted, he did not discard the craft; he consecrated it, and defended the choice with a challenge that became famous in substance if not always in wording: why should the devil have all the best techniques — why should players moving fictions command more feeling than preachers announcing eternal realities? The equipment was formidable. The voice, by every account, was a phenomenon — musical, enormous, effortlessly articulate at distances that seem impossible until one remembers Franklin's experiment: the skeptical printer, standing in the crowd on Market Street in Philadelphia, walked backward down toward the river until the sentences finally blurred, computed the semicircle that distance implied, allowed two square feet per standing auditor, and concluded that the newspapers' thirty-thousand crowds, which he had disbelieved, were acoustically possible. Contemporaries swore Whitefield could break a heart by the mere musical management of a word — Garrick's "Mesopotamia" jest from the parent lesson — and his printed sermons, which read as competent but hardly extraordinary, were universally said to be corpses of the living thing: the power was in the performance, in the pauses, the tears (he rarely preached without weeping, and defended it: "You blame me for weeping; but how can I help it, when you will not weep for yourselves?"), the sudden apostrophes, the acted scenes — the sinner as a blind beggar tottering toward a precipice, rendered so vividly that Lord Chesterfield, of all unlikely auditors, is said to have leapt up crying "Good God! He's gone!" The theater's verdict on its defector was professional respect; the church's, as we will see, was frequently horror.

## The Field

The decisive innovation, though, was not the style but the venue, and it is worth feeling how radical it was. In the parish world — England's and, in its looser way, colonial America's — preaching happened inside an ordered space that was itself a map of the social order: the appointed building, the appointed hour, the assigned and rented pews ranking families by wealth and standing, the preacher authorized by ordination and by right to that particular pulpit, the audience defined by geography and obligation. When Whitefield stepped onto the mining hill at Kingswood in February 1739, every one of those structures fell away at once. The field had no pews and no pew rents — the crowd sorted itself by arrival and eagerness, gentry shoulder to shoulder with colliers; no parish boundary — hearers walked and rode in from twenty miles of countryside, constituting an audience that belonged to no one's cure of souls; no liturgical container — the sermon, which in the parish service was one element among prayers, psalms, and sacrament, became the entire event, freestanding, its beginning and end set by the preacher's own sense of the crowd; and no authorization but effect — nothing licensed the field preacher except that people came, which is to say the audience itself became the credential. Add mobility — Whitefield did not wait for hearers to come to church but carried the event to marketplaces, courthouse steps, racecourses, gallows-grounds, and commons, preaching most days of the week, often twice — and the sum is a new social technology: religion as voluntary mass public event, detached from parish, building, calendar, and hierarchy, held together by nothing but a voice and the desire to hear it. The established clergy who called it "irregular" understood it perfectly: it was not a variation on their system but a rival to it.

## The Event

Reconstruct, from the surviving accounts, what attending actually involved, because the texture explains the impact. Word arrived days ahead — newspaper notices, letters, riders — naming a time and an open place. People began moving early, on foot and horseback, in numbers colonial roads had never carried for any purpose; the next sub-lesson will ride along with one Connecticut farmer whose breathless account of the rush is the classic of the genre. The assembled crowd was itself the first spectacle — ten, fifteen, twenty thousand people in a society whose largest city held sixteen thousand — standing pressed together in a silence contemporaries found uncanny: Franklin noted the "wonderful... change soon made in the manners of our inhabitants," and observers repeatedly marveled that vast multitudes hushed to hear a single unamplified voice, the silence broken by weeping that moved through the crowd in waves, by cries, by occasional faintings. Whitefield preached from a portable field pulpit or a wagon, hat off, robes on — he remained always the Church of England clergyman in appearance — Bible in hand but never a note; sermons ran an hour and more; and when it ended the crowd dispersed carrying the event outward: into taverns and kitchens where it was argued over, into letters, into the newspapers that reported attendance like a public statistic, and into the printed journals in which Whitefield himself narrated his triumphs for those who had missed them. The event, in other words, did not end at the field's edge; it was designed — deliberately, by the first master of religious publicity — to reverberate through every channel the eighteenth-century media world possessed. Historians have called Whitefield the "pedlar in divinity," a merchant retailing the new birth through the same expanding commercial networks that sold cloth and tea, and the description, meant half as criticism, identifies exactly why the method conquered America: it was built for a mobile, commercial, expanding society — which is what America was becoming faster than anywhere on earth.

## The Inheritance

Mark, finally, what the instrument bequeathed, because the bequest outlived every controversy. The extemporaneous, dramatic, audience-facing sermon became the American default: from the Methodist circuit riders and the camp-meeting exhorters of the next century, through Finney's theatrics and Moody's plainspoken urgency, to the radio preachers, the crusade evangelists, and the stage-and-screen pulpits of the present, American preaching has been, overwhelmingly, Whitefieldian — judged by immediate effect on a voluntary crowd, carried by personality, allergic to the manuscript. The mass outdoor religious event became a permanent American institution — the camp meeting, the tent revival, the stadium crusade are Kingswood's direct descendants. And the deepest bequest was a transfer of authority that no one voted on: once the field replaced the parish, the hearers became choosers, and the preacher's standing rested on their verdict rather than on office — a reordering whose consequences for American religion, and eventually American politics, the third Whitefield sub-lesson will pursue. The instrument, in short, was never neutral. A church built around Edwards's method would have been a church of readers and reasoners under settled teachers; the church America actually built was a church of crowds and voices — and it was built that way in the fields of 1739 and 1740. What it felt like to stand in those crowds, and what happened to the people who did, is next.
