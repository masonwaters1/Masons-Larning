On the morning of October 23, 1740, a Connecticut farmer named Nathan Cole was working in his field at Kensington when a messenger galloped past with news: Whitefield would preach at Middletown at ten o'clock. What Cole did in the next three hours, and what he saw when he arrived, he wrote down years later in an unschooled, breathless prose that has become the single most quoted eyewitness account of the Great Awakening — because it does what no statistic can: it shows the Awakening happening to somebody. This sub-lesson stands in the crowd. It follows Cole's ride, watches the multitudes up close — the weeping and the fainting, but also the scoffing, the pickpockets, and the sober people who went home unchanged — and then follows the awakened home, to trace what the experience did to actual American lives: the servants and the enslaved who heard something their masters had not authorized, the women who found a public voice, the young people who defied their parents' religion, and the ordinary farmers, like Cole, whose inner lives were rearranged for decades by a single hour in a field.

## Nathan Cole Drops His Tools

Cole's account deserves its fame line by line.[^1] At the news he "dropt my tool that I had in my hand and ran home" — the detail every historian quotes, the plow abandoned mid-furrow — told his wife to make ready, and set out on one horse for the twelve miles to Middletown, he and his wife riding by turns, he running alongside "as if we were fleeing for our lives," terrified above all of arriving too late. What he saw as he neared the Connecticut River is the Awakening made visible: a low fog rising over the road, which he took for morning mist until he heard it — "a rumbling noise... like a low rumbling thunder" — and realized it was dust, "a cloud of dust made by the running of horses' feet," out of which emerged a river of riders three abreast, "scarcely a horse more than his length behind another, all of a lather and foam with sweat," every rider bending forward urging speed, the whole road for miles "full of people... as if they was fleeing for their lives." At the meetinghouse bank he found three or four thousand already assembled; the Connecticut itself was black with ferry-loads still crossing; the fields stood empty of workers for miles around — "everybody was on the run." And then the hinge of the account, which is inward: Whitefield mounted the scaffold looking, to Cole, "almost angelical," and as he preached, Cole wrote, "he looked as if he was Cloathed with authority from the Great God... and my hearing him preach gave me a heart wound; by God's blessing my old foundation was broken up, and I saw that my righteousness would not save me." One sentence of a farmer's memoir contains the whole theology of the Awakening: the collapse of self-built religion under a felt word. What the account then shows, and the anthologies usually cut, is that the hour was a beginning, not an ending: Cole spent the next two years in spiritual agony — despairing of election, tempted, sleepless — before peace came; he then left his parish church for the radical Separates, was distrained and jailed for refusing church taxes, and spent his life as a marked man of the revival party. One sermon rearranged forty years.

## In the Crowd

Multiply Cole by the tens of thousands and complicate him honestly, because the crowds were not a single mind. The convinced wept, cried out, sometimes fell — the bodily phenomena that so alarmed the critics, and that the revival's own leaders disagreed about, Whitefield generally restraining what some of his imitators encouraged. But the same fields held the whole colonial social spectrum in every posture: scoffers who came for the show and stood at the edges heckling; the merely curious, drawn by the largest gathering anyone had ever seen; pickpockets working the press of bodies; young men and women for whom the crowd was also — the diaries admit it — the greatest social occasion of their lives; and hostile clergy taking notes for next week's counter-sermon. Contemporary opponents charged that the crowds were manufactured — whipped up by advance publicity, inflated by the newspapers, moved by contagion rather than grace — and the modern historian partly agrees with the mechanics while noting what the mechanics cannot explain: the durable minority in every audience, the Nathan Coles, whose lives bent permanently at the field's edge and stayed bent through decades of cost. Franklin, the shrewdest observer in America, watched the same crowds and drew the distinction himself: he never credited the doctrine, but he certified the effects — the changed manners of Philadelphia, the psalms sounding from house to house in the evenings — and kept funding the preacher. The honest picture of the crowd is precisely this mixture: theater, contagion, commerce, curiosity — and, inside the mixture, verifiable in the record by what it cost them later, real and lasting transformation in thousands of particular lives.

## The Unauthorized Hearers

The most consequential listeners were the ones the social order had not consulted. The enslaved heard Whitefield — he preached to them deliberately, addressed them directly in print, and insisted their souls were "as precious as ours" — and they answered in numbers that alarmed the slaveholding gentry, who understood instantly what the preaching of an immediate, experiential new birth, available to every soul without permission from any master, might set loose. From these hearings, and from the awakened Baptist and Methodist preaching that followed in Whitefield's wake, the Black church began to form — the institution whose epic this course follows to the present day — and its founders' spiritual genealogy runs straight through the revival fields, even as (the parent lesson's honesty holds) the evangelist himself worked to expand slavery in Georgia; the enslaved took the gospel and declined the ledger, in what one historian calls the great heist of American religious history. Women, barred from pulpit and vote alike, found in the revival an authorized voice — the convert's testimony, which no one could deny her since experience was now the credential — and awakened women organized prayer meetings, counseled the distressed, and in the radical fringes exhorted publicly, scandalizing the order and rehearsing arguments that would echo for two centuries. And the young, who were the revival's demographic heart — Edwards had noted it in Northampton, and the pattern held everywhere — learned that they might judge, and leave, the religion of their parents' parish on the verdict of their own hearts: a quiet revolution in household authority that thousands of colonial families experienced as domestic crisis before anyone thought to call it social change. In each case the mechanism was identical: once the new birth, experienced and testified, became the thing that mattered, everyone who could experience and testify had standing — and the map of who could speak in America was redrawn from below.

## What Endured

Follow the crowds home, finally, and tally what stayed. Congregations swelled and split — the Separates and Baptists absorbing the awakened whom the parish churches could not hold; family religion intensified — the devotions, the religious reading, the anxious inquiry that made the conversion narrative a household genre; moral reformation, real if uneven, showed in the places contemporaries measured it, the taverns and the courts; and a generation carried for the rest of their lives, as Cole did, the memory of the fields as the hinge of their existence — so that forty years later, old men and women dated their inward histories from the day the dust cloud rose on the Middletown road. Set against this the sober negative findings — the towns where nothing lasted, the converts Edwards himself audited into doubt, the crest that broke by 1744 — and the balanced verdict emerges: the Awakening did not convert America, but it planted in hundreds of thousands of ordinary biographies an experience of unmediated address — God, or at least the question of God, arriving to me, in a field, past every official channel — and a people so addressed would never again relate to official channels, sacred or civil, in quite the old way. Why that was so, and why it happened in America as it could not quite happen in Europe, is the final Whitefield lesson: the field against the parish, and the American flavor of faith.
