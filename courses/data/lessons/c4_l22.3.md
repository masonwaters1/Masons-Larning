The last five lessons have assembled two portraits and a continental event. This one steps all the way back and asks the largest question the Awakening poses: what kind of Christianity was this — and why did it become the American kind? For the contrast that Kingswood and Middletown dramatized was not merely a contrast of preaching styles. Behind the parish stood a whole European model of what a church is: territorial, established, liturgical, hierarchical, inherited — religion as the ordered public framework of a society. Behind the field stood a rival model that the Awakening forged into permanence: voluntary, competitive, experiential, populist, mobile — religion as the free choice and felt transformation of individual souls. Europe, broadly, kept the first. America, decisively, took the second — and was shaped by it in ways that run far beyond religion, into the national temperament itself. This lesson draws the contrast carefully, explains why the field model won here and not there, and states honestly what the claim of an "American flavor" can and cannot mean.

## The Parish World

Feel first the coherence of the model the Awakening disrupted, because it was not a caricature; it was Christendom's mature design.[^1] In the European establishments — Anglican England, Lutheran Scandinavia and Germany, Catholic France and the Habsburg lands, and in their lesser way the Congregational establishments of New England — religion was organized territorially and legally: every soul belonged, by residence and usually by infant baptism, to a parish; the parish belonged to a hierarchy reaching up to bishop or consistory and crown; the clergyman held his pulpit as a legal office (a "living"), supported by tithes or taxes, authorized from above, and was often the only lawful public religious voice in his bounds. Worship was liturgical and calendrical — the prayer book, the church year, the sacraments marking every life from font to grave — and membership was, in the deepest sense, not chosen: one was born into the church as into the nation, and the two were faces of one order. The model's virtues deserve stating, because the Awakening's heirs forget them: it carried the faith to every soul in the land without exception, wove religion into law, charity, education, and art, sustained learning and continuity across centuries, and spared religion the indignities of the marketplace. Its besetting vice was the one Whitefield named from every hillside: a religion coextensive with society tends toward a religion at society's temperature — formal, inherited, unfelt — in which the new birth is presumed of everyone and therefore urgent for no one. The awakeners' whole message was that Christendom's success was its failure: everyone was churched; who was converted?

## The Field Model

Now assemble what the Awakening built in the act of critiquing this, feature by feature, because the features form a system. Voluntary: the field audience chose to come, and the awakened chose their congregations — walking out of parish churches into Separate and Baptist fellowships gathered by conversion rather than geography; membership became a decision, and religion a thing one joins. Experiential: the credential of belonging was the new birth — a datable, testable, narratable personal transformation — not baptism, catechesis, or residence; the convert's testimony, not the priest's register, certified a Christian. Competitive: once hearers were choosers, preachers were rivals — for crowds, for converts, for print attention — and religious life acquired, permanently, the dynamics of a market: innovation rewarded, dullness punished, the audience sovereign. Populist: authority flowed from effect on the people rather than from office over them — the unconverted rector outranked by the converted farmhand, the hierarchy of orders trumped by the democracy of experience. And mobile: the field model needed no ancient endowments, buildings, or boundaries — it traveled in saddlebags, which is why, when the American population poured over the mountains in the next century, it was the Baptists and Methodists, children of this model, who went with them and won the West, while the establishment churches waited for the parish that never formed. Note what this system is, structurally: it is religion organized the way the emerging Anglo-American commercial world organized everything — voluntary association, open competition, sovereign consumers, brand and publicity, expansion into open markets — which is exactly why historians describe Whitefield as retailing the new birth through the same networks that retailed tea. The field model was not merely in America; it was congruent with what America was becoming.

## Why Here and Not There

The honest version of the "American flavor" thesis must begin with a correction: the field model was not American in origin. Whitefield was English; Wesley's Methodism worked the same method through England for half a century; and behind both stood German Pietism — Spener and Francke and the Moravians — with its conventicles of the converted inside the Lutheran establishment. Evangelical revivalism was a transatlantic movement, and Europe had it too. The divergence lies in what happened next, and it is institutional. In Europe, the establishments held: Methodism remained for decades a society within the Church of England and, after separating, one denomination beside a legally established church that retained the parishes, the universities, and the crown; Pietism remained a renewal current inside state Lutheranism; the field model, in short, lived as a movement within or alongside Christendom, contained by it. In America the containers were weak and then were removed. The colonies already held a plurality no establishment could master — the previous unit's lessons on the middle colonies showed a religious map Europe had no parallel for — the Awakening itself multiplied the dissenters (those Separates and Baptists) who would fight establishment in the next unit's disestablishment battles; and when the Revolution and the First Amendment ended state religion, the parish model lost its legal skeleton entirely. What remained standing was the field model — voluntary, competitive, revivalist — now not a movement within a church order but the church order: the default form of American religion. Europe kept Christendom and contained the revival; America dismantled Christendom and was left with the revival. That is the precise sense in which the Awakening's style became the American flavor: not that Americans invented it, but that America became the one Western society organized around it.

## The Flavor, and the Nation It Seasoned

Trace, finally, what marinating in this model for three centuries has done — first to American religion, then to America. The religious consequences are the visible ones: a denominational marketplace of unmatched vitality and churn; per-capita religious practice that has run far above Europe's for two centuries (the sociologists' great puzzle, and the market theorists' answer: established monopolies grow lazy, competitive markets stay warm); the perpetual return of revival as the native reform mechanism; the celebrity preacher as a permanent American type; and the corresponding weaknesses, which the course will meet repeatedly — doctrinal shallowness where the audience is sovereign, fragmentation without end, the temptation to measure truth by crowd. But the deeper consequences are temperamental, and here the Awakening helped season the national character itself. A people whose dominant religious experience was choosing their faith, testing it by their own experience, judging their teachers, and expecting transformation now carried those habits into everything: the anti-hierarchical reflex, the conviction that legitimacy flows from below, the entrepreneurial approach to institutions, the biography organized around reinvention — the second act, the fresh start, the born-again life in its secular versions — and the abiding American suspicion that formal, inherited, official things are less real than immediate, personal, felt things. Tocqueville, arriving ninety years after Whitefield, would marvel that in America religion and liberty advanced together, each supporting the other, where in Europe they warred — and the Awakening is a large part of the explanation: American faith had already been rebuilt on consent, so the age of consent did not break it. The claim must keep its honest limits — the field model did not make America alone (the course has met the covenant towns, the commercial founding, the law), Edwards's churchly, learned, settled Christianity is also a genuine American inheritance, and the parish world's virtues of depth and continuity are real losses that thoughtful American believers have mourned ever since. But the balance of the verdict stands: when the American told the pollster, three centuries later, that his faith was his own choice, judged by his own experience, and no institution's business — he was speaking, whether he knew the name or not, in the accent of a voice first raised over a coalfield in 1739. The Awakening had one more legacy to deliver, and it was ecclesiastical war: the churches themselves split over everything these lessons have described, and the fight between the Old Lights and the New is next.
