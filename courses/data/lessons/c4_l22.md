Jonathan Edwards supplied the Awakening's mind; the man this lesson meets supplied its voice, its legs, and its fame. George Whitefield — a twenty-five-year-old Anglican clergyman with a squint, a theatrical genius, and a voice that could reach thirty thousand people in an open field — arrived in the colonies in 1739 and became, by any serious measure, the first celebrity in American history: the first person famous in all thirteen colonies at once, the most recognized name in America before George Washington, seen in person, by plausible estimates, by a majority of the colonial population. He preached perhaps eighteen thousand sermons to combined audiences in the millions, crossed the Atlantic thirteen times, and turned the local revivals of the 1730s into the continental event the next lesson narrates. He also — the course's honesty requires it in the same breath — campaigned successfully to legalize slavery in Georgia. The first American celebrity was a preacher; the preacher was a paradox; and both facts shaped the nation.

## The Actor Converted

Whitefield's gifts were formed in an inn and a theater.[^1] Born in Gloucester, England, in 1714, son of an innkeeper widowed early, he grew up serving tavern customers and performing — he loved the stage, acted in school plays, and absorbed the actor's craft of voice, gesture, and timing that he would later baptize. He worked his way through Oxford as a servitor — waiting on wealthier students — and there fell in with a small, methodical religious society led by the brothers John and Charles Wesley, mocked by classmates as the "Holy Club" and the "Methodists." Ascetic striving nearly killed him; reading Henry Scougal's The Life of God in the Soul of Man broke him open — true religion, Scougal taught, is not duties or opinions but "the life of God in the soul of man," a new birth — and in 1735 Whitefield experienced that birth and never recovered. Ordained at twenty-one, he discovered almost immediately that something happened when he preached that did not happen for other men. Crowds swelled; churches overflowed; and when sympathetic pulpits began closing to his irregular enthusiasm, he took the step that changed the history of preaching: in February 1739, at Kingswood near Bristol, he preached in the open air to coal miners — men no church reached — and watched, as he famously recorded, the "white gutters made by their tears" down cheeks black with coal dust. The field was now his church, and the field had no walls and no capacity limit. Benjamin Franklin, characteristically, later paced out the acoustics in Philadelphia and calculated that Whitefield could indeed be heard by more than thirty thousand at once — the Enlightenment's most famous skeptic empirically verifying the Awakening's most famous voice.

## The Grand Itinerant

Whitefield's 1739–41 American tour is the hinge on which the next lesson turns, but the method deserves its own attention, because it was new under the sun. He preached everywhere, to everyone, across every line the colonial religious world had drawn — an Anglican preaching for Presbyterians, Congregationalists, Baptists, and in open fields belonging to no one, declaring that denominational walls meant nothing beside the new birth. ("Father Abraham," he cried in a famous flight, "whom have you in heaven? Any Episcopalians? No! Any Presbyterians? No!... All who are here are Christians... God help us to forget party names and to become Christians in deed and truth.") He preached without notes, dramatically — weeping, impersonating Bible characters, acting out the storm and the shipwreck — prompting the great David Garrick's remark that Whitefield could make audiences weep merely by pronouncing "Mesopotamia," and Garrick's confession that he would give a hundred guineas to be able to say "Oh!" like Whitefield. And he was publicized as no religious figure had ever been. His associate William Seward rode ahead placing newspaper notices; Whitefield published his journals in serial, reporting his own triumphs; crowd numbers were announced and disputed like box office. Benjamin Franklin became his printer, friend, and — on religion — unmoved admirer: Franklin printed Whitefield's journals and sermons profitably for years, attended the preaching with his pockets resolved to give nothing, and, as he ruefully recorded in the Autobiography, emptied them entirely — coppers, silver, and gold — under the force of the oratory. The Franklin-Whitefield friendship, printer-deist and revivalist, is itself a small parable of the era: the Awakening was not a retreat from the emerging modern world of print, publicity, and mass audience; it was among the first movements anywhere to master that world.

## What He Preached — and Wrought

The message was as focused as the method was flamboyant: the new birth. "Ye must be born again" was Whitefield's constant text — he joked that he preached it so often because "ye must be born again" — and its edge was aimed at precisely the world the previous lessons described: colonies full of churches, parishes, and baptized, respectable, catechized people who had, he insisted, no life of God in the soul. Salvation was not membership, morality, or assent; it was a supernatural change that a person could and must experience, and — here the social electricity — that the humblest hearer could know directly, without waiting on parson or parish. A Calvinist like Edwards (his public breach with John Wesley over predestination in 1741 divided Methodism permanently into Calvinist and Wesleyan streams), Whitefield nonetheless preached sovereign grace as urgent, universal invitation. The effects ran beyond the churches. By making the individual's direct, verifiable experience the thing that mattered, by teaching ordinary people to judge the spiritual state of their own clergy, by drawing mass audiences across colonial and denominational borders and knitting them, through print, into a single public — the Grand Itinerant was building, without intending it, cultural equipment the Revolution would inherit: a continental common experience, a habit of lay judgment against established authority, and the demonstrated power of the mobilized many. Historians debate how direct the line runs, and the course will weigh that debate in the Black Regiment lessons; that there is a line, few deny.

## The Paradox and the End

Now the shadow, stated without flinching. Whitefield, orphan-hearted all his life, founded the Bethesda orphanage in the new colony of Georgia in 1740 and poured his fame into funding it — and, deciding that the orphanage and the colony could not prosper without enslaved labor, he became an open, persistent advocate for legalizing slavery in Georgia, which had been founded slave-free. He lobbied the trustees, argued in print that Georgia would never flourish without it, purchased enslaved people for Bethesda once the prohibition fell in 1751, and died the owner of dozens of human beings, whom he bequeathed like property to the Countess of Huntingdon. It is true, and should be said, that he also scandalized the South by preaching to the enslaved, insisting publicly that Black men and women had souls "as precious as ours," publishing a stinging open letter rebuking Southern planters for their cruelty, and that enslaved and free Black Christians heard and answered his gospel in numbers that helped seed the Black church — Phillis Wheatley's famous elegy on his death testifies to what he meant to many. But the ledger is what it is: the man who told America it must be born again worked to expand the institution that was America's deepest sin, and did so not in weakness but with arguments, for his charity's bottom line. The course's founding conviction — providential and imperfect — is rarely so compressed into one biography.

He never stopped. Against warnings, gasping with asthma, he preached his final open-air sermon at Exeter, New Hampshire, on September 29, 1770 — two hours, on faith and works — and died at dawn the next morning in the parsonage at Newburyport, Massachusetts, where he is buried beneath the pulpit. The bells of Boston tolled; Georgia's legislature mourned; and in Boston a young enslaved poet, Phillis Wheatley, wrote the elegy that made her name. Whitefield's fame proved mortal — within a century he was strangely forgotten, the celebrity's fate — but what he detonated was not. Thirteen colonies had now wept at the same sermons, argued over the same preacher, and learned to act as one audience. What that detonation did to the churches, the colleges, the social order, and eventually the Revolution is the story of the Great Awakening itself — next.
