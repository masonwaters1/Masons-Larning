The pieces are assembled: Edwards's Northampton template, Whitefield's continental voice, colonies of churched but restless people. This lesson steps back and tells the event whole — the Great Awakening, the wave of revivals that crested between roughly 1739 and 1743 and touched every colony, every denomination, and, directly or indirectly, nearly every colonial family. It was the first experience the thirteen colonies ever shared — before any war, any congress, any common government, there was a common revival — and historians have argued ever since about what exactly it was: the greatest outpouring of the Spirit in American history, on the participants' own account; a social earthquake dressed in theology, on others'; the seedbed of the Revolution, on a thesis this course will handle with care. What no one disputes is the scale of the fact: for a few astonishing years, the question of the soul's state before God became the public business of an entire seaboard, and American religion — and American culture — came out the other side permanently changed.

## The Wave

The Awakening did not begin with Whitefield, and seeing its tributaries matters.[^1] Revivals had flared for decades: among the Dutch Reformed of New Jersey under Theodorus Frelinghuysen in the 1720s; among the Presbyterians under the Tennent family, whose father William ran the derided "Log College" that trained fiery evangelists; and in Edwards's Connecticut Valley in 1734–35, whose Faithful Narrative gave the phenomenon its script. What Whitefield's 1739–41 tour did was fuse the local fires into one continental burn. From his landing in Delaware, up through Philadelphia and New York, across New Jersey, and finally through New England in the fall of 1740 — where his six-week sweep drew the largest crowds ever assembled in America, some twenty thousand or more on Boston Common — the itinerant connected the circuits. Behind him the local ministry caught fire or caught fear; native itinerants multiplied, most famously Gilbert Tennent, whose sermon The Danger of an Unconverted Ministry (1740) said the unsayable — that many settled clergy were themselves unregenerate, "dead men" leading the blind — and told laypeople they might rightly leave such ministries to hear the gospel elsewhere. Conversions surged by the tens of thousands; church admissions in New England spiked to several times their normal rate; sober diarists like Boston's Benjamin Colman marveled that religion had become "the general subject of discourse." The revival had its physical phenomena — crying out, fainting, bodily agitations — its all-night meetings, its lay exhorters including, scandalously to some, women, Black colonists, and the very young; and it had, in Edwards, a theologian watching it all with his surgeon's eye, defending the work as genuinely divine in Some Thoughts Concerning the Revival while cataloguing, with growing alarm, the counterfeits multiplying at its edges.

## The Fracture

The alarm was warranted, for the wave carried wreckage, and the Awakening's second act is the fight over it. The excesses had a name and a face: James Davenport, a Yale-trained minister who out-Whitefielded Whitefield into open mania — declaring ministers unconverted by name after private interrogation, leading crowds through the streets singing, and finally, at New London in 1743, presiding over a bonfire of religious books and — until the crowd balked — the participants' own fine clothing, including, in the famous detail, his own breeches. Davenport later published a retraction, but the damage crystallized opposition. Charles Chauncy of Boston's First Church, the Awakening's ablest enemy, answered Edwards point for point in Seasonable Thoughts (1743): the revival was not the Spirit but enthusiasm — heated imagination mistaken for grace — its fruits disorder, censoriousness, and the overthrow of reason in religion. The churches split into New Lights, who embraced the revival, and Old Lights, who condemned it (the Presbyterians into New Side and Old Side, formally schismed 1741–58); Connecticut criminalized itinerant preaching; Yale expelled a student, David Brainerd, for remarking that a tutor had "no more grace than a chair." The next lesson will map this fracture in full, because it never healed — it became a permanent fault line in American religion, the recurring quarrel between heart and order, revival and establishment, that runs from 1741 through the frontier camp meetings to the tent revivals and megachurches of living memory. For now, mark the honest historical point the fracture proves: the Awakening was not a unanimous blessing gratefully received; it was contested at the time, by serious Christians, on serious grounds, and the historian who tells it as pure descent of the Spirit is repeating one party's account — as is the historian who tells it as pure hysteria.

## What It Changed

Weigh, then, what actually changed, because the changes were structural and permanent. American Christianity's center of gravity moved: the new birth — personal, datable, experiential conversion — became the defining mark of the dominant stream of American Protestantism, the stream we now call evangelical, and the Awakening is as good a birthdate for American evangelicalism as history offers. Authority moved: once laypeople had been taught, by Tennent and the itinerants, to weigh their own ministers' spiritual state and to walk out on the finding, the deference on which the parish order rested was broken in principle; the religious marketplace — competing preachers, voluntary congregations, the believer as chooser — dates from here, and with it the growth of the Baptists (swelled by Separate congregations exiting the establishment) whose collision with state churches will drive the religious-liberty lessons ahead. Institutions moved: the revival party, needing converted ministers, built colleges — Princeton (1746) grew from the Log College men and called Edwards to its presidency; Brown, Rutgers, and Dartmouth followed from Baptist, Dutch Reformed, and New Light Congregationalist impulses — so that half the colonial colleges are the Awakening's children. The gospel crossed lines it had largely respected: the revivals brought the first mass evangelization of enslaved Africans, who heard, in the preaching of new birth and the worth of every soul, something their masters had not intended to send, and began forging the Black Christianity whose epic runs through this whole course; among Native peoples, Brainerd's mission and Edwards's Stockbridge years belong to the same impulse. And — the largest claim, handled with the care it needs — the Awakening rehearsed the Revolution. The thesis, associated with historians from Heimert onward, runs: a generation trained to judge established authority by higher standards, practiced in mass meetings and intercolonial coordination, equipped with a rhetoric of liberty against unconverted hierarchies, was being fitted, unawares, for 1776; the counter-case replies that the line is loose — Old Lights became patriots too, the war came a full generation later, and correlation is doing heavy work. The course's verdict, developed when the Black Regiment lessons arrive: the Awakening did not cause the Revolution, but it helped create the kind of people who could make one — a laity that had learned, in the deepest matter of all, that legitimacy comes from below and from above, and not from official position.

## The Measure of the Thing

Two closing measures, one quantitative and one not. Quantitatively, the Awakening's direct crest was brief — by 1744 the fires were banking, Whitefield's return tour met closed pulpits, and church admissions fell back — and skeptical historians (Jon Butler most famously, calling the very label an "interpretative fiction") remind us that the unified "Great Awakening" is partly a construction of later evangelical memory stitched over scattered local events. The caution is worth keeping; the deflation, most historians judge, overshoots — the intercolonial connections, the shared print, the institutional and denominational consequences were real and datable, whatever we call them. Qualitatively, the deepest change resists counting. The Awakening planted at the center of American culture a conviction that has never left it: that the individual person, however humble, stands in direct relation to God; that this relation is a matter of experienced transformation, not inherited status; and that no institution — parish, hierarchy, eventually crown — holds final title over the soul. That conviction built churches first. What else it would help build, the rest of this course narrates. But first, the fracture itself: the Old Lights and the New, and the fight over the revival that became a permanent American argument — next.
