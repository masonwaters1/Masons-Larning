The Great Awakening lesson ended on its fracture: the churches split over the revival, and the split never healed. This lesson maps the fracture properly, because it is one of those historical fault lines that looks like a period squabble and turns out to be permanent geology. The fight between the Old Lights and the New Lights — between those who condemned the revival as disorderly enthusiasm and those who embraced it as the work of God — reorganized colonial religion within a decade: it split denominations, founded colleges, criminalized preachers, filled the backcountry with new congregations, and manufactured, as an unintended byproduct, a mass constituency for religious liberty that would help write the First Amendment. And beneath the institutional story runs the deeper one: the Awakening forced American Protestantism to choose, publicly and permanently, between two understandings of what religion fundamentally is — settled order or transforming experience — and America's eventual answer to that question became part of what America is.

## The Two Parties

Draw the combatants precisely, because caricature serves neither.[^1] The Old Lights — Charles Chauncy of Boston's First Church at their head, with much of the Harvard faculty and the established clergy of eastern Massachusetts and Connecticut behind him — were not irreligious men defending comfort. Their case, argued in Chauncy's Seasonable Thoughts (1743) as a point-by-point answer to Edwards, was theological and civic at once: God is a God of order, religion's seat is the understanding and the will rather than the overheated passions, and the revival's fruits told against it — the faintings and outcries, the censorious judging of ministers as unconverted, the "exhorting" of laymen, women, and children, the division of hundreds of congregations, the bonfires at New London. Religion torn loose from order, they warned, would not stop at the meetinghouse door; a people taught to defy their ministers on the testimony of inner experience would learn to defy everything else. The New Lights answered that the Old Lights had mistaken the scaffolding for the building: order exists for life, not life for order; a settled, learned, unconverted ministry is precisely the "danger" Tennent had named; and the disorders, real as they were, were the splashing of a genuine flood — Edwards's whole literary labor, as earlier lessons showed, was to defend the flood while condemning the splash. Between the parties stood many moderates trying to hold churches together, and cutting across the line ran an irony worth marking: the Old Light demand for rational, orderly religion led a wing of that party, within two generations, out of orthodoxy altogether — Chauncy himself privately embraced universalism, and the liberal drift he began ended in New England Unitarianism — while the enthusiasts of 1741 became the guardians of orthodoxy. The party of order lost the creed; the party of fire kept it.

## The Institutional Earthquake

Now the visible damage and construction. Among Presbyterians the split was formal and fast: the Old Side synod expelled the revivalist New Brunswick Presbytery in 1741, and for seventeen years America's Presbyterians were two denominations — reuniting in 1758 on terms that were effectively a New Side victory, for by then the revival party had the numbers, the energy, and the college. That college was Princeton (1746), founded by New Side men from the Log College lineage expressly to train converted, learned ministers — the Awakening's institutional flagship, whose line of presidents (Dickinson, Burr, Edwards himself, and eventually Witherspoon, whom Unit 3 will meet as the founders' teacher) made it the seedbed of both evangelical religion and revolutionary politics. Connecticut's establishment chose repression: the Assembly's 1742 anti-itinerancy act stripped ministers who preached outside their parishes of legal support and ordered strange exhorters expelled as vagrants; New Light trustees were purged, and Yale's rector expelled the students who had tasted the revival — driving the movement's energy out of the establishment and into the arms of its future rivals. For the deepest consequence was congregational: perhaps a hundred or more "Separate" churches tore loose from the New England establishment in the 1740s and 50s — pure churches of the converted, done with parish taxes and unconverted clergy — and the Separates, finding infant baptism hard to square with a church of professed believers, drained steadily into the Baptist fold. The Baptists, a marginal sect in 1740, became by the Revolution the fastest-growing communion in America, especially in Virginia, where their unlicensed preachers filled the jails of the Anglican establishment in the 1760s and 70s. Thus the chain this course will follow into Unit 3: revival made Separates; Separates became Baptists; persecuted Baptists became the shock troops of disestablishment; and Isaac Backus and John Leland — Awakening men to the bone — would stand behind Jefferson and Madison when religious liberty was written into American fundamental law. The Old Lights had warned that revival would undermine establishment. They were exactly right, and it is among history's better jokes that the fulfillment of their warning is now counted an American glory.

## The Cost of Separating

Put faces on that chain, because the Separates paid for the future in the ordinary coin of persecution, and the payments were recorded. Under the New England establishment every householder owed taxes for the support of the parish minister; the Separates, having left the parish churches as unconverted, refused on conscience to fund them — and the collectors came. Elisha Paine, a Connecticut lawyer turned Separate preacher and one of the movement's most respected minds, was jailed repeatedly — once for months in Windham County — for preaching without the establishment's license; he noted acidly that the same colony that had prosecuted men a century earlier for not attending the standing churches now prosecuted him for attending too fervently elsewhere. In Norwich, the widow Elizabeth Backus, a woman of property and piety, was taken from her home on a winter night in 1752 and imprisoned for refusing the ministerial rate; her son Isaac — already a Separate preacher, soon a Baptist — carried the memory of his mother's arrest into a fifty-year career as colonial America's greatest advocate of religious liberty, hurling at Massachusetts the argument its own founders had hurled at England: that Christ's kingdom is not advanced by the magistrate's distraint warrants. Cows and household goods were seized and auctioned for church taxes across New England; in Virginia in the 1760s and 70s the pattern hardened further — Baptist preachers horsewhipped, dunked in mockery of their baptisms, and jailed by the dozens for preaching without license, some preaching on through the bars of the Culpeper and Fredericksburg jails to crowds outside, in scenes a young James Madison witnessed with lasting disgust. This is the biographical soil of the First Amendment: the men who would demand disestablishment in the founding era were not theorists of toleration reasoning in the abstract; they were, in remarkable numbers, the sons and cellmates of the Awakening's persecuted — and the establishment that jailed them manufactured, in every county courthouse, another generation of its own gravediggers.

## The Permanent Argument

Step back, finally, to what never got settled, because the Old Light–New Light fight is less an event than a recurring American weather system. The same argument — order against fire, learning against experience, settled institution against awakening — returned in the Second Great Awakening (Unit 4 will watch Yale's heirs fight Finney's "new measures"), in the fundamentalist-modernist wars, in every generation's quarrel between seminary and revival tent, mainline and evangelical, liturgy and praise chorus. And both parties keep being right in the ways they were right in 1743. The New Lights were right that religion which cannot convert is dead, and their heirs have owned American religious vitality ever since — the numbers, the energy, the expansion have belonged, in almost every era, to the experiential party. The Old Lights were right about the costs, and their prophecies read like a syllabus of later American religious history: the censorious judging of hearts, the splintering without end, the elevation of feeling over instruction, the demagogue in the pulpit whom crowds cannot distinguish from the man of God — every excess Chauncy catalogued has recurred on schedule in every awakening since. Edwards, as usual, occupied the position hardest to hold and most worth holding: fire tested by discernment, experience disciplined by truth — and the American church has spent three centuries oscillating around the balance he marked rather than resting on it. The Awakening's last bequest to the colonial era, though, was not ecclesiastical at all. A generation of Americans had now practiced, in the highest matters, the arts of mass mobilization, of judging authority by higher law, of hearing itinerant voices over official ones — and the pulpits that had learned to awaken souls were about to be asked to awaken something else. The clergy the Loyalists would bitterly nickname the Black Regiment were forming, and they are next.
