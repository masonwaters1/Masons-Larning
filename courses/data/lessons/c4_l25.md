The name was coined as a sneer. Peter Oliver, Massachusetts's last royal Chief Justice, writing his embittered Loyalist history of the "horrid rebellion," reserved special venom for the dissenting clergy in their black pulpit gowns — the "black Regiment," he called them, who had been "set to drumming the ecclesiastical drum" until the people were beaten into sedition. Strip the sneer and keep the observation, for Oliver was describing something real: by the eve of the Revolution, the American pulpit — above all the Congregational and Presbyterian pulpit of New England and the middle colonies — had become the most powerful instrument of political communication in America, and it had spent a generation teaching a theology of authority, resistance, and covenant that made the patriot argument feel, to ordinary churchgoing Americans, less like a novelty than like applied religion. This lesson watches the regiment form: the machinery of the political sermon, the theology it carried, the man who fired its most famous early shot, and — with the course's usual honesty — the real limits of the thesis that the pulpits made the Revolution.

## The Machinery of the Political Pulpit

Begin with sheer throughput, because the pulpit's power was first of all a matter of reach.[^1] In colonial New England the sermon was the mass medium: the settled minister preached twice each Sunday plus lectures and occasional days, and historians of the sermon have calculated that the average churchgoer heard on the order of seven thousand sermons in a lifetime — as one scholar put it, the only regular voice of authority most colonists ever heard. And the system had explicitly political genres built in. The election sermon, preached annually before the governor and legislature of Massachusetts and Connecticut since the 1630s, was a formal address on the nature and duties of government — a constitutional lecture from the clergy to the magistrates, printed at public expense and distributed through the towns, so that for a century before Lexington, New England's political theory was literally rehearsed from the pulpit once a year. Artillery sermons addressed the militia; fast and thanksgiving days, proclaimed by the assemblies in every crisis, turned the whole province into a single congregation contemplating public affairs under God — and in the imperial crisis the patriot leadership used them deliberately: when Boston's port was closed in 1774, days of fasting and prayer spread the outrage through every colony's churches. The clergy themselves were the most educated men in nearly every town, bound into intercolonial networks by college, correspondence, and print. When the crisis came, in other words, the patriots did not need to build a propaganda apparatus; a century-old one was standing in every village, staffed, trusted, and trained on exactly the relevant themes.

## The Theology of Resistance

What the machinery carried was a specific political theology, and its deep source is the covenant tradition of the earlier lessons. New England's founding theory held that all legitimate authority — congregational, civil — arises from covenant: rulers and people bound mutually under God, the ruler's authority conditional on his fidelity to the compact and to the higher law of God. From this root the clergy had long preached a doctrine with two edges: obedience to lawful authority as a divine duty — and the strict boundedness of that duty, since a ruler who breaks covenant and commands against God's law forfeits his claim; "we ought to obey God rather than men." The classic statement came a full generation before the Stamp Act. In January 1750, on the centennial anniversary of the execution of Charles I — a day Anglicans kept as the martyrdom of the royal saint — Boston's Jonathan Mayhew preached and published A Discourse Concerning Unlimited Submission and Non-Resistance to the Higher Powers, a sustained demolition of divine-right passive obedience. Romans 13, Mayhew argued, commands submission to rulers as ministers of God for good; it therefore cannot command submission to rulers who become the contrary — "when he turns tyrant... they are bound to throw off their allegiance"; unlimited submission would make God the patron of oppression, and resistance to a tyrant is not rebellion but lawful self-defense, indeed obedience. The sermon was reprinted for decades and read throughout the colonies and Britain; John Adams, looking back, said the Revolution's principles could be found in it, called it a catechism of the resistance, and dated the beginning of the quarrel from its themes. Through the imperial crisis the regiment preached these principles into every controversy — against the Stamp Act as an unconstitutional breach, against standing armies, and with special heat against the rumored plan for an American Anglican bishop, which New England's dissenting clergy read (with more alarm than the evidence warranted, historians judge) as the spearhead of ecclesiastical tyranny to follow the civil kind: a fusion of religious and political liberty in one cause that did as much as any tax to make resistance feel like a sacred duty.

## The Regiment and Its Limits

Now the calibrations that keep the thesis honest, because "the pulpits made the Revolution" overstates in three ways the course must mark. First, the regiment's genealogy was broader than the Awakening: Mayhew himself was an arch Old Light — a theological liberal drifting toward Arminian and unitarian views, despised by the revivalists — and the patriot clergy would draw from both parties of the previous lesson; the Awakening's contribution, as the Great Awakening lesson argued, was less a specific politics than a formed people — practiced in mass mobilization, in judging authority, in intercolonial identity — which both Old Light rationalists and New Light evangelicals then led into the crisis. Second, the pulpit was not monolithic: the Anglican clergy of the middle and southern colonies leaned heavily Loyalist, many praying for the king until the day the new governments forbade it and some into exile; Quakers and other peace churches refused the war outright; and the black-robed unanimity of Oliver's sneer was a regional truth about New England more than an American one. Third, the direction of influence ran both ways — clergy followed as well as formed their congregations, and the Revolution's causes were also constitutional, economic, and political in ways Units 3's lessons will weigh on their own terms. What survives the calibrations is nonetheless substantial and, by the testimony of both sides, decisive at the margin that mattered: the patriot cause reached ordinary Americans, week upon week, through the one voice they had been raised to trust, translated into the one idiom they all shared — covenant, providence, sin, and sacred liberty — so that when the Continental Congress proclaimed its fasts and the militias mustered to artillery sermons, resistance arrived in the churches not as innovation but as duty. Oliver's sneer and Adams's tribute agree on the fact and differ only in the valuation; the historian can hardly ask for better attestation than the agreement of enemies.

## Waiting for the Trumpet

The regiment formed in these years did its famous fighting after 1763, and this course will watch it in action in Unit 3 — Witherspoon at Princeton training half the founding generation, the preachers of 1776 turning Mayhew's doctrine into recruiting sermons, chaplains at the head of militia columns, and the muster-day scenes the Loyalists never forgave. But its formation belongs here, in the colonial story, as the last piece of the pattern this unit has assembled: a covenanted people, schooled by an awakening in judging authority and moving in mass, equipped with a century-old media system in every village, and catechized for a generation in a theology that made submission conditional and resistance, in the extremity, holy. No one had designed this as preparation for revolution — the election preachers of 1730 imagined nothing of the kind — but when the imperial crisis came, Britain would discover that it faced not merely aggrieved subjects but a people whose deepest weekly ritual had long taught them that rulers serve under covenant, and that there is a King above the king. What brought the crisis — the world war that doubled the empire, bankrupted the treasury, and removed the one enemy that had kept the colonies needing Britain — is the final lesson of the unit: the French and Indian War, next.
