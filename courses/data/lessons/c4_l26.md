The colonial era does not fade into the Revolution; it detonates into it, and the detonator was a world war that began with a twenty-two-year-old Virginian's blunder in a Pennsylvania ravine. The French and Indian War (1754–1763) — the American theater of what Europe calls the Seven Years' War, and what some historians fairly call the first true world war, fought on four continents and every ocean — ended with Britain master of North America and, within two years, at open quarrel with the colonies the victory had secured. The paradox is the unit's closing lesson: total victory dissolved the empire that won it. This lesson follows the war itself — including the young Washington's fiery baptism and the deliverance that a generation read as providential — and then traces, item by item, how the triumph of 1763 manufactured the crisis of 1765: the debt, the standing army, the western line, the mutual contempt between redcoat and provincial, and the removal of the French menace that had been, all along, the empire's invisible glue.

## Washington's Baptism

The war began, for practical purposes, with George Washington.[^1] In 1754 Virginia sent the young militia colonel to warn the French off the forks of the Ohio — the strategic junction where Pittsburgh now stands — and in May his party and their Indian allies ambushed a French detachment in a glen; the French commander, Jumonville, was killed in circumstances disputed ever since (Washington's Indian ally Tanaghrisson finishing the wounded man), and France had its casus belli. Weeks later Washington, forted up in the aptly named Fort Necessity in a soggy meadow, was surrounded and forced to surrender — signing, in French he could not read, articles admitting the "assassination" of Jumonville — and marched home defeated at twenty-two, having managed, as Horace Walpole put it, to set the world on fire with a volley in the backwoods. The next year delivered the catastrophe and the legend together. General Edward Braddock's column of regulars, cutting toward the forks with Washington as volunteer aide, was shattered on the Monongahela in July 1755 by a smaller French and Indian force — Braddock mortally wounded, some two-thirds of the British force killed or wounded, the survivors routed. Washington, sick with dysentery, rode through the slaughter rallying troops; two horses were shot from under him, four bullets passed through his coat and hat, and he emerged untouched. He wrote his brother that "the miraculous care of Providence" had protected him "beyond all human expectation"; fifteen years later an Indian chief who had fought that day reportedly told him that his warriors had singled him out, failed to bring him down, and concluded he was under the Great Spirit's protection. The Presbyterian preacher Samuel Davies, recruiting Virginians in 1755, pointed publicly to "that heroic youth, Colonel Washington, whom I cannot but hope Providence has hitherto preserved in so signal a manner for some important service to his country" — a sentence quoted ever after as the era's most startling prophecy. The course's method holds here as everywhere: the deliverance is fact, attested in the sources; the interpretation — Providence preparing an instrument — is the era's own reading and this course's sympathetic frame, offered as the participants held it, not as proof.

## From Disaster to Dominion

The war's first years ran all against Britain — frontier massacre, forts falling, the deportation of the Acadians (a brutality the course records as such) — until William Pitt took the war's direction in 1757 and chose to win America whatever it cost: unprecedented subsidies, oceans of credit, the navy strangling French reinforcement, and a strategy of continental conquest. The tide turned in 1758 (Louisbourg, Duquesne — renamed Pittsburgh); and 1759 became the annus mirabilis, the year of victories — Guadeloupe, Minden, Quiberon Bay, and above all Quebec, where in September General James Wolfe's men scaled the cliffs by night to the Plains of Abraham and won the fifteen-minute battle in which both Wolfe and the French commander Montcalm died — a scene the empire instantly canonized. Montreal fell in 1760; the Peace of Paris in 1763 gave Britain Canada and everything east of the Mississippi; and in the colonies the victory was received in explicitly providential and Protestant terms — the bells, the sermons, the thanksgivings hailing the fall of "New France" as the deliverance of Protestant liberty from popish encirclement, with New England's preachers reading the conquest through frames their grandsons would shortly redeploy against Britain herself. In 1763 the colonists were the proudest Britons alive. No one toasting the king that year imagined that the empire had twelve years to live in America; and yet every cause of its death was already present in the victory, as follows.

## The Victory That Unmade the Empire

Count the consequences, for the next unit stands on all five. First, the debt: Britain's national debt had nearly doubled, to a staggering £133 million, and the war's end left an American garrison costing hundreds of thousands a year; London's perfectly reasonable-sounding conclusion — the colonies the war had saved should help pay for their own defense — produced the Sugar Act, the Stamp Act, and the constitutional crisis of Unit 3, for the colonists had their own perfectly reasonable-sounding axiom: no taxation without representation, an old covenant principle wearing constitutional dress. Second, the standing army: ten thousand regulars remaining in peacetime America struck colonial minds — marinated, the previous lesson showed, in a century of sermons and Whig tracts against standing armies — as an instrument looking for a use. Third, the Proclamation Line of 1763: to pacify the interior after Pontiac's bloody rising, London drew a line down the Appalachians and forbade settlement beyond it — sensible imperial administration that lands-hungry colonists (Washington the surveyor-speculator among them, who privately dismissed it as a temporary expedient) received as a fence built against their future by their own government. Fourth, the schooling in contempt: the war had thrown provincials and regulars together, and each came away despising the other — British officers rating colonial troops as dirty, undisciplined mercenaries (and ranking every provincial officer beneath the newest regular captain, a humiliation Washington never forgot), while New England soldiers recorded their horror at the regulars' floggings and profanity and concluded that they, the covenanted volunteers, were the better men; thousands of future Continental soldiers and officers, Washington first, got their military education and their anti-British grievances in the same school. And fifth — the deepest, argued by contemporaries and historians alike — the removal of fear: so long as Catholic France held Canada, the colonies needed the Royal Navy and the redcoats, and knew it; with France expelled, the last compelling reason for dependence sailed home with the fleet. More than one European observer saw it at once — Choiseul, France's foreign minister, consoled himself that the loss of Canada would cost Britain America, and the Swedish traveler Peter Kalm had predicted years earlier that the colonies' union with Britain would last precisely as long as the French frontier threat. The war, in short, gave Britain an empire and took away every reason the colonists had to remain quiet inside it — while giving them, in the bargain, a trained officer corps, a practiced habit of intercolonial cooperation, a mountain of imperial grievances, and a tall Virginian whom Providence, said the preachers, had preserved for some important service to his country. The colonial story is complete; the Founding begins. Unit 3 opens with the intellectual world of the men who would make it.
