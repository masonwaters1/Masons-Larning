Unit 2 ended with an empire victorious and doomed; Unit 3 begins inside the minds that would replace it. Before the Declaration can be read as a moral document, before the Constitution's anthropology can be weighed, before the Christian-nation question can be answered honestly, one prior question governs everything: what was actually in the founders' heads? What had they read, memorized, argued over, and absorbed so deeply they no longer knew they were quoting it? The answer, recovered by two generations of scholarship, is not one tradition but five — five distinct streams of thought, each with its own vocabulary and heroes, flowing together in the founding generation's writing so thoroughly that a single Adams letter or Hamilton essay can shift among them in a paragraph. The founders were not systematic philosophers; they were working statesmen raiding a very well-stocked common library under fire. This lesson maps the library, because every lesson that follows — Witherspoon, the Declaration, the Constitution's checks, the religious settlement — draws on some blend of these five shelves, and the perennial modern quarrels about "what the founders believed" are mostly quarrels between people who have each read only one shelf.

## The Five Streams

The mapping owes most to Bernard Bailyn's reconstruction of the pamphlet literature and to the studies that followed it, and the streams run as follows.[^1] First, classical antiquity — above all the history of the Roman republic and its fall, read as the great cautionary tale of liberty lost to corruption and Caesarism: Plutarch's lives, Cicero, Sallust, Tacitus, absorbed at school and deployed everywhere (the pseudonyms tell the story — Publius, Brutus, Cato, Cincinnatus — and Washington self-consciously played the Roman, staging his life on Addison's play about Cato). Second, English common law and the constitutional inheritance — Coke and later Blackstone, Magna Carta, the rights of Englishmen, the whole seventeenth-century struggle of Parliament against Stuart absolutism: the colonists' first and most instinctive argument, through the entire imperial crisis, was not that they were inventing new rights but that they were defending old English ones. Third, the Enlightenment — Locke on natural rights, consent, and revolution above all, but flanked by Montesquieu on the separation of powers (the single most-cited secular author of the founding era), by the Scottish Enlightenment's moral-sense philosophy and common-sense realism (Hutcheson, Reid — imported wholesale, as the Witherspoon lesson will show, into the American colleges), and by Hume, whose hard-eyed science of politics Madison mined even while the divines denounced him. Fourth, the radical Whig or "Country" opposition tradition — Trenchard and Gordon's Cato's Letters, Bolingbroke, the pamphleteers who taught two generations of colonists their reflexive vigilance: that power always encroaches, that liberty is always besieged, that standing armies, placemen, and ministerial corruption are the standing symptoms of a conspiracy against freedom. Bailyn's great finding was that this fourth stream, more than high philosophy, supplied the lens through which colonists actually interpreted British conduct in the 1760s and 70s — the frame that made a threepenny tea duty legible as the thin edge of tyranny. And fifth — the stream this course has spent two units filling — Protestant Christianity, and specifically the covenant and dissenting tradition: the Bible saturating the culture (the next lesson measures how deeply), the New England political theology of conditional authority, the Black Regiment's century of election sermons, and the awakened populace of Unit 2. Modern tellings routinely omit this shelf; the founding generation could not have imagined doing so — when the Continental Congress wanted the colonies moved, it proclaimed fasts, and when Massachusetts wrote a constitution, a minister's convention debated it.

## How the Streams Mixed

The interesting question is not which stream was "the real one" — a question the sources refuse — but how minds held them together, because the streams do not obviously agree. Locke's contract-making individual is not Cicero's virtue-bound citizen; Calvin's depraved sinner is not the Enlightenment's improvable man; Hume thought the Whig conspiracy-lens paranoid; and the divines thought Hume an infidel. The founders' answer, mostly unconscious, was a working synthesis with recognizable joints. The classical and Whig streams fused easily — Rome's decline and Walpole's corruption told the same story of virtue lost — and together they supplied the founders' deepest political emotion: the fragility of republics, the certainty that liberty dies from internal rot as often as external conquest. The Protestant stream fused with both at the doctrine that mattered most: human nature is not to be trusted with unchecked power — Calvin's total depravity, the Whigs' encroaching power, and Tacitus's corrupted emperors converging on the single conviction that would build the Constitution's architecture of suspicion (the anthropology lesson ahead). Locke fused with the covenant tradition so smoothly that historians still argue where one ends and the other begins: government by consent, rulers bound by compact, a right of resistance when the compact is broken — New England had preached all of it, from its own sources, for a century before the Second Treatise crossed the water, which is why Lockean argument sounded to American congregations not like philosophy but like Thursday's sermon. And where the streams genuinely clashed — reason against revelation, the Enlightenment's optimism against the pulpit's realism — the founding generation mostly declined the war: the same men subscribed to church pews and to Enlightenment book lists, and the era's dominant intellectual style, Scottish common sense, was popular precisely because it promised that reason rightly used and Christianity rightly understood could not finally conflict. That truce, and its eventual breakdown, is one of the long stories of this course.

## The Library in One Man

To see the five shelves working in a single mind, watch James Madison prepare for Philadelphia. In the year before the Constitutional Convention, the thirty-five-year-old Virginian shut himself up with two trunks of books Jefferson shipped him from Paris and conducted what may be the most consequential literature review in political history. He worked through the ancient confederacies — the Amphictyonic and Achaean leagues, Greece's failed unions — filling notebooks on why they died; through the modern ones — the Dutch, the Swiss, the Germanic — cataloguing the same fatal flaw of central weakness; through Montesquieu on size and liberty, and Hume, whose heretical suggestion that a large republic might be more stable than a small one Madison would turn into Federalist 10's signature argument. Behind the reading stood his formation: a Princeton education under the Calvinist Witherspoon (three lessons hence), where he had studied Hebrew and divinity seriously enough that his family expected the ministry, and where he absorbed the Scottish realism and the Augustinian estimate of human nature that surfaces in his most famous sentence — "If men were angels, no government would be necessary." And behind that stood the Virginia Baptist jails of the previous unit, which the young Madison had visited in disgust, and which made religious liberty his first political cause. Classical failure-analysis, Enlightenment science of politics, common-law constitutionalism, Whig vigilance, and a Calvinist anthropology sharpened by watching an establishment persecute — every shelf of the library, load-bearing, in one working statesman. Multiply Madison by the founding cohort, with the proportions shuffled man by man, and you have the intellectual world this unit now enters.

## Reading the Founders Rightly

Draw the practical morals, because this map is the unit's working equipment. First: single-source explanations of the founding are always wrong. "The founding was Lockean liberalism" (the older textbook line), "the founding was classical republicanism" (the 1970s revision), "the founding was Christian" and "the founding was Enlightenment deism" (the culture war's twin banners) — each names a real stream and drowns the other four; the scholarship's settled verdict is the blend, with proportions varying by author, region, and decade. Second: expect the founders to sound like all five shelves, because they did — Jefferson's Declaration invokes nature's God, self-evident truths, the rights of Englishmen wronged, and a Whig indictment of conspiratorial tyranny in a single document, and the Continental Congress that adopted it also proclaimed a continental fast; the widespread modern instinct to sort founding documents into "religious" and "secular" piles imposes a filing system the eighteenth century did not use. Third — and here the course's own commitments are on the table — the Christian stream was neither the whole of the founding mind nor a decorative fringe on it: it was the most widely shared of the five, the one possessed by the ordinary people whose consent the whole project required, and the medium into which the other four had to be translated to move a congregational nation — which is why the next two lessons measure it directly, first in the founders' Bibles, then in their actual, varied, and sometimes surprising personal beliefs. The library is mapped; now open its most-thumbed volume.
