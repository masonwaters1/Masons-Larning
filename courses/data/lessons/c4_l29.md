The last lesson established the shared water; this one examines the individual swimmers, because on the question of the founders' personal religion the American public square has been fed two opposite mythologies for generations, and both dissolve on contact with the sources. The first mythology: the founders were, essentially to a man, devout orthodox Christians building a Christian government. The second: the founders were, essentially to a man, Enlightenment deists who regarded Christianity as superstition to be politely managed. The record — letters, diaries, church registers, wills, the testimony of families and pastors — supports neither. What it shows is a spectrum: a founding elite that ran from full Calvinist orthodoxy through warm churchly piety, through a distinctive middle position that scholars have labored to name, out to a small radical edge whose fate proves where the culture's boundaries lay. This lesson walks the spectrum with names attached, gives the hard cases the care they deserve, and draws the conclusion the evidence actually supports — which is more interesting, and more useful for the unit ahead, than either mythology.

## The Orthodox End

Start where the culture's center of gravity was, because the devout founders are the ones modern memory most reliably drops.[^1] John Witherspoon — signer of the Declaration, president of Princeton — was an ordained Calvinist minister and gets the next lesson entire. Roger Sherman of Connecticut, the only man to sign all four founding documents, was a rigorous Calvinist who wrote theology, debated fine points of doctrine with clergy, and was described by John Adams as "an old Puritan, as honest as an angel." Samuel Adams, the Revolution's great agitator, was a Congregational puritan of the old stamp who framed the whole struggle in covenantal terms. Patrick Henry, orator of the Revolution, was a fervent Anglican who paid personally to distribute apologetics and declared religion the great pillar of society. John Jay — first Chief Justice, Federalist co-author — was so orthodox he later presided over the American Bible Society; Elias Boudinot, president of the Continental Congress, wrote a book-length refutation of Paine and founded that society; Charles Carroll of Maryland, the Declaration's only Catholic signer and its longest-lived, staked his fortune — the largest in America — on a revolution led by Protestants who had lately despised his church, and credited his endurance to his faith. Benjamin Rush, signer and pioneering physician, mixed evangelical conviction with reformist zeal in equal measures. The roll could run much longer — the point is proportion: across the whole founding cohort of signers, framers, and officers, conventional Protestant belief and practice was the majority condition, and among the second tier — the state politicians, the militia colonels, the delegates history forgets — it was overwhelming. The famous heterodox names are famous partly because they were exceptional.

## The Middle: Theistic Rationalists and Reticent Churchmen

Now the interesting middle, where the era's most celebrated names cluster, and where precision matters most. Franklin, Adams, and Jefferson shared a recognizable position that was neither orthodoxy nor deism proper. The strict deist's God winds the clock and withdraws; these men believed in an active, governing Providence that hears prayer and superintends nations — Franklin's motion for prayers at the deadlocked Convention ("the longer I live, the more convincing proofs I see of this truth — that God governs in the affairs of men") is not a deist's sentence. All three revered Jesus as the supreme moral teacher; all three doubted or denied his deity, the Trinity, and the atonement; all three judged religion substantially by its moral fruits and expected a future state of rewards and punishments; and all three remained, in varying degrees, inside the churches' orbit — pew-holders, financial supporters, users of the liturgy's language — while privately editing the creed. Scholars have coined "theistic rationalism" for this blend of natural religion, selective Christianity, and providentialism; whatever the label, it must be distinguished on both sides — from orthodoxy, which it plainly was not (Jefferson's razor and his "Corruptions of Christianity" letters; Adams's cheerful unitarianism), and from the village-atheist deism into which polemics flatten it (Jefferson, who predicted every young man alive would die a Unitarian, expected Christianity purified, not abolished). Then the hardest case, because he made himself so: Washington. The external record is churchly — lifelong Anglican vestryman, regular attender, proclaimer of thanksgivings, insistent on chaplains and worship for his army, saturated in providential language ("the invisible hand" of the "Great Author" in the first inaugural). The internal record is a locked door: he avoided communion in his mature years (a fact his own pastors noted, one rebuking him from the pulpit for it), used "Providence" and "the Deity" where creedal men wrote "Christ" (whose name is almost absent from his vast correspondence), and declined, on his deathbed, to send for a minister. Scholars have made him everything from devout believer to closet skeptic; the honest verdict is that Washington practiced a disciplined public reverence and kept his private theology private with the same iron self-command he applied to everything — and that both the piety and the reticence are the data.

## The Radical Edge — and Its Fate

The spectrum's far end is instructive chiefly for what happened there. Thomas Paine, the Revolution's indispensable pamphleteer, published in the 1790s The Age of Reason — open, slashing deism, the Bible mocked chapter by chapter — and discovered where the American consensus lay: the man whose Common Sense had made independence thinkable died in 1809 shunned, denied burial in consecrated ground, his funeral attended by a handful; Sam Adams had written him in sorrow, Franklin (told of the manuscript's argument years earlier) had begged its author not to unchain the tiger, and even Jefferson, privately more sympathetic, would not associate publicly with the book. Ethan Allen's earlier deist tract had met the same wall. The lesson the edge teaches is double: real deists existed in founding America — and the culture's tolerance for private heterodoxy in gentlemen coexisted with a firm public boundary against open assault on Christianity, a boundary the elite themselves enforced. Heterodoxy whispered in letters; it did not campaign — with the one exception who proves the rule.

## What the Spectrum Means

Draw the conclusions the unit will build on. First, both mythologies fail on the same evidence: "all orthodox" cannot survive Jefferson's razor, Adams's letters, or Franklin's creed; "all deists" cannot survive Sherman, Jay, Witherspoon, Rush, Carroll — or the fate of Paine. Second, the distribution mattered more than the average: the heterodox cluster sat disproportionately at the top — in the drafting rooms and the diplomatic posts — while orthodoxy thickened at every level beneath, which is why founding documents could be latitudinarian in theology while founding culture remained emphatically Protestant; the men writing for the whole nation wrote, deliberately, in the broadest theistic language they shared with it. Third — and this is the finding that shapes everything ahead — the entire spectrum, orthodox to rationalist, agreed on one proposition with near unanimity: that religion was indispensable to republican virtue, and virtue to liberty. Washington's Farewell Address would call religion and morality the "indispensable supports" of political prosperity and pointedly doubt that national morality could survive without religious principle; Adams would insist the Constitution was made "only for a moral and religious people"; even Jefferson trembled for his country when he reflected that God is just. Men who could not have shared a communion rail shared that civic theology completely — and it, not any private creed, is the founders' actual consensus on religion, the ground on which the unit's coming battles (establishment, liberty, the Christian-nation question) were all fought. How that consensus was taught to the founding generation — by a Scottish Calvinist divine at a New Jersey college whose students included a president, a vice president, and half a government — is the next lesson: John Witherspoon.
