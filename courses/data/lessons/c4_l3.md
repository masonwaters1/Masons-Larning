Jerusalem gave the West its moral and spiritual foundation — the transcendent God, the covenant, the accountable ruler, the dignity of the person. But a foundation is not a building. To construct an actual government, you need more than moral conviction; you need political wisdom: knowledge of how power works, how it corrupts, how it can be divided and restrained, how free people can govern themselves without dissolving into either tyranny or chaos. That knowledge came from two other cities, and the American founders studied them with an intensity that is hard to imagine today. When they sat down to build a republic, they reached constantly for the lessons of Athens and Rome — the one for reason and the perils of democracy, the other for law, virtue, and the architecture of the republic. This lesson is about those two springs and what the founders drew from each.

## Athens: The Birth of Reason

The first and most fundamental gift of Athens was **philosophy** — and beneath it, the very idea of disciplined reason as a way of pursuing truth. Before the Greeks, the deepest questions about the world and human life were answered chiefly by myth, tradition, and priestly authority. The Greeks did something new: they began to subject those questions to systematic argument, to demand reasons, to follow the logic of an inquiry wherever it led, and to criticize even the most revered opinions in the light of rational scrutiny. This is the headwater of Western science, law, and political theory alike. The conviction that runs through the American founding — that political arrangements should be justified by reasoned argument, that one can sit down and *reason out* the best form of government from principles — is a Greek inheritance.

The emblem of this spirit is Socrates, who insisted that the unexamined life is not worth living and who pursued the truth through relentless questioning, exposing the confident ignorance of the powerful and the respectable. But Socrates is also the bearer of a darker lesson, and the founders did not miss it. Socrates was tried and executed — by the democracy of Athens, by a vote of his fellow citizens, for the crime, essentially, of unsettling them. The very city that invented the open pursuit of truth put its greatest philosopher to death by popular vote. That fact sits at the heart of the founders' ambivalence about democracy, and it brings us to Athens' second great lesson.

## Democracy and Its Discontents

Athens is celebrated as the birthplace of democracy, and so it was — a direct democracy in which the citizens themselves, assembled in person, debated and decided the affairs of the city. This was a genuine and inspiring achievement, the first large-scale experiment in collective self-rule. But the founders studied Athenian democracy at least as much for its *failures* as for its glories, and what they saw alarmed them.

Direct democracy, in the Athenian experience, proved dangerously volatile. The assembly could be swayed by skilled demagogues who flattered the people's passions; it could lurch from one extreme to another; it could condemn men to death in a fit of fear or anger and regret it the next week; it could vote for reckless wars and ruinous adventures on a wave of emotion. The execution of Socrates was one instance; the assembly's catastrophic decisions during the long war with Sparta were others. The lesson the founders drew was sharp and consequential: unchecked popular government — the direct, unfiltered rule of the majority's momentary passions — is not the cure for tyranny but merely another of its forms, the tyranny of the many. This is precisely why the founders were so insistent that they were building a **republic**, not a pure democracy — a system of representation, filtration, and restraint designed to capture the people's considered will while guarding against their passing passions. When they designed the Senate, the independent judiciary, and the elaborate checks of the Constitution, they were, in part, trying to avoid the fate of the Athenian assembly.

## Aristotle and the Science of Constitutions

If Socrates and the Athenian assembly supplied cautionary tales, it was Aristotle who supplied the founders with something like a political science. In his *Politics*, Aristotle did what no one had done so systematically before: he gathered and compared the constitutions of many city-states and analyzed, almost like a naturalist, how governments are structured, how they function, and how they decay.[^1]

His framework became part of the founders' mental furniture. Aristotle classified governments by who rules and in whose interest. Rule by one, by a few, or by the many could each take a *good* form, governing for the common good — kingship, aristocracy, and what he called polity — or could degenerate into a *corrupt* form, governing for the rulers' selfish interest — tyranny, oligarchy, and mob rule. Crucially, Aristotle observed that each pure form tends to decay into its corrupt twin, and that history shows a recurring cycle of rise and degeneration. His prescription against this instability pointed toward two ideas the founders prized above almost all others: the **rule of law** rather than the rule of men — the principle that fixed, known laws should govern, not the arbitrary will of whoever holds power — and the **mixed constitution**, which blends elements of the different forms so that each checks the others, producing a stability none can achieve alone. Aristotle also argued that a large, stable middle class was the surest foundation for good government, a thought that would echo in the founders' vision of a republic of independent property-holders.

## Rome: The Republic and the Rule of Law

If Athens gave the founders reason and a warning, Rome gave them their working model. The Roman Republic — the centuries before the Caesars, when Rome governed itself without a king — was the founders' great historical exemplar, the proof that a free, self-governing republic could endure and grow powerful. They named their upper house the Senate after Rome's; they wrote under Roman pen names; they built their capital in a Roman architectural idiom; they saw themselves, consciously, as heirs of the Roman republican tradition.

Two Roman achievements mattered most. The first was the **mixed constitution**, analyzed by the Greek historian Polybius, who admired Rome and sought to explain its astonishing success.[^2] Polybius argued that Rome's stability came from balancing three elements within a single government: a monarchic element in the consuls who held executive power, an aristocratic element in the Senate, and a democratic element in the popular assemblies. Each restrained the others; no single part could dominate; ambition was made to counteract ambition. This is the direct ancestor of the American system of separated powers and checks and balances. When the founders divided the federal government into executive, legislative, and judicial branches and set them against one another, and when they split the legislature itself into two chambers, they were applying the Polybian analysis of why Rome's mixed government had endured.

The second Roman achievement was the towering edifice of **law**. Rome's gift to the West was the conviction, embodied in centuries of legal development from the Twelve Tables onward, that society should be governed by settled, impartial, publicly known law — that even the powerful are bound by it, and that the state itself is a *res publica*, a "public thing" belonging to the people rather than the private possession of a ruler. The very word *republic* descends from that phrase. The American reverence for a "government of laws, not of men" is Roman to its core.

## Virtue and the Citizen

Rome also bequeathed an ideal of character without which, the founders believed, no republic could survive: **civic virtue** — the willingness of citizens to subordinate their private interest to the common good, to serve the republic, to sacrifice for it, and, above all, to relinquish power rather than cling to it. The Romans embodied this ideal in their legendary heroes, and one mattered to the founders beyond all others: Cincinnatus, the farmer who, according to the cherished tradition, was summoned from his plow to take supreme command in a crisis, defeated Rome's enemies, and then — instead of using his power to make himself a tyrant — laid it down and returned to his farm. The relinquishing of power was the supreme republican virtue.

This was not abstract for the founding generation. George Washington was hailed, in his own time and by his own intention, as the American Cincinnatus — the general who, having won the war, astonished the world by resigning his commission and returning to private life rather than seizing power, and who later stepped down from the presidency after two terms. That single act of voluntary surrender did more to establish the American republic than almost any law, and it was performed in conscious imitation of the Roman ideal. The founders understood, from Rome, that institutions alone cannot save a republic; it also requires citizens and leaders of a certain character, and the cultivation of that virtue was, to them, a matter of life and death for free government.

## Cicero and the Higher Law

There is one more Roman gift, and it reaches back to join the inheritance from Jerusalem. The statesman and philosopher Cicero gave the West its most influential classical statement of **natural law** — the idea that above all the particular laws enacted by particular states there stands a true, higher law: right reason in harmony with nature, universal and unchanging, the same in Rome and in Athens, binding on all peoples and all rulers, and not subject to repeal by any senate or king.[^3] An unjust human law, on this view, is not truly law at all, because it violates the higher law that human reason can discern.

Here the springs converge. The Hebrew prophets had proclaimed a moral law above the king, grounded in the will of God. Cicero, reasoning philosophically, arrived at a higher law above all human law, grounded in nature and accessible to reason. These two streams — the revealed higher law of Jerusalem and the rational natural law of Rome — would flow together through the centuries and emerge, transformed, in the most famous sentence of the American founding: the appeal in the Declaration of Independence to "the Laws of Nature and of Nature's God," and to truths held to be self-evident. When Jefferson grounded the right to revolution in a law higher than the British Parliament, he was drawing on both Jerusalem and Cicero at once.

## The Classical Education of the Founders

To grasp how directly Athens and Rome shaped the American founding, it helps to see how thoroughly the classical world lived in the founders' own minds. The leading men of the revolutionary generation were schooled, from boyhood, in Latin and often Greek; entrance to colleges like Harvard, Yale, and William and Mary required the ability to read the classical authors in the original. They did not encounter Cicero, Tacitus, Livy, and Plutarch as distant figures but as familiar companions, quoted from memory and invoked as living authorities in their letters, speeches, and debates.

The single most influential of these texts may have been Plutarch's *Lives*, the great series of paired biographies of noble Greeks and Romans, which functioned as a vast schoolbook of character — a gallery of exemplary statesmen, patriots, and tyrants from which a young man learned what virtue and corruption looked like in action. The founders measured themselves and one another against these ancient models. Their fondness for the republican hero is everywhere: Washington's favorite play was Addison's *Cato*, a drama celebrating the Roman senator who chose death over submission to Caesar's tyranny, and he had it performed for his freezing troops at Valley Forge to steel their resolve. When the authors of the Constitution's defense wrote their essays, they signed them *Publius*, after the founder of the Roman Republic; their opponents answered as *Cato* and *Brutus*, the names of Rome's great defenders of liberty against tyranny. The veterans of the Revolution who formed a society to preserve its memory named themselves the Society of the Cincinnati, after the farmer-hero who relinquished power. This was not antiquarian decoration. The founders reached for these names because they understood their own enterprise as a continuation of the ancient struggle for self-government, and they wanted to be worthy of the company they were claiming to keep.

## The Cautionary Tale of Rome's Fall

The founders learned from Rome's triumph, but they were perhaps even more obsessed with Rome's *fall* — with how the great republic decayed into the dictatorship of the Caesars. For they did not regard their own republic as safe; they regarded it as fragile, and Rome was the autopsy they studied to learn how republics die.

The lessons they drew shaped the Constitution's anxieties. The republic fell, they believed, through a combination of forces they were determined to guard against: the corruption of civic virtue as wealth and luxury grew; the widening inequality that set rich against poor and bred faction; the rise of ambitious generals commanding armies loyal to themselves rather than to the republic; the standing armies that became instruments of personal power; and finally the willingness of a weary people to trade their liberty for the security and spectacle a strongman offered. This is the source of the founders' deep fear of standing armies, their worry about executive ambition, their obsession with virtue and corruption, and their conviction that liberty, once won, must be jealously and perpetually guarded. They built their republic in the shadow of Rome's tomb, hoping to escape its fate.

## How the Founders Fused the Inheritance — and Its Limits

The founders did not simply copy the ancients; they studied, selected, and adapted. From Athens they took reason and a warning against unchecked democracy. From Rome they took the mixed constitution, the rule of law, the ideal of civic virtue, and the natural-law tradition — and a vivid fear of republican decay. Then they fused all of it with the Hebrew inheritance of human dignity and covenant, and, as we will see, with the English constitutional tradition, into something genuinely new.

Honesty requires marking the limits and the tensions in this borrowing. The classical republics the founders admired rested on slavery and excluded the great majority of people — women, slaves, foreigners — from citizenship altogether; classical "virtue" was often martial, aristocratic, and exclusive, a far cry from the universal dignity of Jerusalem. There was real tension between the two inheritances: the classical ideal of the heroic citizen of a small, homogeneous city did not obviously translate to a vast, diverse continental nation, and many doubted a republic could survive at such a scale at all — a doubt the founders would have to answer with a genuine innovation of their own, the extended republic, which we will examine in due course. And the founders, like the ancients they revered, tolerated in their own republic the very slavery that exposed the gap between their professed ideals and their practice. The inheritance from Athens and Rome was indispensable, but it was incomplete, and parts of it sat uneasily beside the moral vision of Jerusalem. The American achievement would lie partly in fusing these inheritances — and the American tragedy partly in failing, for generations, to extend their best principles to all.

With reason, virtue, and the republic now in hand alongside the moral foundation of Jerusalem, two springs remain before we reach America's own shores: the English constitutional tradition that taught the practical habits of ordered liberty, and the Reformation that would radicalize everything it touched. If Jerusalem supplied the soul of the American order and Athens and Rome supplied its political architecture, it was England that would supply its working machinery — the courts, the charters, the rights, and the stubborn habit of self-rule — and the Reformation that would charge the whole inheritance with a new and explosive energy. To London we turn next.
