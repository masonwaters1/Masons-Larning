Witherspoon showed the pulpit taking a seat in Congress; this lesson shows the pew taking charge of the Revolution's politics — because the two most effective popular agitators of the entire independence movement, the men who moved assemblies and mobs and made rebellion feel like duty, were both devout, doctrinally serious Protestant laymen whose politics grew directly out of their religion. Samuel Adams of Boston and Patrick Henry of Virginia operated a thousand miles apart, in opposite religious establishments, with opposite temperaments — the one a grey, frugal, tireless committee-man, the other a flame-tongued courtroom orator — and they arrived at the same place by the same road: a Protestant understanding of liberty as a sacred trust, tyranny as a species of sin, and resistance as obedience to God. Around them stood a wider company of believing patriots this lesson gathers at the end. Together they answer, from the lay side, the question the Witherspoon lesson answered from the clerical: whether the Revolution's Christianity was ornament or engine.

## The Last of the Puritans

Samuel Adams was called "the last of the Puritans" by contemporaries, and the label was analysis, not decoration.[^1] Born in 1722 to a pious Boston family (his father a deacon and political organizer before him), trained at Harvard where he argued in his master's thesis that it is lawful to resist the supreme magistrate when the commonwealth cannot otherwise be preserved, Adams was a Congregationalist of old-covenant conviction: family devotions, strict sabbaths, psalm-singing (he had a fine voice and used it), a personal austerity so complete that his political enemies could never find the handle of bribery or vanity on him — Hutchinson, the royal governor he destroyed, complained that Adams was of such "obstinate and inflexible disposition" that no office could buy him. His political genius was organizational: the Boston town meeting as his instrument, the newspapers under a dozen pseudonyms, the Committees of Correspondence (his invention, 1772) knitting the towns and then the colonies into a single nervous system — and through all of it ran, uncontrived, the covenant frame of Unit 2: Britain's measures as violations of compact; liberty as an inheritance held in trust from God; public virtue as the republic's life-blood and luxury its disease; New England's cause as the cause of the fathers who had crossed the ocean for a free church and a free state. His 1772 Rights of the Colonists, drafted for the Boston committee, grounded colonial rights in a triple title — as men, as Christians, and as subjects — with the Christian title resting on "the institutes of the great Law Giver... which are to be found clearly written and promulgated in the New Testament." He pushed fast days, mourned the theater, fretted over the morals of the army, and dreamed aloud of Boston as a "Christian Sparta" — a phrase that captures the whole man: republican virtue and Puritan piety fused past separating. When the news of Lexington reached him, hiding with Hancock, his recorded exclamation was not a war cry but a doxology: "Oh! what a glorious morning is this."

## The Trumpet of Virginia

Patrick Henry worked the other establishment. Raised in Hanover County under a double formation — his father and uncle staunch Church-of-England men, his mother carrying him to hear Samuel Davies, the great Presbyterian evangelist of the Virginia awakening, whose oratory Henry later called the greatest he ever heard — Henry fused Anglican loyalty with dissenting fire, and his career began at the exact junction of religion and politics: the Parsons' Cause of 1763, where the young lawyer, arguing against Anglican clergy suing for back pay, electrified a courtroom with the doctrine that a king who annuls good laws forfeits the obedience of his people (cries of "treason" then, too), and made his name. The Stamp Act resolves of 1765 ("Caesar had his Brutus..."), and then, in St. John's Church, Richmond, in March 1775, the speech every American child once memorized — the war-or-slavery alternatives, the appeal "to the God of hosts," the argument that "the battle, sir, is not to the strong alone; it is to the vigilant, the active, the brave," with "a just God who presides over the destinies of nations" raising up friends for the righteous cause, and the close that needs no quoting. (The course's honesty notes what schoolbooks don't: the text we have was reconstructed decades later by Henry's biographer from hearers' memories — the substance well attested, the exact words a monument more than a transcript.) Henry's faith was not rhetorical costume: he read Doddridge and Butler, paid from his own pocket to print and distribute Soame Jenyns's defense of Christianity against the deists, declared in his last years that the religion of Christ "is a religion of peace, love and universal philanthropy" and worried in his will that if his heirs lacked it, having everything else, they had nothing. And his religion drew his politics' boundaries in both directions the course must record: the same providential Protestantism that armed his revolution made him, later, a champion of state support for religion — his 1784 general-assessment bill for teachers of the Christian religion is the measure Madison's Memorial and Remonstrance was written to kill (the disestablishment lesson ahead) — and the most formidable Anti-Federalist in America, who fought the Constitution partly because it never named God and left religion, he thought, dangerously unsupported. The Christian patriots, this is to say, did not emerge from their faith with one politics; the Revolution they shared, the settlement they contested — a fault line Unit 3 will keep meeting.

## The Wider Company

Around the two chiefs, sketch the company briefly, because the pattern is the argument. John Hancock, the merchant-prince president of Congress, was a deacon's grandson and Boston's great church patron, whose proclamations as governor ring with covenant piety. Roger Sherman and John Jay and Elias Boudinot the course met on the orthodox end of the founders' spectrum; add Henry Laurens of South Carolina, president of Congress, a devout Huguenot-descended churchman who anguished over slavery in his letters while profiting from it in his business — the era's contradiction in one conscience; John Dickinson, the "Penman of the Revolution," Quaker-formed, whose scruples kept him from signing the Declaration he had done as much as any man to make arguable — proof that serious faith could counsel caution as well as revolution; and Abigail Adams, the minister's daughter whose letters run on providence and scripture, and whose husband — heterodox in doctrine, Puritan in marrow — insisted all his life that the Revolution's real change had been "in the minds and hearts of the people," a change of "religious sentiments of their duties and obligations." The pattern: at every level of the movement's leadership below (and often beside) the famous rationalist elite, the Revolution was led, funded, argued, and organized by men and women for whom Protestant Christianity was operative conviction — the frame within which British measures registered as not merely unwise but wicked, and resistance as not merely permissible but bound. Two calibrations keep the claim honest. Faith did not sort the sides cleanly: devout Loyalists matched devout patriots text for text — Romans 13 against Mayhew's gloss on it — and the peace churches sat out on principle; belief armed both armies, as it usually does. And the Christian patriots allied without embarrassment with the heterodox — Adams with Franklin, Henry with Jefferson against the assessment's enemies and then bitterly over the Constitution — because the civic theology of the founders' spectrum lesson gave them common ground their creeds did not. But the claim itself stands and matters: strip the believing patriots from the Revolution and there is no Revolution — no Boston machine, no Virginia fire, no mobilized pews. The pulpits that had formed such a people were, meanwhile, doing more than forming them; when the war came, the Black Regiment marched. That is the next lesson.
