Unit 2 watched the Black Regiment form — the patriot clergy Peter Oliver blamed for drumming a people into rebellion. This lesson watches it fight. When the war came, the American pulpit did not retire to the spiritual sidelines; it recruited, marched, chaplained, financed, suffered reprisal, and interpreted the whole struggle, week upon week, as a sacred cause under Providence — while the Loyalist pulpit, with equal conviction and opposite texts, was silenced, exiled, and broken. The lesson gathers the famous scenes — Muhlenberg's robe, Caldwell's hymnbooks — with the honesty about sources this course practices, counts the machinery (the chaplaincies, the fast days, the sermons that functioned as the era's war journalism), and closes on the two-sided truth the era itself understood: the Revolution was preached into being and preached through to victory, and it was also, for American religion, a war in which Christians shot at Christians while both invoked the same God — a fact the founders remembered when they built the peace.

## A Time to Fight

The emblematic scene belongs to Peter Muhlenberg, and it must be told with its footnote attached.[^1] Muhlenberg was Lutheran royalty — eldest son of Henry Melchior Muhlenberg, patriarch of American Lutheranism — pastoring in Woodstock, in Virginia's Shenandoah valley, and already a colonel by Washington's and Henry's urging. In January 1776, the story runs, he preached a farewell sermon on Ecclesiastes 3 — to every thing there is a season — declared that there is "a time to preach and a time to fight, and now is the time to fight," threw off his clerical robe to reveal a Continental uniform, and had the drums beat for recruits at the church door; some three hundred men of his congregation and county enlisted, the core of the 8th Virginia, the "German Regiment." The dramatic robe-drop rests on family memoir written long after (his grandnephew's biography), and historians flag the embellishment; what stands beyond dispute is the substance the legend compresses: the pastor did leave the pulpit for command in the winter of 1776, did raise his regiment substantially from his own religious community, and rose by war's end to major general — his own brother Frederick, a New York pastor, first condemning the step ("you should not have gone further than the pulpit") and then, after the British burned his church district, following into politics, where he became the first Speaker of the House. The Muhlenberg brothers are the Black Regiment's arc in one family: from sermon to sword to statehouse.

## Chaplains, Hymnbooks, and the Machinery of Sacred War

Behind the emblem stood a system. The Continental Army carried chaplains from its first establishment — Washington, who ordered divine service, punished profanity in general orders, and requisitioned chaplains at pay he fought Congress to raise, regarded them as instruments of discipline and morale in an army whose men had been raised in Unit 2's pews; over two hundred clergymen served as army and militia chaplains through the war, and dozens more irregularly. Some fought outright. James Caldwell of Elizabethtown, New Jersey — the "Fighting Parson," so hated that Loyalists put a price on him and he preached with pistols on the pulpit cushion — served as chaplain and commissary; at the battle of Springfield in June 1780, with wadding running short, he famously ran to the Presbyterian church for Isaac Watts's psalm books and dealt them out to the muskets crying "Give 'em Watts, boys!" — a scene attested in the era's own lore and beloved because it is the whole alliance in one image: the hymnal literally rammed down the gun. Caldwell's war exacted the full price: his wife Hannah was shot dead through a window by a British soldier that same month, and Caldwell himself was killed by a sentry the next year, leaving nine orphans the new nation helped raise. Beyond the chaplaincies, the pulpits ran the war's information and morale system as they had run the crisis's: the Declaration was, by Congress's design, proclaimed to most Americans from pulpits and at meetinghouse doors; congressional fast and thanksgiving days — a dozen and more across the war — turned every congregation into a national assembly at the government's request; and the sermon remained the medium through which victories were interpreted (Saratoga and Yorktown as providences), defeats absorbed (as chastisements calling for repentance, in the old jeremiad frame), and the cause continually re-sanctified: liberty as God's gift, the war as defensive and just, America as a people in covenant whose arms would prosper as their virtue held. Historians who count such things find the New England clergy delivering, over the war years, what amounted to more than two thousand discourses a week, overwhelmingly patriot — a propaganda capacity, if the cold word is wanted, that the Continental cause could never have built and never had to.

## The Other Regiment, and the Price of Both

Honesty now requires the mirror. The Anglican clergy of the middle and southern colonies, and the Society for the Propagation of the Gospel missionaries in New England, were bound by ordination oaths to pray for the king — and most would not break the oath. Their spokesmen argued Romans 13 with entire sincerity against Mayhew's gloss: Jonathan Boucher of Maryland, who preached his last years in America with loaded pistols on the cushion (the mirror of Caldwell) and left in 1775, published from exile the era's ablest Loyalist political theology — obedience as the Christian's duty, the rebellion as sin. For fidelity to their oaths the Loyalist clergy paid what the patriot clergy paid for treason to theirs: churches closed or burned, missionaries mobbed, tarred, imprisoned, and exiled by the hundreds; the SPG network in New England effectively destroyed; and after the war the Anglican establishment in America lay in ruins — a collapse that cleared the ground, as the disestablishment lessons will show, for the religious settlement to come. British forces, for their part, singled out dissenting meetinghouses — "sedition shops," one officer called them — for stabling, burning, and desecration, and patriot clergy captured (like New Jersey's John Rosbrugh, bayoneted at Trenton while pleading as a chaplain) died in the war they had blessed. Set the two regiments side by side and the era's hardest religious fact emerges: this was, among other things, a war within Protestant Christianity, in which both sides fasted to the same God, cited the same Scriptures, and buried clergy — and the generation that lived it drew a conclusion the course will watch them write into law: that the union of political and ecclesiastical power, whoever holds it, ends with someone's churches burning.

## What the Fighting Pulpit Wrought

Tally the regiment's war, then, in the double entry this course keeps. On the credit side, by the testimony of friend and enemy alike, the patriot clergy were indispensable: they supplied the cause's moral vocabulary, its recruiting sergeants, its morale system, and its interpretation of events — Ambrose Serle, secretary to the British commander, wrote home that the Presbyterian clergy were the war's great engine, and historians from that day to this have agreed that without the pulpits the Revolution could not have been sustained through its terrible middle years. On the debit side: the sanctification of a war is a dangerous art — the jeremiad frame that steadied morale also taught Americans to read national causes as God's causes, an equation that would bless much (abolition's crusade ahead) and curse much (every later war has had its Caldwells on both sides); and the intra-Protestant bloodletting left scars and lessons in equal measure. The regiment itself came out of the war with unmatched moral capital — the churches, nearly alone among colonial institutions, emerged from the Revolution stronger in public standing — and spent that capital shaping the settlement the rest of this unit narrates. The war the preachers blessed still had to be declared, argued, and won on paper before it was won in the field; the document that did it, and what it actually says about God, nature, and rights, is the Declaration — next.
