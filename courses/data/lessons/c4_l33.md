The war was a year old before the argument was finished. Through the spring of 1776 the pulpits preached, the pamphlets flew (Common Sense had sold its staggering hundred-plus thousand by spring), and the colonies, one by one, instructed their delegates; and when Richard Henry Lee's resolution for independence came to the floor in June, Congress appointed five men to draft a public justification and the five gave the pen to the thirty-three-year-old Virginian with the reputation for felicity. What Jefferson produced — cut, tightened, and in two places significantly amended by Franklin, Adams, and the full Congress — was ostensibly a press release for a fait accompli: an announcement, with reasons, that the colonies were leaving. What it became is the most consequential moral document in the history of politics: a fifty-five-word philosophical preamble that has outlived its occasion by two and a half centuries, been quoted against its own authors, armed every subsequent American reform, and forced a nation to spend its whole history either honoring or explaining a single sentence. This lesson reads the Declaration as what it is — a moral and, in a precise sense, theological document — including the passage Congress cut, which is the era's whole contradiction preserved in a deletion.

## The Architecture of the Argument

Read structurally, the Declaration is a syllogism with an evidence file attached.[^1] The preamble states the major premise — a theory of what governments are for: all men are created equal and endowed by their Creator with unalienable rights; governments exist to secure those rights and draw their just powers from consent; when a government becomes destructive of those ends, the people may alter or abolish it — though prudence (the Whig realism of the founders' library) counsels suffering evils while sufferable, so revolution requires "a long train of abuses" evidencing a design of despotism. The body supplies the minor premise: twenty-seven grievances against George III — note, against the king, not Parliament, a deliberate legal strategy, since the colonists had spent a decade denying Parliament's authority altogether and now severed the last tie, allegiance to the crown, by indicting its wearer as a tyrant. The conclusion follows: these colonies are, and of right ought to be, free and independent states. The genius is in where the argument grounds itself. Rights come from the Creator — not from government, which merely secures them and therefore cannot legitimately revoke them; not from English tradition, which an English Parliament could redefine; not from the sovereign's grant, which the sovereign could withdraw. Jefferson's draft opened with "Nature's God"; Congress, revising, added two more references — the appeal to "the Supreme Judge of the world for the rectitude of our intentions," and the reliance on "the protection of divine Providence" — so that the document as adopted invokes God four times, as lawgiver (nature's God), creator and endower (of rights), judge (of the appeal), and protector (of the cause): deliberately broad theistic language, as the founders'-spectrum lesson would predict, in which the Deist and the Calvinist could both say amen — and both did, which was the point. Jefferson later described his aim exactly: not to invent new principles but to state "the common sense of the subject," an "expression of the American mind" harmonizing "the sentiments of the day, whether expressed in conversation, in letters, printed essays, or in the elementary books of public right." The five shelves of the founders' library, in other words, compressed to a page — Locke's rights and consent, the Whig long-train, the covenant tradition's conditional authority (congregations had heard this doctrine, minus the signatures, for a century), under the adjudicating God of the era's shared theism.

## The Paragraph That Fell

Now the deletion, because the course's charter lives in it. Jefferson's draft contained, as the climactic grievance, a furious paragraph on slavery: the king "has waged cruel war against human nature itself, violating its most sacred rights of life and liberty in the persons of a distant people who never offended him, captivating and carrying them into slavery in another hemisphere... this piratical warfare, the opprobrium of infidel powers, is the warfare of the Christian king of Great Britain" — and, compounding, that he now incited those same slaves to rise against the colonists. Congress struck it. Jefferson recorded why: South Carolina and Georgia would not abide censure of the trade, and Northern shipping interests "felt a little tender" too, having carried it. Read honestly, the deleted paragraph is double all the way down. It is morally piercing — the enslaved are "a distant people who never offended," their enslavement a violation of "sacred rights of life and liberty," the trade a war against human nature: the Declaration's own premises, applied — and it is evasive, laundering America's domestic institution into a crime committed by George III against the colonists' better nature, written by a man who owned more than a hundred human beings and would free almost none. And the deletion itself is the era in miniature: the founding generation saw the contradiction — saw it clearly enough to draft it, debate it, and find it unbearable to publish — and chose the union with slavery over the argument without it, a choice the Constitution's compromise lesson will meet again and the nation would finance, four score and seven years on, in blood. What survived into the adopted text, though, was the premise the deleted paragraph had merely applied — and premises, once published, do their own work.

## The Promissory Note

For that is the Declaration's real career: the preamble outgrew the document. In 1776 "all men are created equal" functioned as a premise about political legitimacy — why these colonists owed George III nothing. But the sentence does not say "all Englishmen" or "all colonists," and Americans excluded from its benefits began quoting it back almost immediately: enslaved petitioners in Massachusetts within months ("we have in common with all other men a natural and unalienable right to that freedom, which the great Parent of the universe hath bestowed"); free Black petitioners and pamphleteers citing the "self-evident" truths against the slave codes; the first women's-rights convention at Seneca Falls in 1848 re-drafting the document itself ("all men and women are created equal"); and above all the abolitionists and Lincoln, who called the sentence "a standard maxim for free society... constantly labored for, and even though never perfectly attained, constantly approximated" — an "abstract truth, applicable to all men and all times," planted by the founders, he argued, precisely as a stumbling block to tyranny for ages to come; Frederick Douglass, in the greatest Fourth-of-July oration ever delivered (this course reaches it in Unit 4), would wield the Declaration against the nation that ignored it while insisting — against the Garrisonians who burned the Constitution — that the founding's principles were freedom's arsenal, not slavery's charter. The metaphor that stuck is King's: the architects had signed a promissory note to which every American was to fall heir; the movement had come to cash the check. That is the mature reading this course adopts, and it requires holding both truths the sentimental and cynical tellings each drop: the men who published the principle did not live it — many held slaves, all tolerated the compromise, and "created equal" coexisted for generations with chattel bondage, which convicts the founders by their own document; and the principle they published was real, radical, and consequential — the standing indictment that made American slavery permanently indefensible in America's own terms, the lever every reformer since has pulled. A nation that had merely practiced its injustices would have nothing to be measured against; a nation that proclaimed that sentence measured itself. The proclamation now had to survive a war whose winning was, by any sober military accounting, wildly improbable — and the war is next.
