The Declaration was a wager that had to be collected at bayonet point, and the sober facts of 1776 said it could not be. Great Britain was the superpower of the age — the largest navy on earth, a professional army seasoned by the world war of Unit 2's close, the credit of the City of London — and it deployed against the rebellion the largest expeditionary force it had ever sent anywhere. The colonies had no navy, no treasury, no manufacturing base, an army of short-term enlistees that repeatedly dissolved in Washington's hands, and a population a third of which (by John Adams's rough famous arithmetic) was lukewarm and a good part actively Loyalist. That the war ended seven years later with a British army playing "The World Turned Upside Down" — the tune tradition assigns to the Yorktown surrender — is a story the participants themselves could only explain in the vocabulary this course has been tracing; Washington, who watched every improbability from the inside, told a correspondent that the man who could review the war's turnings and not acknowledge the hand of Providence was "worse than an infidel." This lesson compresses the war into its hinges — and keeps the course's double ledger, because the war for liberty was also a civil war, a frontier catastrophe, and a crucible in which enslaved Americans made their own calculations of freedom.

## From Boston to the Brink

The war's first year flattered the cause.[^1] The New England militia system — the armed congregation of Unit 2 — rose overnight after Lexington and Concord (April 1775), bottled the British in Boston, and at Bunker Hill in June traded a hill for a quarter of the attacking force in casualties, teaching both sides that amateurs behind earthworks could maul regulars. Washington, appointed by Congress partly as a Virginian to nationalize a Yankee war, besieged Boston through the winter; when Henry Knox dragged the captured cannon of Ticonderoga three hundred frozen miles and Washington crowned Dorchester Heights with them in a single March night, the British evacuated — and the amateurs concluded, disastrously, that the war might be short. The professionals then arrived in earnest: in the summer of 1776, weeks after the Declaration, General Howe landed on Staten Island with more than thirty thousand men and the fleet to move them, and proceeded to give Washington a season-long education. Brooklyn, Kip's Bay, White Plains, Fort Washington — defeat after defeat, the new army routed, captured by thousands, and chased across New Jersey; by December Washington commanded a few thousand expiring enlistments on the wrong bank of the Delaware, Congress had fled Philadelphia, and Paine, marching with the army, wrote the sentence that named the moment: "These are the times that try men's souls." One detail from the debacle belongs to this course's ledger of providences, because the army itself so read it: after the Brooklyn defeat, Washington's whole surviving force — nine thousand men — lay trapped against the East River; on the night of August 29–30 he ferried them across in silence, and when dawn threatened the operation's exposure, a fog settled on Brooklyn thick enough to hide the last boats, lifting only when the army was gone. The escape saved the Revolution's army in its first campaign; the men in the boats called it what Washington's letters call such things.

## The Hinges

Then the war turned on a handful of hinges, and they can be counted. Trenton and Princeton (December 26, 1776–January 3, 1777): with the cause at its nadir, Washington recrossed the icy Delaware on Christmas night through a storm that made the password grimly apt — "Victory or Death" — surprised the Hessian garrison at dawn, then slipped a pursuing army to strike Princeton; two small battles, militarily minor, that resurrected the Revolution, re-enlisted its army, and taught Europe the rebels could not be finished. Saratoga (October 1777): Burgoyne's invasion from Canada, meant to sever New England, was ground down in the northern woods and surrendered entire — five thousand men — the victory France had waited for; the French alliance of 1778 turned a colonial rebellion into another world war, stretched the Royal Navy across the globe, and supplied the money, arms, and ultimately the fleet without which the ending is unimaginable. Valley Forge (winter 1777–78): not a battle but a refining fire — a quarter of the army dead of disease and want within sight of a fat countryside, and out of it, under Steuben's drill, a professional force; the army that limped in came out able to stand against regulars in open field at Monmouth. And the Southern gambit (1778–81): Britain, betting on Loyalist strength, took Savannah and Charleston (the war's worst American defeat, an entire army captured) and shattered another at Camden — and then the bet failed in blood: the backcountry war of militias turned savage and partisan (King's Mountain annihilating the Loyalist left, Cowpens the tactical masterpiece), Greene's campaign traded ground for attrition ("We fight, get beat, rise, and fight again"), and Cornwallis, mauled at Guilford Court House, staggered to the Virginia coast to await the navy. What arrived instead was France's: de Grasse turned back the British fleet at the Virginia Capes in September 1781, the allied armies closed the trap, and on October 19 eight thousand British regulars marched out of Yorktown between French and American lines. "Oh God," Lord North said when the news reached London, "it is all over" — and, at the level of governments, it was; the Treaty of Paris (1783) conceded independence and a boundary at the Mississippi, a diplomatic triumph outrunning the military facts.

## The Other Wars Inside the War

The course's honesty requires the accounting the parades omit, because the War for Independence was several wars at once. It was a civil war: Americans against Americans, Loyalist regiments and patriot militias burning each other's farms from the Mohawk Valley to the Carolina backcountry, with atrocity answering atrocity (Waxhaws' "quarter" massacre; the reprisals after King's Mountain), and it ended with some sixty to eighty thousand Loyalists in permanent exile — proportionally a greater emigration than the French Revolution's. It was a frontier catastrophe: most Native nations, forced to choose, chose Britain as the lesser threat to their lands; the frontier war was merciless in both directions (Wyoming and Cherry Valley; then Sullivan's 1779 expedition burning forty Iroquois towns and their winter stores), and the peace, made without the tribes, delivered their country to the victors — the war's most unambiguous losers were people who had signed nothing at Paris. It was, for enslaved Americans, a war of terrible arithmetic: Dunmore's proclamation (1775) and later Clinton's offered freedom to the enslaved of rebels who fled to British lines, and tens of thousands ran — Jefferson's own Monticello lost slaves to it — many dying in the army camps' epidemics, some evacuated at war's end to Nova Scotia and eventually Sierra Leone (Britain honoring, to its credit, promises made in its own name); while on the patriot side perhaps five thousand Black soldiers and sailors served — integrated in the ranks to a degree the American military would not see again until Korea — from Lexington green to the Rhode Island regiment's stand at Newport, fighting for a liberty the Declaration's deleted paragraph had already told them was negotiable. And it was, throughout, a war of endurance more than battles — an army unpaid in collapsing currency, a people war-weary by 1780, mutinies in the Continental line — which is why the moral machinery of the previous lessons mattered materially: the fasts, the sermons, the covenant frame that made persistence a duty were, as the British themselves testified, a weapons system. The victory, when it came, was received across America in the register the course expects — proclamations, thanksgivings, Washington's own general orders crediting "the reiterated and astonishing interpositions of Providence" — and the man who wrote those orders, whose personal conduct through eight impossible years had become the cause's chief moral asset, now faced the most dangerous moment of the entire Revolution: victory. Washington at prayer and at war — and at the surrender of power — is next.
