For four months in the summer of 1787, behind locked doors and shuttered windows in the Pennsylvania State House — sentries at the doors, the delegates sworn to secrecy so absolute that Madison's notes waited fifty years for publication — fifty-five men conducted the most consequential deliberation in the history of self-government. They had been sent to amend the Articles; they decided, in the first week, to replace them, and spent the summer inventing, piece by disputed piece, a form of government that existed nowhere on earth: an extended federal republic with a real executive, resting directly on the people rather than on the states, engineered throughout for the fallen human nature the 1780s had re-taught. Washington, presiding in silence, called the result "little short of a miracle" — and this course, which has traced the providential frame from Bradford to Brooklyn's fog, will let him say it while doing the historian's work: the miracle, examined, resolves into preparation, compromise, exhaustion, and a shared anthropology — which does not necessarily make it less providential, only more interesting.

## The Room

Begin with who was there and how they worked, because the Convention's character decided its product.[^1] The delegates were young (average early forties; Franklin at eighty-one the great exception), practical (planters, lawyers, merchants; more than half had served in the war, three-quarters in Congress — they knew the Articles' failures from inside), and formidably educated in exactly the five-shelf library of this unit's opening — a majority college men in an age when almost no one was, nine of them Princeton's, Witherspoon's stamp on the largest single contingent. The absences mattered as much: Jefferson and Adams abroad, Patrick Henry refusing (he "smelt a rat in Philadelphia, tending toward the monarchy," runs the attributed quip — the substance, his suspicion, is documented in his conduct), Rhode Island boycotting entirely. The rules made the deliberation possible: secrecy, so positions could change without public humiliation — the delegates understood, as the modern age has forgotten, that men negotiate honestly only when they are not performing; one vote per state delegation; and every question reopenable, so no defeat was final. Washington's silent presidency supplied the one thing no rule could: the assumption of good faith, and the unspoken knowledge that whatever executive they designed, he would be its first occupant — the office of the presidency is, in real measure, a suit tailored to the one man everyone trusted to wear it. And Madison, who had done the homework of the founders'-library lesson, arrived with the Virginia Plan ready: not amendments but a new government — bicameral legislature proportioned to population, national executive, national judiciary, a federal veto on state laws — which seized the agenda in the first days and forced every other delegation to argue on his ground.

## The Great Battles

The summer's fights sorted into a few great questions, and their resolutions are the Constitution. Representation nearly broke the Convention: the large states behind Virginia's proportional plan, the small states behind New Jersey's one-state-one-vote counter, the debate running for weeks into deadlock and threats of walkout — Delaware's Gunning Bedford flinging at the large states that the small ones might "find some foreign ally of more honor and good faith"; this was the crisis behind Franklin's famous motion (June 28) that the sessions open with prayer, "the longer I live, the more convincing proofs I see of this truth — that God governs in the affairs of men; and if a sparrow cannot fall to the ground without his notice, is it probable that an empire can rise without his aid?" The motion, the course notes honestly, was not adopted — the Convention adjourned without voting on it, Hugh Williamson observing dryly that they had no funds to pay a chaplain, and the often-told tale that Hamilton scoffed about "foreign aid" is unsupported legend — but the speech itself, from the least orthodox member, registered how near the thing was to failure. The escape was the Connecticut Compromise (July 16): proportional House, equal Senate — a settlement of pure prudence that no theory endorsed and everyone could live with, and which Madison and Wilson thought a defeat; the course marks the pattern, because it recurs: the Constitution is not a treatise but a treaty, and its "defects" are frequently the visible seams of peace. The executive consumed more debate than anything else — one man or three, chosen how, for how long, removable how — resolved late by the Electoral College, another jury-rigged compromise between congressional selection and popular election that no one much loved. The federal ratio of power was settled less by grand theory than by enumeration, supremacy, and the decision — momentous beyond its procedural look — to ratify by popular conventions rather than state legislatures: the new government would rest on "We the People," not on the states that would have voted their own demotion down. And slavery ran under every debate like a fault line — the three-fifths ratio, the twenty-year trade protection, the fugitive clause — bargains this course quarantines to their own lesson (L42), where they can be weighed with the care they demand, noting here only Madison's admission that the real division in the room "did not lie between the large and small states: it lay between the Northern and Southern."

## The Signing and the Sun

By September the delegates were exhausted into agreement, and the closing scenes carry the Convention's meaning better than any clause. Franklin, too weak to stand, had Wilson read his final speech — the wisest short statement on constitutional politics in the American canon: "I confess that there are several parts of this constitution which I do not at present approve, but I am not sure I shall never approve them: for having lived long, I have experienced many instances of being obliged by better information, or fuller consideration, to change opinions... the older I grow, the more apt I am to doubt my own judgment, and to pay more respect to the judgment of others." He asked each member who still had objections to "doubt a little of his own infallibility" and sign — and all but three present did (Mason, Randolph, Gerry refusing, chiefly over the missing bill of rights, a refusal the next lesson vindicates in part). As the last members signed, Franklin gestured at the sun carved on the back of Washington's chair and made the remark Madison thought worth recording: painters had found it hard, he said, to distinguish a rising from a setting sun; "now at length I have the happiness to know that it is a rising and not a setting sun." And at the door — the tradition comes from Maryland delegate James McHenry's journal — a Philadelphia lady, Mrs. Powel, asked Franklin what they had made: "A republic, madam — if you can keep it." The three scenes together are the Convention's self-understanding: fallible men, doubting their own infallibility, produced an instrument they knew was imperfect, bet on it anyway, and handed the wager to posterity conditionally.

## Reading the Miracle

Now the course's double duty. The honest deflations first: the Convention was an elite body acting beyond its mandate in total secrecy — charges its enemies made at once and historians (Beard's famous economic reading foremost) have pressed since; its compromises included bargains with slavery that the course will neither excuse nor euphemize; and the "miracle" had thoroughly human machinery — Madison's preparation, Washington's presence, the secrecy rule, the shared terror of the 1780s, and the brute fact that every alternative looked worse. The scholarly answer to Beard — that the delegates' economic interests predict their votes poorly, and their stated reasons track their conduct better than their portfolios — restores the deliberation without restoring the halo. And then the honest wonder, which survives the deflations: fifty-five quarreling men, in one summer, produced the longest-functioning written constitution in the world — an instrument that has organized the freest and most powerful republic in history, absorbed a civil war and a dozen crises that have shattered every comparable regime, and done it, as the moral-anthropology lesson will argue next, precisely because its authors declined to flatter mankind. Washington's "miracle" letter and Madison's cooler judgment — that it is "impossible to consider the degree of concord which ultimately prevailed as less than a miracle" — were written by the two men who knew best how close it came to failing. The instrument now had to survive the people it claimed to speak for; the ratification war is next.
