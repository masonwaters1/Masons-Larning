The Convention adjourned in September 1787; the Constitution then had to run the gauntlet its own Article VII prescribed — ratification by conventions in nine states — and for ten months the outcome was genuinely in doubt. The ratification contest was the largest, freest public debate over first political principles any people had ever conducted: hundreds of pamphlets and newspaper essays, taverns and pulpits and courthouse greens engaged from Georgia to New Hampshire, and state conventions where the votes ran terrifyingly close. It also created, as a byproduct of its polemics, the most enduring works of American political thought. This lesson stages the fight fairly — which requires effort, because the winners named both teams (their opponents were no more "anti-federal" than they; the label was a branding coup), wrote the retrospective histories, and consigned the losers to the role of the shortsighted. The course's verdict, argued below, is different: the Anti-Federalists lost the vote and won permanent concessions, their darkest predictions have a discomfiting way of reading like descriptions, and the American constitutional order as it actually exists — Constitution plus Bill of Rights plus perpetual vigilance about federal power — is the joint work of both parties.

## The Case for the Constitution

The Federalist campaign combined argument, organization, and prestige.[^1] The prestige was Washington and Franklin, whose signatures answered, for thousands of ordinary voters, every theoretical objection at a stroke. The organization was superior throughout — the Federalists were concentrated in the ports and towns, moved faster, and controlled most newspapers. The argument received its monument in the eighty-five essays of "Publius" — Hamilton's project, Madison's profundity, Jay's five contributions — published serially in New York and known ever since as The Federalist. Its core case: the Union itself was at stake — reject the Constitution and the confederation would dissolve into rival regional blocs, armed against each other, courted by European powers, replaying Europe's bloody state system on American soil (the opening essays are less about the Constitution than about the wars that would follow its failure). The Articles' disease was structural — a government over governments, legislating for states rather than persons, is either ignored or must coerce its members by arms; the cure was a government acting directly on individuals through its own law, courts, and revenue. And — the argument that reversed a century of settled theory, and which this course flagged from Madison's study in the library lesson — the extended republic was not liberty's enemy but its safeguard: against Montesquieu and the whole small-republic tradition, Federalist 10 argued that faction, the mortal disease of popular governments, is tamed not by shrinking the republic (small republics are majority tyranny's natural home) but by enlarging it — multiplying interests and sects until no single passion can command a stable majority. Federalist 51 completed the design with the anthropology the next lesson treats in full: ambition made to counteract ambition, because men are not angels. Publius's honest concessions deserve notice too — the Constitution was imperfect, a compound of theory and necessary compromise; the question, Madison insisted, was never "is it flawless?" but "compared to what?"

## The Case Against

The Anti-Federalists — Patrick Henry thundering through three weeks of the Virginia convention, "Brutus" and the "Federal Farmer" reasoning in the New York press, Mason circulating his objections, backcountry delegates and old revolutionaries across the states — were not a rabble of the shortsighted; they were, in the main, the custodians of the Revolution's original political science, pointing out, accurately, that the Constitution violated it at every point. The received wisdom of the age (Montesquieu, the Whig canon, the states' own experience) held that republics must be small, homogeneous, and near their citizens; the proposed government would rule four million people across half a continent from a distant capital — "Brutus" predicted with precision that representation would grow so attenuated (one representative per thirty thousand, and thinning) that the House would become an aristocracy of the notable, strangers to their constituents. The taxing power was unlimited; the "necessary and proper" and supremacy clauses were, Brutus argued, engines of consolidation that would swallow the states — and his forecast that the federal judiciary, armed with those clauses, would expand federal power case by irreversible case is, whatever one's politics, among the most clairvoyant documents of the founding era. A standing army in peacetime; a president re-eligible for life (Henry: "your president may easily become king"); no rotation in office; and — the objection that united all others and commanded the broadest public — no bill of rights. The Federalist answers (a government of enumerated powers needs no bill of rights; enumerating rights implies powers not granted; parchment barriers stop nothing) were clever, and on this point cleverness lost: the people of the states, whose own constitutions all carried declarations of rights, simply refused to accept a national government without one. The religious note in the chorus belongs to this course: several conventions heard objections that the Constitution was godless — no acknowledgment of the Deity, and Article VI's ban on religious tests meaning, as a North Carolina delegate protested, that "pagans, deists, and Mahometans might obtain offices among us" — while on the other side the persecuted Baptists of Unit 2's jails split pragmatically, Isaac Backus supporting ratification in Massachusetts precisely because the godless clauses guaranteed that no sect, including the ones that had jailed his brethren, could capture the new state. The "godless Constitution" was, to its most heartfelt religious defenders, religious liberty's charter — an inversion the Christian-nation lesson will sit with.

## The Verdict of the Conventions

The count itself was a cliffhanger managed with consummate politics. The early small states ratified fast (Delaware first, unanimously — the equal Senate had purchased them); Pennsylvania's Federalists rammed ratification through with a haste (dissenters dragged bodily to make a quorum) that scandalized even friends. The turning point was Massachusetts (February 1788): the convention leaned against, until the Federalists conceded the mechanism that saved the Constitution everywhere — ratify now, with recommended amendments to follow — and old Sam Adams, moved by Boston's tradesmen and the amendment promise, came round; 187 to 168. New Hampshire made nine in June, but the government was fiction without Virginia and New York. In Richmond, Henry's oratory ("I look upon that paper as the most fatal plan that could possibly be devised to enslave a free people") dueled Madison's quiet precision for twenty-three days — 89 to 79, with recommended amendments; in Poughkeepsie, Hamilton and Jay held the line until news of Virginia broke the opposition — 30 to 27, the closest of all. North Carolina refused until the Bill of Rights was actually pending; Rhode Island held out until 1790, ratifying by two votes under commercial threat. Total the concessions and the joint authorship appears: ratification was purchased with the promise of amendments, and Madison — converted from opponent of a bill of rights to its congressional shepherd, partly by Jefferson's letters and partly by the Baptist voters of his own district, whose support he needed and whose price was religious liberty — distilled the states' two hundred proposals into the ten amendments the course reaches in two lessons. The Constitution as Americans actually revere it — the charter plus its rights — no party proposed; the fight produced it.

## Both Sides Were Right

Close with the assessment the course will carry forward. The Federalists were right about the immediate question — the confederation was dying, disunion meant rival confederacies and European re-entry, and the extended republic has in fact proven more hospitable to liberty than the small ones the tradition prescribed; the two centuries since are their vindication on the main point. The Anti-Federalists were right about the trajectory — the federal government has consolidated, the judiciary has enlarged national power much as Brutus drew the mechanism, representation has attenuated past anything they imagined, and the citizen's distance from the capital is now measured in more than miles; every era's constitutionalists, of every party, eventually find themselves quoting Anti-Federalist warnings, usually without knowing it. And the deeper agreement beneath the quarrel is the unit's recurring find: both parties reasoned from the same anthropology — power corrupts the fallen, and must be caged — and disagreed only about which cage, national or state, held the more dangerous animal. What that shared anthropology was, where it came from, and how it got built into the machine's very gears, is the next lesson: the Constitution's moral anthropology.
