Every constitution is a portrait of humanity as its framers understood it, and the American Constitution is the most legible such portrait ever drawn. Read its machinery — the divided legislature, the veto, the staggered terms, the independent courts, the impeachment clauses, the double government of federalism — and a single conviction stares back from every joint: the men who built this machine did not trust the men who would run it, including themselves. This lesson names that conviction, traces where it came from, and completes the argument this unit has been assembling since the founders'-library lesson: that the Constitution's celebrated realism about power is, in significant and documented part, a theological inheritance — the Calvinist doctrine of human fallenness, secularized just enough to be engineered — and that the same framers who built for depravity insisted, in the same breath, that the machine could not run without virtue. Both halves are the founding's anthropology; modern politics keeps trying to keep one and discard the other, and the framers would have predicted the results.

## If Men Were Angels

The canonical text is Federalist 51, and it rewards slow reading.[^1] Madison is explaining why the Constitution does not merely separate powers on paper but arms each branch with weapons against the others, and he grounds the design in a sentence of political theology: "But what is government itself, but the greatest of all reflections on human nature? If men were angels, no government would be necessary. If angels were to govern men, neither external nor internal controls on government would be necessary." Government exists because men are not angels; government must be controlled because governors are not angels either — the double problem, as he states it, of first enabling the government to control the governed, and next obliging it to control itself. The solution is not to find better men — the Constitution nowhere assumes their supply — but to arrange offices so that self-interest polices itself: "Ambition must be made to counteract ambition. The interest of the man must be connected with the constitutional rights of the place." The president defends his veto because it is his; the Senate guards its treaties, the House its purse, the states their residuum, each out of institutional self-love — "a policy," Madison writes in the essay's most quietly radical phrase, "of supplying, by opposite and rival interests, the defect of better motives." Federalist 10 had already grounded the whole in a diagnosis: the causes of faction are "sown in the nature of man" — remove liberty and you cure faction as amputation cures a fever; the causes being incurable, only effects can be managed. This is engineering for a fallen species: not the perfectibility projects of the coming French Revolution, not the classical hope of forming virtuous guardians, but a machine designed on the assumption that every actor in it, given the chance, will encroach.

## The Genealogy of Distrust

Where did the assumption come from? The library lesson mapped the convergence; here it can be documented. One stream is frankly secular: Hume's maxim — that in designing constitutions "every man ought to be supposed a knave" though the supposition be false in fact — was in Madison's notebooks, and the Whig canon's obsession with encroaching power (Cato's "power renders men wanton") supplied two generations of colonial reflexes. But the stream that made the doctrine common sense to the American public was the one this course has traced from Geneva through the covenant towns to Witherspoon's classroom: the Reformed doctrine of sin. New England had heard total depravity — not that men are as bad as possible, but that no faculty and no office escapes corruption — preached weekly for a century and a half; the political corollary, that unchecked power in fallen hands is certain to be abused, was the axiom of the election-sermon tradition long before it was the axiom of Philadelphia. Witherspoon taught precisely this synthesis to Madison and his cohort — man's fallen nature as the datum from which the science of politics begins — and the fingerprints show in the record: it was the orthodox delegates and divines who most instinctively applauded the machinery of counterpoise, and the era's preachers who explained checks and balances to their congregations as applied hamartiology. The honest genealogy is braided, not single — a Deist could reach the same design through Hume, and some did — but the American consensus behind the design, the reason ratifying farmers found "ambition against ambition" obvious rather than cynical, was formed in the pews. Adams put the braid in one sentence: whoever would found a state on the supposition of human goodness "will find himself woefully mistaken"; the Constitution, as a Reformed historian has framed it, may be the most Augustinian public document of the age precisely because it never mentions Augustine's God — it simply builds as if his diagnosis were true.

## The Other Half: No Machine Without Fuel

Now the half the cynical reading drops, because the framers stated it as plainly as the distrust. The Constitution's checks manage vice; they do not manufacture the honesty, restraint, and public spirit the system consumes daily — and the framers said so on the record. Madison, defending the Constitution at the Virginia ratifying convention against Henry's darkest scenarios, made the dependence explicit: "To suppose that any form of government will secure liberty or happiness without any virtue in the people, is a chimerical idea"; if there is no virtue among us, "no theoretical checks, no form of government, can render us secure." Washington's Farewell (the next unit's opening lesson) would name religion and morality the "indispensable supports" of political prosperity; Adams compressed the whole into the sentence this course quoted in the founders'-spectrum lesson — "Our Constitution was made only for a moral and religious people. It is wholly inadequate to the government of any other" — written, note, not as piety but as engineering specification: avarice and ambition unrestrained by morals, he said, would snap the Constitution's cords "as a whale goes through a net." Here the unit's threads tie: the founders' civic-theology consensus (L29), the disestablishment about to be examined (L40–41), and this anthropology form one system — a state built for sinners, refusing to establish the churches, while counting on the churches (and families, and schools: the Northwest Ordinance's "religion, morality, and knowledge") to supply the virtue the state deliberately declined to coerce. Liberty requires virtue; virtue, the founders near-unanimously judged, requires religion; religion, to be real, requires liberty — the triangle closes, and every side of it is load-bearing.

## The Portrait's Limits

The course's honesty requires the audit. The framers' realism had famous blind spots: they designed against demagogues and factions while failing to foresee the political party — within a decade the branch-versus-branch machinery was overlaid by party loyalty running across the branches, and ambition, contrary to the blueprint, attached itself to party more than place; Washington's Farewell is partly a lament over the miscalculation. The anthropology could be selectively applied: men so clear-eyed about power's corruption in kings and congresses maintained, many of them, a studied blindness about absolute power exercised in their own households — the slavery lesson two ahead is the anthropology's own indictment of its authors. Critics from Jefferson's day to ours have argued the distrust overshoots — that a system this braked breeds gridlock, and that treating men as knaves can help make them so; defenders answer with the twentieth century, whose regimes of trusted virtue and unchecked vanguards supplied history's clearest controlled experiment on the question. And the framers themselves would insist on the deepest caveat: the machine was never self-sufficient — a fact which makes the modern condition, in which the checks endure while the framers' assumed reservoirs of virtue are treated as optional, precisely the experiment Adams warned against running. The portrait, limits and all, remains the founding's most durable achievement: a government that has never needed its citizens to be angels, built by men who knew — from Scripture, from Hume, from the 1780s — that none would be available. What such a government owed to the conscience it refused to command is the next lesson: the Bill of Rights.
