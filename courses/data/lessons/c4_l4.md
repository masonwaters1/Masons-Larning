We now hold the great springs of the Western inheritance in our hands: from Jerusalem, the moral law, the covenant, and the dignity of the person; from Athens and Rome, reason, philosophy, and the architecture of the republic. But these are not one thing. They are two very different sources of truth — revelation and reason, faith and philosophy, the prophet and the philosopher — and there is no guarantee they will agree. They might have remained separate streams, or even turned against each other. That they were instead woven together into a single, coherent worldview is one of the decisive achievements in the history of Western thought, and it is the work of the long Christian centuries that lie between ancient Rome and the modern world. Two men did more than any others to accomplish the weaving: Augustine in late antiquity and Thomas Aquinas in the high Middle Ages. This lesson is about how they fused Jerusalem and Athens into the intellectual backbone of the civilization America would inherit.

## What Has Athens to Do with Jerusalem?

The question was posed, sharply, by the early Christian writer Tertullian: what has Athens to do with Jerusalem? What does the academy have to do with the church? It was a real question, and it had a real answer on the rejectionist side: nothing. On this view, Christian faith rests on revelation and needs no help from pagan philosophy; the wisdom of the Greeks is at best a distraction and at worst a corruption of the pure gospel. Had that view prevailed, the history of the West would have been entirely different — a Christianity walled off from reason, and a classical heritage left to gather dust.

But it did not prevail. The dominant path, taken by the greatest Christian minds, was synthesis: the conviction that the truths reason had discovered and the truths revelation had delivered must ultimately cohere, because they flow from the same source — and that the task of the Christian thinker is to weave them together. The first master of that task was Augustine.

## Augustine: The Bridge Between Two Worlds

Augustine of Hippo stands at the hinge of history, with one foot in the classical world and one in the Christian. Born in Roman North Africa in 354, he received the finest classical education of his day, steeped himself in pagan philosophy — Platonism above all — and lived a worldly young life before his famous conversion to Christianity, the story of which he told in the *Confessions*, a book that invented a whole new kind of writing. His genius was to take the best of Greek philosophy, especially the Platonic vision of an eternal realm of truth beyond the material world, and to put it into the service of Christian theology. In Augustine, Athens entered the church not as an enemy but as a servant.

Two of his contributions shaped the Western political imagination permanently. The first is the great image of his masterwork, *The City of God*, written as the Roman Empire was collapsing: the vision of two cities intermingled throughout human history — the earthly city, built on the love of self and the lust for domination, and the heavenly city, built on the love of God.[^1] Every actual society is a mixture of the two, and no earthly kingdom — not even Rome, which pagans had thought eternal — is the City of God. This single idea had enormous consequences. It *desacralized politics*: it stripped every earthly state of the divine pretensions that ancient kingdoms had claimed, insisting that no government is the Kingdom of God and that all earthly power is provisional, fallen, and under judgment. Here is a deep root of the Western distinction between religious and political authority — the refusal to let any state declare itself sacred and absolute — which would eventually flower into the separation of church and state.

Augustine's second great contribution was a sober and realistic view of human nature. Drawing on the doctrine of original sin, he taught that the human will is disordered and corrupt, that even our virtues are shadowed by pride, and that we cannot be trusted to be good merely because we know the good. This Augustinian realism — the conviction that human beings are fallen creatures whose appetite for power must be restrained because it can never be trusted — runs straight through Western political thought and emerges, transformed, in the American founders' bedrock assumption that power must always be checked. When the authors of the Constitution built their elaborate machinery of checks and balances on the premise that men are not angels and that ambition must be made to counter ambition, they were applying, whether they named it or not, an essentially Augustinian view of the human heart. And in the *Confessions*, Augustine did something else of lasting importance: by turning his gaze inward, examining the depths of his own memory, will, and conscience before God, he helped invent the Western sense of the inner self — the rich, examined interior life that later centuries would come to take for granted.

## Aquinas: The Great Synthesis

If Augustine baptized Plato, Thomas Aquinas, eight centuries later, performed the even more daring feat of baptizing Aristotle — and in doing so produced the most complete synthesis of reason and revelation the West has ever known.

The setting matters. In the twelfth and thirteenth centuries, the full body of Aristotle's works came flooding back into Latin Christendom, preserved and transmitted in large part through the Islamic world and its great commentators. This was thrilling and threatening at once: Aristotle offered a magnificent, comprehensive philosophy of nature and reason, but one developed by a pagan with no knowledge of Christian revelation, and some of its conclusions seemed to clash with the faith. Some churchmen wanted Aristotle banned. Aquinas took the opposite course. He embraced Aristotle and set out to show that the philosopher's reason and the church's revelation, rightly understood, did not contradict but completed each other.

His master principle was that **faith and reason are two distinct but complementary paths to truth, and they cannot ultimately conflict because God is the author of both.**[^2] Human reason, unaided, can discover a great deal — the order of nature, the principles of morality, even, Aquinas argued, the existence of God. Revelation supplies what reason cannot reach on its own — the Trinity, the Incarnation, the path of salvation. Far from being enemies, reason and faith are partners, each with its proper domain, together forming a single coherent understanding of reality. This reconciliation did something of incalculable importance for the West: it *sanctified rational inquiry*, declaring the use of reason to investigate the natural and moral world a holy task rather than a threat to faith. The medieval universities, the scholastic method of rigorous argument, and ultimately the soil from which Western science would grow all owe a debt to this Thomistic confidence that the universe is rational, ordered, and knowable because a rational God made it so.

## Natural Law: The Crown of the Synthesis

For our purposes — the story of the American order — Aquinas's single most consequential achievement was his systematic theory of **natural law**, which gathered the scattered insights of Aristotle, the Stoics, Cicero, and Scripture into one coherent architecture. He distinguished a hierarchy of law. At the summit is the *eternal law* — God's own rational governance of all creation. Beneath it is the *natural law* — the rational creature's participation in that eternal law, the moral principles that human beings can discern by reason alone, written, as it were, into our nature: that good is to be done and evil avoided, and the basic precepts that follow. Then comes *human law* — the specific, positive enactments of states and rulers, which are legitimate only insofar as they are derived from and consistent with the natural law. And finally *divine law* — the law revealed in Scripture, which guides us toward our supernatural end.

The explosive implication lies in the relationship between human law and natural law. For Aquinas, a human law that contradicts the natural law is not merely a bad law — it is, in his striking phrase, a perversion of law, not truly law at all, and it does not bind the conscience.[^2] Here is the philosophical foundation of the entire Western higher-law tradition: the conviction that there is a standard of justice above all human legislation, accessible to reason, against which the laws of kings and parliaments may be measured and found wanting. From this root grow the ideas of constitutionalism, of inalienable rights that no government may violate, and of the right — even the duty — to resist or refuse an unjust law. The line runs directly to the modern world: when Martin Luther King Jr., sitting in the Birmingham jail, defended his civil disobedience by declaring that an unjust law is no law at all, he cited Aquinas by name. And when Jefferson grounded American independence in self-evident truths and unalienable rights derived from the laws of nature, he was drawing water from this well, however many channels it had flowed through to reach him.

## How Aristotle Came Back: The Debt to Islam

The story of the great synthesis contains a chapter that a triumphalist Western account often skips, and honesty — as well as accuracy — requires telling it. When Aquinas set out to reconcile Aristotle with Christianity, he was able to do so only because Aristotle's works were available to him at all — and for much of the early medieval period, in Western Europe, they largely were not. Through the centuries after Rome's collapse, a great deal of classical Greek learning was lost to the Latin West. It was preserved, studied, and brilliantly developed elsewhere — above all in the Islamic world, which during its own golden age translated the Greek philosophers into Arabic, wrote profound commentaries on them, and advanced mathematics, medicine, astronomy, and philosophy while much of Christian Europe was intellectually impoverished.

The recovery of Aristotle in the twelfth and thirteenth centuries came in large part through this Islamic transmission, and through the translation centers where Arabic learning flowed back into Latin. The great Muslim philosophers — Avicenna and, especially for Aquinas, Averroes, known to the schoolmen simply as "the Commentator" — had wrestled with exactly the problem Aquinas faced: how to reconcile Greek philosophy with revealed monotheist religion. Aquinas read them closely, learned from them, and argued with them. The Christian synthesis, in other words, was built with materials carried into Europe on the shoulders of Muslim and also Jewish scholars, who had their own parallel projects of harmonizing faith and reason. This is a humbling and important corrective to any story of Western civilization as a sealed, self-sufficient achievement. The inheritance was always more entangled, more indebted, and more genuinely a product of civilizational exchange than the tidy version admits — and acknowledging that makes the history truer, not weaker.

## The University and the Habit of Reason

The synthesis was not merely a set of ideas in great men's books; it was institutionalized, and the institution it created may be its most durable legacy of all: the university. The medieval universities — Bologna, Paris, Oxford, and the rest — arose in this period as the organized homes of the synthesis, places where reason and revelation were studied together under the confident assumption that they belonged together. And the method practiced there was itself a training in rigorous reason: the scholastic *disputation*, in which a question was posed, the strongest arguments on every side were marshaled and stated fairly — often more fairly than their own proponents could state them — objections were raised, and a reasoned resolution was reached that had to answer every objection.

This habit of disciplined, adversarial reasoning, of taking opposing views seriously enough to state them at their strongest before answering them, is one of the quiet glories of the medieval mind, and it shaped the Western intellect far beyond the church. The university as an institution, the degree, the faculty, the very structure of organized higher learning that the founders' colleges inherited and that endures worldwide today, descends from this medieval creation. So does the deep Western confidence that truth is pursued through reasoned argument among people who disagree — a confidence that would later animate the law court, the scientific society, and the legislative debate. When the founders reasoned out their Constitution through argument and counterargument, and when *The Federalist* answered its opponents point by point, they were practicing, in a secular key, a discipline of the mind that the medieval synthesis had institutionalized centuries before.

## The Backbone — and How It Reached America

The result of Augustine's and Aquinas's labors was a unified worldview: a vision in which reason and revelation, the classical and the biblical, the philosopher and the prophet, all cooperated within a single coherent understanding of God, nature, and the human person. This medieval synthesis was the intellectual backbone of Western civilization for centuries — the framework of its universities, its law, its art, and its deep conviction that the cosmos is rationally and morally ordered.

The line from this synthesis to the American founding is real but it is not a straight pipe, and honesty requires saying so plainly. The natural-law and natural-rights tradition reached the founders mostly *indirectly* — transformed by the Protestant Reformation, refracted through later Protestant natural-law theorists, and above all reshaped by John Locke, who secularized and individualized the inheritance into the language of natural rights, consent, and limited government that the founders actually read and quoted. Most of the founders were Protestants or deists who would never have called themselves disciples of a medieval Catholic theologian. Yet the deep structure of their thought — the moral law accessible to reason, the higher law above human law, the rational and ordered universe, the fallen and dangerous human heart that must be checked — bears the unmistakable imprint of the synthesis Augustine and Aquinas built. They inherited the cathedral even as they rebuilt its façade.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Two cautions keep this account truthful. First, the synthesis was never as stable or as final as a tidy summary suggests. The marriage of faith and reason was always under strain, and it would later come apart: late-medieval thinkers began to pull the two domains back into tension, the Reformation would challenge the whole edifice of medieval Catholic thought, and the Enlightenment would eventually pry reason loose from revelation altogether, turning the offspring against the parent. The "synthesis" is a real and towering achievement, but it is also a contested one whose unraveling is itself part of the Western story.

Second, the same centuries that produced this luminous intellectual achievement also produced grave injustice carried out in the church's name — the persecution of heretics, the violence against Jews and Muslims, the machinery of the Inquisition, the crushing of dissent. The universal dignity proclaimed in the natural-law tradition coexisted, in practice, with brutal exclusion and coercion. To honor the genuine grandeur of the medieval synthesis is not to whitewash the cruelty that often accompanied it; both belong to the honest record, and we will hold them together rather than choosing one and forgetting the other.

With Jerusalem, Athens and Rome, and now the great synthesis that fused them into the backbone of Western thought, the inheritance is nearly complete. Two forces remain before we cross the Atlantic: the practical machinery of ordered liberty forged in England — Magna Carta, the common law, the rights of Englishmen — and the Reformation, which would crack the medieval synthesis wide open and release energies that would help make the modern world, and America with it. If Augustine and Aquinas built the cathedral of the Western mind, England would build its courtroom and the Reformation would unlock its doors. To England, and to the great rupture of the Reformation, we turn next.
