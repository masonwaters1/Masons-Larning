Every thread of honesty this course has laid down — the deleted paragraph of the Declaration, the double reading of the civil war, the founders convicted by their own document — converges on the room where the contradiction was written into fundamental law. The Constitution of 1787 contains three provisions protecting slavery, secured by hard bargaining, without which there would have been no Constitution; it also refuses, by deliberate choice of words, to concede that a human being can be property, and it was read within a lifetime as both a covenant with death and a glorious liberty document — by men who had each escaped slavery or fought it, and who knew the text equally well. This lesson does what the subject demands and the culture war never does: it lays out exactly what was bargained and why, lets the framers' own recorded consciences speak, and then stages the great interpretive war over what they had made — because that war, between Garrison's reading and Douglass's, is not a modern debate about the founding; it is the founding's own unfinished argument, and the course's position must be earned, not assumed.

## The Three Clauses

The bargains, precisely.[^1] First, the three-fifths clause: representation and direct taxes apportioned by adding to the free population "three fifths of all other Persons" — the enslaved counted fractionally, not as a judgment of their humanity (the perverse arithmetic ran the other way: slaveholders wanted them counted fully, to swell Southern power; opponents of slavery wanted them counted not at all, to deny it) but as a settlement of political weight, which handed the South a bonus of congressional seats and electoral votes that shaped the republic for seventy years — the "slave power" arithmetic behind Jefferson's 1800 margin and a long train of doughface majorities. Second, the slave trade clause: Congress forbidden to prohibit "the Migration or Importation of such Persons as any of the States now existing shall think proper to admit" before 1808 — twenty years' protection for the Atlantic trade, demanded as an ultimatum by South Carolina and Georgia ("South Carolina and Georgia cannot do without slaves," their delegates said flatly, and would not join a union that could immediately strangle their labor supply), and purchased in a logrolled bargain with New England votes in exchange for a bare-majority commerce power. Third, the fugitive slave clause: no person "held to Service or Labour" escaping into another state shall be freed by that state's laws, but "shall be delivered up" — the clause that nationalized slavery's reach, made every free state complicit in recapture, and would generate, in the Fugitive Slave Acts, the most hated federal statutes in antebellum America. Around the three stand the auxiliary facts: the insurrection and domestic-violence clauses pledging federal force that could suppress slave revolts; and the studied absence — the words slave and slavery appear nowhere, the drafters resorting to "persons" throughout, because, as Madison recorded of his own scruple, it would be "wrong to admit in the Constitution the idea that there could be property in men." Paterson of New Jersey noted the delegates "had been ashamed to use the term 'slaves'" — and shame, the reader should mark, is evidence: men do not launder language they believe innocent.

## The Consciences in the Room

The Convention's own debates preserve the moral knowledge the bargains defied, and the course sets the passages on the record. Gouverneur Morris of Pennsylvania delivered the most sustained denunciation heard in the room: slavery was "a nefarious institution... the curse of heaven on the states where it prevailed," and he would "sooner submit himself to a tax for paying for all the Negroes in the United States than saddle posterity with such a Constitution." George Mason of Virginia — himself the holder of hundreds — pronounced the trade "infernal" and prophesied in the covenant register this course knows from Unit 1: slaves "bring the judgment of heaven on a country. As nations cannot be rewarded or punished in the next world, they must be in this. By an inevitable chain of causes and effects, providence punishes national sins by national calamities" — a sentence that reads, after 1865, like the Second Inaugural drafted eighty years early. Against these stood not a moral defense — almost no one in the room defended slavery as good; that ideology awaited the next century — but an ultimatum and a calculation: the Deep South would walk, and a union without it meant (the framers believed) rival confederacies, foreign alliances, and war on the continent, with slavery surviving anyway, uncontained. The bargainers' own consciences salved themselves with a forecast: slavery was, they widely supposed, a dying institution — tobacco exhausted, the trade closable in twenty years, emancipation advancing in the North (every state north of Maryland had begun abolition by 1804), the Northwest sealed by the Ordinance passed that very summer. The forecast was not absurd in 1787; it was destroyed in 1793, when a Connecticut Yankee's cotton gin made short-staple cotton profitable and the dying institution rose from its bed to devour half a continent — the hinge of contingency on which the whole tragedy swings, and a standing caution to every generation that bargains with an evil on the assumption that history will retire it quietly.

## The Interpretive War

What, then, had they made? The nineteenth century's two greatest answers were both given by abolitionists, and their debate is the lesson's core. William Lloyd Garrison read the three clauses and pronounced the verdict of Isaiah: the Constitution was "a covenant with death and an agreement with hell," a proslavery compact whose righteous course was disunion — he burned it publicly, on the Fourth of July. Frederick Douglass began in Garrison's camp and worked his way out by the text: the Constitution, he argued in his mature position, is "a glorious liberty document" — its preamble speaks for "We the People," not we the white people; its guarantees (republican government, due process, the blessings of liberty) contain no exception for the enslaved; the property-in-man principle was deliberately kept off its face, so that, as he put it, if the Constitution were found with no knowledge of its framers' practices, "not a man... could show that it was intended to protect slavery"; the compromises were scaffolding — temporary concessions to a wrong the instrument's principles condemned — and the abolitionist's duty was therefore not to burn the charter but to seize it, and wield the founding against the founders' descendants. Lincoln's reading, which won the argument at Cooper Union and then at law, split the difference with historical precision: the founders had compromised with slavery as a necessity while marking it as a wrong — excluding it from the territories, ending the trade at the first constitutional instant (Congress banned it effective January 1, 1808, the earliest day allowed), refusing the word — placing it, in his formula, "in the course of ultimate extinction"; the sin of the present generation was not the fathers' compromise but the sons' repudiation of its direction, the new insistence that slavery was a positive good to be extended forever. Modern scholarship continues the same war in footnotes — the "proslavery compact" school tallying the protections and the slaveholding presidencies they produced; the "antislavery constitutionalism" school recovering how much Douglass's reading shaped the Republican party and the Reconstruction amendments — and the course, having listened, takes its position with Douglass and Lincoln, for their reason: the Constitution's slavery clauses were real, shameful, and consequential, and the Constitution's principles were the weapon that killed slavery — both facts are load-bearing, and a nation was eventually required, in Mason's own predicted currency of national calamity, to pay the difference between them. The compromise bought a union; the union's price came due at Gettysburg; and the document that had sheltered the institution supplied, in its own amended text, the words that abolished it. Whether such a founding — biblically saturated, formally godless, principled and compromised at once — was "Christian" is the question this unit was built to answer, and it is next.
