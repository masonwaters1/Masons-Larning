Seventeen lessons of evidence are now on the table, and the question that titles this lesson can finally be answered like a historian instead of a combatant. "Was America founded as a Christian nation?" is the most quarreled-over question in American memory, wielded as a club from two directions: yes, and therefore the present owes the churches its public square; no, and therefore religion's public claims are gate-crashing. The course's answer, argued below, is that the question as usually posed is broken — it bundles four different questions, which have different answers — and that when the bundle is untied, the honest findings embarrass both culture-war positions about equally, while being far more interesting than either. This is the unit's capstone; nothing new is introduced that the prior lessons have not established, which is the point: the answer should fall out of the record.

## Untying the Question

Ask it as four questions, and answer each from the lessons.[^1] Was the founding population Christian? Overwhelmingly, in profession and formation: a people of Protestant establishment and dissent, biblically literate to a depth the citation studies measured (L28), churched and awakened (Unit 2), whose common moral language was Scripture — with a small Jewish population, a Catholic minority concentrated in Maryland, the enslaved forging their own Christianity, and an elite leavening of rationalists. As demography and culture, "Christian nation" is simply a description, and no serious historian denies it. Was the cause animated by Christian conviction? Substantially, as Unit 2's close and this unit's opening documented: the Black Regiment, the covenant frame, the fast days, the Christian patriots — alongside, inseparably, the classical, legal, Enlightenment, and Whig streams of the founders' library; the Revolution was preached and reasoned in the same breath. Were the founders Christians? The spectrum lesson (L29) answered: many orthodox, a famous few heterodox theists, essentially no atheists, and a near-universal civic theology holding religion indispensable to the virtue on which the republic's machinery (L39) depended. Was the federal government founded as Christian — established, confessional, deriving its authority from Christian revelation? No — deliberately, structurally, no: a Constitution that never names God (its sole religious references being the Article VI test ban and the customary "Year of our Lord" dating), that forbids religious tests for office against the practice of every state and nation of the age, and whose First Amendment (L40) barred Congress from the establishment business entirely. The government was designed non-confessional on purpose, and — the finding the secular telling always omits and the Christian telling rarely absorbs — it was designed so in large part at the demand of the most fervent Christians in America, the dissenters of L41, who had learned in Virginia's jails what established Christianity does.

## The Two Proof-Texts

Each side of the quarrel owns a proof-text, and the course examines both honestly. The "no" side's exhibit is the Treaty of Tripoli (1797), whose Article 11 declares that "the Government of the United States is not, in any sense, founded on the Christian religion" — negotiated under Washington, ratified unanimously by the Senate, signed by John Adams, published without recorded protest. The document is real and probative, and its context must be stated with equal care: it was diplomatic assurance to a Muslim power that America's government — unlike the crusading crowns of Europe — had no confessional quarrel with Islam (the treaty bought peace from corsairs enslaving American sailors, the Barbary thread of L36); scholars later found the Arabic original contains no such article, making the English text a curiosity of drafting — but the English text is what the Senate read and ratified, and what it asserts about the government is, on this unit's evidence, simply accurate: the government, as such, was not founded on the Christian religion. The "yes" side's exhibit is the founders' civic-theology corpus — Washington's Farewell naming religion and morality the indispensable supports; Adams's "our Constitution was made only for a moral and religious people"; the proclamations, the chaplains, the Northwest Ordinance's "religion, morality, and knowledge." These too are real and probative, and they establish what they establish: that the same founders who built a non-confessional state assumed, publicly and repeatedly, that the society it governed would remain religious — that the machine required moral fuel the state itself would not manufacture. Set the two exhibits side by side and they do not contradict; they describe the two halves of one settlement. Adams signed the Tripoli treaty and wrote the moral-and-religious-people letter within eighteen months of each other, and saw no tension, because there was none: a government not founded on Christianity, presiding over a people whose virtue was expected to come substantially from it.

## The Settlement, Named

So the founding's actual arrangement, assembled from the unit: a Christian people (demographically), whose deepest political instincts were formed by Protestant covenant and dissent (Units 1–2); a plural religious landscape no single church could master (L17, L29); a non-confessional federal government of checked powers built for fallen men (L37, L39, L40); religion disestablished — at the insistence of an evangelical-rationalist alliance — precisely so that it could be free, voluntary, and vigorous (L41); and an expectation, near-universal across the spectrum, that the whole experiment's survival depended on the people remaining virtuous and therefore, as they understood the chain, religious (L29, L39). Historians have reached for names for this: a republic "founded by Christians" but "not founded as a Christian state"; a nation with "the soul of a church" and the constitution of a commercial republic; Lincoln's phrase — the one this course adopts as truest to the evidence and to its own charter — an "almost chosen people": a nation saturated in biblical self-understanding, formally unconsecrated, perpetually arguing about the difference. Both culture-war answers fail the record in mirrored ways. The secular founding myth must explain away the people, the preachers, the proclamations, the civic theology, and the Christian motives of disestablishment itself; the Christian-nation myth must explain away the godless text, the test ban, the Tripoli assurance, the heterodox drafters, and the founders' own refusal — when they had every opportunity and precedent — to write Christ into the charter. The historical settlement rebukes both: it gave the churches no throne and the state no altar, and bet the republic on the wager that faith would do more for freedom, and freedom more for faith, unarmed than armed. Two hundred years of the loudest religious vitality in the Western world — and of unceasing quarrel over this very question — are the wager's twin results, and the course will follow both threads to the present day.

## The Unit Closes

Step back to what the unit has done, because its shape is the course's argument in miniature. It opened in the founders' library (L27) and their Bibles (L28); walked the spectrum of their souls (L29); watched the faith teach the founders (Witherspoon), arm the agitators (Adams, Henry), and march with the armies (the Regiment in action); read the Declaration as covenant-shaped moral argument (L33) and the war as the era read it, providences and atrocities together (L34–35); learned from the Articles that virtue unaided fails (L36), from Philadelphia that fallen men can build for fallen men (L37, L39), from ratification that the losers' fears purchase the winners' liberties (L38, L40); and found religious freedom won by the religious (L41) beside the compromise that mortgaged the whole estate (L42). A providential and imperfect founding, exactly as the course's subtitle promised — too Christian for the secular myth, too free for the confessional one, and carrying in its two unresolved tensions (a virtue-dependent republic that established no source of virtue; a liberty document sheltering slavery) the entire agenda of the American future. That future begins immediately: a general with no precedent to follow, inventing the presidency and then — most consequentially — giving it up again, with a farewell warning the next unit opens by reading. The Early Republic is next.
