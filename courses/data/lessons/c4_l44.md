Unit 4 opens where the founding hands itself to the future: a president walking away from power a second time, and leaving behind, in place of himself, a letter. Washington's Farewell Address — never delivered as a speech, but published in a Philadelphia newspaper in September 1796 as a communication "to the PEOPLE of the United States" — is the founding generation's last will and testament: the departing father's judgment of which dangers would actually kill the republic. He named three — disunion, party spirit, and foreign entanglement — and bound them together with the claim this course has been tracking since the founders' civic theology: that the whole machine runs on virtue, and virtue on religion and morality, the "indispensable supports." The Address was read annually in Congress for generations and quoted in every foreign-policy quarrel since; it has also been used, misquoted, and weaponized as much as any American text. This lesson reads what it actually says, tells the honest story of how it was made — including the ghostwriters and the partisan timing — and weighs its prophecies against what happened, which is the sternest test a warning can face.

## The Decision and the Document

The largest fact about the Farewell is the act it announced.[^1] Washington could have been president for life — every elector, twice, had voted for him unanimously, and no constitutional limit stood in the way; his declining a third term converted his personal exhaustion into the republic's most important unwritten law, the two-term tradition that held for a century and a half until FDR broke it and the Twenty-second Amendment restored it in writing. The renunciation of Annapolis, repeated: the man who defined the office defined its surrender, and monarchs again marveled. The document itself has a candid, complicated history. Washington had wanted to retire in 1792 and asked Madison for a draft farewell then; four years later — Madison now leading the opposition — he sent that draft to Hamilton, who reworked and largely rewrote it under Washington's close direction, the president editing for plainness ("plain style" his explicit instruction) and taking full ownership of every sentiment. The course states the ghostwriting plainly because honesty costs the document nothing: the ideas are demonstrably Washington's — traceable through his Circular of 1783 (L35) and years of letters — and the arrangement was the era's norm; but readers should know that the most famous farewell in American history is a Washington-Hamilton collaboration built on a Madison foundation: the founding's three great pens, awkwardly, in one text. So was the timing candid politics: published six weeks before the 1796 election, the announcement's lateness kneecapped the opposition's campaign planning, and Jefferson's partisans read the whole document — with some justice, given Hamilton's hand and its coded warnings against French attachment — as a Federalist campaign paper in valedictory dress. All true; and the text outgrew the occasion, as the founding's texts have a way of doing.

## The Three Warnings

The Address's body is architecture, not miscellany: each warning guards the same treasure — the Union — against a different solvent. First, sectionalism: Washington begs Americans to think of the Union as "a main pillar in the edifice of your real independence," walks each region through its material dependence on the others (northern commerce and southern produce, east and west bound by rivers and markets), and warns against "characterizing parties by geographical discriminations — Northern and Southern, Atlantic and Western," by which designing men misrepresent the views of other districts; the passage reads, after 1861, like a man describing the exact fault line. Second, the spirit of party — the warning posterity most ignores because posterity is made of parties. Washington grants that the spirit is "inseparable from our nature," rooted in the strongest passions, but insists that in popular governments it is "truly their worst enemy": it distracts councils, enfeebles administration, "agitates the community with ill-founded jealousies and false alarms, kindles the animosity of one part against another, foments occasionally riot and insurrection," and "opens the door to foreign influence and corruption" — and, in the passage the anthropology lesson (L39) predicted, the alternate domination of one faction over another, sharpened by revenge, "is itself a frightful despotism" that leads men, weary of disorder, to seek security "in the absolute power of an individual." Third, foreign attachment: the "great rule of conduct" — extend commercial relations with all nations, "with them as little political connection as possible"; honor existing engagements, but "steer clear of permanent alliances with any portion of the foreign world," trusting instead to "temporary alliances for extraordinary emergencies." (The famous phrase "entangling alliances," the course notes for precision, is not here — it is Jefferson's, from his first inaugural five years later; the two are quoted interchangeably and shouldn't be.) And beneath the geopolitics, a psychological warning modern readers skip at their cost: "passionate attachments" to favorite nations and "inveterate antipathies" against others are themselves a form of slavery — the nation that loves or hates another nation is "in some degree a slave" to it, its policy captured by sentiment; a warning written against the Francophile and Anglophile streets of 1796 and aged not a day since.

## The Indispensable Supports

At the Address's center stands the paragraph this course has been approaching for two units, and it deserves quotation at length: "Of all the dispositions and habits which lead to political prosperity, religion and morality are indispensable supports. In vain would that man claim the tribute of patriotism, who should labor to subvert these great pillars of human happiness... The mere politician, equally with the pious man, ought to respect and to cherish them... Let it simply be asked: Where is the security for property, for reputation, for life, if the sense of religious obligation desert the oaths which are the instruments of investigation in courts of justice? And let us with caution indulge the supposition that morality can be maintained without religion. Whatever may be conceded to the influence of refined education on minds of peculiar structure, reason and experience both forbid us to expect that national morality can prevail in exclusion of religious principle." Read it with the precision the course practices. It is not a creedal statement — no church, no Christ, no doctrine is named; it is addressed to "the mere politician" on grounds of civic engineering, the exact register of the founders' consensus (L29): the republic consumes virtue; virtue, for a whole people (whatever "minds of peculiar structure" may manage), is sustained by religion; therefore the statesman who undermines religion saws the branch the republic sits on. It is, equally, not a throwaway: the passage sits at the Address's structural heart, elaborates the Circular's benediction of 1783, and was underlined by the generation that received it — the civic theology of the founding, canonized in its farewell. The honest calibrations attach here as everywhere: the claim is empirical and has been argued ever since (secular democracies would later test the "reason and experience" clause); and the passage has been conscripted both by those who would establish what Washington never named and dismissed by those who cannot believe he meant it. He meant it; and he meant no establishment — the two halves of the settlement L43 named, held together one last time by the one man who could.

## The Grades of Prophecy

Score the warnings honestly, because the Farewell invites it. On parties, Washington lost immediately and totally — the first contested election followed within weeks, the man who signed the Address's fair copy (Hamilton) and the man whose draft underlay it (Madison) already led the rival machines, and American politics has been party politics ever since; yet the deeper clause has aged into the warning's vindication: the description of faction's endgame — jealousies and false alarms, animosity of part against part, the door opened to foreign influence — reads to every generation like its own newspapers, and the two-party system's defenders themselves concede the diagnosis while pleading no alternative. On disunion, prophecy: the geographical parties he begged against formed on the exact line he drew, and the Union was saved only at the price this course's Unit 5 will count. On foreign policy, the great rule governed a century — the republic grew behind its oceans, honoring the letter until the world wars forced the question of whether "temporary alliances for extraordinary emergencies" could describe a permanent nuclear standoff; the argument between the Address's heirs and its revisers is the entire history of American foreign policy since 1917, and both sides still open with this text. And on the supports — the wager runs yet, with the course as its chronicle. Washington rode home to Mount Vernon with thirty months to live; the government he left behind immediately fell into the hands of the two parties he had warned against, led by the two friends whose quarrel had defined his cabinet — and the first great American political war, Hamilton against Jefferson, is the next lesson.
