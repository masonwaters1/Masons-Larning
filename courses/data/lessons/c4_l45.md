The party spirit Washington warned against was not an abstraction to him; it had been sitting at his own cabinet table for four years, wearing two faces. Alexander Hamilton at Treasury and Thomas Jefferson at State conducted, from 1790 to 1793 and by proxy long after, the first great American political war — and this course treats it at full length because it was never really about the 1790s. Hamilton and Jefferson each carried a complete, coherent, incompatible vision of what the Revolution had been for: a commercial-industrial republic of energy and credit, or an agrarian republic of independent freeholders; a strong national government reading its charter generously, or a strictly fenced federation; the future as manufacture, finance, and cities, or the future as land, husbandry, and virtue. Every American economic and constitutional argument since — bank wars, New Deals, federalism fights, the permanent quarrel between Wall Street and Main Street — is recognizably a continuation of this one, which is why historians call American history an argument between Hamilton and Jefferson refereed by events. The lesson draws both visions at their strongest, narrates the fights that made the parties, and marks — because this course watches this thread — the religious geography that was already sorting beneath the politics.

## Two Americas in Two Men

The visions were autobiographies before they were philosophies.[^1] Hamilton — illegitimate, orphaned, Caribbean-born, raised in the counting-houses of a trading island and lifted to power by pure talent through war staff-work and a New York marriage — believed in what had made him: energy, credit, commerce, mobility, the career open to ability; he admired the British system he had fought because it worked — funded debt, national bank, integrated finance — and he feared, from watching the 1780s, not tyranny but dissolution: weakness, not strength, was the American disease. His program at Treasury followed with a system-builder's logic: fund the national debt at face value; assume the states' war debts (binding creditors' interest to the national government's survival — "a national debt, if it is not excessive, will be to us a national blessing," he had written, meaning a tie, not a treasure); charter a national bank to supply currency and credit; encourage manufactures by bounty and tariff so the republic need never again beg abroad for the sinews of its own defense. Jefferson — born to Virginia's planter aristocracy, master of a mountaintop, the most learned man in American public life — believed in what had made him, too: land, independence, and the cultivator's virtue. "Those who labour in the earth are the chosen people of God, if ever he had a chosen people," he had written; dependence "begets subservience and venality"; the mobs of great cities add just so much to the support of pure government "as sores do to the strength of the human body." Where Hamilton saw credit, Jefferson saw corruption — the whole apparatus of debt, bank, and speculation was, to his eyes, the British disease imported: a money interest fattening on the treasury, buying legislators, and re-erecting by finance the aristocracy the Revolution had abolished. Each man, note, saw the other not as mistaken but as a counter-revolutionary: Hamilton read Jefferson's states-rights agrarianism as the Articles' anarchy returning; Jefferson read Hamilton's system as monarchy on the installment plan. The paranoia was mutual, sincere, and — the anthropology lesson would observe — exactly what the Whig lens of the founders' library trains its wearers to see.

## The Battles of the 1790s

The war's set-pieces built the first party system. Assumption and the capital (1790): Hamilton's state-debt assumption stalled in Congress until the famous dinner at Jefferson's lodgings brokered the era's archetypal logroll — assumption passed, and the permanent capital went south to the Potomac; Jefferson spent the rest of his life insisting he had been "duped" into the worst error of his career. The Bank (1791): Washington, doubtful, solicited opinions, and received the two founding documents of American constitutional interpretation — Jefferson's strict construction (the Constitution grants enumerated powers; "necessary and proper" means necessary, not convenient; a bank is nowhere granted, therefore forbidden — else the enumeration is waste paper) and Hamilton's implied powers (a government granted ends is granted the ordinary means of ends; "necessary" in law means needful and useful; the bank is a means to the granted powers of taxation, borrowing, and commerce). Washington signed; Hamilton's opinion became, through Marshall's McCulloch a generation later, the constitutional law of the United States; and the two readings have contested every expansion of federal power from that day to this — the reader now holds the origin of a two-century argument. The French Revolution then poured ideology on the economics: Jefferson's party embraced the republican cause in France (long past the point, his critics charged fairly, where its guillotines forfeited the embrace — his "Adam and Eve" letter, willing to see half the earth desolated for liberty's victory, is the low point his admirers must own); Hamilton's party recoiled toward Britain; Citizen Genêt's outrages, the Neutrality Proclamation, and above all the Jay Treaty (1795) — peace with Britain purchased at a price the Republicans called dishonor — turned division into organized, national, newspaper-armed parties: Fenno's Gazette of the United States against Freneau's National Gazette, the latter edited by a State Department clerk hired by Jefferson for the purpose, both cabinets thus subsidizing the press war against each other while the president above them aged visibly under the abuse.

## The Religious Geography Beneath

This course adds the layer the economic histories skip: the parties sorted religiously from birth, and the sorting explains much that follows. The Federalists were the party of the New England Standing Order — the established Congregational clergy (L41's holdouts) preached Federalism nearly as a doctrine, and would shortly preach against Jefferson as an infidel in the campaign of 1800 (next lessons); order, hierarchy, and the alliance of magistrate and minister came naturally to the establishment's heirs. The Jeffersonians gathered the dissenters: the Baptists whose jails and petitions won Virginia's disestablishment (L41) voted their deliverer's party with near unanimity — Leland's people, for whom the anti-establishment party was the godly choice precisely because it would keep the state out of the soul; add the Presbyterian and Methodist backcountry and the immigrant sects, and the "infidel" candidate's coalition was, on the ground, substantially more evangelical than the "Christian" party's. Mark the inversion, because it is the founding settlement (L43) working exactly as designed and confounding every simple mapping of faith onto party: the devout divided over the state, not over God — establishment believers against free-church believers — and American religious politics has replayed the pattern, in shifting costumes, ever since.

## The Verdict of Time

Close with the honest scorecard, which is double like everything in this course. Hamilton won the country: the funded debt, the bank's descendants, the manufacturing republic, implied powers, the energetic executive — the modern United States is built on Hamiltonian chassis, a verdict sealed when Jefferson in power kept the system he had denounced (and stretched implied powers past anything Hamilton dared, buying Louisiana). Jefferson won the story: the yeoman ideal, the suspicion of finance and central power, the language of equality against aristocracy — Americans have lived in Hamilton's economy while reciting Jefferson's creed for two centuries, and both parties in every era compete to claim his rhetoric. Each vision carried a shadow the course will not launder: Hamilton's system did breed the speculation and money-politics Jefferson foretold (the first insider-trading scandal, Duer's, arrived on schedule in 1792), and his taste for energy flirted, in the Adams years, with the repression the next lesson examines; Jefferson's agrarian virtue was grown on slave labor — the chosen people of God, on his own plantation, were driven in the fields he philosophized above, the contradiction this course has met before and will meet again at Monticello (L47). Two flawed prophets, one permanent argument — and in 1797 the argument inherited the government itself, as Washington's warnings went home with him and the presidency passed to a man who belonged wholly to neither camp and would be destroyed by both: John Adams, the Alien and Sedition Acts, and the republic's first survival test are next.
