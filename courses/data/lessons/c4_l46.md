Ten years after the First Amendment's framers promised that Congress would make no law abridging the freedom of speech or of the press, Congress made exactly that law, a Federalist administration enforced it against its critics, and the courts — staffed by the founding generation — upheld it. The Adams presidency (1797–1801) is the republic's first survival test, and this course gives it a full lesson because it proves, within a decade of ratification, every dark theorem the Constitution's own architecture assumed: that parchment barriers stop nothing by themselves (Madison's phrase, vindicated against his hopes); that party spirit under war fever will trample the freedoms its members swore to; that the anthropology of Federalist 51 applies to the men who wrote it. It is also the story of the strange, honest, impossible man at the center — John Adams, who whipped up the storm, rode it, was nearly consumed by it, and then, in the act he counted the greatest of his life, defied his own party to end it — losing the presidency and saving the peace.

## War Fever

Adams inherited a French crisis with a one-party cabinet and a three-vote margin.[^1] France, reading the Jay Treaty as an Anglo-American alliance, had turned its privateers loose on American shipping; Adams sent a peace commission, and the French foreign minister Talleyrand's agents — anonymized in the dispatches as X, Y, and Z — demanded a douceur: a large loan and a quarter-million-dollar bribe before negotiations could even begin. When Adams published the dispatches in the spring of 1798, the country ignited. "Millions for defense, but not one cent for tribute" swept the toasts; Adams — dour, unpopular, suddenly cheered in the streets — got his navy department, his frigates, and an undeclared naval war (the Quasi-War) that American captains waged with relish in the Caribbean; Washington was called from Mount Vernon to head a provisional army with Hamilton as its effective commander — an army with no French invasion to fight and, Republicans noted with alarm, plenty of domestic critics to overawe. The fever's religious register belongs to this course's record: Adams proclaimed national fast days in 1798 and 1799 in language more explicitly Christian than Washington had ever used, the New England pulpits preached the crisis as a war of Christian order against French infidelity — the same year brought the Illuminati panic, respectable divines warning from Boston pulpits of an atheist conspiracy behind the Jacobins and their American friends — and the whole apparatus of covenant rhetoric, fast and jeremiad, was mobilized by one party against the other: the civic theology of the founding, weaponized for faction within a decade of the Farewell's warning. (Adams himself, years later, half-seriously blamed the fast days for his defeat — the spectacle of presidential religion had smelled, he thought, of establishment to every dissenter in America, and "the National Fast recommended by me turned me out of office." The founders' settlement, policing its own violators.)

## The Acts

On the fever's crest the Federalist Congress passed, in the summer of 1798, the four measures remembered together as the Alien and Sedition Acts. The Naturalization Act stretched citizenship's waiting period from five years to fourteen — the immigrant vote ran Republican, and everyone knew it. The Alien Friends Act empowered the president to deport any alien he judged dangerous, without charge, hearing, or trial; the Alien Enemies Act (the one act of the four never repealed, and invoked in later American wars) governed enemy nationals in wartime. And the Sedition Act made it a federal crime, punishable by fine and imprisonment, to "write, print, utter or publish... any false, scandalous and malicious writing" against the government, Congress, or the president, "with intent to defame" or bring them "into contempt or disrepute." Its partisan engineering was naked: the vice president — Jefferson — was pointedly omitted from the protected list, and the act was set to expire March 3, 1801, the last day of Adams's term, so the weapon could not fall into Republican hands. Enforcement matched design: some twenty-five arrests and more than a dozen prosecutions, aimed with precision at the Republican press — Congressman Matthew Lyon of Vermont, jailed four months for accusing the president of "ridiculous pomp, foolish adulation, and selfish avarice," campaigned from his cell and was re-elected by a landslide, his constituents paying his fine by subscription; the scandalmonger James Callender, convicted before Justice Samuel Chase in a trial so partisan it later furnished articles for Chase's impeachment; editors of the leading opposition papers hauled in seriatim; even a New Jersey tavern drunk fined for a joke about the presidential backside. Federalist judges charged juries like party men; the truth-as-defense provision proved hollow (prove the "falsity" of an opinion); and the First Amendment, invoked at every trial, stopped nothing — the courts of the 1790s read it narrowly, and no Supreme Court test ever came. The lesson the course draws is the one Madison had feared and the anthropology lesson (L39) predicted: rights on parchment are as strong as the political culture defending them, and in 1798 the culture failed its first real test — the generation that wrote the First Amendment suspended it for a newspaper war.

## The Opposition and the Dangerous Remedy

The Republican counterattack created its own long shadow. With the federal courts closed as a venue, Jefferson and Madison — secretly, the vice president drafting against the government he served — turned to the state legislatures: the Kentucky Resolutions (Jefferson's, 1798–99) and Virginia Resolutions (Madison's, 1798) declared the acts unconstitutional and asserted that the states, as parties to the constitutional compact, possessed the right to judge infractions — Kentucky's second set using the fateful word "nullification." The resolutions' immediate career was failure (no other state concurred; several answered that constitutionality was the judiciary's business), their argumentative career immense: the compact theory they floated would be seized by Calhoun for nullification in 1832 and by secessionists in 1861 — Madison, still alive for the first of these, protesting in his last years that his resolutions had meant interposition-by-protest, not state veto, and being quoted against himself anyway. The course marks the tragedy's structure without excusing anyone: an unconstitutional repression begot an extra-constitutional remedy, and both precedents outlived their occasion — the Sedition Act furnishing every later war's censors their example, the Resolutions furnishing every later disunionist his theory. The system's actual self-correction came, instead, through the channel the framers built and the one they hadn't: an election, conducted by the parties they never wanted.

## Adams Chooses Peace

The fever broke because the president broke it. Through 1799, with his party clamoring for declared war and Hamilton dreaming of armies (and of Louisiana, and of marching them through the opposition's Virginia — the letters exist), Adams received signals that Talleyrand would now negotiate — and, alone, against his cabinet (still secretly loyal to Hamilton), without consulting his party, he sent a new peace mission to France. It split the Federalists irreparably; Hamilton wrote and circulated a pamphlet attacking his own party's president; and the mission succeeded — the Convention of 1800 ended the Quasi-War and dissolved the old entangling alliance of 1778 on honorable terms. Adams had traded his political life for peace with open eyes, and he knew the exchange's worth: "I desire no other inscription over my gravestone," he wrote afterward, "than: 'Here lies John Adams, who took upon himself the responsibility of the peace with France in the year 1800.'" The course pauses on the act, because it is the era's cleanest specimen of the virtue the machine cannot manufacture (L39): a president spending power for principle, against his faction, at known personal cost — the system's indispensable support, in one stubborn New England conscience. It was not enough to save him. The election of 1800 arrived with the Sedition Act's prisoners as Republican martyrs, the fast days as Republican talking points, the Federalists at each other's throats — and with the most savage religious campaign in American history, the pulpits of New England against the "infidel" of Monticello, whose complicated soul, and whose wall of separation, are the next lesson.
