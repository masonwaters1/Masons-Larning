We come now to the spring that lies closest to America's own shores, and in some ways the most directly influential of them all. Jerusalem gave the moral law and the dignity of the person; Athens and Rome gave reason and the architecture of the republic; the Christian synthesis wove them into the backbone of the Western mind. But ideas, however lofty, must be embodied in working institutions, or they remain dreams. It was England that took the inheritance and forged it into practical machinery — into courts, charters, rights, and the stubborn, lived habit of self-government. The American colonists did not arrive as blank-slate revolutionaries inventing liberty from scratch. They arrived as Englishmen, carrying with them the most developed tradition of ordered liberty the world had yet produced, and when they finally rebelled, they did so, at first, in the name of rights they believed were their ancient birthright as Englishmen. This lesson traces that tradition — limited government before there was even a word for it.

## A Charter Wrung from a King

The tradition has a famous symbolic beginning: a meadow called Runnymede, beside the Thames, in the year 1215. King John of England was a disaster — militarily incompetent, financially rapacious, and contemptuous of the customs that were supposed to bind even a king. He taxed his barons ruinously to fund failed wars, seized property, and trampled established rights, until the barons rose in revolt and forced him, at sword's point, to set his seal to a document of their demands. That document was Magna Carta, the Great Charter.[^1]

It is essential to be honest about what Magna Carta actually was, because it has been heavily mythologized. It was not a democratic charter or a declaration of universal human rights. It was a practical, feudal settlement, largely concerned with the specific grievances of a class of powerful barons against a particular bad king — protections for their property, limits on the taxes and feudal dues the crown could demand, guarantees about inheritance and the treatment of widows. Most of its sixty-some clauses dealt with technical feudal matters long since irrelevant. The "free men" it protected were, in 1215, a small minority; the vast majority of English people, the unfree peasantry, were not its beneficiaries.

And yet — this is the crucial thing — Magna Carta came to mean far more than it originally said, and that meaning became one of the foundation stones of the free world.

## The Principle That Outgrew the Document

What made Magna Carta immortal was a single revolutionary principle embedded in its bargain: that **the king himself is bound by the law.** By forcing the king to agree, in writing, that there were things he could not lawfully do — that he could not tax at will, seize property arbitrarily, or imprison men on his own whim — the barons established, in concrete and enforceable form, the idea that royal power has limits, that the ruler is *under* the law and not above it. This is the same conviction we traced from Jerusalem (the king under God's law) and from Aquinas (the higher law above human law), but now it was no longer a moral or theological principle alone. It was a legal fact, written on parchment, with the king's own seal attached, to which his subjects could and did appeal for centuries afterward.

Two of its clauses, in particular, grew into pillars of liberty. One promised that the crown would not proceed against a free man — seizing, imprisoning, or dispossessing him — except by the lawful judgment of his peers and the law of the land. Here, in embryo, are two of the most precious protections in the Anglo-American legal tradition: **due process of law** (the government may act against you only through proper legal procedure) and **trial by jury** (judgment by one's peers, not by the ruler's command). The seed planted at Runnymede would grow, over centuries, into the guarantee that no person may be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process — words that would echo, almost unchanged, in the American Bill of Rights five and a half centuries later.

## The Common Law: Liberty as Inheritance

Magna Carta did not stand alone; it was one landmark in a larger and even more important English achievement — the **common law**. Unlike legal systems imposed from above by a ruler or codified all at once by a legislator, the English common law grew organically, case by case, over centuries, as judges decided particular disputes and their decisions accumulated into a body of precedent that bound future decisions. It was, in the language we learned from the Scottish thinkers, a spontaneous order — law discovered and refined through experience rather than decreed by any single mind, the wisdom of generations of judges sedimented into a living tradition.

This produced a distinctive and momentous way of thinking about liberty. The "rights of Englishmen" were not understood as abstract philosophical propositions handed down by theorists, but as concrete, inherited possessions — liberties their ancestors had enjoyed and defended, passed down like property from generation to generation, and woven into the fabric of the law itself. An Englishman's freedoms were his birthright, his inheritance, as real and as defensible as his land. This gave English liberty a peculiar toughness: it was rooted not in the fragile conclusions of argument but in the unbroken possession of custom, and an Englishman who felt his rights violated could point not to a philosopher's theory but to the settled law and ancient usage of his own people. This habit of mind — liberty as concrete inheritance rather than abstract theory — would cross the Atlantic intact and shape how the colonists understood their own quarrel with the mother country.

## Parliament and the Power of the Purse

Alongside the law grew an institution that would prove decisive: **Parliament**. Out of the medieval councils in which kings consulted the great men of the realm, there developed gradually the principle that the crown could not levy taxes without the consent of the realm, represented in Parliament. The power of the purse — the right of the people's representatives to grant or withhold the money the government needed — became the great lever of English liberty, the practical check that again and again forced kings to respect rights and redress grievances in exchange for funds. Here was representative government taking root: the conviction that the community has a rightful voice in its own governance, and above all that it may not be taxed without its consent. When American colonists later raised the cry of "no taxation without representation," they were not inventing a slogan; they were invoking one of the oldest and most fundamental principles of the English constitution, and accusing Parliament of violating it.

## The Seventeenth-Century Crucible

These principles might have remained a loose collection of medieval customs had they not been tested, sharpened, and re-forged in the fire of the seventeenth century, when the Stuart kings tried to rule as absolute monarchs and provoked a constitutional struggle that would settle the character of English government — and, downstream, of American government too.

The great legal champion of liberty in this struggle was Sir Edward Coke, a formidable jurist who reached back to Magna Carta and the common law to wield them as weapons against royal absolutism. Coke insisted that even the king was subject to the law, revived the Great Charter as a living guarantee of English liberties, and helped produce the Petition of Right in 1628, which reasserted limits on the crown's power to tax without consent, to imprison without cause, and to impose military rule. The conflict between Crown and Parliament escalated into civil war, the execution of one king, and decades of upheaval. It culminated, after further crisis, in the Glorious Revolution of 1688, when Parliament deposed a king it judged to have violated the constitution and installed new monarchs on agreed terms. The settlement was sealed in the **English Bill of Rights of 1689**, which established the supremacy of Parliament, barred the crown from suspending laws or levying taxes on its own authority, guaranteed free elections and free debate in Parliament, and affirmed a series of subjects' rights.[^2] By the end of the century, England had established, in practice, a limited, constitutional monarchy in which the rule of law and the consent of the governed had triumphed over the claim of absolute royal power.

## The Great Writ: Habeas Corpus

One particular protection that emerged and hardened in this struggle deserves its own mention, because it may be the single most powerful legal shield against tyranny ever devised, and it passed directly into American law. It is the writ of **habeas corpus** — Latin for "you shall have the body" — which the English courts developed and which Parliament strengthened in the Habeas Corpus Act of 1679. The writ is deceptively simple in form and revolutionary in effect: it commands whoever is holding a prisoner to bring that prisoner before a court and *justify the detention* under the law. If no lawful cause can be shown, the prisoner must be released.

The power of this lies in what it makes impossible. Tyranny's oldest and favorite instrument is the secret, indefinite imprisonment of enemies without charge or trial — the disappearance into the dungeon at the ruler's pleasure. Habeas corpus strikes directly at that instrument, for it means that no one, however powerful, may simply lock a person away and forget them; the jailer can always be compelled to answer to a court, "By what authority do you hold this person?" It converts the abstract principle that the ruler is under the law into a concrete, usable remedy that an imprisoned person, or their friends, can actually invoke. The American founders prized it so highly that they wrote it into the body of the Constitution itself — not merely the Bill of Rights — providing that the privilege of the writ may not be suspended except in the gravest emergencies of rebellion or invasion. It is the English constitutional tradition distilled into a single, life-saving procedure.

## Locke and the Philosophy of Liberty

The Glorious Revolution found its philosopher in John Locke, whose *Two Treatises of Government*, published in 1689, gave the English constitutional achievement a systematic theoretical foundation — and provided the single most direct philosophical bridge to the American founding.[^3] Locke argued that human beings possess **natural rights** — to life, liberty, and property — that exist prior to government and that no government may rightly violate. Government, he held, is not the property of kings by divine right but a trust, established by the **consent of the governed** for the limited purpose of protecting these natural rights. And if a government betrays that trust and turns to tyranny, the people retain the right to alter or abolish it — the **right of revolution**.

One can hear, in that summary, the American Declaration of Independence almost word for word. When Jefferson wrote that all men are endowed with unalienable rights to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness, that governments derive their just powers from the consent of the governed, and that the people may alter or abolish a government that becomes destructive of these ends, he was transcribing Locke into the founding charter of a new nation. Locke took the natural-law inheritance we traced through Aquinas, secularized and individualized it into the language of natural rights, and fused it with the English constitutional tradition of consent and limited government — and the founders read him as gospel.

## How It Reached America

The transmission to America was therefore not indirect or accidental; it was direct, conscious, and complete. The colonists were Englishmen, governed by English common law, who carried the rights of Englishmen across the ocean and planted them in colonial charters, assemblies, and courts. They held town meetings and elected legislatures in the deep English habit of local self-government. They claimed Magna Carta, the common law, trial by jury, habeas corpus, due process, and no taxation without representation not as foreign borrowings but as their own inheritance.

This is why the American Revolution began not as a demand for new and untried rights but as a defense of *ancient* ones. The colonists' first and central argument was that the king and Parliament were violating the traditional liberties that were the colonists' birthright as Englishmen — taxing them without their consent, denying them trial by jury, imposing standing armies, subverting the rule of law. They appealed to Coke, to Magna Carta, to the Glorious Revolution, to Locke. Only when these appeals failed did the defense of English liberty transform into the assertion of universal human rights and the founding of a new nation. And when that nation came to write its Constitution and its Bill of Rights, the English inheritance was everywhere: due process, trial by jury, habeas corpus, the prohibition on unreasonable searches, the power of the purse lodged in the people's representatives, the whole architecture of limited, law-bound government. America's liberty was, in its bones, English liberty — extended, universalized, and given a new foundation, but unmistakably inherited.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Truthfulness requires the usual corrective, and here it is twofold. First, the heroic story of English liberty is partly a *retrospective myth*. Magna Carta, as we said, was a baronial power grab whose protections originally reached only a privileged few; the "rights of Englishmen" long excluded the poor, the propertyless, women, and the colonized; and much of the charter's grand later meaning was read back into it by seventeenth-century lawyers like Coke, who needed an ancient pedigree for the liberties they were fighting for in their own day. The tradition was, in part, invented in the act of being remembered.

But — and this is the subtle and important point — the myth did real historical work, and that work was genuine even if the history behind it was embroidered. Because people *believed* that Magna Carta guaranteed their liberty, because they *believed* limited government was their ancient birthright, they acted on that belief, defended it, fought and died for it, and made it true through their actions. A founding myth that inspires people to build and defend free institutions becomes, in its consequences, as real as any literal fact. The second corrective is the familiar shadow: this same tradition of liberty coexisted, without apparent contradiction in the minds of its bearers, with the slave trade, with imperial conquest, and with the dispossession of other peoples — and the colonists who so eloquently demanded the rights of Englishmen for themselves frequently denied them to the enslaved and the native peoples in their midst. The inheritance was magnificent and real; it was also, for centuries, monstrously incomplete in its application. Both belong to the honest record, and the long American struggle to close the gap between the professed inheritance and its actual reach is much of the story this course will tell.

With England's practical machinery of ordered liberty now added to the moral inheritance of Jerusalem, the reason of Athens and Rome, and the synthesis of the Christian centuries, the springs of the American order are nearly all gathered. One great force remains before we finally cross the Atlantic: the Protestant Reformation, which would crack the old order open, place the conscience of the individual at the center, and supercharge every element of the inheritance with a new and revolutionary energy. To the Reformation we turn next — and then, at last, to America itself.
