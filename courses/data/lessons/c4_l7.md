The previous lesson described the Reformation's political earthquake — the doctrine of the priesthood of all believers, the empowered conscience, the fracturing of Christendom. But there was a more specific and more dangerous idea that emerged from the Reformed wing of Protestantism, an idea that would travel across the Atlantic and help arm a revolution: the doctrine that subjects may lawfully resist, and even depose, a tyrannical ruler. This was no abstract speculation. It was hammered out by men whose fellow believers were being slaughtered, and it gave the modern world some of its most important arguments for limited government, popular sovereignty, and the right of resistance. To understand why American colonists believed they could rightfully take up arms against their king, we must understand the Calvinist resistance theorists who came two centuries before them.

## The Problem of the Godly Subject

The Reformers did not begin as revolutionaries. Quite the opposite. Luther and Calvin both inherited and largely affirmed the traditional Christian teaching on obedience to authority, rooted in the Apostle Paul's command in Romans 13 that every soul be subject to the governing authorities, for there is no authority except from God. The magistrate, even a bad one, was God's instrument, and the Christian's duty was generally to obey and to endure. Calvin himself was deeply wary of rebellion and disorder, and his default teaching counseled submission to rulers and patience under unjust ones.

But the Reformed churches soon found themselves in an agonizing situation that pressed hard against this counsel of submission. Across Europe, Protestant minorities lived under Catholic monarchs who regarded them as heretics to be suppressed, and who frequently moved to persecute, imprison, and kill them. The question became unavoidable and urgent: what is a faithful Christian to do when the God-ordained magistrate commands him to violate the law of God, or sets out to destroy God's true church? Is obedience still owed to a ruler who has become an instrument not of God's justice but of God's enemies? The pressure of persecution forced the Reformed tradition to develop, carefully and reluctantly, a theory of when and how a tyrant might lawfully be resisted. The decisive catalyst was a massacre.

## The Massacre That Changed Everything

In 1572, on the feast of St. Bartholomew, the simmering religious conflict in France erupted into horror. In what became known as the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre, thousands of French Protestants — the Huguenots — were slaughtered in Paris and across France in a wave of killing that the crown was widely seen to have sanctioned. It was a trauma that seared itself into the Protestant memory of Europe, and it shattered, for many Reformed thinkers, any remaining confidence that patient submission to Catholic monarchs was a sufficient or faithful response.

In the wake of the massacre, a series of remarkable political works poured from the pens of Huguenot and other Reformed writers — works that have come to be called the writings of the *monarchomachs*, the "king-fighters." Driven by the desperate need to justify resistance to a murderous crown, these thinkers articulated, with new boldness and rigor, the principles that would justify lawful opposition to tyranny. They were not writing political philosophy for its own sake; they were writing to answer the question of whether their slaughtered brethren might have been defended, and whether the survivors might lawfully resist. Out of that bloody necessity came some of the foundational texts of modern resistance theory.

## Beza and the Lesser Magistrates

The first towering figure was Theodore Beza, Calvin's hand-picked successor as the leader of the Reformed church in Geneva. In his work on the rights of magistrates, Beza moved carefully beyond Calvin's caution to articulate a crucial principle that would become central to the whole tradition: the doctrine of the **lesser magistrates**.[^1]

Beza's argument was subtle and important. He did not endorse private individuals taking up arms against the king on their own authority — that would be mere rebellion and anarchy. Rather, he argued that when a higher ruler becomes a tyrant, the *lower* or *inferior* magistrates — the lesser officials, regional governors, nobles, and local authorities who also hold genuine God-given authority — have not only the right but the *duty* to resist him and to protect the people from his tyranny. These lesser magistrates are themselves ordained by God; their authority is real and not merely delegated by the king; and when the supreme ruler violates his sacred trust, the lower magistrates are bound to interpose themselves, to restrain him, and if necessary to lead organized resistance. Resistance, in other words, was to be *constitutional* and *ordered*, led by those who already bore lawful authority, not chaotic and private. This doctrine of the lesser magistrate as the lawful agent of resistance to tyranny would prove enormously influential, threading through two centuries of Protestant political thought and reaching, eventually, the legislatures and leaders of colonial America.

## The Vindiciae and the Covenant of Government

The most famous and systematic of these works appeared in 1579 under a pseudonym — *Vindiciae contra tyrannos*, "A Defense of Liberty Against Tyrants," published under the name "Stephanus Junius Brutus," its true author almost certainly one of the leading Huguenot thinkers of the day. This book gathered the emerging resistance theory into a powerful and enduring whole, and its central idea was the *covenant*.[^2]

The *Vindiciae* argued that political authority rests on a double covenant. There is a covenant between God on one side and the king and people together on the other, by which both are bound to uphold true religion and God's law. And there is a covenant between the king and the people, by which the king promises to rule justly and according to law, and the people promise their obedience in return. This covenantal framework — which the reader will recognize as a direct descendant of the biblical covenant idea traced back to Jerusalem in an earlier lesson — transformed the understanding of kingship. The king is not an absolute master who owns his subjects; he is a party to a binding agreement, holding his authority *conditionally*, on the terms of the covenant. When a king breaks that covenant — when he subverts true religion or rules as a tyrant in violation of law and justice — he forfeits his claim to obedience, and the people, acting through their lesser magistrates and representatives, may lawfully resist him. Embedded in this argument was a momentous idea: that political authority flows, under God, *through* the people, and that rulers are accountable to the community they serve. Here, in embryo, is the contract theory of government and the seed of popular sovereignty.

## Scotland: Knox and Buchanan

The same fire burned north of England, in Reformed Scotland, where it produced some of the most radical formulations of all. John Knox, the thunderous founder of Scottish Presbyterianism, pressed the right of resistance with characteristic boldness, arguing that the people and the nobility had a duty to resist and even remove an idolatrous ruler who led the nation away from true religion. His was a fierce and uncompromising voice for the principle that ungodly tyranny need not be endured.

Even more systematically, the Scottish scholar George Buchanan produced a treatise on the law of government among the Scots that advanced a strikingly modern theory of popular sovereignty. Buchanan argued that political power originates with the people, that kings are created by the people for the people's benefit, that the king is bound by the law and subject to it rather than above it, and that a king who becomes a tyrant may be called to account, resisted, and removed by the community that made him. The people, not the king, were sovereign; the king was their servant, accountable to them and to the law. These were ideas of great daring for their age, and they laid down principles — government by consent, the accountability of rulers, the sovereignty of the people under law — that would echo through the next two centuries and find their fullest political expression on the far side of the Atlantic.

## From Romans 13 to Resistance

How did thinkers steeped in the Apostle Paul's command to obey the governing authorities come to justify armed resistance to a king? This was the theological knot the resistance theorists had to untie, and the care with which they did it is precisely what kept their doctrine from collapsing into a license for any malcontent to rebel. Their solution turned on a series of crucial distinctions.

The first was the distinction between the *office* and the *person* who holds it. The authority that God ordains, the resistance theorists argued, is the office of the magistrate — the lawful institution of rule for the common good and the punishment of evil. Obedience is owed to that divinely-ordained office. But a ruler who turns tyrant ceases to act according to his office; in persecuting the innocent and warring against God's law, he acts not as a magistrate but against the very purpose for which the office exists. To resist him in such acts is therefore not to resist God's ordinance but to defend it against a man who has betrayed it. The second distinction was between *private* and *public* persons. A private individual, acting on his own, has no authority to take up the sword against the ruler; for him, the counsel of patient endurance and suffering still stands. But the *lesser magistrates* — the lower officials who themselves hold a genuine, God-given share of public authority — stand on different ground. When they resist a tyrant, they act not as private avengers but in their own lawful public capacity, discharging their own duty to protect the people. This is why the theory insisted that legitimate resistance be led by those who already bore authority: it kept resistance within the framework of ordained order rather than dissolving it into anarchy. With these distinctions, the resistance theorists could honor Paul's command in full while denying that it required the godly to stand by as a tyrant destroyed both church and people. Obedience to the office, yes; submission to a covenant-breaking tyrant's war on the innocent, no.

## Lex, Rex: The Law Is King

If we want the single clearest link in the chain that carried Calvinist resistance theory into the English-speaking world and on to America, we find it in Scotland, in the work of the Presbyterian theologian Samuel Rutherford. In 1644, amid the upheaval of the English Civil War, Rutherford published a treatise whose very title was a manifesto: *Lex, Rex* — "The Law and the King," or more pointedly, "The Law Is King."[^3]

The phrase inverted the reigning doctrine of the age. Against the claim of kings to rule by divine right, answerable to God alone and above all human law, Rutherford argued that the law stands above the king, not the king above the law. The king is made by the people, for the people's good; his authority is a trust conferred conditionally; he is bound by the law as much as any subject; and a king who becomes a tyrant may be lawfully resisted by the people and their representatives. Here the whole tradition — the covenant, the conditional authority of rulers, the lesser magistrate, popular sovereignty under law — was gathered into a single, forceful, systematic English-language statement, written by a Scottish Presbyterian in the thick of a constitutional revolution. *Lex, Rex* was burned by royal authority after the monarchy was restored, which is its own testimony to how dangerous the established powers found it. But the book and its ideas crossed the Atlantic with the Presbyterian and Puritan stream, and "the law is king" became, in substance, an American conviction. When American revolutionaries insisted that even the king was subject to law and that the people were the ultimate source of legitimate authority, they were speaking Rutherford's language — and through him, the language of Beza, the *Vindiciae*, and the whole Reformed tradition of resistance.

## The Road to America

It is here that the deep relevance of all this to the American story comes into focus. The Calvinist resistance theory of the sixteenth century did not stay in sixteenth-century Europe. It was carried into the English-speaking world by the Puritans and the broader Reformed tradition, and it became part of the political inheritance of the American colonies, which were settled so heavily by Calvinists steeped in exactly these ideas.

When American colonists, generations later, came to justify their resistance to the British crown, they reached — often instinctively, through a political culture saturated with Reformed assumptions — for precisely the principles the monarchomachs had forged. The idea that government rests on a covenant or compact; that rulers hold authority conditionally and are bound by higher law; that a king who violates the people's rights becomes a tyrant who may lawfully be resisted; that resistance, to be legitimate, should be led by the people's lawful representatives and lower magistrates rather than by mobs — all of this was the native language of the Reformed political tradition, and all of it can be heard in the arguments of the American Revolution. When the Continental Congress and the colonial assemblies — themselves a kind of lesser magistrate — led organized, constitutional resistance to King George III, and when the Declaration of Independence justified that resistance by appeal to a covenant of rights that the king had broken, they were drawing, whether they fully knew it or not, on a stream of political thought that ran back through the Puritans to Beza, the *Vindiciae*, and the blood of St. Bartholomew's Day.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Truthfulness requires two important qualifications, lest we paint these thinkers as liberals before their time. First, the Calvinist resistance theorists were emphatically *not* democrats, and we should not anachronistically claim them as such. Their theory empowered the *lesser magistrates* — the nobles, officials, and elites who already held authority — not the common people directly; resistance was to be led from above, by those with lawful standing, not by the populace at large. And their immediate concern was frequently confessional self-interest: they wanted liberty for *their own* religion against persecution, not a general principle of religious freedom for all. A Calvinist resistance theorist defending the right to resist a Catholic tyrant might have few qualms about a Calvinist regime suppressing dissenters in turn.

Second, intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that resistance theory was not uniquely Protestant. Catholic thinkers, especially the brilliant Jesuit theologians of the same era — men like Mariana, Suárez, and Bellarmine — developed sophisticated and in some ways parallel theories of popular sovereignty, the conditional nature of royal authority, and even the right of tyrannicide. The idea that authority originates with the community and that tyrants may be resisted was, in this period, a broadly Christian development drawing on older medieval and scholastic roots, not a Protestant monopoly. What the Calvinists distinctively contributed was the covenantal framing, the doctrine of the lesser magistrate, and above all the transmission of these ideas into the Anglo-American Puritan world that would become the seedbed of the United States. Crediting them fairly means neither inflating their modernity nor denying their decisive influence on the political tradition America would inherit.

With the springs of the inheritance now fully gathered — the covenant from Jerusalem, the republic from Rome, the higher law from the Christian synthesis, ordered liberty from England, and the right of resistance from the Reformation — our long journey upstream is complete. In the next unit, at last, the ships set sail, and the American story proper begins.
