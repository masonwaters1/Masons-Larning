The fortunes of the titans rested on the labor of millions, and to see the Gilded Age whole we must turn from the mansions on Fifth Avenue to the tenements a few miles south, where the workers who built the industrial economy lived crowded, striving, and often desperate lives. In 1890 a journalist named Jacob Riis published a book whose title gave the age one of its enduring phrases: *How the Other Half Lives*. Riis, armed with a camera and a reporter's eye, took his middle-class readers into the slums of New York and showed them a world they had preferred not to see — the overcrowded, disease-ridden, poverty-stricken existence of the immigrant working poor. This lesson enters that world: the labor, the immigration, and the swelling cities that were the underside of Gilded Age splendor, and the conflicts they bred.

## The Cost of the Machine

Industrial wealth was produced by industrial labor, and the conditions of that labor were, for millions, brutal.[^1] Workers in the mills, mines, and factories of the age commonly toiled ten, twelve, or more hours a day, six days a week, for wages that kept families perpetually on the edge of want. The work was dangerous — machines maimed and killed with grim regularity, mines collapsed and exploded, and there was no meaningful safety regulation, no workers' compensation, no insurance against injury, illness, or unemployment. Child labor was widespread; children worked in mills and breakers and factories, their small hands valued for certain tasks, their childhoods consumed by the machine. When a worker was hurt, or sickened, or grew too old to labor, there was no safety net beneath him but the charity of family, church, and neighbors. The contrast with the palaces of the wealthy, only blocks away in the great cities, was the moral scandal of the age, and Riis's photographs made it impossible to ignore.

The city itself was the crucible of this new life. American cities exploded in size during these decades, swelling with workers drawn from the countryside and, above all, from abroad. They were places of astonishing energy and innovation — the age of the skyscraper, the electric light, the streetcar, the department store — and of appalling squalor, where families packed into airless tenements, sanitation failed, disease spread, and the poor lived heaped upon one another in conditions that shocked reformers into action. The Gilded Age city was both the engine of national prosperity and the site of its deepest human misery, and the two were inseparable: the same growth that raised the towers filled the tenements.

## The Great Migration of Peoples

Much of the labor that powered the age came from an immigration of unprecedented scale, and this migration reshaped the nation permanently. Where earlier arrivals had come largely from Northern and Western Europe, the new wave, cresting in these decades, drew increasingly from Southern and Eastern Europe — Italians, Poles, Slavs, Greeks, and millions of Jews fleeing the persecution and poverty of the Russian Empire and beyond. They came seeking work and freedom, and they poured through the great gateway of Ellis Island, which opened in New York Harbor in 1892 to process the flood of newcomers. They provided the muscle for the mines, mills, and sweatshops, and they transformed the religious and cultural character of the country, swelling the Catholic and Jewish populations and remaking the American city into a mosaic of languages, faiths, and traditions.

This transformation, examined more fully in the lessons on Catholic and Jewish immigration that follow, provoked a powerful backlash. Nativism — hostility to the foreign-born — flared repeatedly, expressed in suspicion of Catholics, discrimination against Jews, and eventually in laws restricting immigration from particular regions. The newcomers were often met with contempt, exploited for their labor, crowded into ethnic enclaves, and blamed for the very poverty and disorder that the industrial system imposed upon them. Yet within those enclaves they built churches and synagogues, mutual aid societies, newspapers, and communities that sustained them and, in time, wove themselves into the fabric of American life. And into the vacuum of services that the era's minimal government left unfilled stepped the urban political machine — organizations like New York's Tammany Hall, which, under bosses like the notorious William Tweed, traded jobs, favors, food, and help to immigrant voters in exchange for their loyalty at the polls, mixing genuine assistance with breathtaking corruption.

## The Labor Wars

The chasm between capital and labor could not remain peaceful, and the Gilded Age was punctuated by industrial conflict of a violence that has largely faded from popular memory but that contemporaries experienced as something close to open class warfare. Workers, powerless as individuals against the great corporations, began to organize — first in bodies like the Knights of Labor, which sought to unite all workers in a broad reforming movement, and then in the more durable American Federation of Labor under Samuel Gompers, which concentrated on skilled trades and concrete gains in wages and hours. Against them stood the corporations, backed by private security forces, the courts, and, when it came to it, the power of the state.

The result was a series of bitter and bloody confrontations. The Great Railroad Strike of 1877 erupted across the country and was suppressed by federal troops, leaving scores dead. The Haymarket affair in Chicago in 1886, when a bomb thrown at a labor rally led to deaths, a sensational trial, and executions, seared the specter of anarchist violence into the public mind and set back the labor cause. The Homestead strike of 1892, at one of Carnegie's own steelworks, became a pitched battle between strikers and hired guards. The Pullman strike of 1894 paralyzed the nation's railroads and was again broken by federal intervention. In each case, workers with genuine grievances confronted overwhelming power, and the violence ran in both directions; the strikes were often crushed, and the broader public, frightened by disorder and by the rhetoric of radicalism, frequently sided with the forces of order against the workers. The labor question — whether and how the interests of those who worked could be balanced against the power of those who owned — remained unresolved, and it would echo through American politics for generations.

## The Churches Respond

The suffering of the industrial poor did not go unnoticed by the nation's Christians, and out of the tenement districts and the labor conflicts arose a movement that sought to bring the gospel to bear on the social crisis. The Social Gospel, treated at length in the next lesson, insisted that Christianity was not merely a matter of individual salvation but demanded justice in the here and now — that the misery of the slums and the exploitation of the worker were not simply unfortunate but sinful, an offense against a God who cared for the poor. Its great voice, the Baptist minister Walter Rauschenbusch, forged in a poor immigrant parish in New York, called the churches to confront the structures of an unjust economic order rather than merely to console its victims. The Social Gospel was the religious conscience of the age turning against the Social Darwinism and laissez-faire complacency examined in the previous lesson, and it would feed directly into the reform politics of the coming Progressive Era.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The picture of Gilded Age labor and immigration is one of genuine hardship, but it must be held in tension with an equally genuine story of opportunity and rising fortune, and neither half should be allowed to erase the other. The conditions of industrial labor were often brutal, and the inequality of the age was real and shocking; yet over these same decades, wages and living standards, taken as a whole, gradually rose, and for millions of immigrants the crowded, difficult American city nonetheless offered a freedom and a prospect of advancement that the old country had denied them. The tenement was misery, but it was also, for many, a way station toward something better, and the descendants of those who passed through Ellis Island largely made good on the promise that drew their forebears across the ocean. To tell only the story of exploitation is as partial as to tell only the story of opportunity.

The labor conflicts, too, resist a simple moral. The workers' grievances were real and their cause largely just, and the power arrayed against them was often used ruthlessly; yet some of the movements of the age did embrace genuine radicalism and violence, and the public's fear of disorder was not wholly irrational. Immigration was both an enormous enrichment of American life and a genuine disruption that strained cities, wages, and social cohesion, and the nativist reaction, though frequently ugly and unjust, arose from real dislocations as well as from prejudice. The political machines that preyed upon the immigrant poor also, undeniably, served them when no one else would. The Gilded Age city was neither simply a hell of exploitation nor simply a ladder of opportunity, but both at once, tangled inseparably together — and it is precisely that entanglement, rather than any tidy verdict, that the honest student must learn to hold.
