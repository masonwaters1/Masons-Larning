Beneath the industrial transformation and the crowded cities ran the machinery of Gilded Age politics, and it was, by common consent, the most corrupt and least inspiring period in the history of American government. Yet it was also an age of fierce economic conflict and rising demands for reform, in which farmers revolted, workers organized, panics shook the nation, and a great debate over money and power convulsed the republic. And it was, tragically, the age in which the promise of Reconstruction was abandoned and the long night of Jim Crow descended over the South. To complete the portrait of the Gilded Age, we must look at how the nation was governed, how its economy lurched from boom to bust, how the discontented fought back, and how, amid it all, the country turned its back on the freedpeople it had so recently emancipated.

## The Politics of the Spoils

Gilded Age national politics was an arena of patronage, corruption, and remarkable mediocrity.[^1] The presidents of the era are, with few exceptions, forgotten men, and power lay less in the White House than in the party machines that ran on the spoils system — the practice of rewarding political supporters with government jobs regardless of merit. Elections were fiercely contested and closely divided, but they turned more on party loyalty, patronage, and sectional memory than on great questions of principle, and the government they produced was notable chiefly for its passivity and its venality. Corruption was pervasive and often spectacular: the Crédit Mobilier scandal exposed the bribery of congressmen in the financing of the transcontinental railroad; the Tweed Ring plundered New York City of staggering sums; and the general atmosphere of graft led critics to speak, only half in jest, of the best Congress that money could buy. The federal government, wedded to a philosophy of laissez-faire, largely stood aside while the industrial economy remade the country, intervening mainly to protect property and suppress disorder.

Yet the very scale of the corruption eventually provoked a reaction. The assassination of President James Garfield in 1881 by a disappointed office-seeker dramatized the evils of the spoils system and led to the Pendleton Civil Service Reform Act of 1883, which began, haltingly, to establish a professional civil service based on merit rather than patronage. It was a first, modest step toward the reform of American government, and it signaled that the excesses of the Gilded Age order were beginning to generate their own correction.

## The Money Question

If corruption was the era's political scandal, the "money question" was its great economic battleground, and it pitted the interests of creditors against debtors, of industrial capital against the agrarian countryside. At its heart lay a technical-sounding dispute over the currency — whether the nation's money should rest solely on gold, or whether silver should also be freely coined to expand the money supply. Behind the technicality lay a fierce clash of interests. A currency anchored only to gold tended toward deflation, in which the value of money rose and prices fell; this benefited creditors and bondholders, whose loans were repaid in dearer dollars, but it fell like a hammer upon farmers and other debtors, who watched the prices of their crops sink while the real burden of their mortgages grew heavier year by year. The demand for the free coinage of silver became the rallying cry of the debtor and the farmer, who saw in cheaper, more abundant money their deliverance from a system rigged against them.

The economy of the age lurched violently, magnifying these grievances. The Panic of 1873 and the long depression that followed, and then the still more severe Panic of 1893, threw millions out of work, bankrupted businesses, and devastated the countryside. For farmers, already squeezed by falling crop prices and by their dependence on the railroads and banks that set the terms of their existence, these crises were catastrophic. The Gilded Age, for all its glittering wealth, was an age of profound economic insecurity for those at the bottom, and their suffering fueled one of the great protest movements in American history.

## The Revolt of the Farmers

Out of the agrarian distress arose Populism, a genuine mass uprising of the rural discontented against the Gilded Age order. It began with the Grange and the Farmers' Alliances, cooperative movements through which farmers sought to defend themselves against the railroads, the banks, and the middlemen who controlled their fate, and it swelled into the People's Party, or Populists, who mounted a sweeping challenge to the political and economic establishment. Their demands were radical for the age: government regulation or ownership of the railroads, the free coinage of silver, a graduated income tax, and reforms to give ordinary people more direct control over their government. Populism gave voice to a deep sense that the promise of American life was being betrayed, that a small class of the wealthy had captured the nation's economy and government, and that the common people — above all the farmers who fed the country — were being ground down.

The movement reached its climax in the election of 1896, when the Democratic Party, fusing with the Populist cause, nominated the young orator William Jennings Bryan, who electrified the nation with a speech demanding the free coinage of silver and clothing the farmers' economic cause in the language of Scripture, declaring that mankind should not be crucified upon a cross of gold. Bryan's campaign, examined more fully in a later lesson, fused agrarian protest, monetary reform, and religious fervor into a single crusade against the moneyed interests of the East. He lost, and with his defeat the Populist moment passed; but the grievances it expressed did not die, and many of its once-radical demands would be enacted, in time, by the Progressive movement that followed.

## The Nadir

There is one more strand to the Gilded Age story, and it is the darkest. The same decades that saw the nation absorbed in industry, immigration, and the money question saw it turn its back, deliberately and comprehensively, on the freedpeople of the South. With the end of Reconstruction in 1877, the federal government withdrew its protection of Black citizens, and the white South moved to reestablish its supremacy through law and terror. The system that emerged — the disenfranchisement of Black voters, the imposition of rigid racial segregation under the Jim Crow laws, and the enforcement of the whole order by the horror of lynching — reduced the freedpeople to a subordinate caste and stripped away, one by one, the rights the Civil War amendments had promised. Historians have called these decades the "nadir" of American race relations, the lowest point since emancipation, and it coincided precisely with the North's growing eagerness for sectional reconciliation and industrial progress. The nation, hungry to reunite white Americans and to get on with the business of building wealth, purchased that reunion at the price of the Black American, whose claim to the fruits of freedom was quietly, and then brutally, set aside.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The Gilded Age resists both the cynical verdict that reduces it to greed and corruption and the triumphal one that celebrates it as the making of modern America; the truth includes both and more, and the honest student must weigh them together. The era's politics were genuinely corrupt and its government genuinely passive, yet these same years laid the industrial foundations of national power and began, in the civil service reform and the first stirrings of regulation, the slow construction of a more capable state. The money question and the panics inflicted real suffering, and the Populist revolt gave authentic voice to real grievances; yet the Populists were not always right in their economics, their movement carried strains of paranoia and, at times, prejudice, and the eventual triumph of the gold standard was not the simple villainy their rhetoric suggested. The reform impulse the age generated was real but partial, and its fuller flowering lay in the Progressive Era still to come.

The racial tragedy, however, admits of far less balancing, and it should not be softened. The abandonment of the freedpeople and the construction of the Jim Crow order was a moral catastrophe and a betrayal of the nation's own recent commitments, carried out in the full light of day and acquiesced in by a North grown weary of the Black American's cause. It is a permanent stain on the era, and no account of the Gilded Age's material achievements can be allowed to obscure it. To hold all of this together — the genuine building and the genuine corruption, the real progress and the real suffering, the making of an industrial nation and the unmaking of a promise to four million freed people — is the difficult, necessary work of understanding an age that was, like its name, gilded: brilliant on its surface, and a great deal more troubling beneath.
