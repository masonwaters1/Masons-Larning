We have traced the American inheritance back through Jerusalem, Athens, and Rome, through the Christian synthesis of the Middle Ages, through Magna Carta and the Reformation's political earthquake. Now we arrive at the inheritance that lay closest of all to the American founders — so close that they breathed it as air and quoted it from memory: the tradition of the English common law, and the "rights of Englishmen" that the colonists claimed as their birthright. When the Americans of 1776 rose against the Crown, they did not at first imagine themselves as revolutionaries inventing a new order. They imagined themselves as Englishmen defending ancient liberties that were being stolen from them. To understand the American Revolution, and the constitutional order it produced, we must understand the legal inheritance the revolutionaries thought they were defending. That inheritance has two great expositors — Sir Edward Coke and Sir William Blackstone — and one great organizing idea: that liberty is not an abstract theory but a concrete inheritance, handed down and held in trust.

## The Genius of the Common Law

The common law of England is a peculiar and remarkable thing, and its peculiarity shaped the whole Anglo-American conception of law and liberty. Unlike the legal systems of the European continent — which descended from Roman law and took the form of comprehensive codes, handed down from above by a sovereign legislator and applied deductively — the English common law grew *from the bottom up*. It was built, case by case, over centuries, out of the accumulated decisions of judges resolving real disputes, and out of the immemorial customs of the English people. It was less *made* than *discovered* — found in the practices and precedents of the past, refined through application, and passed on.

This gave the common law a distinctive character with profound political consequences. Because it was built on precedent — on the principle that like cases should be decided alike, and that the rulings of the past bind the present — the common law embodied a kind of accumulated wisdom, the distilled experience of generations of practical judgment, rather than the abstract design of any single mind. Law, in this conception, was an *inheritance*: a patrimony received from one's ancestors, to be conserved and handed on, not a blueprint to be redrawn at will. And crucially, the common law was understood to stand *above* the king. It was not the creation of the monarch's will; it was the ancient law of the land, which the king himself was bound to observe. This conception — of a law older and higher than the ruler, binding even the sovereign — would prove to be one of the most important political ideas in the world.

## Coke Against the King

The towering champion of the common law against royal power was Sir Edward Coke (1552–1634), perhaps the greatest English jurist of his age — judge, member of Parliament, and relentless defender of the law's supremacy.[^1] Coke lived in an era when the Stuart kings, James I and Charles I, were pressing the theory of the divine right of kings — the claim that the monarch ruled by God's direct appointment and stood, in significant respects, above the law. Coke set himself against this with extraordinary courage, insisting that the king, too, was subject to the law of the land.

A famous confrontation captures the stakes. King James, asserting his right to decide legal cases personally, was met by Coke with the argument that cases must be decided according to the artificial reason and judgment of the law — the trained reason of the common law, mastered only by long study — and not by the king's natural reason. When the king protested that this seemed to place him under the law, Coke is reported to have answered, citing the medieval jurist Bracton, that the king is under no man, but under God and the law. It was a breathtaking assertion: that even the monarch stands beneath the law. Coke gave this principle institutional teeth as well. In his role as a judge, in the celebrated Dr. Bonham's Case, he suggested that when an Act of Parliament is against common right and reason, the common law may control it and adjudge such an act void — a startling idea that the law itself sets limits even on the legislature, and one that later generations would look back upon as a seed of the doctrine of *judicial review*. Coke also stood at the center of the parliamentary resistance that produced the Petition of Right of 1628, reasserting fundamental liberties against arbitrary royal power. Through his Reports and his Institutes — vast works expounding the common law — Coke handed down to posterity, and especially to the American colonists who studied him closely, the doctrine that liberty is secured by a law above the ruler.

## Blackstone and the Commentaries

If Coke was the common law's great warrior, Sir William Blackstone (1723–1780) was its great systematizer and teacher. His *Commentaries on the Laws of England*, published in four volumes between 1765 and 1769, did something no one had done before: it presented the sprawling, tangled mass of English law as an ordered, intelligible, and even elegant whole, in prose clear enough for an educated layman to read.[^2] The *Commentaries* became one of the most influential law books ever written — and nowhere more so than in America, where, on the eve of the Revolution, Blackstone was a bestseller and became the foundation of legal education for generations of American lawyers. When the founders reasoned about law, rights, and government, they reasoned very often in Blackstone's categories and Blackstone's language.

At the heart of Blackstone's exposition stood his account of the rights of individuals. He distinguished the *absolute* rights of persons — those belonging to individuals simply as human beings, prior to and independent of society — from the relative rights that arise from social relations. The absolute rights he reduced to three great heads: the right of *personal security* (life, limb, body, health, and reputation), the right of *personal liberty* (freedom of movement, freedom from arbitrary imprisonment), and the right of *private property* (the free use and enjoyment of one's possessions). Around these three core rights, Blackstone explained, the law erected a series of *subordinate* or auxiliary rights and procedures — such as the right to petition, the limits on the Crown's power, and access to the courts — whose function was to *protect* the three primary rights. Liberty, in Blackstone's presentation, was not a vague aspiration but a structured system of concrete legal protections, each with a history and a mechanism. This was the architecture of freedom the American colonists absorbed.

## The Rights of Englishmen

This brings us to the central and characteristic idea: the "rights of Englishmen." When the colonists protested British policy in the decade before the Revolution, this was the phrase ever on their lips. They did not, at first, claim abstract universal rights belonging to all humanity in the manner of a philosophical treatise; they claimed the specific, concrete liberties that belonged to them *as Englishmen* — their inheritance under the common law and the English constitution.

And what were these rights? They were tangible and well-defined: trial by jury; the writ of habeas corpus, protecting against arbitrary detention; the principle of no taxation without the consent of one's representatives; the security of one's property against seizure; the right to due process and the equal protection of the settled law; freedom from standing armies quartered among the people; the right to petition for redress of grievances. These were not deduced from first principles; they were *inherited* — won over centuries of struggle, embodied in Magna Carta and the Petition of Right and the English Bill of Rights of 1689, and passed down as the birthright of every freeborn Englishman. The colonists' grievance was precisely that the Crown and Parliament were denying *them*, as Englishmen living in America, the very rights that Englishmen at home enjoyed — taxing them without their consent, denying them jury trials, subjecting them to arbitrary power. Their initial demand was conservative: not to invent new rights, but to receive the old ones to which they were entitled by their inheritance. This is the deeply *conservative* cast of Anglo-American liberty — liberty conceived as a concrete patrimony to be conserved, not an abstraction to be constructed.

## The Through-Line to America

The line from this inheritance to the American founding is direct and unbroken, and it explains much about the constitutional order that emerged. The American Revolution began as a defense of the rights of Englishmen and only gradually, as the breach with Britain widened, broadened its claims into the universal language of the Declaration — "all men," "unalienable rights." But even after the rhetoric ascended to universal principles, the *substance* of the rights Americans sought to secure remained overwhelmingly the inherited rights of Englishmen, now to be guaranteed in writing against any government, including their own.

This is plainly visible in the Bill of Rights of 1791. Its provisions read like a catalogue of the common-law liberties: trial by jury, due process, protection against unreasonable searches and seizures, the right against self-incrimination, protection against cruel and unusual punishment, the right to confront witnesses — the very rights Coke and Blackstone had expounded as the patrimony of Englishmen, now lifted into the supreme written law of a new republic. Coke's radical intuition in Dr. Bonham's Case — that there is a higher law that even the legislature cannot transgress — flowered in the American doctrine of a written constitution as fundamental law, enforced by courts through judicial review. The American achievement, in this light, was not to repudiate the English legal inheritance but to *complete* and *secure* it: to take the rights of Englishmen, which in England rested on custom and could be altered by Parliament, and to entrench them in a written constitution beyond the reach of ordinary politics. American liberty was the rights of Englishmen, made permanent and put in writing.

## The Honest Counterpoint

A faithful account must acknowledge the serious objections to this inheritance, and they are weighty. The first is *exclusion*. The "rights of Englishmen" were, by their very name and history, the rights of a particular people — and in practice they were extended unevenly and often denied entirely to those outside the charmed circle. The same colonists who eloquently demanded their liberties as Englishmen held African slaves to whom no such liberties were granted; the common law itself accommodated slavery, and the rights tradition coexisted for centuries with the dispossession of native peoples and the subordination of women, who were largely excluded from its protections. There is a genuine tension — some would say hypocrisy — between the universal language into which the inheritance eventually flowered and the exclusions written into its actual practice. The rights were proclaimed as the birthright of all and reserved, in fact, for some.

The second objection concerns the *myth of the "ancient constitution."* Coke and others appealed to an immemorial English liberty, supposedly handed down unbroken from time out of mind. Modern historians have shown that this "ancient constitution" was substantially a *construction* — a useful and powerful myth, projecting backward onto the medieval past a coherence and a libertarian content it did not really possess, in order to lend the authority of antiquity to claims that were in truth innovations. The rights tradition, on this view, was less a faithful recovery of ancient liberties than a creative reinvention dressed in the costume of inheritance. A defender of the tradition may respond that the *myth* of the ancient constitution, whatever its historical inaccuracies, did real and beneficial work — that the belief in inherited liberty helped secure actual liberty, and that a partly invented inheritance honorably extended is better than no inheritance at all; and that the gradual universalization of the rights of Englishmen, however incomplete, was a genuine moral achievement that eventually turned the tradition's own principles against its exclusions. Both the criticism and the defense contain truth. What is beyond dispute is that this inheritance — concrete, customary, conserved, and embodied in Coke and Blackstone — supplied the American founders with the raw material of their liberty, and that the constitutional order they built is unintelligible without it.

With this we complete the long story of the inheritance that preceded America's founding — the deep sources, running from Sinai to Westminster Hall, on which the new nation would draw. In the next unit we cross the Atlantic at last and turn to the founding itself: the settlement of the colonies, the ideas they carried, and the slow gathering of the forces that would produce a new nation conceived in this long inheritance and dedicated to securing it.
