Every course must begin somewhere, and a course in theology must begin by asking what theology is — for the answer shapes everything that follows. The word itself is simple enough: *theology* comes from two Greek words, *theos* (God) and *logos* (word, study, or reason), and so means, at its most basic, the study of God, or speech about God, or the knowledge of God. But that simple definition opens onto the deepest questions a human being can ask. Can the creature truly know the Creator? And if so, what is such knowledge *for*? The Reformed tradition gives a distinctive and bracing answer to both questions, and that answer is the foundation on which this entire course is built. Theology, rightly understood, is not a cold academic exercise reserved for specialists. It is the knowledge of God — and the knowledge of God is the highest aim, and the deepest joy, of human life.

## Knowing God and Knowing Ourselves

John Calvin opened his great work, the *Institutes of the Christian Religion*, with a sentence that has shaped Reformed thought ever since: nearly the whole of the wisdom we possess, he wrote, consists of two parts — the knowledge of God and the knowledge of ourselves.[^1] These two knowledges, Calvin argued, are bound together so tightly that it is hard to say which comes first or produces the other. We cannot truly know ourselves without first looking upward to God, against whose holiness and majesty we measure our own smallness and sinfulness; and we are never genuinely driven to seek God until we have first become dissatisfied with ourselves, aware of our own weakness, poverty, and need.

This insight sets the tone for the whole Reformed approach. Theology is not the study of a distant object that leaves the student unchanged, the way one might study geology or astronomy. To know God is, inescapably, to come to know oneself truly — as a creature, as a sinner, as one made for fellowship with the Maker. The knowledge of God and the knowledge of self illuminate each other. This is why theology, in the Reformed vision, can never be a merely intellectual affair. It reaches into the conscience and the heart. To study God seriously is to be confronted, exposed, humbled, and ultimately remade. The subject studies the student.

## Theology Is for Doxology

If the first great conviction is that knowing God and knowing ourselves go together, the second is that the knowledge of God exists for the purpose of worship. The Reformed tradition insists that theology is, in the end, *practical* and *doxological* — that is, it leads to the praise and glory of God (*doxa* meaning glory). Theology that does not issue in worship, obedience, and love has missed its purpose entirely. We do not study God in order to master a body of information, as though God were a specimen to be dissected. We study God in order to know him, and to know God truly is to be moved to adore him.

The most famous summary of this conviction comes from the Westminster Shorter Catechism, which opens by asking, "What is the chief end of man?" — that is, what is the very purpose of human existence? The answer is one of the great sentences of the Christian tradition: "Man's chief end is to glorify God, and to enjoy him forever."[^2] Notice the two verbs, and notice that they are joined. To glorify God and to enjoy God are not two competing aims but one. We were made to know God in such a way that we honor him, and to honor him in such a way that we delight in him. The Reformed tradition has always refused to drive a wedge between truth and joy, between rigorous doctrine and heartfelt devotion. The most serious theology and the most fervent worship are meant to be the same act. Right knowledge of God *fuels* the love of God; it does not compete with it. This is why the great Reformed theologians have so often been men of deep piety, and why this course, for all its careful doctrine, aims finally not at the head alone but at the whole person.

## God at the Center

A third conviction runs through everything the Reformed tradition says about theology: it is relentlessly *God-centered*. The technical term is *theocentric*, from *theos* (God) and *centrum* (center) — as opposed to *anthropocentric*, centered on man. This is perhaps the single most distinctive mark of the Reformed mind, and it will surface again and again throughout this course. The starting point, the organizing principle, and the final goal of all theology is God himself — his glory, his purposes, his sovereign will — rather than human need, human experience, or human benefit.

This does not mean that human beings and their salvation are unimportant; they matter enormously. But in the Reformed vision they are not the *center*. The center is God. Salvation itself exists ultimately for the glory of God, not the other way around. The great motto that captures this is *soli Deo gloria* — "to God alone be the glory" — one of the rallying cries of the Reformation. When Reformed theology asks why anything exists, why God created, why he saves, why he does all that he does, the deepest answer is always the same: for the glory of God. This God-centeredness gives the Reformed tradition its characteristic seriousness, its sense of the weight and majesty of the divine, and its instinctive suspicion of any theology that makes man the measure of all things. We will see this principle at work in every doctrine we examine, from creation to election to the last judgment.

## The Branches of the Study

Before we go further, it helps to see the shape of the territory we will be exploring, for theology, like any great discipline, has its divisions and its map. Systematic theology — the orderly study of what the whole of Scripture teaches on each great topic — has traditionally been organized into a series of major areas, and these will, in effect, form the structure of much of this course.

There is *theology proper*, the study of God himself — his existence, his attributes, his triune being. There is *bibliology*, the doctrine of Scripture, with which this opening unit is concerned. There is *anthropology*, the study of humanity as God's creature, made in his image and fallen into sin. There is *Christology*, the study of the person of Christ, and *soteriology*, the study of salvation — the application of Christ's work to sinners. There is *ecclesiology*, the doctrine of the church, and *eschatology*, the doctrine of the last things. Around these stand related studies: the doctrine of the covenants, of the Holy Spirit, of providence, of the sacraments. Each of these will receive its due in the units ahead. For now, simply notice that theology is not a shapeless mass of opinions but an ordered whole, in which each doctrine connects to and illuminates the others. The God who is one has given a revelation that, rightly understood, coheres into a single, magnificent structure of truth.

## Natural and Revealed

One more distinction belongs at the outset, because it will occupy the next several lessons: the distinction between *natural* theology and *revealed* theology. Natural theology refers to what can be known of God through nature, reason, and conscience — apart from any special divine disclosure. Revealed theology refers to what God has made known through his special revelation, supremely in Scripture. The question of how much we can know of God by each route, and how the two relate, is one of the central questions of theological method, and the Reformed tradition has a distinctive and carefully balanced answer to it, which we will develop in the lessons on revelation that follow.

For now, the essential point is that all genuine knowledge of God depends, in the end, on God's own self-disclosure. We do not discover God the way an explorer discovers an undiscovered land, by our own searching initiative. We know God only because, and insofar as, he has chosen to reveal himself to us. Theology is therefore always a *response* — a response to a God who has spoken first. This will become the great theme of our next lessons: that before there can be any human knowledge of God, there must first be divine revelation, the gracious act by which the infinite God makes himself known to finite and fallen creatures.

## Can We Know God at All?

A serious objection arises at the very threshold of theology, and honesty requires facing it now rather than later. If God is truly God — infinite, eternal, transcendent, utterly beyond the created order — then how can finite, time-bound creatures like us possibly know him? Is not the whole enterprise of theology presumptuous, an attempt to capture the uncapturable, to define the indefinable, to speak about what lies forever beyond the reach of human words? This is no idle worry; it has been raised by serious thinkers in every age, and the Reformed tradition takes it with full seriousness.

The Reformed answer, which we will develop more fully in the unit on the doctrine of God, is a careful balance. On one hand, the tradition insists that God is *incomprehensible* — never able to be fully grasped, mastered, or exhaustively understood by the creature. We will never reach the bottom of God; our knowledge is always partial, always finite, always that of the creature contemplating the Creator. On the other hand, the tradition insists that God is genuinely *knowable* — truly, if not exhaustively. Because God has revealed himself, and because he has made us capable of receiving that revelation, we can know him truly: not everything about him, but real and reliable knowledge, sufficient for salvation, worship, and life. Theology lives in the space between these two truths — humbled by the first, emboldened by the second. We know enough to fall down in worship; we never know so much that worship gives way to mastery.

## The Objections of the Skeptics

Theology has its critics, and a course that means to be honest must let them speak. Some object that theology is mere speculation — talk about an unobservable being, incapable of proof or disproof, a castle built in the air. Some object that it is divisive — that doctrinal disputes have fractured the church and fueled centuries of conflict, and that we would be better off with simple ethics or shared experience than with contested dogma. Some object, with the skeptic, that since God (if he exists) cannot be seen, measured, or tested, any confident speech about him is unwarranted, and humility demands silence.

These objections deserve a fair hearing, and the Reformed tradition does not pretend they are foolish. To the charge of speculation, it answers that theology rests not on human speculation but on divine revelation — that we speak of God not because we have reasoned our way up to him but because he has spoken down to us, a claim we will examine carefully in the lessons ahead. To the charge of divisiveness, it answers that truth matters, that not all disagreements can be dissolved by good will, and that the cure for bad theology is not *no* theology but *better* theology — though it grants, soberly, that doctrine has too often been pursued without love, and that this is a real sin and not a small one. To the skeptic, it answers that the living God is not an object within the world to be tested by us, but the Lord of the world who tests us — and that he has made himself known, not to those who demand to put him in the dock, but to those who come humbly to hear him speak. Whether that claim of divine revelation can be sustained is the question to which we now turn.

## The Joy at the End of It All

We began by saying that theology is the highest aim and the deepest joy of human life, and it is worth dwelling on that note as we close, lest the rigorous work ahead obscure it. For all its careful distinctions and demanding doctrines, theology is finally about a Person — the living God — and about coming to know him. The prophet Jeremiah recorded the Lord's own declaration that the wise should not boast in wisdom, nor the mighty in might, nor the rich in riches, but that the one who boasts should boast in this: that he understands and knows the Lord. To know God is presented in Scripture as the greatest of all goods, surpassing wisdom, power, and wealth.

This is the conviction that animates the Reformed tradition and this course. The doctrines we will study are not dry abstractions to be filed away; they are the contours of the face of God, traced out so that we might know him better and love him more. The doctrine of God's sovereignty is meant to bring the comfort of resting in a Lord who reigns. The doctrine of grace is meant to bring the relief of the sinner who discovers he is loved freely. The doctrine of the covenant is meant to bring the security of belonging to a God who binds himself to his people in promise. Every doctrine, rightly understood, opens onto worship. And so we begin this study not as an academic duty but as a pilgrimage toward the knowledge of God — the knowledge for which we were made, in which alone the restless human heart finds its rest. To begin that pilgrimage, we must ask how the unknowable God can be known at all; and the answer, as the next lesson will show, is that he has written us two books.
