We have walked through the world that made the Reformation: the magnificent sacramental civilization of the medieval church and the cracks running through its foundations; the scandal of the indulgence trade and the anxious conscience that hungered for assurance; the forerunners Wycliffe and Hus, who glimpsed the dawn and paid for it; and the humanist revolution that, with Erasmus's Greek New Testament and Gutenberg's press, forged the tools of reform. Now we draw these strands together and stand, with the men and women of 1517, on the very eve of the storm. For the deepest question about the Reformation's origins is this: why *then*? Reformers had arisen before and been crushed. Why did this one succeed where Wycliffe and Hus had failed? The answer is that, for the first time, every necessary element converged at once — and a Europe long primed for the explosion needed only a spark.

## The Tinder Piled High

Consider first how much tinder had accumulated, dry and ready. The *crisis of authority* we traced through Avignon, the Great Schism, and the conciliar movement had cracked the medieval certainty about where authority in the church finally lay; the question "who speaks for God?" stood open and unresolved. The *corruption and worldliness* of the church had bred a century-long, frustrated cry for the "reformation of the church in head and members," and a deep reservoir of anticlerical resentment. The *indulgence trade* had carried the commercializing of grace to a scandalous extreme. And beneath these visible problems lay the *anxious conscience* — the spiritual hunger of earnest souls for an assurance the late medieval system could not give.

To this combustible mixture the forerunners had added their contribution. Wycliffe and Hus had shown that the central convictions of reform — the supremacy of Scripture, the critique of the sacramental and papal system, the church as the company of the elect — could be articulated and held; and the Hussite movement in Bohemia had shown, against all expectation, that a reformation could *survive* the wrath of Rome and carve out space for itself. The path had been walked, if not yet to victory. The tinder was piled high. What was needed was a spark hot enough to catch — and the conditions that would let the fire spread rather than be stamped out.

## The Tools in Hand

Here the humanist revolution proved decisive, for it supplied what every earlier reform had lacked. Wycliffe and Hus had labored without the recovered Greek New Testament, without the critical recovery of the original biblical text, without the full apparatus of *ad fontes* scholarship that could challenge the church's Latin tradition from the sources. By 1517, all of this was in hand. Erasmus had given Europe the New Testament in its original Greek; humanist scholars had recovered Hebrew and Greek learning; the method of returning to the sources was the common possession of educated Europe, and it pointed inexorably toward Scripture as the standard by which the church must be judged.

And then there was the press. Wycliffe's English Bibles had to be copied laboriously by hand and could be hunted down and burned; Hus's writings circulated slowly among a limited circle. But Gutenberg's printing press, now established across Europe for two generations, meant that a reformer's words could be reproduced in thousands of copies and scattered across a continent in weeks, faster than any authority could suppress them. The recovered Scriptures, the critical scholarship, and the reformer's own arguments could now reach the masses with unprecedented speed. The forerunners had lacked this weapon; the next reformer would wield it. The combination of the recovered Word and the printing press was the technological key that had been missing before — and it was now turned in the lock.

## The Political Tinderbox

Tinder and tools were not enough; earlier reformers had possessed real popular support and still been crushed. What finally distinguished the sixteenth century was a set of *political* conditions that gave a reformer room to survive. Chief among these was the peculiar fragmentation of Germany. The Holy Roman Empire was not a centralized state but a patchwork of hundreds of semi-autonomous territories — principalities, duchies, free cities — each with its own ruler and jealous of its own liberties, loosely presided over by an emperor whose power over the princes was limited. This fragmentation meant that a reformer condemned by pope and emperor might still find protection under a sympathetic local prince, beyond the easy reach of central authority. As we shall see, Luther would owe his life and his freedom to exactly this: the protection of Frederick the Wise, elector of Saxony, who shielded his troublesome professor from those who would have silenced him as they had silenced Hus.

To this was added the force of *rising national feeling*, especially German resentment of Rome. Germany seethed with grievances against the papacy — against the endless flow of German money to Italy, the foreign domination of the church, the exactions and abuses that a distant and worldly Rome imposed upon the German nation. These grievances had been formally catalogued and pressed; they were a standing source of resentment that a reformer who appealed to German feeling against a grasping foreign church could turn to powerful account. A reformation that wedded the recovery of the gospel to German national sentiment against Rome would have political wind in its sails that a purely theological movement could not have summoned.

## The Distracted Emperor

One final political factor must be named, for it proved crucial in the early, vulnerable years of the Reformation. The man who might have crushed the movement at its birth — the Holy Roman Emperor Charles V, a devout Catholic who ruled the vast Habsburg dominions and genuinely wished to suppress the German heresy — was, at the decisive moments, fatally *distracted*. Charles was perpetually entangled in great wars on other fronts: a long and bitter rivalry with the king of France, which repeatedly consumed his armies and his attention, and, above all, the relentless pressure of the Ottoman Turks, who were advancing into central Europe and who would besiege Vienna itself in 1529.

The Turkish threat in particular forced Charles, again and again, to seek the cooperation of the German princes — including the Protestant ones — against the common Muslim enemy, and so to make concessions and tolerate delays that he would never have granted in time of peace. The need for unity against the Turk repeatedly stayed the emperor's hand against the Protestants at moments when decisive action might have strangled the movement in its cradle. Had Charles been free to turn the full force of his empire against Luther in 1521, the Reformation might have ended as the Hussite revolt nearly had. Instead, the great Catholic emperor was pulled away to other battlefields, and the German Reformation was given the breathing room it needed to take root and spread. Contingency, here, ran in the Reformers' favor.

## The Convergence

Now we can see the whole. Each earlier attempt at reformation had failed because one or more necessary elements was missing. Wycliffe and Hus had the convictions and the courage, and even some protection and popular support — but they lacked the recovered biblical scholarship, the printing press, the full convergence of political conditions, and the ripeness of the wider crisis. By 1517, for the first time in history, *all* the elements came together at once: a cracked and questioned ecclesiastical authority; a corrupt and bitterly resented church; an indulgence scandal that crystallized the corruption; an anxious conscience hungry for grace; the example of forerunners who had shown both the way and that survival was possible; the recovered Word of God in its original tongue; the printing press to spread it; the fragmented German political order that could shelter a reformer; the surge of national feeling against Rome; and an emperor too distracted by France and the Turk to crush the movement at its birth.

The tinder was piled high and bone dry; the tools were forged and in hand; the political winds were favorable; and the watchman's hour had come. All that remained was the spark. And the spark, when it fell, was almost absurdly small: a single Augustinian monk and professor, tormented in his own conscience and outraged by a tawdry indulgence campaign, who in October 1517 drew up ninety-five theses for academic debate and set them loose upon the world. He did not intend a revolution; he intended a disputation about indulgences. But the world was ready, and the small flame caught the great pile of tinder, and Western Christendom began to burn.

## The Honest Counterpoint

A word of caution must close this unit, lest the "perfect storm" we have described mislead us into a false sense of inevitability. It is tempting, telling the story this way, to conclude that the Reformation was *bound* to happen — that the convergence of factors made it certain, that 1517 was the inevitable culmination of a century of pressure. But history is rarely so tidy, and *contingency* mattered enormously. Had Luther been seized and burned like Hus before his protectors could shield him; had Frederick the Wise chosen not to protect his troublesome subject; had Charles V made peace with France and the Turk and turned his full power against the German heresy at the crucial moment — the Reformation as we know it might never have unfolded. The elements were in place, but their convergence into a successful revolution depended on a host of particular choices, accidents, and circumstances that could easily have gone otherwise.

We should also recognize that the very framing of this unit — everything "leading up to" 1517, the long preparation culminating in the great event — is a *teleological* and characteristically Protestant way of telling the story, reading the past as a road to a destination we already know. A Catholic historian, surveying the same facts, might arrange them quite differently: not as the gathering of a storm that needed to break, but as the tragic accumulation of pressures that a wiser and more patient church might have relieved without rupture. There is no single, neutral way to narrate these origins, and the student should hold this account as one illuminating arrangement of the evidence rather than the only possible one. With that caution registered, the unit is complete. We have seen the world that made the Reformation; we have stood on the eve of the storm. In the units to come — the history thread resuming with Luther himself — we will watch the storm break. But first, the theology thread continues, for the doctrine the Reformation recovered is as much our subject as the drama, and to it we now return.
