In the last lesson we learned that God has written two books — the book of nature, by which he reveals himself to all people everywhere, and the book of Scripture, by which he reveals himself savingly to his people. We must now open each book in turn, and answer two questions that stand at the gateway of all theology. First: what exactly does the book of nature declare? And second: why is it not enough — why must God also speak in the book of Scripture if sinners are to be saved? The answer to the first question reveals the genuine glory of general revelation; the answer to the second reveals why, glorious as it is, it leaves us still in need. What nature declares, and why it cannot save — to this double question we now turn.

## The Witness Without

The most majestic statement of general revelation in all of Scripture is the nineteenth Psalm: the heavens declare the glory of God, and the sky above proclaims his handiwork.[^1] Day after day, the psalm says, the creation pours forth speech; night after night it reveals knowledge — and yet it is a speech without words, a voice without sound, reaching to the ends of the earth. Here is general revelation in its grandeur: the whole cosmos, in its vastness, order, and beauty, is a ceaseless, wordless proclamation of the glory and power of its Maker.

This external witness comes through the created order itself. The immensity of the heavens speaks of God's greatness; the intricate order of nature speaks of his wisdom; the beauty woven through the world speaks of his goodness; the sheer existence of anything at all speaks of his creative power. It comes too through God's providential governance of history and human life. To the attentive observer, every creature is, in the words of the Belgic Confession, a letter in a beautiful book, and the whole of creation spells out the eternal power and divine nature of God. The world is not silent about its Maker.

## The Witness Within

But general revelation does not shine only upon us from without; it rises up within us as well. The Reformed tradition, following Calvin, has placed great weight on an internal witness — an innate awareness of God implanted in every human heart. Calvin gave this its famous name: the *sensus divinitatis*, the "sense of the divine," and he also called it the *semen religionis*, the "seed of religion." God has sown in every human mind, Calvin argued, by natural instinct, an awareness that he exists and that we are accountable to him. The evidence lies in the universality of religion: in every age and culture, however remote, human beings have worshiped and reached toward the transcendent. Even idolatry, in Calvin's analysis, is a perverse testimony to the *sensus divinitatis* — a distorted expression of an awareness of God that fallen humanity cannot wholly suppress.

Closely joined to this is the witness of *conscience* — the moral awareness, present in every person, of right and wrong, of duty and guilt. The apostle Paul writes that even the Gentiles, who do not have the written law, show that the work of the law is written on their hearts, their conscience bearing witness, their thoughts accusing or excusing them. Through the conscience, God reveals something of his own moral character and holy demands. The very fact that human beings everywhere feel the pull of *ought*, and experience guilt before a standard they did not invent, points beyond themselves to a Lawgiver whose law is inscribed on the heart. Through the world without and the conscience within, the testimony of God is constant, universal, and inescapable.

## What General Revelation Accomplishes — and Its Tragic Twist

What does this witness accomplish? Something real, and something specific. It reveals the existence of God, his eternal power, his divine nature, his glory as Creator, and the broad outlines of his moral law. This is genuine knowledge of God, truly given to every person who has ever lived. And it accomplishes a definite purpose, stated with great force by Paul in the first chapter of Romans: it renders all humanity *without excuse*. Ever since the creation of the world, Paul writes, God's invisible attributes have been clearly perceived in the things he has made, so that people are without excuse.[^2] This is the great function of general revelation: it establishes the universal accountability of the human race. No one will stand before God and plead total ignorance.

But here the doctrine takes a tragic turn that we must not miss. For Paul does not say merely that God is revealed to all; he says that all, in their fallenness, do something terrible with that revelation. They *suppress the truth* in unrighteousness — they push the unwelcome knowledge of God down, hold it under, and turn away. And the result is not honest atheism but *idolatry*: having suppressed the true God revealed in creation, the human heart does not become irreligious but manufactures false gods, exchanging the glory of the immortal God for images and serving the creature rather than the Creator. The fallen reading of the book of nature does not yield true worship; it yields idols. The witness is clear; the witnesses are guilty; and their guilt is precisely that they have suppressed and distorted a truth they genuinely possessed.

## Why Nature Cannot Save

We are now in a position to answer the second question — why the book of nature, real and glorious as it is, cannot finally be enough. There are two reasons, and they correspond to a limit in the message and a corruption in the reader.

The first reason lies in the *content* of general revelation, or rather in what it lacks. General revelation simply does not contain the gospel. From the created order and the moral law, a person might come to know that God exists, that he is powerful and wise, that there is a moral standard we have broken. But from nature alone, no one could ever learn the Trinity, or the incarnation, or the cross, or justification by faith, or the covenant of grace — the way a guilty sinner may be reconciled to a holy God. These are not truths waiting to be deduced from the stars; they are free decisions of God's saving will, hidden in his counsel, which could be known only if God chose to announce them. The gospel is *news* — and news must be told. The heavens can proclaim that there is a God; they cannot proclaim that this God so loved the world that he gave his Son.

The second reason lies not in the message but in the *reader*. As we have just seen, fallen human beings do not receive even the genuine knowledge that nature gives; they suppress and distort it into idolatry. Sin has corrupted not only our standing before God but our very capacity to perceive him rightly — what theologians call the *noetic effects of sin*, the corruption of the mind itself. So even the clearest general revelation is, in practice, turned to idols by the sinner who reads it. The book of nature lies open, but the fallen eye sees only a blur. Calvin captured this with a famous image: Scripture functions like a pair of *spectacles*, which, placed before our weak and clouded eyes, bring the blurred book of nature into focus so that we may read it rightly and see the true God in his works. Special revelation is therefore necessary not only to supply the missing message of the gospel, but also to *correct* our distorted reading of the revelation we already have.

## How God Has Spoken

If special revelation is necessary, how has God given it? The opening of the letter to the Hebrews gives the grand summary: long ago, at many times and in many ways, God spoke to our fathers by the prophets, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son. Special revelation is a history — a progressive, unfolding self-disclosure across the ages, climaxing in a person. God revealed himself through theophanies and direct speech to the patriarchs, through mighty acts of deliverance like the exodus, through the giving of the law, through the words of the prophets. And then came the climax: the supreme and final special revelation of God is the Lord Jesus Christ himself, the eternal Word made flesh, the very image of the invisible God, in whom the fullness of deity dwells. To see him is to see the Father. All previous revelation pointed toward him; all subsequent revelation points back to him.

This raises a final question that will occupy the rest of this unit: if the supreme revelation is the person of Christ, who lived two thousand years ago, how does that revelation reach *us*? The answer is *Scripture* — the inscripturated Word of God. In his wisdom, God ensured that his special revelation would not be lost with the passing of the generations who first received it, but would be committed to writing by prophets and apostles whom he inspired, so that it might be preserved and made available to his people in every age. The Bible is the deposit of special revelation in written form — the means by which the Word once spoken and enacted in history now comes to us. The living Word (Christ) reaches us through the written Word (the Bible). And this is why the doctrine of Scripture stands at the foundation of Reformed theology, and why it occupies this opening unit.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Two honest qualifications must close this lesson. The first concerns the long-running debate over *natural theology* — the question of how much fallen human reason can actually derive from general revelation. The more optimistic answer, associated with Thomas Aquinas, holds that reason, even after the fall, retains substantial power to reason from creation up to God and to construct proofs of his existence. The more cautious answer, characteristic of much of the Reformed tradition, stresses the noetic effects of sin and holds that while general revelation is objectively clear, the fallen sinner's capacity to read it rightly is gravely impaired, so that unaided reason tends to wander into idolatry rather than arriving at the true God. The bare *fact* of general revelation is affirmed by all sides; what is disputed is how far fallen reason can travel on its light alone. The student need not settle that debate today, only note that it is a genuine one, conducted within the family of those who take Scripture seriously.

The second qualification is a hard one, and honesty forbids us to pass it by lightly. If special revelation is *necessary* for salvation, what of the countless human beings who never received it — who had the book of nature, which renders them accountable, but never the book of Scripture, which alone reveals the remedy? This is the difficult question of the destiny of the unevangelized, and the Reformed tradition does not pretend it is easy. Its sober answer is that general revelation genuinely reveals God and renders all without excuse, but offers no saving knowledge, and that salvation is found only in Christ. This means the universal availability of general revelation does not translate into a universal opportunity for salvation — a hard saying the tradition holds with a heavy heart, trusting the perfect justice and mercy of God where its own knowledge runs out. We name the difficulty plainly, leave it reverently with him, and press on.

With this, the foundation is laid. God has revealed himself in two books, and the saving revelation reaches us through Scripture. Everything now turns to that written Word — and to the most basic question about it: what is its *authority*? That is the question the Reformation pressed with world-changing force, in the principle of *sola scriptura*, to which we turn next.
