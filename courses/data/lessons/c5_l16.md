In the last lesson we established that Scripture is inspired — God-breathed, the very Word of God in the words of men. Two great consequences flow directly from that foundation, and they form the subject of this lesson. The first is the *authority* of Scripture: because the Bible is God's own Word, it carries God's own authority, binding the conscience above every human power. The second is the *inerrancy* of Scripture: because the Bible is the Word of a God who cannot lie or err, it is wholly true in all that it affirms. Authority tells us that Scripture *commands* us; inerrancy tells us that Scripture does not *deceive* us. Both are corollaries of inspiration, and both are essential to the Reformation's confidence in the Word.

## The Authority of the Word

We have already touched the authority of Scripture under the principle of *sola scriptura*, but now we can ground it where it truly rests: in inspiration. The authority of Scripture is not a property the church grants to the Bible, nor a status the Bible earns by its excellence; it is the authority of God himself, inhering in the text because God is its author. This is the decisive point. Since Scripture is God-breathed, what Scripture says, *God* says. Its words are God's words, and they therefore carry the full weight of divine authority — the authority of the Creator over his creatures, of the Lord over his servants, of the King over his subjects.

This means that Scripture's authority is *intrinsic*, not derived. It does not depend on the church's recognition, on the believer's acceptance, or on any external warrant; it is authoritative in itself, by virtue of its divine origin, whether or not any human being acknowledges it. And this authority is *final* and *supreme*: it stands above every competing authority — pope, council, tradition, reason, conscience, experience — and binds them all. To the Word of God, the conscience of the believer is bound absolutely, for to obey Scripture is to obey God and to resist it is to resist God. This authority extends over the whole of life: over what we are to *believe* (faith) and over how we are to *live* (practice). Scripture is not merely a resource to be consulted but a Lord to be obeyed. When the Reformers placed Scripture above the church, they were not exalting a book over an institution; they were submitting the church, and themselves, to the voice of the living God who speaks in his Word.

## Inerrancy: The Truth of the Word

The second consequence of inspiration is *inerrancy* — the doctrine that Scripture, in all that it affirms, is wholly true and free from error. The argument is simple and rests on the character of God. God is the author of Scripture (inspiration). God is perfectly truthful — he is, as Scripture says, the God who never lies, and Jesus declared to the Father, "your word is truth." A perfectly truthful God cannot inspire falsehood. Therefore his Word, rightly understood, contains no error; it tells the truth in all that it teaches and affirms. Inerrancy is thus not an arbitrary add-on but a necessary inference from the truthfulness of the God who breathed out the Scriptures.

We must define the doctrine with care, however, for it is easily caricatured and easily misunderstood. Inerrancy is the claim that Scripture, *in its original manuscripts* and *rightly interpreted*, does not affirm anything contrary to fact. Note the precision in that statement. It concerns what Scripture *affirms* — what it actually teaches and asserts — not every incidental feature of how it speaks. It concerns the original texts as God gave them, not every copy made since. And it concerns Scripture *rightly interpreted* — read according to the intention of its authors and the nature of its language. With these clarifications, inerrancy is the historic conviction that the Bible, because it is God's Word, will not mislead us in anything it sets out to teach.

## What Inerrancy Does Not Claim

Much confusion is avoided by stating clearly what inerrancy does *not* mean, for the careful doctrine guards against a wooden literalism that its critics often mistake for it. Inerrancy does not require that Scripture speak with scientific or technical precision where it does not intend to. The Bible uses ordinary, everyday, *phenomenological* language — it speaks of the sun rising and setting, as we all do, describing things as they appear, and this is not an error any more than a weather forecaster errs in speaking of sunrise. It employs round numbers and approximations, as ordinary speech always has; it is not falsified by failing to provide decimal precision it never claimed.

Inerrancy also reads Scripture according to its many literary *genres*. The Bible contains history, law, poetry, proverb, parable, prophecy, and apocalyptic vision, and each must be read according to its kind. Poetry uses metaphor and hyperbole; parables are illustrative fictions; these are not errors but forms of truthful communication, and to demand a flat literalism of them is to misread them, not to honor them. Inerrancy concerns the original *autographs* — the texts as first given — and acknowledges that in the long process of hand-copying, minor variants entered the manuscript tradition; but the science of textual criticism has recovered the text with very high confidence, and no doctrine of the faith hangs in doubt on a textual variant. Inerrancy is a claim about *truth*, not about a particular style of precision; it affirms that Scripture tells the truth in all it affirms, read as it was meant to be read.

## The Problem of the Phenomena

The most serious challenge to inerrancy is what is sometimes called the problem of the "phenomena" of Scripture — the apparent errors, discrepancies, and tensions that critics point to within the text. Parallel accounts of the same event sometimes differ in detail; numbers occasionally vary between passages; certain statements seem to sit uneasily with modern historical or scientific knowledge. How does the doctrine of inerrancy respond to these?

The response has several layers, and honesty requires that we state it without overclaiming. First, a great many alleged discrepancies dissolve on closer examination: they prove to be complementary perspectives on the same event rather than contradictions, differences of selection and emphasis among authors writing for different purposes, or matters explained by the conventions of ancient writing. Second, some apparent errors trace to the transmission of the text — a copyist's slip in a number, for instance — rather than to the original autograph, which inerrancy concerns. Third, and most honestly, some difficulties remain genuinely *unresolved*: there are passages whose apparent tensions have not been fully harmonized, and the inerrantist does not pretend otherwise. Here the doctrine takes a particular posture: it rests its confidence primarily on the Bible's own testimony to its divine origin and on the character of God, and it treats the remaining difficulties as problems for *our* limited knowledge rather than as proof of *Scripture's* error — trusting that fuller understanding would resolve what now perplexes us, as it has resolved so much that once seemed insoluble. This is a posture of faith, but not a blind one; it is the reasonable confidence that a God of truth has not filled his Word with falsehood, held in the face of difficulties that have, again and again, yielded to patient study.

## Inerrancy and Infallibility

A brief clarification of terms will help, for two words are often used together here. *Inerrancy* means "without error" — Scripture does not affirm what is false. *Infallibility* means "incapable of failing or misleading" — Scripture cannot fail to accomplish its purpose or lead us astray. The two are closely related and, in the fullest Reformed view, mutually entailing: a Word that is without error will not mislead, and a Word that cannot mislead will be without error. Some have tried to drive a wedge between them, affirming that Scripture is infallible in its saving *purpose* while allowing that it may err in incidental matters of history or science — a position we will weigh in a moment. The historic Reformed and evangelical position holds both terms together in their strongest sense: Scripture is both inerrant and infallible, wholly true and wholly reliable, in all that it affirms.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Inerrancy is contested, and the objections are serious. The central one is the problem of the phenomena just discussed: critics argue that the apparent factual and historical errors, and the internal discrepancies, simply *do* falsify the claim of complete inerrancy, and that the inerrantist's harmonizations are strained and his appeal to unresolved difficulties an evasion. This is a genuine and ongoing debate, and the student should know that thoughtful people land on different sides of it.

A particularly important alternative is the position of *limited* inerrancy or infallibility, held by many sincere Christians — including some within the broadly Reformed world and reflected in the Second Vatican Council's careful statement that Scripture teaches "without error that truth which God willed to be recorded for the sake of our salvation." On this view, Scripture is fully trustworthy in its saving message — in what it teaches "for our salvation" — but is not necessarily inerrant in incidental matters of history, geography, or science, where it reflects the limited knowledge of its human authors and their age. The advocates of *full* inerrancy reply that this distinction cannot finally be sustained, because the saving message of the Bible is itself deeply *historical* — the gospel asserts that certain things happened in space and time, supremely the resurrection — so that one cannot cordon off the "spiritual" content from the "factual" without unraveling the faith; and they argue that a God of truth would not affirm falsehoods even in passing. The limited-inerrancy advocates respond that this demands of Scripture a kind of precision it never claims and makes faith hostage to every difficult harmonization. Both sides take Scripture's authority with full seriousness; they differ on the scope of its inerrancy, and the debate is real.

Finally, fairness requires distinguishing the careful doctrine from its *caricature*. Inerrancy is sometimes wielded crudely — as a wooden literalism that flattens genre, denies the human character of the text, and stakes the faith on forced harmonizations of every detail. This is not the considered doctrine, which the most careful statements of inerrancy explicitly guard against, insisting on attention to genre, language, intent, and the difference between truth and technical precision. Held carefully, inerrancy is not anti-intellectual credulity but the reasonable confidence that the God-breathed Word of a truthful God will not, rightly read, deceive us. Whatever one concludes about its precise scope, the underlying conviction is the engine of the Reformation's whole relation to Scripture: that here, in this book, we have a Word from God that we may trust without reserve. But a trustworthy Word is of little use if it cannot be understood, or if it leaves us needing some further authority to complete it. So we turn next to two more attributes of Scripture — its clarity and its sufficiency.
