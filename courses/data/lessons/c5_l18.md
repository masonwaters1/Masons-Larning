We have affirmed that Scripture is inspired, authoritative, inerrant, clear, and sufficient. But all of these claims raise a prior and pressing question: *which* Scripture? When we speak of "the Bible," we mean a particular collection of books — sixty-six of them in the Protestant Bible — and we must ask how this collection came to be, and how we know that these books, and no others, are the inspired Word of God. This is the question of the *canon*. And the answer the Reformed tradition gives is subtle and important: the church did not *create* the canon or *confer* authority upon these books; it *recognized* the canon, discerning and acknowledging the authority that God had already given the books in their inspiration. The distinction between creating and recognizing will prove to be everything.

## What "Canon" Means

The word *canon* comes from a Greek term meaning a rule, a standard, or a measuring rod. Applied to Scripture, it refers to the authoritative list of books recognized as the inspired Word of God — the collection that serves as the rule of faith and life for the church. To ask about the canon is to ask: which writings belong in this collection, and on what basis? It is a question with enormous stakes, for the canon defines the boundaries of the very Word on which everything else in this unit depends.

The crucial Reformed claim, which we glimpsed earlier and must now develop, is this: the books of Scripture are canonical *because they are inspired*, not because the church declared them so. Their authority is *intrinsic*, given by God in the act of inspiration, and it existed from the moment they were written, long before any council met to discuss them. The church's role was not that of a *judge* conferring authority upon the books, but that of a *witness* discerning and acknowledging the authority they already possessed. The church received the canon as one recognizes authentic coin — testing it, identifying it, and accepting it as genuine — but the coin's value does not come from the one who recognizes it. The books were the Word of God before the church said so; the church, led by the Spirit, came to confess what was already true.

## The Old Testament Canon

The Old Testament canon was, in large measure, settled before the Christian era. The Hebrew Scriptures had been recognized and received by the Jewish community as the Word of God, and this is the canon that Jesus and the apostles knew, used, and affirmed. Throughout the New Testament, Jesus and the apostles cite the Hebrew Scriptures as authoritative — as "Scripture," as that which "is written," as the very oracles of God — treating them as the settled Word of God. The Protestant Old Testament corresponds to this Hebrew canon, the Scriptures that Jesus himself received.

Here, however, arises a genuine and substantive disagreement among Christians: the question of the *Apocrypha*. The Greek translation of the Old Testament known as the Septuagint, widely used in the early church, included a number of additional books — Tobit, Judith, the Wisdom of Solomon, Sirach, the books of Maccabees, and others — which were not part of the Hebrew canon. These books, called the Apocrypha by Protestants and the "deuterocanonical" books by Catholics, became the subject of lasting dispute. The Reformers, following the Hebrew canon and noting that the early scholar Jerome himself had distinguished these books from the canonical Scriptures, excluded them from the canon proper — valuing many of them as edifying reading, useful for instruction, but not as authoritative Scripture for establishing doctrine. The Roman Catholic Church, at the Council of Trent in 1546, formally affirmed these books as fully canonical. The disagreement turns on real arguments on both sides — the authority of the Hebrew canon and Jerome's doubts on the one hand, the wide early use of the Septuagint on the other — and it remains one of the genuine differences between the Protestant and Catholic Bibles.

## The New Testament Canon

The recognition of the New Testament canon was a process that unfolded over the early centuries of the church, and understanding that process is essential. The twenty-seven books of the New Testament were not handed down in a single authoritative list from the beginning; rather, the church came to recognize them gradually, as it discerned which writings bore the marks of divine inspiration and apostolic authority. The core of the canon was recognized very early: the four Gospels and the letters of Paul were functioning as Scripture, read and revered in the churches, well within the second century. The recognition of the whole collection took longer.

What criteria guided the church's discernment? Several worked together. The first was *apostolicity* — whether a book was written by an apostle or by someone closely associated with the apostolic circle, and so carried apostolic authority. The second was *orthodoxy* — whether a book's teaching agreed with the received "rule of faith," the apostolic gospel handed down in the church; writings that contradicted the apostolic teaching, such as the later Gnostic gospels, were rejected. The third was *catholicity* or universal acceptance — whether a book was widely and continuously received and used across the churches, rather than confined to a corner or a sect. Underlying all these external marks was the conviction that the canonical books possessed a *self-authenticating* divine quality, recognized by the Spirit-led people of God. Some books — Hebrews, James, 2 Peter, 2 and 3 John, Jude, and Revelation, the so-called "disputed" books — were debated and took longer to win universal acceptance, precisely because the church was careful. By the latter half of the fourth century the recognition had crystallized: the great bishop Athanasius, in a letter of 367, listed exactly the twenty-seven books we now receive, and church councils such as that at Carthage in 397 affirmed the same. This was *recognition*, the settling of what the church had increasingly received, not the invention of a canon by decree.[^1]

## The Pressure of Heresy

It is worth noting why the church was driven to define the canon more explicitly when it did. Much of the impetus came from the pressure of *heresy*. In the second century, the heretic Marcion proposed a drastically truncated canon, rejecting the entire Old Testament and accepting only a mutilated version of Luke and a selection of Paul's letters, to fit his anti-Jewish theology. At the same time, Gnostic groups were producing their own "gospels" and writings, claiming secret apostolic authority. These challenges forced the church to articulate more clearly what it had in fact received — to distinguish, publicly and explicitly, the genuine apostolic Scriptures from the counterfeit and the heretical. The defining of the canon was thus largely a defensive and confessional act: the church, confronted with false claimants, bore clearer witness to the true books it had long been receiving. Heresy compelled the church to say plainly what had been its implicit conviction all along.

## How We Know the Canon Is Right

This brings us to the deepest question, and to the point where the Reformed and Roman Catholic accounts diverge most sharply. *How do we finally know* that the church recognized the right books — that the canon is correct? The Roman Catholic answer locates the final ground in the authority of the church: it is the church, guided by the Spirit and exercising its teaching authority, that authoritatively determines the canon, and we know the canon is right because the church, which is logically prior to it, has declared it so. The Reformed tradition answers differently. To make the church the final authority that *determines* the canon would be to place the church above Scripture — to make the church the judge rather than the witness, and so to overturn *sola scriptura* at its root.

Instead, the Reformed tradition grounds our confidence in the *self-authenticating* character of the inspired books, recognized through the internal testimony of the Holy Spirit. The canonical books bear within themselves the marks of their divine origin, and the Spirit who inspired them enables the church and the believer to recognize them as the Word of God — much as one recognizes light by its own shining. The historical marks — apostolicity, orthodoxy, universal reception — are genuine and valuable supports, real evidence that confirms the recognition; and the church's collective witness across the centuries is a weighty testimony not to be despised. But the *ground* of canonicity is the books' inspiration, and the *means* by which we come to rest in it is the Spirit's witness, not the church's decree. The church is the Spirit-led witness to the canon, not its author. We will develop this doctrine of the Spirit's internal testimony fully in the next and final lesson of this unit, for it is the key that unlocks the whole question of how we know.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The Roman Catholic objection at this point is serious and must be faced squarely — it is, indeed, one of the strongest arguments against the Protestant position. The objection runs: Scripture contains no inspired table of contents listing its own books; the canon had to be discerned and settled by the church over time; therefore the church's authority is, in the order of knowing, *prior* to the canon — you cannot know which books are Scripture except through the church that identified them. And if the church's authority is prior to and necessary for the canon, then *sola scriptura* is undermined, for the very Bible to which it appeals depends on the church's authority to be known at all. This is the "canon problem," and it is not easily dismissed.

The Reformed reply, as we have seen, turns on the distinction between *recognizing* and *conferring*, and on the difference between an *epistemic* and an *ontological* priority. The church is prior to the canon only in a limited, epistemic sense — we learn which books are canonical through the church's historical witness, as we learn many true things through reliable testimony. But the church is not prior in the deeper, ontological sense — it did not make the books authoritative; their authority comes from God in their inspiration, and the church discovered rather than created it. Recognizing authentic currency does not give the currency its value; testifying to a truth does not make it true. Honesty requires admitting that this is a careful needle to thread, and that there is a real sense in which we receive the canon through the church's Spirit-guided discernment — the Reformed tradition does not deny the church a genuine and indispensable role as witness. The difference from Rome is precise but momentous: the church serves the Word as witness; it does not stand over the Word as judge. A second honest acknowledgment concerns the *historical messiness* of the process: the canon was genuinely disputed at its edges, some books were doubted for centuries, and the boundaries were fuzzy for a time. The Reformed tradition grants this freely, observing that the *core* was recognized early and with remarkable unanimity, that the disputed books were few and at the margins, and that the Spirit guided the church, over time and through real human deliberation, to the right and settled result. The gradualness of recognition does not impugn the outcome; God's providence works through history, not around it. With the boundaries of the Word thus established, one final question of the doctrine of Scripture remains — how the believer comes to know, with the assurance of faith, that this Word is from God. To the witness of the Spirit we turn last.
