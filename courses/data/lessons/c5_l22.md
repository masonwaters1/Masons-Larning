Luther had struck a match, expecting to light a candle. The church's response turned it into a conflagration. For the church did not quietly concede the abuses Luther had named; it moved against him — and each confrontation, rather than silencing him, pushed him to follow the logic of his convictions further than he had yet gone. In the three years between the Ninety-Five Theses and the Diet of Worms, Luther was transformed from a critic of indulgences into an open rebel against the authority of pope and council, armed with a developed alternative theology. This lesson traces that widening quarrel — through the great debate at Leipzig, the revolutionary treatises of 1520, and the dramatic burning of the papal bull that made the breach irreversible.

## From Indulgences to Authority

The early confrontations already revealed where the conflict was heading. Summoned in 1518 to answer to Cardinal Cajetan, the learned papal legate at Augsburg, Luther was simply commanded to recant. He refused — but the ground of his refusal is what mattered: he would not recant, he said, unless he were convinced from Scripture that he was wrong. Already the issue was shifting from indulgences to *authority*: by what standard is a disputed question to be settled — the bare command of the church, or the testimony of Scripture? Luther fled Augsburg, appealing to a future general council to judge his case. The quarrel about a particular abuse was becoming a quarrel about the very seat of authority in the church.

This deeper issue came to a head in 1519, at the great public disputation in Leipzig, where Luther faced one of the ablest Catholic theologians of the age, Johann Eck. Eck was a formidable debater, and he understood, better than Luther himself perhaps yet did, where Luther's premises led. His strategy was not to argue about indulgences but to expose the radical implications of Luther's position on authority — and to do so, he steered Luther toward the most dangerous ground of all.

## The Leipzig Debate

Eck pressed Luther on the authority of the pope and the councils, and step by step maneuvered him into admissions that could not be retracted. First, Luther was led to deny the divine right of the papacy — to argue that the supremacy of the pope is a human institution of relatively recent origin, not an office established by Christ, and therefore not binding by divine law. This alone was grave. But Eck pressed further, and drew from Luther a far more explosive admission. He maneuvered Luther into acknowledging that certain of the views of Jan Hus — the Bohemian reformer condemned as a heretic and burned at the Council of Constance a century before — were in fact genuinely Christian and biblical.[^1]

The implications were staggering, and Eck knew it. To say that Hus had held true and Christian views was to say that the Council of Constance — a general council of the whole church — had *erred*, and had wrongly condemned and burned a faithful man. Luther, cornered, embraced the conclusion: councils, too, can err and have erred; even a general council and a pope can be wrong. And if pope and council alike can err, then there remains only one final, infallible authority: Holy Scripture. Here, in the heat of debate, the formal principle of the Reformation crystallized — *sola scriptura*, Scripture alone as the supreme judge, above pope and council both. Eck had achieved his aim: he had exposed Luther, before all of Germany, as a man who held that the church's highest authorities could be wrong and who aligned himself openly with a convicted heretic. But the exposure cut both ways, for what Eck meant to discredit, Luther came to embrace. Leipzig was the point of no return. Luther left it holding, openly, that Scripture stands above pope and council — a position that could not be reconciled with the medieval church.

## The Treatises of 1520

The following year, 1520, was Luther's *annus mirabilis*, his year of wonders, in which he poured out a torrent of writing that set forth the positive program of the Reformation. Three treatises in particular were revolutionary, and together they amounted to a comprehensive assault on the foundations of the medieval church and a sketch of the new evangelical faith.

The first, *To the Christian Nobility of the German Nation*, appealed to the German princes to take up the cause of reform, since the church hierarchy would not reform itself. In it Luther attacked what he called the "three walls" behind which Rome had sheltered itself from correction, and proclaimed the *priesthood of all believers* — the teaching that every baptized Christian is a priest before God, so that the sharp medieval division between a sacred clergy and a secular laity, with the clergy alone holding the keys to grace, is abolished. All believers have direct access to God through Christ. The second treatise, *The Babylonian Captivity of the Church*, struck at the sacramental system itself: Luther argued that of the seven sacraments only two — baptism and the Lord's Supper — were genuinely instituted by Christ, and he attacked the doctrine of the Mass as a sacrifice and the whole apparatus by which the church's priesthood controlled the channels of grace. This cut at the very root of the church's power over salvation. The third, *The Freedom of a Christian*, was the gentle, positive heart of the matter — a statement of the gospel of justification by faith and its fruit, summed up in a famous paradox: the Christian is a perfectly free lord, subject to none, by faith; and at the same time a perfectly dutiful servant, subject to all, in love. Freed by grace from the need to earn salvation, the Christian is liberated to serve his neighbor freely. In these three works, the Reformation's program — its critique of Rome and its evangelical alternative — stood forth fully formed.

## The Bull and the Fire

Rome's patience was now exhausted. In June 1520, Pope Leo X issued the bull *Exsurge Domine* — "Arise, O Lord" — which condemned forty-one propositions drawn from Luther's writings as heretical or offensive, ordered his books burned, and threatened him with excommunication unless he recanted within sixty days. The bull was the church's formal demand for surrender: submit, or be cut off.

Luther's answer was as dramatic as it was irreversible. In December 1520, before a crowd of students and citizens at the Elster Gate in Wittenberg, he publicly *burned* the papal bull, together with volumes of the canon law on which the church's legal authority rested. It was a deliberate, defiant, unmistakable act — the public repudiation of papal authority itself, a burning of bridges that could never be rebuilt. The man who three years earlier had respectfully sent his theses to his archbishop now consigned the pope's own decree to the flames before a cheering crowd. In January 1521 the threatened sentence fell: Luther was formally excommunicated, cut off from the church of Rome. The widening quarrel had become a complete rupture.

## The Logic of the Breach

It is worth pausing to see the shape of what had happened. In a little over three years, Luther had traveled an immense distance — from a loyal friar questioning an abuse, to an excommunicated rebel who denied the divine authority of the pope, held that councils can err, recognized only two sacraments, proclaimed the priesthood of all believers, and rested everything on Scripture alone and justification by faith. He had not set out on this road; he had been driven down it, step by step, by the collision between his convictions and the church's demand for unconditional submission. Each confrontation — Augsburg, Leipzig, the bull — forced him to choose between recanting and following his premises further, and each time he chose to follow. The result was a thoroughly worked-out alternative to the medieval church, and an open, public, irreversible breach with Rome.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Two framings of this story deserve a hearing, for they correspond to the deep interpretive divide we noted in this course's first lesson. From the Protestant side, Luther's radicalization appears as a story of *courage and fidelity*: confronted by a church that would not reform and that demanded he deny what Scripture had taught him, Luther followed the truth wherever it led, refusing to betray his conscience and the Word of God even at the cost of excommunication. His burning of the bull was the heroic act of a man who would not bow to a corrupt human authority against the authority of God. On this reading, the church's intransigence, not Luther's stubbornness, drove the breach; a church willing to be corrected by Scripture would have retained him.

From the Catholic side, the same events look quite different — as a story of *escalating pride and schism*. On this reading, Luther's positions were genuinely heretical by the church's standards, and the church had not only the right but the duty to condemn them; Luther's refusal to submit to the lawful authority of the church, his denial of the papacy and the councils, and above all his public burning of the bull, were the acts not of a faithful reformer but of a man who had set his own private judgment above the God-given authority of the church, and who chose schism over obedience. There is also a real question, which thoughtful people on both sides have weighed, of whether a more patient man — an Erasmus rather than a Luther — might have pressed for reform without rupture, sparing Christendom the division and the wars to come. The course holds, with the Reformed tradition, that the issues at stake were too fundamental for compromise and that Luther's stand was a recovery of the gospel; but it does not pretend that the breach was costless or that the Catholic reading is foolish. What is beyond dispute is that, by early 1521, the rupture was complete and irreversible — and that the matter would now be settled not in a lecture hall but before the assembled power of the empire. To the Diet of Worms we turn next.
