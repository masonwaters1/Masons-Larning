An outlaw of the empire, condemned by church and emperor alike, Luther vanished from the world into a castle on a wooded hilltop — the Wartburg, where Frederick the Wise had hidden him for safety. To the outside world he had disappeared; some thought him dead. In fact he had entered the most productive seclusion of his life. Disguised as a bearded knight, "Junker Jörg," he spent the better part of a year in the Wartburg, and out of that hidden year came the single greatest gift the German Reformation would give to the German people: the Bible in their own tongue. If the previous lessons showed Luther the protester and the rebel, this one shows Luther the translator — and reveals that the Reformation was, at its heart, a movement of the Word.

## The Hidden Months

Luther's time in the Wartburg, from the spring of 1521 into the following year, was a strange and intense season. Cut off from the swirling events he had set in motion, living under a false name and a false beard, he wrestled with isolation, illness, and spiritual turmoil. This is the period of his famous "struggles with the devil," in which he experienced the assaults and depressions he attributed to Satan's attacks — the legend that he hurled an inkwell at the devil belongs to these months. But Luther was not idle in his hiding. He wrote prolifically, sending out a stream of treatises and letters that kept the movement alive while its leader was concealed. And he undertook the labor for which the Wartburg is chiefly remembered.

In a span of roughly eleven weeks, Luther translated the entire New Testament from Greek into German. He worked not from the Latin Vulgate but from the original Greek text — the very text that Erasmus's humanist scholarship had made available in print, the tool the new learning had placed in the Reformers' hands. The result, published in September 1522 and known as the "September Testament," put the New Testament into vigorous, living German for the first time in a form that ordinary people could read and love. Luther later turned to the Old Testament, translating from the Hebrew with the help of colleagues skilled in the biblical languages, and the complete German Bible was finished in 1534. It was the work of his life, revised and refined until his death.

## Why It Mattered So Much

The significance of Luther's German Bible can hardly be overstated, and it operated on several levels at once. At the most basic, it embodied the very principles for which the Reformation stood. If Scripture alone is the final authority (*sola scriptura*), and if every believer is a priest with direct access to God (the priesthood of all believers), then the Word of God must not remain locked in Latin, the property of a clerical elite; it must be given to the people in their own language, so that they may read and hear it for themselves. Luther's translation took the conviction that Wycliffe and the humanists had championed and made it a reality on a vast scale: it broke the clergy's monopoly on the interpretation of Scripture by placing the Scripture directly into the hands of the laity. The plowman and the housewife could now encounter the Word of God without a priest standing between.

But the German Bible was more than a vehicle of a principle; it was a work of extraordinary literary power, and this too was deliberate. Luther did not translate woodenly, word for word, producing a stiff and foreign-sounding text. He translated into the living idiom of the German people, striving to make the Word speak as naturally and forcefully in German as it had in its original tongues. His own famous description of his method was that one must look the common people in the mouth — listen to how ordinary men and women, the mother in the home, the children in the street, the merchant in the market, actually spoke — and render the Scriptures in that living speech. The result was a translation of such vigor, warmth, and beauty that it entered the bloodstream of the German language and became one of the supreme monuments of German literature.

## The Word That Shaped a Language

Here we encounter one of the great themes of this course: the unintended consequences of the Reformation, the way its recovery of the gospel sent ripples far beyond religion into the whole fabric of culture. Luther's Bible did not merely translate the Scriptures; it helped to *create modern standard German*. Before Luther, the German lands spoke a patchwork of regional dialects, often barely intelligible to one another, with no single literary standard. Luther's Bible, printed in enormous numbers and read across all the German territories, supplied a common language — a unified, elevated, widely shared German that knit the dialects together and became the foundation of the modern literary tongue. For centuries afterward, German literature, German identity, and the German language itself bore the deep imprint of Luther's Bible.

This is a pattern we will see again. The Reformation's drive to put Scripture into the language of the people had consequences its authors never intended and could scarcely have imagined: it drove literacy, as people learned to read in order to read the Bible; it shaped national languages and literatures; it helped forge national identities. What Luther's Bible did for German, the later English translations — culminating in the King James Version — would do for English. The recovery of the Word for the people was, among its other effects, one of the great engines of the modern world. The Reformers sought to give people the gospel; they ended by reshaping languages, nations, and cultures.

## A Movement of the Word

The German Bible also reveals something essential about the character of the Reformation itself. It was, at its heart, a movement of *the Word*. Luther's protest had begun with the gospel he found in Scripture; his authority rested on Scripture alone; and now his greatest constructive labor was to make Scripture available to all. The Reformation was not merely a negative movement of protest against abuses; it was a positive movement to restore the Word of God to the center of the church's life and to place it in the hands of every believer. The printed Bible in the vernacular became the engine of Protestant faith and piety — read in homes, expounded from pulpits, memorized, sung, argued over, lived by. Luther's translation was the indispensable foundation of this Bible-centered religion.

And the impulse spread. Luther's German Bible inspired and emboldened vernacular translations across Europe. In England, William Tyndale — working, like Luther, from Erasmus's Greek text — produced his English New Testament in 1526, a translation of such power that it shaped the English Bible for centuries to come, though Tyndale himself would pay for the work with his life, betrayed and burned in 1536. Everywhere the Reformation went, the Bible in the language of the people went with it, multiplied by the printing press, carrying the Word to ordinary readers in their thousands. The Reformation was the Word of God, recovered, translated, printed, and let loose among the people.

## Trouble at Home

Even as Luther labored over his translation in the Wartburg, the movement he had begun was running ahead of him in Wittenberg — and not always in directions he approved. In his absence, leadership had passed to more radical figures, and the reform in Wittenberg accelerated into disorder: images were torn from churches, the Mass was abruptly altered, and self-proclaimed prophets arrived claiming new revelations of the Spirit. What had been a movement for the recovery of the gospel threatened to dissolve into radicalism and tumult. The reports alarmed Luther, and at last, defying the danger to his own life as an outlaw, he left the safety of the Wartburg and returned to Wittenberg in March 1522 to restrain the radicals and steady the course of reform. That confrontation — between Luther and the more radical spirits his movement had unleashed — opens a new and crucial chapter, and to it we will turn in the next lesson.

## The Honest Counterpoint

A few honest qualifications complete the picture. First, Luther's translation, magnificent as it was, was not a perfectly neutral rendering of the original — and Luther knew it. The most famous instance is his translation of a verse in Romans, where Paul writes that a person is justified by faith; Luther rendered it "justified by faith *alone*," inserting the word "alone" (*allein*), which is not in the Greek text. Challenged on this, Luther defended the addition vigorously, arguing that "alone" captured the true sense of the passage in good German and was demanded by the meaning of the gospel — but his critics charged, not without reason, that he had allowed his doctrine to shape his translation at a crucial point. The episode is a fair and concrete reminder that even the greatest translation bears the hand and the convictions of the translator.

Second, Luther was not, strictly speaking, the *first* to translate the Bible into German; earlier German versions existed before him. But these were generally made from the Latin Vulgate rather than the original languages, were often clumsy and obscure, and had little circulation or influence. Luther's achievement was not that he did something never done before, but that he did it incomparably better — translating from the original tongues, into living and powerful German, at the moment when the printing press could carry it to the whole nation. And third, the claim that Luther single-handedly "created" modern German, while pointing to a genuine and enormous influence, is somewhat romanticized; the standardization of German was a longer and more complex process to which Luther's Bible made a decisive but not solitary contribution. These qualifications temper the legend without diminishing the substance: Luther's German Bible was a work of genius and a turning point in the history of both the church and the German people. With the Word now placed in the hands of the people, the Reformation had its foundation. But the movement Luther had unleashed was proving harder to control than to start — and the next lessons must follow it into the conflicts, the radicalism, and the bloodshed that its sudden success unleashed.
