While Luther lay hidden in the Wartburg, translating the New Testament, the movement he had begun did not wait for him. In Wittenberg, his own city, the reformation ran ahead of its absent leader — and ran, Luther came to fear, off the rails. More radical men seized the initiative, pushing change faster and further than Luther had intended, until the city teetered on the edge of disorder. When the reports reached him, Luther made a fateful decision: he left the safety of his hiding place, at the risk of his life as an outlaw, and returned to take command. The confrontation that followed — between Luther and the radicals his own movement had unleashed — defined the character of the Reformation he wanted, and opened the first great fault line within Protestantism itself.

## Reform Without Luther

In Luther's absence, leadership in Wittenberg passed largely to his senior colleague Andreas Karlstadt, an older and learned man who now pressed the practical work of reform with impatient zeal. Where Luther had so far concentrated on doctrine and left many external practices untouched, Karlstadt moved to overturn them. At Christmas 1521 he celebrated a radically simplified evangelical communion: he set aside the priestly vestments, conducted the service in German rather than Latin, dropped the language of sacrifice, and gave the people both the bread *and* the cup — communion in both kinds, long denied to the laity. He soon married, a public act of defiance against clerical celibacy. The pace of change accelerated week by week, and Karlstadt grew steadily more radical, increasingly disparaging learning and academic theology and exalting the inner promptings of the Spirit over external forms.

Most disruptive of all was the turn against religious images. Convinced that statues and paintings in churches violated the commandment against idolatry, radical reformers and crowds began tearing down and destroying the images — an outbreak of *iconoclasm* that filled the churches with smashed statues and stripped walls, and spilled easily into mob action and disorder. What had begun as a movement for the recovery of the gospel was, in Wittenberg, threatening to become a tumult.

## The Prophets of Zwickau

Into this volatile situation came a still more unsettling element. Late in 1521 there arrived in Wittenberg three men from the town of Zwickau — the so-called "Zwickau prophets" — who claimed to receive direct revelations from the Holy Spirit. They spoke of dreams, visions, and prophecies; they claimed an immediate, inner word from God that needed no Scripture, no learning, no external authority. God spoke to them directly, they said, Spirit to spirit. These men unsettled even the calm and scholarly Melanchthon, Luther's closest colleague, who found himself unable to refute their claims and anxiously awaited Luther's judgment.

The Zwickau prophets embodied an error we have already named in the theology thread — the error of *Spirit without Word*, the claim that the Holy Spirit speaks immediately to the individual soul, apart from and even above the written Scripture. This was a profound challenge, for it threatened to cut the Reformation loose from its anchor. If the Spirit speaks directly and authoritatively to each person through inner revelation, then Scripture is no longer the final standard, and the movement dissolves into a chaos of competing private inspirations. The radicals had unleashed not only social disorder but a theological principle that, carried through, would undo the very foundation Luther had laid: Scripture alone.

## Luther's Return

The reports alarmed Luther deeply. He saw his movement endangered from within — its message of evangelical freedom curdling into compulsion, iconoclasm, and disorder, and its foundation in the Word threatened by claims of new revelation. And so, in March 1522, defying the imperial ban that made him an outlaw liable to death, Luther left the Wartburg and returned to Wittenberg to reclaim and steady his reformation. Over eight successive days he preached a series of sermons — known as the Invocavit Sermons — that calmed the city and reversed the radical excesses.[^1]

Luther's message was a model of pastoral wisdom and a clear statement of how he believed reform must proceed. Reform, he insisted, must come through the *Word* — through preaching, teaching, and persuasion — and never through force, compulsion, or mob violence. Faith cannot be coerced; it must come freely, won by the Word working in the heart. He would preach the truth, he declared, teach it, and write it, but he would constrain no one by force, for the Word must do its work without compulsion. He rebuked the haste and harshness of the radicals, insisting on patience and on consideration for the *weak* — those whose consciences were not yet ready for rapid change, who must be led gently rather than driven. He distinguished the essentials of the gospel, which could not be compromised, from the externals and ceremonies that were matters indifferent (*adiaphora*) and could be reformed gradually, in order and love. Within days, the tumult subsided, the radical innovations were moderated, and order was restored. Luther had saved his reformation from dissolving into anarchy.

## Christian Liberty and the Weak

Beneath Luther's handling of the Wittenberg crisis lay a principle he had drawn from Scripture and would make central to his whole vision of the Christian life: the principle of *Christian liberty* rightly used. The radicals had treated their reforms — abolishing images, changing the liturgy, casting off ceremonies — as matters that must be imposed at once on everyone, as though the gospel commanded them. Luther answered that in such *external* matters, things neither commanded nor forbidden by God (the *adiaphora*, or "things indifferent"), the Christian is genuinely free — free to keep them or to lay them aside. But precisely because they are matters of freedom and not of command, they must never be forced; and the use of one's liberty must be governed by *love*.

Here Luther drew on the apostle Paul's teaching about the strong and the weak. The "strong" believer, who understands his freedom, must not trample the conscience of the "weak" believer, who is not yet ready for change and might be wounded or driven away by reforms imposed too suddenly. Love limits the exercise of liberty for the sake of the brother. To rush ahead, smashing images and overturning customs without regard for the consciences of ordinary people, was therefore not a mark of evangelical zeal but a failure of love — a using of Christian freedom as a weapon rather than a gift. Reform must proceed at the pace that love and the weak could bear, taught by the Word and won by persuasion. This wedding of liberty and love, of evangelical freedom and pastoral patience, would remain one of Luther's most characteristic and enduring contributions to Protestant ethics.

## The First Fault Line

This was a defining moment, and its significance reaches far beyond Wittenberg. In confronting the radicals, Luther established the character of what would be called the *magisterial* Reformation — the mainstream Reformation of Luther, and later of Zwingli and Calvin, which worked in cooperation with the lawful magistrates and authorities, proceeded in order, centered itself on the preached Word and the sacraments, respected the weak, and rejected both compulsion and the claims of new revelation. Against this stood the more *radical* reformation — the iconoclasts, the spiritualists who claimed immediate inspiration, and later the Anabaptists — which pressed further and faster, often rejecting the cooperation of the state and the structures of the established church.

The break was personal as well as principled: Luther and Karlstadt became opponents, and Karlstadt left Wittenberg to pursue his more radical course. Luther later poured scorn on the spiritualists and "heavenly prophets," mocking those who claimed to have swallowed the Holy Spirit, as he put it, feathers and all. Here was the deep theological issue we met in the lesson on Word and Spirit: Luther insisted that the Spirit works *through* the external Word and sacrament, not apart from them, and that every claim of the Spirit must be tested by Scripture. He stood, therefore, against two errors at once — against Rome, which he believed bound the Spirit to the institutional church, and against the enthusiasts, who loosed the Spirit from the Word altogether. The Spirit and the Word belong together; to divide them is to fall into either dead authority or wild delusion. This conviction would mark the magisterial Reformation permanently, and the fault line that opened in Wittenberg in 1522 would run through the whole subsequent history of Protestantism.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Luther's conservative turn has been judged in opposite ways, and both judgments deserve a hearing. From within the magisterial tradition — including the Reformed tradition of this course — Luther's restraint appears as wise and necessary *pastoral statesmanship*: he saved the Reformation from discrediting itself in anarchy and violence, kept it anchored to the Word against the chaos of private revelation, and insisted rightly that faith cannot be forced and that the weak must be led with patience rather than driven. Without Luther's steadying hand, the argument runs, the movement might have dissolved into the fanaticism and disorder that would later erupt at Münster, and been crushed as a public menace.

But the radical tradition, and its later heirs, saw the same events very differently — as a *failure of nerve*, even a betrayal. On this reading, Luther proclaimed a gospel of evangelical freedom and the priesthood of all believers, and then, when ordinary people began to take that freedom seriously and to reform their churches and challenge established powers, he drew back, subordinated the reformation to the princes and magistrates, and halted the movement halfway for the sake of order. The radicals charged that Luther stopped short of his own principles, compromising with worldly power rather than following the Word wherever it led. And honesty requires noting two further complications. First, several of the very reforms Karlstadt pioneered — communion in both kinds, worship in the vernacular, the marriage of clergy — Luther himself would later adopt; the quarrel was partly about *pace and method* (persuasion versus force, gradual versus immediate) rather than ultimate destination. Second, on the contested matter of *images*, it is the Reformed wing of the Reformation — Zwingli, Calvin, and the tradition this course follows — that would side largely with the iconoclasts against Luther, stripping the churches of images as Karlstadt had urged, while Luther remained comparatively conservative and tolerant of them. The first fault line within Protestantism, in other words, did not run simply between Luther and fanatics; it ran through genuine and enduring questions about how far, how fast, and by what means the church should be reformed — questions on which the Reformation's own heirs would continue to divide. That disorder in Wittenberg, however, would prove mild beside the social earthquake about to strike all of Germany, as the common people took the language of Christian freedom into the fields and rose against their lords.
