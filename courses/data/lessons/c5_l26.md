The disorder in Wittenberg had been a tremor; now came the earthquake. In 1524 and 1525, the German lands were convulsed by the largest popular uprising Europe would see before the French Revolution — the Peasants' War. Tens of thousands of peasants, joined by townsfolk and miners, rose against their lords across southern and central Germany, and they rose, in part, in the name of the gospel that Luther had unleashed. The revolt confronted Luther with the most agonizing dilemma of his career, and his response to it — savage, ill-timed, and lastingly controversial — is among the darkest and most debated episodes of his life. It also revealed, in blood, the deep question of how the Reformation's gospel of freedom related to the social and political order of the world.

## The Rising

The German peasantry labored under heavy and worsening burdens — feudal dues and rents, the bondage of serfdom, the loss of traditional common lands and rights, the exactions of lords both secular and ecclesiastical. Grievances had long been building, and in 1524 they boiled over into open revolt, spreading rapidly across the south and center of the empire until much of Germany was aflame with rebellion. It was a vast, sprawling, often disorganized uprising, but its scale was immense and its fury real.

What gave the revolt its distinctive character — and its connection to our story — was that the rebels framed their cause in the language of the Reformation. They had heard Luther proclaim the gospel, the freedom of a Christian, the authority of the Word of God against human tradition, the equality of all believers before God — and they took these principles into the fields and applied them to their own oppression. Their famous manifesto, the Twelve Articles, grounded the peasants' demands in Scripture, citing biblical texts in support of each: the right of congregations to choose and depose their own pastors; relief from oppressive dues and tithes; and, most strikingly, the abolition of serfdom, on the ground that Christ had shed his blood to redeem all people, high and low alike, so that no Christian should be held as another's property. They appealed to Luther by name, expecting the great champion of Christian freedom to be their ally. The "freedom of a Christian" had been carried from the realm of the soul into the realm of society, and turned into a charter for social revolution.[^1]

## Müntzer and the Apocalypse

The revolt also drew to itself a figure of fiery radicalism: Thomas Müntzer, a former associate of Luther turned bitter enemy. Müntzer was a spiritualist and apocalyptic preacher who had embraced the very "Spirit without Word" radicalism Luther opposed, claiming direct divine inspiration and proclaiming that the last days had come. He preached not patience but holy violence — the overthrow of the godless rulers by the sword, the establishment of God's kingdom on earth through the slaughter of the ungodly, the elect rising to purge the world. In Müntzer the social grievances of the peasants fused with revolutionary apocalyptic fervor into a call for armed, righteous revolution. He took a leading role among the rebels, promising them divine protection in battle, and led a peasant force to confront the princes' army at Frankenhausen in 1525 — where the poorly armed peasants were slaughtered, and Müntzer was captured and executed.

Müntzer represented Luther's nightmare made flesh: the gospel of evangelical freedom twisted, through radical spiritualism, into a program of bloody social revolution. For Luther, who had labored to keep the Reformation anchored to the Word and distinct from social upheaval, Müntzer was the proof of where the radical path led — and a mortal threat to the whole movement's credibility.

## Luther Caught in the Middle

Luther's response unfolded in two stages, and the second has shadowed his name ever since. At first he tried to mediate. In an "Admonition to Peace," written in response to the Twelve Articles, he addressed both sides. He rebuked the *lords* for their genuine oppression and greed, acknowledging that some of the peasants' grievances were just and that the rulers had brought trouble on themselves by their tyranny. But he also warned the *peasants* sternly against rebellion: the gospel, he insisted, is about spiritual freedom, not social revolution; Christians are called to suffer injustice rather than to take up the sword against legitimate authority; to rise in armed revolt, even against real oppression, was to confuse the kingdom of God with the kingdom of this world and to sin against the order God had ordained. He pleaded with both sides to seek peace.

But events outran his admonition. As the revolt spread and turned violent — with plundering, burning of castles and monasteries, and killing — Luther's tone changed utterly. In a furious tract titled *Against the Murderous, Thieving Hordes of Peasants*, he called upon the princes to crush the rebellion without mercy. He urged that the rebels be struck down like mad dogs, that anyone able to do so should smite, slay, and stab them, secretly or openly, and that a ruler who died fighting the rebels died a martyr while a peasant killed in rebellion went to hell. The language was savage beyond anything the occasion could justify. And the timing made it worse: by the time the tract circulated widely, the revolt was already being crushed with horrific brutality, the princes' armies slaughtering the poorly armed peasants by the tens of thousands. Luther's tract, calling for merciless suppression, appeared as a blessing upon a massacre already in full course.

## The Reckoning

The consequences were grave and lasting. The revolt was drowned in blood; perhaps a hundred thousand peasants were killed, and the survivors gained almost nothing — in many places their condition grew harsher than before. And Luther's reputation among the common people suffered a wound that never fully healed. Many of the poor who had looked to him as a champion now felt betrayed, abandoned to the swords of the princes by the man whose gospel of freedom had helped inspire their hopes. The Reformation lost much of its popular, revolutionary energy and bound itself more firmly than ever to the princes and the established authorities — the "magisterial" character of the movement hardening into permanence. Henceforth the Reformation would advance in Germany chiefly through the cooperation of rulers and magistrates, not through popular upheaval. The radical wing, tainted by association with the violence and with Müntzer's apocalyptic revolutionism, was discredited and would be savagely persecuted in the years to come.

Why did Luther respond as he did? His underlying conviction was rooted in his theology of the *two kingdoms* — the distinction between the spiritual realm, governed by the gospel, and the temporal realm, governed by law and the sword and ordained by God for the restraint of evil. The gospel, Luther held, does not abolish the temporal order; rebellion against legitimate authority is a grave sin against the order God himself has established, and the apostle Paul had taught that the governing powers are ordained by God and bear the sword for a reason. Luther was also genuinely horrified by the chaos and bloodshed, and terrified that his gospel would be permanently discredited if the Reformation became identified with anarchy and revolution. These were not contemptible concerns. But they cannot excuse the ferocity of his language, and Luther himself, in calmer moments, seems to have felt the weight of what he had written.

## The Two Kingdoms

To understand Luther's response to the revolt, and much of his political thought, we must grasp the doctrine that underlay it: his teaching on the *two kingdoms*, or two governments, through which God rules the world. God reigns, Luther held, in two distinct ways. In the *spiritual* kingdom, he governs believers through the gospel, the Word, and the work of the Spirit, bringing them to faith and ruling their hearts by grace; here the sword has no place, for faith cannot be coerced and the gospel works only by persuasion. In the *temporal* kingdom, God governs the outward life of all people — believers and unbelievers alike — through the civil authorities, the law, and the sword, restraining evil, punishing wrongdoing, and preserving order in a fallen world. Both kingdoms are God's, but they operate by different means and must not be confused.

From this followed Luther's conviction that the gospel does not abolish or overturn the temporal order. The Christian's spiritual freedom does not release him from his civic obligations or authorize him to rebel against the magistrate, for the governing authorities are, as the apostle Paul taught, ordained by God and bear the sword by his appointment. To take up arms against legitimate authority, even in a just cause, was therefore to confuse the two kingdoms — to drag the spiritual freedom of the gospel into the temporal realm where it did not belong, and to rebel against an order God himself had established. This is why Luther, however much he sympathized with the peasants' grievances, could not sanction their revolt: their error, in his eyes, was not merely tactical but theological, a collapsing of the two kingdoms into one. The doctrine secured a vital truth — that the gospel is not a political program and Christ's kingdom is not of this world — but it carried, as its critics have long charged, a tendency toward *quietism*, a readiness to leave injustice unchallenged in the temporal realm and to align the church with the powers that be. It is a tension the Reformed tradition would later address with a bolder political theology.

## The Honest Counterpoint

This is one of the episodes where honesty requires plain speech, and the criticism of Luther is largely just. The tract *Against the Murderous, Thieving Hordes of Peasants* is, by almost any measure, indefensible in its savagery — a call for merciless slaughter, issued as that slaughter was already underway, against the poor and oppressed whose grievances Luther himself had partly acknowledged. Even many who revere Luther find this writing a genuine stain on his record, a failure of charity, proportion, and pastoral judgment. We should not explain it away.

And yet a fair reckoning must also weigh the other side. Luther's underlying *principle* — that the gospel is not a charter for violent social revolution, that rebellion against legitimate authority is a serious matter, that the spiritual and temporal kingdoms must be distinguished — was a defensible position with deep roots in Scripture and Christian tradition, and not merely a cowardly siding with power. He had genuinely tried to mediate first; the violence and disorder of the revolt were real and terrible; and Müntzer's apocalyptic revolutionism was a true danger that threatened to engulf the Reformation. The deeper issue the Peasants' War raises is one that has no easy resolution: the relationship between the gospel and the social and political order. Luther drew a sharp line, confining the gospel to the spiritual realm and leaving the social order to the sword of the magistrate — a position that secured order but at the cost of a certain quietism in the face of injustice, and that aligned the church with the powerful against the poor. The Reformed tradition, as this course will later show, would develop a different and more activist political theology, with its theories of lawful resistance to tyranny. The peasants' genuine grievances, and Luther's ultimate choice of order over their cause, leave a permanent question hanging over the magisterial Reformation: whether, in binding itself to the princes and confining the gospel to the soul, it was faithful to the limits of Christ's kingdom or unfaithful to the demands of justice. There is no tidy answer, and the blood of the peasants will not let the question rest. From this dark and divisive episode, we turn to a conflict of a very different kind — not of swords but of ideas — in which Luther confronted the greatest scholar of the age over the deepest question of all: the freedom, or the bondage, of the human will.
