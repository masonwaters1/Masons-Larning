We met the great clash between Luther and Erasmus once before, in the history of the humanists, where it marked the parting of humanism and the Reformation. Now we return to it, for it deserves a place of its own — not merely as a quarrel between two famous men, but as the moment when the deepest question of the entire Reformation was brought into the open and fought out. For beneath all the disputes about indulgences, the papacy, and the sacraments lay a single, fundamental question: in the matter of salvation, is the human will *free* to turn to God and cooperate with his grace, or is it *bound*, so that salvation must be entirely the work of God? When Erasmus and Luther joined battle over this question, Luther recognized that they had reached, at last, the heart of the matter.

## The Challenge

For years Erasmus had tried to remain above the fray — sympathetic to reform, critical of abuses, but recoiling from Luther's vehemence and his rupture of the church's unity. At last, under mounting pressure from both the church and his own convictions, he was forced to take up his pen against Luther, and in 1524 he published *On the Freedom of the Will (De libero arbitrio)*. Characteristically, he chose his ground with care, selecting not the inflammatory questions of papal authority or the sacraments, but the quieter and more philosophical question of the human will — and he wrote in his usual moderate, cautious, undogmatic manner.

Erasmus defended the traditional view that the human will, though wounded by sin, retains a genuine freedom and a real capacity to cooperate with God's grace in salvation. His arguments were those of reason and moral common sense. Scripture is full of commands, exhortations, and calls to "choose," "turn," and "repent" — and surely, Erasmus argued, God would not command what we are wholly unable to perform; the very existence of these commands implies that we have some power to obey. Moral responsibility, too, seems to require freedom: if we cannot choose otherwise, how can we be justly praised or blamed, rewarded or condemned? And to deny all freedom, Erasmus warned, seemed to make God the author of sin and human beings mere puppets. The question, he urged, was obscure and difficult, and wisdom counseled a modest, moderate position rather than the harsh dogmatism Luther brought to it. He defended, in the end, a "small power" of the free will, cooperating with the grace that God supplies.

## Luther's Answer

Luther's reply, *On the Bondage of the Will (De servo arbitrio)*, published in 1525, was one of the most powerful and uncompromising works he ever wrote — and he regarded it as among his very best, one of the few of his books he thought worth preserving.[^1] Against Erasmus's gentle moderation, Luther brought a thunderous absolutism, and he denied the freedom of the will utterly.

The fallen human will, Luther insisted, is not free but *bound* — enslaved to sin and to Satan, incapable of turning to God by its own power. He pressed the point with a famous and stark image: the human will is like a beast of burden, ridden either by God or by the devil, and it goes where its rider directs; it cannot choose its own rider, nor can it leap free to choose for itself. The will is not idle — it acts, it chooses, it wills — but it is not *free*, for apart from grace it can only will according to its bondage, which is to say, against God. Therefore salvation, from first to last, is entirely the work of God's sovereign grace, not a cooperation between God and the free human will. To attribute even a small power to the will, as Erasmus did, was to rob God of his glory and to set the creature up as a partner in his own salvation.

Luther bound this doctrine tightly to the assurance he had won so dearly. If salvation depended, even in the smallest part, on the free human will, he argued, then no one could ever be certain of it — for one could never know whether one had willed rightly, or willed enough, the very torment that had nearly destroyed him in the monastery. Only if salvation rests *entirely* on God's sovereign and unchanging grace, with nothing left to the uncertain human will, can the sinner have a sure and steadfast hope. The bondage of the will, far from being a doctrine of despair, was for Luther the ground of all assurance: precisely because my salvation does not depend on me at all, but wholly on God, I can rest in it.

## The Vital Spot

What is most striking is Luther's reaction to Erasmus's choice of topic. Far from resenting it, Luther *thanked* Erasmus — for, he said, unlike all his other opponents, who had assailed him over trifles, over indulgences and the papacy and other secondary matters, Erasmus alone had seized upon the real issue, the one thing that mattered, the hinge on which everything turned. Erasmus had grasped him by the throat, gone for the vital spot, attacked the essential point. And that point was precisely this: whether salvation is by grace alone, with the will in bondage, or whether the free will contributes its part. Luther saw, with perfect clarity, that this was *the* question of the Reformation — that all the other disputes were downstream of it. If the will is free to cooperate, then the whole medieval system of merit and cooperation could stand; if the will is bound and salvation is wholly of grace, then that system must fall, and justification by faith alone follows of necessity.

This is why the debate belongs at the heart of the Luther unit, and why it matters so profoundly for the tradition this course traces. The bondage of the will is the foundation of the doctrine of *sovereign grace* — the conviction that gives this course its very name. If the will is bound, then salvation must be *monergistic* — the work of God alone (from the Greek *monos*, "alone," and *ergon*, "work"); if the will is free to cooperate, salvation is *synergistic* — a working together of God and man. This is the watershed. On one side stands the Augustinian and Reformed understanding: grace alone, the bound will, unconditional election, salvation wholly of God. On the other stands the view that human freedom cooperates with grace — the position that the church would later defend at the Council of Trent, and that the Arminians would defend within Protestantism, provoking the Synod of Dort. From the seed of *On the Bondage of the Will* would grow the whole Reformed doctrine of grace: the five points of Calvinism, the sovereignty of God in salvation, the eternal decree of election. We will examine the bondage of the will, and the doctrines of grace that flow from it, in their full theological depth in the units ahead. Here the point is historical and decisive: at the very birth of the Reformation, its deepest principle was identified and defended — that salvation is by grace alone, because the will of the sinner is bound.

## The Common Ground of the Reformation

It is worth noting that on this question — uniquely, perhaps — Luther and the Reformed tradition stand in complete agreement. Luther and Calvin would differ on other matters, and Lutheran and Reformed churches would later divide. But on the bondage of the will and the sovereignty of grace, they were one. Indeed, *On the Bondage of the Will* is in some respects more congenial to the Reformed tradition than to later Lutheranism, which would partly draw back from the strongest predestinarian implications Luther had pressed. The doctrine of the bound will and sovereign grace is the deepest common ground of the magisterial Reformation, the bedrock beneath both its great branches — and it is the foundation of everything this course means by "sovereign grace."

## The Honest Counterpoint

Intellectual honesty requires that Erasmus's side be given its full strength, for the case for the freedom of the will is not weak, and it has been held by serious Christians in every age. The moral intuition that "ought implies can" — that a command to do something presupposes the ability to do it — is powerful and not easily dismissed; Scripture's pervasive commands and exhortations do seem to assume that the hearer can respond. The demand of moral responsibility, that praise and blame require genuine freedom, is weighty. And the deepest worry of all is the one Erasmus pressed: that a wholly bound will seems to make God the author of sin and to render human beings puppets, condemned for failing to do what they were never able to do — raising the acute problems of God's justice and the origin of evil. The Arminian tradition within Protestantism, and the Roman Catholic tradition without, have sided with Erasmus on these grounds, and they are not foolish to do so.

The Reformed tradition does not ignore these difficulties; it develops careful answers to them — a compatibilist account of freedom, the distinction between God's sovereign decree and his authorship of sin, the role of secondary causes — which we will take up when we reach the doctrines of providence and election. But honesty compels the admission that this is *hard* doctrine, and that Luther in *On the Bondage of the Will* pressed it to a stark and uncomfortable extreme, affirming a sweeping divine determination of all things that even some later Reformed theologians would handle with more careful qualification. The bondage of the will raises the deepest and most painful questions in all of theology — about freedom, evil, justice, and the character of God — and it would be dishonest to pretend that Luther's thunderous answer dissolved them. What the debate established, beyond dispute, is where the Reformation's deepest commitment lay: not with the free will of the sinner, but with the sovereign grace of God. Whether that commitment can be reconciled with God's justice and human responsibility is a question the theology thread of this course will have to face squarely. For now, we return to the history, where the Reformation Luther had launched was about to take formal shape as a confessing church. To the Augsburg Confession we turn next.
