We have followed Martin Luther from the tormented monk in his monastery to the outlawed reformer whose movement remade the church. Now, as the Luther unit closes, we must step back and take his measure whole — for Luther was a figure of immense greatness and immense flaw, and an honest assessment can neither worship him nor dismiss him. The Reformed tradition, which owes him so much, has every reason to honor his recovery of the gospel; but it has no reason, and no wish, to canonize the man. Luther himself would have been the first to forbid it. To understand his legacy is to hold together, without flinching, both the gift he gave and the sins he committed — and to find, in that very tension, a confirmation of the gospel he proclaimed.

## The Gift

Begin with the greatness, for it was real and world-changing. Luther recovered the gospel of justification by faith — the liberating truth that a sinner is made right with God not by his own works, merits, or religious achievements, but freely, by grace, through faith in Christ, whose righteousness is received as a gift. This was the heart of everything, the discovery that opened heaven to him in the tower and that he gave to the world. From it flowed his recovery of grace alone, his insistence on the bondage of the will and the sovereignty of God in salvation, his theology of the cross, his doctrine of the priesthood of all believers, and his restoration of Scripture to the center of the church's life and to the hands of the people.

He shattered the medieval system that had bound the conscience and obscured the gospel; he stood, with extraordinary courage, against the combined might of pope and emperor, staking his life on the Word of God; he gave the German people the Bible in their own tongue and, in doing so, helped to forge their very language. The movement he launched remade Western Christianity, gave birth to Protestantism, and set loose forces that would reshape the modern world. From the standpoint of this course — a course in the Reformed theology of sovereign grace — Luther's supreme and lasting gift is precisely this: the recovery of the gospel of God's free grace to undeserving sinners. Whatever else is said of him, this stands, and for this the church has reason to give thanks to God.

## The Man

Luther was also, simply, a person of overwhelming and complex humanity — and this too belongs to his legacy. He was earthy, passionate, blunt, and uproariously funny; warm and tender with his friends and family; given to deep depressions and exuberant joys; a man of enormous vitality in everything he did. In 1525 he married Katharina von Bora, a former nun who had fled her convent, and together they made a home that became the model of the Protestant parsonage — the married clergy family, with its household of children, students, and guests, that would become a fixture of Protestant life. His "Table Talk," the recorded conversations of his household, reveals a man of earthy wit and profound faith in equal measure.

He was a pastor before he was anything else, and he poured himself out in preaching, teaching, and the care of souls. He was a hymn-writer of genius, who gave the church songs it sings to this day — above all "A Mighty Fortress Is Our God," the great battle-hymn of the Reformation. He was a translator, a teacher, a counselor, a controversialist, a man who worked himself to exhaustion in the service of the gospel he had found. To know Luther is to know a figure of vast, vivid, unmistakable humanity — and that humanity included, as all humanity does, a capacity for grievous sin.

## The Faults

For Luther was also deeply, and at times terribly, flawed, and a faithful account must name his faults without softening them. He could be coarse, vulgar, and violent in his language, and his polemics against his opponents — Catholics, radicals, fellow reformers who disagreed with him — were frequently savage, abusive, and cruel, far beyond what any disagreement could warrant. We have already seen the worst political instance of this: his ferocious tract against the peasants, calling for their merciless slaughter as the massacre was already underway, a writing that stained his name among the common people and stains it still.

But the gravest stain of all lies elsewhere, and it must be stated plainly and without any attempt at excuse. In his later years, embittered when the Jews did not convert to the gospel as he had once hoped, Luther wrote a series of viciously antisemitic tracts — most notoriously *On the Jews and Their Lies*, of 1543 — in which he urged that synagogues be burned, Jewish homes destroyed, their sacred writings seized, and the Jewish people subjected to forced labor or driven out. The content is hateful, and there is no softening it: it was a moral horror, a betrayal of the gospel of grace Luther himself proclaimed, and a grievous sin against the Jewish people. It would be seized upon and exploited, four centuries later, by the Nazis in their campaign of persecution and genocide — a fact that makes the reckoning with these writings all the more urgent and unavoidable. Modern Lutheran churches have formally and rightly repudiated these tracts, expressing repentance and sorrow for them. The right response of the Christian, and of this course, is not to explain them away or to set them in "the context of the times," as though that could dissolve their evil, but to name them as sin, to grieve them, and to condemn them without reservation. Luther's antisemitism was wicked, and we do not honor him by pretending otherwise; we honor the truth by saying so.

## Holding It Together

How, then, are we to hold together the man who recovered the gospel of grace and the man who wrote *On the Jews and Their Lies*? The answer, strikingly, is found in Luther's own theology. For Luther taught that the Christian is *simul iustus et peccator* — "at once righteous and a sinner." The believer, justified by faith, is at the same time still a sinner, in whom sin remains and wars against grace until death; his righteousness is not his own achievement but Christ's gift, credited to him, while in himself he remains flawed and capable of great evil. This doctrine, which Luther applied to every Christian, applies with peculiar force to Luther himself. He was a sinner saved by grace — genuinely saved, genuinely a sinner — and his life displays both realities in the starkest terms: the recovered gospel and the savage tracts, the man of faith and the man of hatred, together in one person.

This is precisely why Luther's own theology forbids his veneration. He would have been horrified to be made a saint, an object of reverence, a hero beyond criticism — for his whole gospel was that no one is righteous in himself, that all our righteousness is Christ's gift, that the holiest believer remains a sinner who lives only by grace. To canonize Luther would be to deny Luther's gospel. The Reformed and Protestant tradition therefore honors his recovery of the truth while refusing to whitewash his sins, and finds in the very combination of his greatness and his wickedness a sober confirmation of what he taught: that God works his purposes through flawed and sinful instruments, that the treasure is carried in cracked and unworthy vessels, and that the gospel does not depend on the goodness of the messenger.

For this is the final and liberating point: the gospel Luther recovered is not validated by Luther's virtue, nor invalidated by his vice. The truth that a sinner is justified by grace through faith stands on the Word of God, not on the character of the man who proclaimed it. If it stood on Luther's holiness, it would have fallen with his sins; but it does not. We can therefore receive the gift with gratitude while grieving the sins of the giver — taking the gold, as it were, from a flawed hand, because the gold is not the hand's but God's. A great and terrible man, through whom God did a great work: that is the honest verdict on Martin Luther.

## The Work Passes On

Luther was the volcanic prophet of the breakthrough, the man who recovered the gospel and broke the medieval system open. But he was not, by temperament or gifts, the systematizer who would build the recovered faith into a complete theological and ecclesiastical structure, nor the organizer who would carry it across Europe and give it lasting institutional form. That work would fall to others — and supremely to the second generation of reformers, and to a Frenchman in a Swiss city whose ordered, comprehensive mind would shape a whole tradition. Luther recovered the gospel; the Reformed tradition would systematize and organize it into the structure this course traces. If Luther was the prophet who tore open the heavens, others would build the cathedral beneath.

And so, even as the theology thread of this course continues to unfold the doctrine the Reformation recovered, the history thread now prepares to move beyond Luther and beyond Germany — to the *second front* of the Reformation, the Reformed wing, where Zwingli would carry the gospel to Zürich, and where a converted French humanist named John Calvin would make the city of Geneva the seedbed of an international movement. The storm that broke in Wittenberg was about to become a flood. But first, the theology: having heard how God has spoken and recovered the gospel of grace, we turn in the theology thread to ask who this God is — the God who reigns, in his being, his attributes, and his triune glory.
