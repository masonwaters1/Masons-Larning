We turn now to open the first of God's two books — the book of nature, or *general revelation* — and to read it closely. In the last lesson we established that this book is given to all people, that it reveals real truths about God, and that it leaves the whole human race without excuse. But what, exactly, does it reveal? Through what channels does it speak? And what are the limits of what it can tell us? To answer these questions is to understand both the genuine glory of general revelation and the reason it cannot, by itself, be enough. The heavens, the Scripture says, declare the glory of God; and the conscience within echoes that declaration. Let us listen to both.

## The Witness Without: The Heavens Declare

The most majestic biblical statement of general revelation comes in the nineteenth Psalm, which opens with words that have echoed through the centuries: the heavens declare the glory of God, and the sky above proclaims his handiwork.[^1] Day after day, the psalm continues, the creation pours forth speech, and night after night it reveals knowledge — and yet it is a speech without words, a voice without sound, going out to the ends of the earth. Here is general revelation in its grandeur: the entire cosmos, in its vastness, order, beauty, and intricacy, is a ceaseless, wordless proclamation of the glory and power of its Maker.

This external witness comes through the created order itself. The immensity of the heavens speaks of God's greatness; the precise order of the natural world speaks of his wisdom; the beauty woven through creation speaks of his goodness and artistry; the sheer existence of anything at all speaks of his creative power. And it comes too through God's providential governance of history and of human life — the seasons, the harvests, the provision of food and gladness — through which, as the apostle Paul told the pagans at Lystra, God has not left himself without witness. The world is not silent about its Maker. To the attentive observer, every creature, in the words of the Belgic Confession, is a letter in a beautiful book, and the whole of creation spells out the eternal power and divine nature of God.

## The Witness Within: The Sense of the Divine

But general revelation does not only shine upon us from without; it also rises up within us. Alongside the external witness of creation, the Reformed tradition has placed great emphasis on an internal witness — an innate awareness of God implanted in every human heart. Calvin gave this its famous name: the *sensus divinitatis*, the "sense of the divine," or, as he also called it, the *semen religionis*, the "seed of religion."[^2]

Calvin argued that God has sown in the mind of every human being, by natural instinct, an awareness of the divine — an ineradicable sense that God exists and that we are accountable to him. This is not a conclusion reached by argument but a basic awareness given with our very humanity, prior to all reasoning. The evidence for it, Calvin held, lies in the universality of religion: in every age and every culture, however remote, human beings have worshiped, have sensed the sacred, have reached toward the transcendent. Even idolatry and superstition, in Calvin's analysis, are perverse testimonies to the *sensus divinitatis* — distorted expressions of an awareness of God that fallen humanity cannot fully suppress, evidence that the seed of religion is sown in all, even where it grows up twisted. The atheist who insists there is no God is, on this view, laboring to silence a witness that will not finally be silenced, suppressing a knowledge written into his own constitution.

## The Witness of Conscience

Closely related to the *sensus divinitatis* is the testimony of *conscience* — the moral awareness, present in every person, of right and wrong, of duty and guilt. The apostle Paul gives this its classic description in the second chapter of Romans, where he writes that even the Gentiles, who do not have the written law of Moses, show that the work of the law is written on their hearts, their conscience bearing witness, their conflicting thoughts accusing or even excusing them.[^3]

This is a remarkable claim. Every human being, simply by being human, carries within an awareness of a moral law — a sense that some things are right and others wrong, an inner voice that accuses when we do evil and approves when we do good. This moral sense, the Reformed tradition holds, is itself a form of general revelation: through the conscience, God reveals something of his own moral character and holy demands. The very fact that human beings everywhere feel the pull of *ought*, that they experience guilt and the awareness of having failed a standard they did not invent, points beyond themselves to a Lawgiver whose law is inscribed on the human heart. Conscience, like the heavens, is a witness — not to God's power, as the cosmos is, but to his holiness and his moral claim upon us. Through the world without and the conscience within, the testimony of general revelation is constant, universal, and inescapable.

## What General Revelation Accomplishes

Having heard both witnesses, we can now state precisely what general revelation accomplishes — and the answer is both more and less than we might expect. It accomplishes something genuinely significant: it reveals the existence of God, his eternal power, his divine nature, his glory and wisdom as Creator, and the broad outlines of his moral law. This is real knowledge of God, truly given and truly available to all. General revelation is not nothing; it is a true, if limited, disclosure of the living God to every person who has ever lived.

And it accomplishes a specific, weighty purpose, stated with great force by the apostle Paul in the first chapter of Romans: it renders all humanity *without excuse*. Paul writes that what can be known about God is plain to all, because God has shown it to them — that ever since the creation of the world, God's invisible attributes, his eternal power and divine nature, have been clearly perceived in the things that have been made, so that people are *without excuse*.[^4] This is the great function of general revelation in the Reformed scheme. It establishes the universal accountability of the human race. No one will be able to stand before God and plead total ignorance, for God has made himself known to all through the works of his hands and the witness of conscience. Every human being possesses enough light to be responsible, and therefore enough light to be justly held to account for what they have done with it.

## The Tragic Twist: Suppression and Idolatry

But here the doctrine takes a tragic and crucial turn, and we cannot understand general revelation without it. For Paul does not say merely that God is revealed to all; he says that all, in their fallenness, *do something terrible with that revelation*. The same passage in Romans declares that the wrath of God is revealed against all who, by their unrighteousness, *suppress the truth* — who, although they knew God, did not honor him as God or give thanks, but became futile in their thinking, and exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images, worshiping and serving the creature rather than the Creator.[^4]

This is the dark heart of the matter. General revelation comes to fallen human beings who do not receive it gladly but *suppress* it — who push the unwelcome knowledge of God down, hold it under, and turn away from it. And the result is not honest atheism but *idolatry*: having suppressed the true God revealed in creation, the human heart does not become irreligious but rather manufactures false gods, exchanging the glory of the Creator for substitutes of its own making. The fallen reading of the book of nature does not yield true worship; it yields the worship of idols. This is why, as we saw in the last lesson, general revelation cannot save: not only because it lacks the content of the gospel, but because its readers are corrupt and turn its true light into the darkness of idolatry. The witness is clear; the witnesses are guilty; and the guilt is precisely that they have suppressed and distorted a truth they genuinely possessed.

## What General Revelation Cannot Do

We are now in a position to state the limits of general revelation with full clarity, for its limits are as important as its reach. General revelation cannot reveal the way of salvation. It discloses the Creator, his power, and his law; it does not disclose the Redeemer, his grace, and his gospel. It can tell us that God exists and that we have offended him; it cannot tell us how a sinner may be forgiven and reconciled. It contains no word of Christ, no word of the cross, no word of the covenant of grace, no word of justification by faith. It can condemn, but it cannot console; it can establish guilt, but it cannot announce pardon.

And, as we have just seen, even the limited knowledge it offers is suppressed and distorted by the fallen heart, so that in practice general revelation, left to itself, leads not to the true God but to idols. Its function, therefore, is real but largely negative: it establishes that there is a God, that he is glorious and powerful, that there is a moral law and that we have broken it, and that we stand accountable and without excuse — and then it falls silent, unable to offer the remedy it has shown us we need. It is a faithful witness to the disease that has no power to give the cure. This twofold limitation — the absence of saving content, and the suppression of even the content it has — is precisely why the second book, special revelation, is necessary; and that necessity is the subject of our next lesson.

## The Debate Over Natural Theology

Honesty requires acknowledging that the precise scope of general revelation has been a matter of vigorous debate, even within the broadly Reformed world, and the student should know where the fault lines lie. The central question is this: granting that general revelation is real, how much can fallen human reason actually derive from it? Can it build a reliable "natural theology" — a body of demonstrated truths about God, including proofs of his existence — prior to and apart from Scripture?

The more optimistic answer, associated with the medieval scholastic tradition and Thomas Aquinas, holds that human reason, even after the fall, retains substantial power to reason from creation up to God, establishing his existence and many of his attributes by argument. A more cautious answer, characteristic of much of the Reformed tradition, stresses the *noetic effects of sin* — the corruption of the mind itself — and holds that while general revelation is objectively clear, the subjective capacity of the fallen sinner to read it rightly is gravely impaired, so that natural theology built by sinful reason tends to go astray into idolatry rather than arriving at the true God. A still more radical position, taken by Karl Barth, denies natural theology altogether. Within confessional Reformed thought, the debate continues between those who allow a chastened, Scripture-corrected use of natural theology and those, like the influential apologist Cornelius Van Til, who insist that no neutral, autonomous reasoning from nature to God is legitimate at all. The honest student need not settle this debate today, but should know that the bare fact of general revelation is affirmed by all sides; what is disputed is how far fallen reason can travel on its light alone. What none of them dispute is the conclusion to which the whole doctrine drives: that the first book, glorious as it is, is not enough — and that we are shut up to the need for the second.
