We have seen the medieval church at its height: a magnificent, all-embracing sacramental order, crowned by a papacy that could humble emperors, commanding the loyalty of all Christendom. But the height did not last. In the very centuries after Thomas Aquinas and Innocent III, that towering edifice was shaken by a series of institutional crises that cracked its authority from within — long before any reformer raised a theological objection. The papacy was carried off into a long captivity; then it split into two, and even three, rival claimants, each cursing the others; and the church was driven to a desperate remedy that raised a dangerous question about where final authority truly lay. By the time Luther was born, the medieval certainty about the church's authority had already been badly shaken. To understand the Reformation, we must understand these cracks, for they had weakened the foundations that Luther would later strike.

## The Avignon Papacy

The first great crisis was the long exile of the popes from Rome. In 1309, for a tangle of political reasons bound up with the rising power of the French monarchy, the papal court relocated from Rome to the city of Avignon, on the borders of France, where it would remain for nearly seventy years. To contemporaries, this was a scandal and a wound. The pope was the bishop of *Rome*, the successor of Saint Peter, whose authority was rooted in the apostolic see of the eternal city; to find him instead settled comfortably in Avignon, under the long shadow of the French crown, seemed a betrayal of his very office.

The poet Petrarch gave the episode its enduring and damning name: the "Babylonian Captivity" of the church, evoking the exile of ancient Israel in Babylon. The phrase stuck because it captured a widespread perception — that the papacy had become a captive, worldly and compromised, its independence subordinated to French interests, its court a byword for luxury, ambition, and the relentless pursuit of money. Whether every accusation was fair, the *perception* was corrosive: the supreme spiritual authority of Christendom appeared to many to be neither supremely spiritual nor authoritative, but a political instrument grown rich and worldly far from the tomb of the apostles. The prestige of the papal office, which depended so heavily on its sacred aura, was gravely damaged. And worse was to come.

## The Great Schism

In 1377 the papacy at last returned to Rome — and almost immediately the crisis deepened into catastrophe. The next papal election, held amid mob pressure in Rome in 1378, produced a pope whose legitimacy was swiftly challenged; a faction of cardinals declared the election invalid and chose a rival, who set up his court back in Avignon. Now there were *two* popes — one in Rome, one in Avignon — each insisting he was the true vicar of Christ, each excommunicating the other and all his followers, each commanding the allegiance of different kingdoms according to the tangled politics of Europe. This was the Great Western Schism, and it would tear at the church for nearly forty years.[^1]

The damage this did to the church's authority is difficult to overstate. The pope was supposed to be the visible center of the church's unity, the guarantor of truth, the one to whom all Christians owed obedience as to Christ's own representative on earth. What, then, could it mean that there were *two* of him — and that the holiest and most learned authorities of the age disagreed, in good conscience, about which was real? Devout souls were left in genuine anguish: by following one pope, were they following an impostor and imperiling their salvation? Even saints lined up on opposing sides. The spectacle of rival vicars of Christ hurling anathemas at each other made a mockery of the papal claim and forced upon all of Christendom a question that had long been dormant: *where, finally, does authority in the church reside?* If the pope himself could be doubtful, multiple, and mutually condemned, then the foundation of the whole system was suddenly, frighteningly, in question.

## The Conciliar Remedy

The schism became so intolerable that the church was driven to a radical remedy — one whose logic would prove more dangerous than the disease. To resolve the impasse, churchmen turned to the idea of a *general council*: a great assembly representing the whole church, which might stand in judgment over the rival claimants and restore unity. After one failed attempt, the Council of Constance, meeting from 1414 to 1418, at last cut the knot. It deposed or accepted the resignation of the rival popes, swept the board clean, and elected a single, universally recognized pope, Martin V. The schism was healed; the church was one again. Constance had worked.

But Constance had healed the schism by acting on a momentous principle. To depose popes, the council had to claim an authority *higher than the pope* — and it did so explicitly, decreeing that a general council, representing the universal church, holds its power directly from Christ, and that everyone, even the pope, is bound to obey it in matters of faith and the reform of the church. This was the theory known as *conciliarism*: the claim that supreme authority in the church lies not in the pope alone but in the whole church gathered in council, which may judge, correct, and even depose an erring pope. It was, in effect, a constitutional check upon the papal monarchy — the assertion that the pope is not an absolute sovereign above all judgment, but a servant of the church who can himself be called to account.

## The Crisis of Authority

Conciliarism was, in the end, defeated. Over the following decades the papacy patiently reasserted its supremacy, condemned the conciliar theory, and restored the doctrine of papal monarchy; the great age of the councils passed, and Rome emerged with its claims to absolute authority formally intact. In that narrow sense, the system righted itself, and one might tell the story as a triumph of the church's resilience: confronted with captivity and schism, it found within itself the resources to recover.

But something had been let loose that could not be wholly recaptured. The crises of Avignon and the schism, and the conciliar remedy they provoked, had together shattered the unquestioned medieval confidence about where authority in the church finally lay. The papacy had been shown to be capable of worldliness, of captivity, even of multiplication and self-contradiction. The idea had been openly canvassed — by the church's own assembled leaders — that the pope might be fallible, judgeable, subordinate to the wider church. The burning question *who speaks for God? where is final authority?* had been forced into the open and never decisively closed. And this is precisely the question the Reformation would answer, a century later, with a wholly different solution: not the pope, and not even a council, but Scripture alone. When Luther would one day stand before pope and emperor and declare that councils and popes had erred and that his conscience was captive to the Word of God, he would be speaking into a space that these earlier crises had already pried open. The authority of the medieval church had been cracked from within long before it was challenged from without.

## The Cry for Reform

The institutional crises of the papacy were the most dramatic of the cracks, but they were not the only ones, and beneath them ran a broader and deeper current of discontent that would feed directly into the sixteenth century. Across the later Middle Ages there arose, again and again, a cry for the "reformation of the church in head and members" — a demand that the church be purified and renewed from the pope at its head down through the bishops, clergy, and monasteries that made up its body. This was not yet the Protestant Reformation; the great majority who raised the cry sought reform *within* the existing church, not a break from it. But the very persistence of the demand testifies to a widespread sense that the church had grown corrupt and worldly and stood in urgent need of correction.

The grievances were many and concrete. The wealth of the church was vast, and its higher clergy often lived in a luxury that mocked the poverty of Christ; bishoprics and abbacies were treated as sources of income, sometimes held by men who never set foot in their dioceses. The buying and selling of church offices — the sin of simony — was rampant. Many parish priests were poorly educated, some scarcely able to perform their duties; clerical immorality, including the open keeping of concubines despite the rule of celibacy, was a frequent scandal. And the machinery of papal finance reached its long arm into every corner of Christendom, extracting fees, taxes, and payments that bred deep resentment, especially in lands like Germany that felt themselves milked for the benefit of a distant and worldly Rome.

Out of this grew a rising tide of *anticlericalism* — a popular hostility, not necessarily toward the faith itself, but toward a clergy seen as greedy, idle, immoral, and oppressive. Sensitive and devout souls were troubled by the gap between the church's holy claims and its all-too-human reality. The demand for reform echoed through sermons, satires, and the writings of earnest churchmen for generations before Luther. The point is crucial: the longing for a reformed church was not invented in 1517. It had been building for more than a century, frustrated again and again, gathering force. The tinder was dry and piled high. What remained was for a spark to fall — and for the question of *authority*, pried open by Avignon and the schism, to be answered in a way no previous reformer had dared.

## A Foreshadowing at Constance

There is one more thing to mark about the Council of Constance, for it points forward ominously to a theme we will take up in a later lesson. The same council that healed the schism and asserted the authority of the church also turned its attention to those it deemed heretics — and in 1415 it tried, condemned, and burned at the stake a Bohemian priest and reformer named Jan Hus, who had dared to challenge aspects of the church's teaching and the worldliness of its clergy, drawing on the earlier criticisms of the Englishman John Wycliffe. Hus had come to Constance under a promise of safe conduct; it was not honored. His burning would not be forgotten. A century later, Luther would recognize in Hus a forerunner, and the Bohemian's memory would haunt the church that had killed him.

We will return to Wycliffe and Hus, the "morning stars" of the Reformation, in lessons of their own. For now, the lesson is this: the late medieval church was not the serene and unassailable monolith of its own self-image. It was an institution that had passed through profound crisis — its papacy captive, then divided, its authority openly questioned, its capacity to silence reformers exercised in the flames at Constance. The grand edifice still stood, and would stand for another century; but its foundations had been shaken, and the question of authority lay open and unresolved. Into this weakened and questioning world, other forces were now flowing — new currents of theology that troubled the conscience, and a new movement of learning that would return to the sources of the faith. To those forces we turn next, beginning with the anxious religion of the late medieval soul.
