The history thread has shown us Luther recovering the gospel of grace; now the theology thread must lay bare why grace is needed at all. And to understand the human need for grace, we must begin not with sin but before it — with humanity as God created it, in dignity and in covenant. For the doctrine of grace presupposes a story: a creature made good, set in a relationship of obligation to its Maker, who fell from that estate into ruin. This unit, "Grace Alone," therefore opens with the original human condition — the image of God in which we were made, and the covenant of works by which we were tested. Only against this backdrop of created glory and covenantal trust can the catastrophe of the fall, and the wonder of grace, be rightly seen.

## Made in the Image of God

The foundational truth about humanity is stated at the very beginning of Scripture: God made man in his own image, in the image of God he created him, male and female he created them.[^1] No other creature is described this way. The human being alone, among all that God made, bears the *imago Dei* — the image of God — and in this lies humanity's unique dignity, status, and calling. To be made in God's image is to be set apart from the rest of creation as God's special representative, made to reflect him, to know him, to commune with him, and to exercise dominion over the world as his stewards.

What exactly does the image of God consist of? The Reformed tradition has distinguished two senses, and both matter. There is a *broad* or structural sense — the rational, moral, relational, and volitional capacities that constitute our humanity: the mind that knows, the will that chooses, the heart that loves, the capacity for relationship and responsibility. This structural image makes us the kind of creature that can know and answer to God, and it is retained, though marred and defaced, even after the fall — which is why murder remains an assault on God's image even in fallen humanity. And there is a *narrow* or moral sense — the original righteousness, true knowledge, and holiness in which Adam was created, his moral conformity to God and his right relationship with him. This moral image was *lost* in the fall and is *restored* in Christ, in whom believers are renewed in true knowledge, righteousness, and holiness. Humanity was created good, upright, and in fellowship with God — bearing his image in both its structural endowments and its moral perfection.

## The Calling of the Image

The image of God is not only a status but a *calling*. Having made humanity in his image, God immediately gave a commission: to be fruitful and multiply, to fill the earth and subdue it, and to have dominion over the creatures and the creation.[^cm] This commission — often called the cultural mandate or the creation mandate — flows directly from the image. Because human beings are made to represent and reflect God, they are appointed his stewards and viceroys over the world, charged to cultivate it, order it, develop its potential, and rule it under God and for his glory. The image-bearer is a king under the King, a gardener in God's garden, set over the works of God's hands.

This calling gives human life its dignity and its purpose. Work, culture, family, the ordering of society, the development of the world's resources, the pursuit of knowledge and beauty — all of these are, at root, the exercise of the image-bearer's God-given vocation to fill and steward the earth. They are not consequences of the fall or distractions from spiritual life, but the original and good calling of humanity as created. The fall would corrupt and frustrate this calling without abolishing it; redemption would restore it; and in the end the redeemed will exercise it perfectly in the new creation. To be made in God's image, then, is to be made for a task: to reflect God's character and to extend his rule over the world he has made. This too is part of the glory from which humanity fell, and to which grace restores us.

## The Covenant of Works

But God did not relate to the first human being merely as Creator to creature; he entered into *covenant* with him. The Reformed tradition teaches that in the garden, before the fall, God established with Adam what is called the *covenant of works* — also named the covenant of life or the covenant of creation. The Westminster Confession states it concisely: the first covenant made with man was a covenant of works, in which life was promised to Adam, and in him to his posterity, upon the condition of perfect and personal obedience.[^2]

The terms of this covenant were these. God, who owed Adam nothing, graciously condescended to bind himself in a covenant relationship, promising *life* — continued life, confirmed and unbreakable blessedness in fellowship with himself — on the condition of perfect, personal, and complete obedience. The test of this obedience was concentrated in a single command: Adam might eat freely of every tree of the garden, but not of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, for "in the day that you eat of it you shall surely die." Here was the probation: would Adam trust and obey his Maker, or would he reach for the forbidden? Life was the promise; death was the threat; obedience was the condition; and a single tree was the test. The whole destiny of humanity hung upon it.

## Adam as Federal Head

The most consequential feature of the covenant of works is that Adam acted in it not merely as a private individual but as the *representative* of the entire human race — its *federal head*, from the Latin *foedus*, "covenant." Adam stood for all his posterity; he represented us, as a head represents a body, as a king represents his people, as a delegate represents those who send him. And therefore his obedience or disobedience would determine not only his own destiny but the destiny of all whom he represented — that is, the whole human race that would descend from him.

This principle of *federal headship* is foundational to everything that follows, and it cuts in two directions. On the one hand, it explains the catastrophe of original sin: when Adam, our representative, broke the covenant, he broke it not only for himself but for all of us whom he represented, so that his fall is our fall, his guilt our guilt — a truth we will examine in the lesson on original sin. On the other hand, it establishes the pattern of redemption: for just as the first Adam represented his people in the covenant of works and failed, so the *second Adam*, Jesus Christ, would represent his people in a covenant of grace and succeed — rendering the perfect obedience the first Adam failed to give, and bearing the death the broken covenant required. The apostle Paul builds his whole understanding of sin and salvation on this parallel of the two Adams: as in Adam all die, so in Christ shall all be made alive; by one man's disobedience the many were made sinners, by one man's obedience the many will be made righteous. The covenant of works, with Adam as its federal head, is thus the indispensable foundation for understanding both how we fell in Adam and how we are saved in Christ. Lose the representative principle, and the logic of both ruin and redemption unravels.

## Why This Foundation Matters

We can now see why this lesson stands at the head of the unit on grace. The doctrine of the image of God establishes human *dignity* and *accountability*: we are not accidents or animals merely, but image-bearers made for God, answerable to him, capable of knowing and obeying or rejecting him. The doctrine of the covenant of works establishes the *framework* within which the fall and its consequences make sense: humanity was placed under a covenant with a condition, a promise, and a threat, represented by a federal head whose action would bind the race. And the parallel of the two Adams establishes the *shape of redemption*: grace will come through a second representative who succeeds where the first failed. The whole drama of sin and salvation presupposes this created order — the image-bearing creature, the covenant of obedience, the representative head. Grace does not float free; it is God's answer to the breaking of *this* covenant by *this* representative, restoring *this* image. To understand grace, we had first to understand the glory from which humanity fell.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Several honest qualifications belong here, for some of these doctrines are debated even within the Reformed family and beyond. First, the *covenant of works* itself is a matter of intramural discussion. Critics note that Genesis does not use the word "covenant" in the garden narrative, that the term "works" can sound legalistic, and that the doctrine received its developed form only in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries rather than in the early church. Some Reformed theologians have preferred other names — "covenant of life," "covenant of creation" — or have reframed the concept to stress that even this arrangement was gracious in its origin. Defenders respond that all the *elements* of a covenant are plainly present in the text — a sovereign-imposed relationship, a stipulated condition of obedience, a promised reward, a threatened penalty, a representative head — even if the word is absent, and that Paul's parallel of the two Adams in Romans requires precisely this representative structure. The label may be debated; the underlying reality, the tradition holds, is biblical.

Second, the precise *content of the image of God* — whether it is chiefly structural, functional, relational, or moral — has been variously understood, and the tradition holds these emphases together rather than choosing rigidly among them. Third, and most pressingly for the modern reader, these doctrines raise the question of the *historical Adam*. The representative structure of the covenant of works, and the doctrine of original sin that flows from it, have traditionally assumed a real, historical Adam as the first human being and the federal head of the race — and the Adam-Christ parallel seems to require it. The challenge of evolutionary science, with its account of human origins from a population rather than a single couple, has made this one of the genuinely contested frontiers of contemporary theology. Many in the Reformed tradition maintain that the doctrine requires a historical Adam and that the gospel's logic depends on it; others have sought various ways to reconcile the theological claims with the scientific account. This course holds the traditional doctrine while acknowledging, honestly, that the relationship between the biblical Adam and the findings of modern science is a real and unresolved question on which thoughtful believers disagree. Finally, it should be noted that other Christian traditions frame the original human condition differently — Catholic theology with its distinction of nature and superadded grace, Eastern Orthodoxy with its emphasis on the image-and-likeness and the calling to participate in the divine life. With the glory of the created estate before us, we are ready to turn to its tragic loss. To the fall we turn next.
