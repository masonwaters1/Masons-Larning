Humanity was made in the image of God, placed in a garden, and bound in a covenant whose single condition was obedience and whose promise was life. And then humanity fell. The third chapter of Genesis tells the story that explains the world as we know it — a world of death, suffering, alienation, and evil — and that creates the need for everything this course is about. The fall is the hinge on which the whole drama turns: the moment when a good creation was ruined, when sin and death entered the world, and when the human race, made for fellowship with God, was cast into guilt and exile. To understand grace, we must understand the catastrophe that grace exists to repair.

## The Temptation

The account opens with the serpent, who comes to tempt the woman, and his strategy repays close attention, for it is the pattern of all temptation since. He begins by questioning God's word: "Did God actually say...?" — sowing doubt about what God had commanded and whether it could be trusted. When the woman affirms the command and its penalty, the serpent moves to flat denial: "You will not surely die" — contradicting God's plain warning, calling God a liar. And then he impugns God's very goodness and motive: God forbade the fruit, the serpent insinuates, not for your good but to keep you down, for "God knows that when you eat of it your eyes will be opened, and you will be like God, knowing good and evil."[^1] The temptation, fully unfolded, is an invitation to distrust God's word, deny his warning, suspect his goodness, and reach for a forbidden autonomy — to be "like God," determining good and evil for oneself rather than receiving them from the Creator.

The woman saw that the tree was good for food, a delight to the eyes, and desirable to make one wise; and she took and ate, and gave to her husband, who was with her, and he ate. In that act the covenant was broken and the world was changed.

## The Essence of the First Sin

What was the essence of this first sin? On the surface it was the eating of forbidden fruit — but beneath the act lay something deeper, and the Reformed tradition has identified it with care. At root, the first sin was *unbelief* and *pride*. It was unbelief in that it doubted God's word and God's goodness — refusing to trust that God had spoken truly and meant well, believing the serpent's lie over the Creator's command. And it was pride in that it grasped at being "like God," at autonomy, at the right to determine good and evil for oneself, at independence from the Maker. The outward act of disobedience flowed from this inward rebellion: the creature refused to remain a creature, trusting and obeying its God, and reached instead to put itself in God's place. It was, at bottom, the rejection of God's authority and the assertion of self-sovereignty — the will to be one's own god.

This is why the fall is not merely a story about ancient people and a piece of fruit, but a revelation of the nature of all sin. Every sin since has the same shape: distrust of God's word and goodness, and the proud assertion of the self against God's rule. The first sin is the archetype of all sin — unbelief flowering into rebellion, the creature seizing the prerogative of the Creator.

## The Consequences

The consequences of the fall were immediate and catastrophic, and they fell upon every dimension of human existence. First came *guilt and shame*: their eyes were opened, and they knew they were naked, and they sewed fig leaves and hid — the first experience of shame, the consciousness of having done wrong. Then came *broken fellowship with God*: when they heard the Lord walking in the garden, they hid themselves in fear among the trees, and the open communion they had enjoyed with their Maker gave way to terror, concealment, and estrangement. The relationship for which they were made was shattered.

Then came *death*, exactly as the covenant had warned. There was spiritual death — immediate separation from God, the source of life — and there was physical death, the sentence of mortality: "you are dust, and to dust you shall return." The threatened penalty of the broken covenant now fell upon the race. And the curse spread outward into all of creation and human life: the ground was cursed, so that it would yield thorns and thistles and require painful toil; childbearing would be filled with pain; the harmony between man and woman was disordered; and the whole created order was subjected to frustration and decay. Finally came *expulsion*: the man and woman were driven out of the garden, away from the tree of life and the immediate presence of God, into a world now under the shadow of the curse. The world we inhabit — with its death and suffering, its labor and pain, its evil and alienation — is this fallen world, the world east of Eden.

## The Cosmic Scope

We must grasp that the fall was not merely Adam's personal lapse, a private failure with private consequences. Because Adam was the federal head of the race, his fall was the fall of all humanity; because he was given dominion over creation, his fall dragged creation down with him. Sin and death entered the world through this one act and spread to the whole human race, so that, as the apostle Paul writes, through one man sin entered the world, and death through sin, and so death spread to all. The fall is the catastrophe that ruined not one man but a race, and not a race only but a world. It explains why the creation that God pronounced "very good" is now so full of evil and death: not because God made it so, but because it fell. The human condition — our mortality, our proneness to evil, our alienation from God and from one another — is the condition of a fallen race in a fallen world.

## The First Gleam of Grace

And yet — and this is the wonder that turns the whole story toward hope — even in the midst of the curse, grace appears. In pronouncing judgment on the serpent, God uttered a promise: that he would put enmity between the serpent and the woman, between his seed and her seed, and that the seed of the woman would crush the serpent's head, though the serpent would bruise his heel.[^2] The Christian tradition has called this the *protoevangelium* — the "first gospel" — for here, in the very hour of humanity's ruin, God announced the coming of a Redeemer: one born of the woman who would crush the serpent and undo the work of the fall, at the cost of his own wounding. Before the man and woman were even expelled from the garden, the promise of grace was already given. The God against whom they had rebelled did not abandon them to the ruin they had chosen, but spoke, in the midst of judgment, a word of hope. The whole story of redemption that this course traces — the long history culminating in Christ, the seed of the woman — begins here, in a promise spoken over the wreckage of the fall.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Several honest difficulties attend the doctrine of the fall, and we must name them. The first, as in the previous lesson, is the question of *historicity*. The Reformed tradition has generally read Genesis 3 as real history — a real first couple, a real act of disobedience, a real entry of sin and death into a good creation — and it has strong reasons for doing so, for the doctrine of redemption seems to require a real fall: if there was no historical fall into sin, what is it that Christ came to redeem us from? Yet many modern readers, including many Christians, read the narrative as theological truth conveyed in symbolic or mythic form — a profound account of the human condition rather than a literal report of events — and the tension with evolutionary science (which finds death in the world long before humanity and no single first couple) presses hard here. This course holds to a real fall, on which the logic of redemption depends, while acknowledging that the relationship between this narrative and modern science is a genuine and contested question among believers.

The second difficulty is the *problem of evil* in its sharpest form. Why did God permit the fall? Why create a being capable of falling, and place before him a tree he could be tempted to eat? Did God merely permit the fall, or did he, in his sovereignty, ordain it? These questions press upon the doctrine of God's sovereignty and goodness, and we will face them more fully in the unit on the eternal decree; here we can only mark that the fall raises the problem of evil acutely and that the Reformed tradition does not pretend to dissolve it easily. A third difficulty concerns *justice*: is it fair that Adam's sin should affect us all — that we should suffer and die and be born sinners because of one man's act long ago? This is the question of original sin, to which we turn in the next lesson, and it is among the most objected-to doctrines in all of theology. Finally, a clarification: although it was the woman who was first deceived, Scripture and the Reformed tradition lay the decisive responsibility for the fall upon *Adam*, the federal head — it is "in Adam" that all fell, "by one man" that sin entered. The doctrine does not rest on blaming Eve, as a long and unfair tradition of interpretation has sometimes implied, but on the representative role of Adam. With the fall before us, and its consequences spreading to the whole race, we must now ask precisely how Adam's sin became *our* sin. To the doctrine of original sin we turn next.
