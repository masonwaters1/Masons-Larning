We have seen that Adam fell as the federal head of the race, so that his fall was the fall of all humanity. But we must now ask precisely *how* Adam's sin becomes our sin — how the disobedience of one man in a garden long ago bears upon every human being born since. This is the doctrine of *original sin*, and it is at once one of the most important, most realistic, and most objected-to doctrines in all of Christian theology. It teaches that every human being, descended from Adam by ordinary generation, is born already in a state of sin — inheriting both the *guilt* and the *corruption* of Adam's fall, before ever committing a single actual sin. We do not become sinners merely by sinning; we sin because we are, from birth, sinners.

## Born in Sin

The doctrine of original sin concerns the sinful condition into which we are born and with which we come into the world — prior to, and as the source of, the actual sins we go on to commit. Scripture testifies to this inherited sinfulness with striking directness. David confesses that he was brought forth in iniquity, and that in sin his mother conceived him — not claiming his conception was sinful, but that from its very beginning his existence was marked by sin.[^1] The apostle Paul declares that we were "by nature children of wrath" — sinful and under judgment by our very nature, not merely by our choices. And he sets forth the whole doctrine in his letter to the Romans: through one man's disobedience the many were made sinners; sin and death entered through one man and spread to all. We are not born morally neutral, to become sinners only when we first choose wrongly; we are born already sinful, and our actual sins flow from a nature corrupted at the root.

The doctrine, then, looks beneath the surface of our individual misdeeds to the condition that produces them. Why do all human beings sin, universally, without exception? Because all are born with a sinful nature, inherited from Adam. Original sin is the root; our actual, personal sins are the fruit that inevitably grows from it.

## Two Aspects: Guilt and Corruption

The Reformed tradition distinguishes two aspects of original sin, and both must be held together. The first is *imputed guilt* — sometimes called original guilt. Because Adam was the federal head and representative of the race, his first sin is *imputed*, or credited, to all his posterity. When Adam sinned, we sinned in him, as our representative; and therefore all are born already *guilty* before God, liable to condemnation, on account of Adam's representative transgression. Paul makes the point explicitly: by one man's disobedience the many were "made sinners" — constituted guilty — just as by Christ's obedience the many are "made righteous." This is the legal or forensic aspect of original sin: a guilt reckoned to us because our head and representative sinned.

The second aspect is *inherited corruption* — sometimes called original pollution. We inherit from Adam not only his guilt but a *corrupted nature* — a heart and disposition polluted by sin from the moment of conception, inclined toward evil, lacking the original righteousness in which Adam was made. This is the moral or natural aspect of original sin: not merely a legal verdict against us, but an actual corruption within us, a sinful bent woven into our nature from the start. From this polluted nature flow all our actual sins, as a poisoned spring yields poisoned water. So the doctrine has two sides: we are born *guilty* (condemned in Adam, the forensic aspect) and *corrupt* (polluted in nature, the moral aspect) — and the corruption is the inward fountain from which our personal sins pour forth.

## The Universality of Original Sin

This condition is universal. It belongs to every human being descended from Adam by ordinary generation, without exception — "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God"; "none is righteous, no, not one." There is no human being who escapes it, no one born innocent, no pristine exception. We are, as Paul says, all by nature children of wrath. The one exception that proves the rule is the Lord Jesus Christ, who was *not* descended from Adam by ordinary generation but was conceived by the Holy Spirit, and who therefore did not inherit original sin — the sinless one, born of the woman yet untouched by the corruption of the race, precisely so that he might be the spotless Savior of sinners. Apart from him, the whole race lies under the inheritance of Adam's fall.

This doctrine received its classic formulation in the early church through Augustine, in his great controversy with the monk Pelagius. Pelagius denied original sin: he held that human beings are born innocent, with a free will fully able to choose good or evil, and that we become sinners only by imitation and choice, not by inheritance — and therefore that grace, while helpful, is not strictly necessary for salvation. Augustine answered, on the basis of Scripture, that all sinned in Adam, that we inherit both his guilt and a corrupted nature, that we are unable to save ourselves, and that grace is therefore absolutely necessary. The church sided with Augustine and condemned Pelagianism as heresy, and the Reformed tradition stands squarely in this Augustinian line: the human predicament is so deep that nothing but grace can remedy it.

## Federal and Realistic

How exactly is Adam's sin *ours*? The tradition has offered two complementary explanations, and it is worth distinguishing them. The *federal* or representative view — the one the Reformed tradition has chiefly emphasized — holds that Adam's sin is imputed to us because he was our appointed *representative*, our covenant head, so that what he did as our delegate counts as ours, much as the act of an ambassador binds the nation he represents or the act of a king commits his people. The connection is one of *representation*: God appointed Adam to act for the race, and so his transgression is justly reckoned to all whom he represented.

The older *realistic* or seminal view, associated with Augustine and rooted in a particular reading of Romans, holds that we were somehow *really present* in Adam — that the whole human race existed seminally in him, as a tree in its seed, so that when Adam sinned, human nature itself sinned, and we sinned in him not merely by representation but by real participation. On this view the connection is one of *real union*: we were truly there, in the loins of our first father, and his act was the act of the nature we all share. The Reformed tradition has generally held the federal view as primary, sometimes combining it with elements of the realistic, but it stresses representation as the surer ground — partly because it preserves the clean parallel with Christ, who saves his people by representing them, not by their being seminally present in him. Whether by representation or real union or both, the point stands: Adam's sin is not merely his own but ours, and we enter the world already implicated in it.

## Why It Matters

The doctrine of original sin is not a gloomy curiosity but the very foundation of the gospel of grace, and everything in this unit depends upon it. For it establishes the *depth* and *universality* of the human predicament. We are not basically good people who occasionally stumble and need a little help; we are fallen creatures, guilty and corrupt from birth, radically and universally in need of grace. The disease is not superficial but congenital, not occasional but pervasive, not curable by our own effort but requiring the intervention of God.

This is why the doctrine is the indispensable ground of the gospel. If human beings were born innocent and able to save themselves, the gospel of grace would be unnecessary — a mere assist to people who could manage well enough on their own. But if we are born guilty and corrupt, unable to remedy our own condition, then grace is not a luxury but a necessity, and salvation must come wholly from outside us, from God. Deny original sin, and you deny the need for the gospel; affirm it, and the gospel of grace shines forth as the only hope of a ruined race. Original sin also prepares directly for the doctrines that follow — total depravity, which describes the *extent* of this inherited corruption, and the bondage of the will, which describes its effect on our power to turn to God.

## The Honest Counterpoint

No doctrine has drawn more objection than this one, and the objections deserve a serious hearing. The central and most forceful is the objection from *justice*: how can it be fair that we are held guilty for Adam's sin, which we did not personally commit? To be condemned for another man's act, committed before we existed, strikes many as a manifest injustice. The Reformed tradition offers several responses, though it does not claim to remove all difficulty. It points to the reality of *representation*, which we accept in many spheres of life — a nation is bound by the acts of its rulers, children bear the consequences of their parents' choices; federal headship is not an alien principle. It observes that we *ratify* Adam's sin by our own — we show ourselves truly Adam's children by sinning as he did, so that none can claim he would have done otherwise. Most powerfully, it presses the *parallel with Christ*: the very same principle of representation by which we are condemned in Adam is the principle by which we are saved in Christ. If we reject imputed guilt in Adam as unjust, we must equally reject imputed righteousness in Christ — and then there is no salvation. The two stand or fall together; to object to being lost in our first representative is to object to being saved in our second. Still, the difficulty is real, and the tradition holds the doctrine in humility, not pretending it is free of mystery.

Other traditions frame the matter differently, and honesty requires noting the spectrum. Eastern Orthodoxy, while affirming that we inherit mortality and a corrupted nature from Adam ("ancestral sin"), generally *rejects* the inheritance of Adam's *guilt* — holding that we are not born condemned for Adam's personal sin. Roman Catholicism affirms original sin but understands it with its own nuances, including the role of baptism in removing it. The inheritance of Adam's *guilt*, in particular, is a distinctively Augustinian and Western emphasis, carried into the Reformed tradition, and not shared by all Christians. And the broader modern objection holds that the whole doctrine is too dark, conflicting with confidence in human goodness, autonomy, and progress. Yet here the doctrine displays surprising strength: original sin has been called the most empirically verifiable of all Christian teachings, for the universality of human selfishness, cruelty, and evil — across every culture and every age, resisting every reform and every utopia — is precisely what the doctrine predicts and what a more optimistic anthropology struggles to explain. The doctrine accounts for both the grandeur of humanity (the marred image of God) and its misery (the inherited corruption), and in doing so it explains the human condition better than its rivals. Whatever its difficulties, it tells the truth about us. And that truth presses us toward the next question: just how far does this corruption reach? To the doctrine of total depravity we turn next.
