We come now to the deepest point of the doctrine of sin, the place where the whole question of grace is finally decided: the bondage of the will. We met it already in the history thread, when Luther flung his *On the Bondage of the Will* against Erasmus and thanked his opponent for going at last for "the vital spot." Now we must develop it as systematic theology — for the bound will is the logical culmination of original sin and total depravity, and the hinge on which sovereign grace turns. Yet it is also the most misunderstood and most resisted of all the doctrines of grace, for it seems, at first hearing, to deny human freedom and responsibility altogether. Everything therefore depends on understanding precisely what it does and does not claim — and that turns on a single crucial distinction.

## The Doctrine

The doctrine is this: the fallen human will, with respect to spiritual good and the turning to God, is not free but *bound* — enslaved to sin. The sinner cannot, by his own will, choose God, repent, believe, or do anything to save himself, because his will is in bondage to his corrupt nature. This follows directly from total depravity: if sin has corrupted the whole person, including the will, then the will itself is enslaved, and the sinner's choices, left to themselves, run always away from God and never savingly toward him. Jesus stated it plainly: everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin.[^1] The will is not neutral, poised freely between God and sin; it is in bondage, and apart from grace it serves only its master.

But here the whole matter hangs on a distinction that, once grasped, dissolves most of the apparent difficulty — the distinction between two things both loosely called "free will."

## Two Meanings of "Free Will"

In one sense, the fallen sinner most certainly *does* have free will: he has *voluntariness*, freedom from external compulsion. He chooses what he wants; he acts according to his own desires; he is not forced or dragged against his will. When a person sins, he sins because he *wants* to — freely, willingly, without anyone compelling him. In this sense human beings are not robots, puppets, or machines; they are genuine agents who make real choices according to their own inclinations. The Reformed tradition affirms this freedom wholeheartedly. We are not coerced; we do as we please.

But in another sense, the fallen sinner does *not* have free will: he lacks *moral ability*, the freedom to will spiritual good. For though he freely chooses what he wants, what he *wants* — his desires, his inclinations, the bent of his corrupt nature — is, apart from grace, always set against God. He is free to do as he pleases, but it does not please him to come to God. He is, in a striking phrase, a *free slave*: freely, willingly choosing his bondage, because his very wants are enslaved to sin. He will not come to God, and because he will not, he cannot — for the inability lies precisely in the will itself, in the corruption of his desires. He is perfectly free to act on his desires, and perfectly unable to desire God savingly without grace.

This understanding is what theologians call *compatibilism*: the conviction that genuine freedom (acting according to one's own will and desires) is *compatible* with the will being determined by the nature and inclinations — which, in the sinner, are corrupt. The sinner sins both *freely* and *necessarily*: freely, in that he does what he wants and is not compelled; necessarily, in that, given his corrupt nature, he cannot do otherwise. The bondage of the will is not the abolition of choice but the enslavement of the desires from which choice flows. We choose freely; but apart from grace, we cannot choose God, because we will not.

## Augustine's Four States

The Augustinian tradition has illuminated this with a beautiful fourfold scheme, tracing the will through the whole drama of creation, fall, redemption, and glory. Before the fall, Adam was *able to sin and able not to sin* — possessing a genuine freedom in both directions, able to stand or to fall. After the fall, fallen humanity is *not able not to sin* — bound, unable to refrain from sinning, the state of the bondage of the will we are now describing. After regeneration, the believer is *able not to sin* — freed by grace to begin to choose and do the good, though still struggling against remaining corruption. And in glory, the redeemed will be *not able to sin* — perfected and confirmed in righteousness, freed forever from even the possibility of falling, in the highest freedom of all.

This scheme shows that the bondage of the will is not the permanent or original condition of humanity, but the *fallen* condition — and that grace progressively liberates the will, until in glory it reaches a freedom higher even than Adam's. Bondage is the disease of the fall; grace is the cure; glory is the perfect health. The will that was free to fall, and then bound by falling, is freed by grace and at last perfected in a freedom that can never fail.

## How Grace Liberates

This points to the answer the whole doctrine has been driving toward: grace alone frees the bound will, and it does so not by violating the will but by *transforming* it. The objection naturally arises that if God saves the sinner whose will is bound, he must drag the unwilling against his will — a kind of violence to the person. But this is not how Reformed theology understands it. In *regeneration*, God does not force the unwilling sinner to come against his will; rather, God gives the sinner a *new heart*, new desires, a new nature — so that the sinner who would not come now *wants* to come, and freely, gladly does. Grace changes what we want, and from the new wants flows a new and free choosing of Christ.

So saving grace works *with* freedom, not against it. It does not coerce a will that remains hostile; it liberates a will that was enslaved, by giving it new and holy desires. The sinner comes to Christ freely — more freely than he has ever chosen anything — precisely because grace has made him willing. This is the resolution of the whole matter: the bound will is freed not by being overpowered but by being renewed, so that the very freedom the sinner exercises in coming to Christ is itself the gift of the grace that made him willing. We will develop this further when we reach regeneration and the effectual call; here it completes the doctrine of the bound will by showing that its liberation, like its bondage, is a matter of the heart's desires.

## Why It Matters

The bondage of the will is the hinge of sovereign grace, exactly as Luther perceived. If the will is bound — if the sinner cannot, of himself, will his own salvation — then salvation must be *monergistic*, the work of God alone. God must act first, regenerating the dead, giving the new heart, granting the very faith by which we believe. The sinner contributes nothing to his salvation but the sin from which he is saved. This grounds the whole structure of the doctrines of grace: unconditional election, the effectual call, irresistible grace, all of it. It is the deepest reason that salvation is by grace alone.

And the doctrine carries immense pastoral fruit, the very fruit Luther found in it. It grounds *assurance*: because my salvation rests not on my own uncertain and feeble will but on God's sovereign grace, I may be certain of it — for the God who began the work will complete it. It grounds *humility*: I contribute nothing to my salvation, and so have nothing to boast of, but owe everything to grace. And it grounds the *glory of God alone*: if salvation is entirely God's work from first to last, then the glory belongs entirely to God — *soli Deo gloria*. Far from being a doctrine of despair, the bondage of the will is, rightly grasped, the foundation of the deepest assurance and the highest praise.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The objections are serious and ancient, and we met them first with Erasmus; here we state them plainly and give the Reformed responses, without pretending the difficulties vanish. First, the objection that "ought implies can": God's commands to repent and believe imply that we are able to do so, for it would be unjust to command the impossible. The Reformed reply is that the commands reveal our *duty* and expose our *inability*, driving us to grace — the law shows us we cannot, that the gospel may give us what we cannot achieve; and our inability is no excuse, because it is a self-inflicted, culpable inability, an unwillingness, not an external barrier. Second, the objection that *responsibility requires freedom*: how can we be justly blamed for not doing what we cannot do? The reply lies in compatibilism: we are responsible because we sin *willingly* — we do exactly what we want; the inability is *moral* (we will not, because we do not want to), not *physical* (as though we were forced or chained against our will). We are responsible for our corrupt wants, which are our own. Third, the gravest objection: that this makes God the author of sin or unjust in condemning the bound. The Reformed answer is that the bondage results from humanity's own fall, not from God's compulsion, and that God is not the author of our corrupt desires — though the deepest forms of this objection press into the problem of evil and divine sovereignty, which we will face squarely in the unit on the eternal decree.

Behind all these lies a profound and genuine divide, philosophical as much as theological: between *compatibilist* freedom (the will is free in acting according to its strongest desire, even though that desire is determined by the nature) and *libertarian* freedom (the will is undetermined, genuinely able to choose either way regardless of the nature). The Arminian tradition affirms libertarian freedom, restored to all by prevenient grace, and with it a salvation that the sinner may genuinely accept or reject; and this position has real intuitive and moral appeal — it preserves a robust sense of freedom and responsibility, and it keeps God clear of determining the sinner's bondage. Honesty requires acknowledging that compatibilism is contested, in philosophy as in theology, and that the libertarian case is not foolish. The Reformed tradition embraces compatibilism because it judges that Scripture teaches both the genuine responsibility of the sinner and the absolute sovereignty of grace, and that only compatibilism holds these together. But the difficulty is real, and it would be dishonest to pretend the bound will dissolves all questions about freedom, justice, and evil. What it does establish, beyond doubt, is where the Reformation's deepest commitment lay: not in the free will of the sinner, but in the sovereign grace of God, who frees the bound by giving them a new heart.

With this, the doctrine of sin is complete. We have seen humanity made in God's image and placed in covenant; we have seen it fall; we have traced the inherited guilt and corruption of original sin, its total reach in depravity, and its enslavement of the will. The diagnosis is finished, and it is grave: a race guilty, corrupt, and bound, unable to save itself. Now the theology thread turns from the disease to the cure — from sin to salvation, from the bound will to the grace that frees it. To justification by faith alone, the heart of the gospel and the material principle of the Reformation, we turn next.
