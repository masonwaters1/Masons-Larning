We have seen the medieval church at its height and traced the institutional cracks that ran through its foundations. Now we arrive at the specific abuse that would, in 1517, light the fuse — the practice that drove an obscure German monk to nail his protest to a church door and, without intending it, to split Western Christendom. That practice was the sale of indulgences. To understand why it provoked such an explosion, we must understand the theology behind it: the doctrine of purgatory, the treasury of merit, and the claim that the church could dispense the remission of sin's penalty — a claim that, by Luther's day, had curdled into something that looked very much like the selling of salvation itself.

## The Treasury of Merit

The doctrine of indulgences rested on a particular and ingenious theological structure. Recall that medieval theology distinguished between the *guilt* of sin and its *temporal punishment*. When a penitent confessed and was absolved in the sacrament of penance, the guilt of sin and its eternal punishment were forgiven — but a *temporal* penalty remained, a debt of satisfaction to be paid through acts of penance in this life or through purifying suffering in purgatory after death. The forgiven sinner was still liable to this temporal punishment, and it was here that indulgences did their work.

The church taught that there existed a vast *treasury of merit* — a *thesaurus meritorum*. Christ, by his infinite sacrifice, and the saints, by their heroic holiness, had accumulated a superabundance of merit, far more than was needed for their own salvation. This surplus formed a spiritual treasury, a storehouse of accumulated grace, placed at the disposal of the church. And the pope, holding the keys of the kingdom committed to Peter, was understood to have authority to draw upon this treasury and apply its merit to others, remitting the temporal punishment they owed. An *indulgence*, then, was precisely this: a remission, granted by the church out of the treasury of merit, of the temporal punishment due to sin. It did not forgive guilt — that was the work of penance and absolution — but it cancelled some or all of the penalty that remained. In its origins, an indulgence was tied to acts of genuine devotion: a pilgrimage, a crusade, a work of charity, a sincere penitential discipline.

## From Devotion to Commerce

Over time, however, the practice drifted from devotion toward commerce, and the drift was fateful. Increasingly, the "act" attached to an indulgence became a *monetary contribution* — a payment toward some worthy cause, such as the building of a church or the funding of a crusade — for which the giver received the remission of penalty. The logic was not, at first, simple bribery: the gift of money was framed as an act of charity, a good work like any other. But the line between a charitable gift that merited an indulgence and the simple *purchase* of an indulgence was perilously thin, and in popular practice it all but vanished. What had begun as the church's pastoral application of grace to the truly penitent became, in effect, a transaction: money in exchange for the remission of sin's punishment.

The drift went further still. The church came to teach that indulgences could be applied not only to the living but to the *dead* — to souls suffering in purgatory. A living person could purchase an indulgence on behalf of a deceased relative, drawing on the treasury of merit to shorten or end that soul's purgatorial suffering and speed it to heaven. Here the emotional and financial power of the system reached its height. For who, loving a dead parent or child believed to be suffering in the cleansing fires of purgatory, would not give what they could to relieve them? The grief of the bereaved became the engine of a spiritual economy. And it was at exactly this point — the sale of indulgences for the dead — that the abuse would reach its most notorious and provocative form.

## Tetzel and the Indulgence of 1517

The specific occasion of the crisis was a great indulgence campaign launched in Germany in the years around 1517. Its official purpose was the rebuilding of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome — the magnificent church then rising, which required enormous sums. But beneath the official purpose lay a more sordid arrangement. A young German nobleman, Albrecht of Brandenburg, had acquired the wealthy and powerful archbishopric of Mainz — along with other offices he was not strictly entitled to hold — by borrowing vast sums from the banking house of the Fuggers to pay the required fees to Rome. To repay this debt, Albrecht was permitted to run the St. Peter's indulgence in his territories, with half the proceeds going secretly to settle his debt and half to Rome. The sale of grace, in other words, was financing a corrupt traffic in church offices.[^1]

The chief salesman of this indulgence was a Dominican friar named Johann Tetzel, a gifted and aggressive preacher who marketed the indulgence with theatrical flair. He is remembered above all for a jingle attributed to him, which captured the crassness of the whole enterprise: as soon as the coin in the money-box rings, the soul from purgatory springs. Whether or not Tetzel used those exact words, the spirit was real: he pressed his hearers to think of their dead relatives crying out from the flames of purgatory, begging to be released for the price of a coin. He promised that the indulgence offered forgiveness so complete that, in his salesman's exaggeration, it would avail even for the most outrageous sins. It was this campaign, with its naked commercializing of grace and its emotional manipulation of the grieving, that crossed Luther's path and provoked his protest.

## The Press and the Paper

One detail deserves notice, for it joins this lesson to the whole drama that follows. The indulgence trade of Luther's day ran on *paper* — and on the new technology that made paper cheap and words plentiful. Printed indulgence certificates, mass-produced on the recently invented printing press, were among the very first products of that press to be turned out in bulk; the buyer received a printed slip attesting to the grace he had purchased. The commercializing of salvation and the mechanizing of the written word had grown up together.

The irony is profound, and it would not be lost on history. The same printing press that churned out indulgence certificates by the thousand was about to become the engine of the Reformation. When Luther posted his objections, they were seized upon, printed, and scattered across Germany within weeks — translated from scholarly Latin into German, reproduced faster than any authority could suppress them, read and argued over in towns Luther had never visited. The press that sold the indulgence would publish the protest against it, and would carry the Reformation's message across Europe with a speed no earlier reformer could have dreamed of. Wycliffe and Hus had lacked this weapon; Luther would wield it. The technology that helped corrupt the late medieval church would help to break it.

## Why It Provoked Luther

When Martin Luther, then a monk and professor at Wittenberg, learned of Tetzel's preaching, he was outraged — and his outrage operated on several levels at once. There was, first, the *pastoral* damage. Luther watched his own parishioners returning from Tetzel with indulgence certificates, convinced that they had purchased forgiveness and needed no true repentance, no contrition, no amendment of life. The indulgence trade, as popularly understood, severed forgiveness from genuine repentance, offering cheap absolution for cash and lulling souls into a false and deadly security. As a pastor responsible for these souls, Luther saw the indulgence as spiritually ruinous.

There was, second, the *theological* problem. The whole apparatus raised questions Luther found increasingly impossible to answer within the existing system. Did the pope truly have authority over purgatory? If he could release souls from suffering by his power over the treasury of merit, then why — Luther would later ask pointedly — did he not, out of simple love, empty purgatory altogether and for free, rather than for money? And deeper still: could the remission of sin really be a *transaction* at all, something bought and sold, dispensed by the church for a fee? These questions, raised in Luther's Ninety-Five Theses of October 1517 — which were, in their original form, specifically a set of academic objections to the indulgence traffic — would prove to be the thin end of a very large wedge. For to question indulgences was to question the treasury of merit; to question the treasury was to question the church's whole economy of grace; and that economy ran to the very heart of medieval religion. Luther began by attacking an abuse. He would end by challenging the entire structure of which the abuse was a symptom.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Fairness requires a careful distinction here, for the popular abuse and the church's official theology were not identical, and to conflate them would be to caricature. The church's formal doctrine of indulgences was, in important respects, more careful than Tetzel's salesmanship. Officially, an indulgence required the recipient to be truly contrite and to have confessed; it was never, in strict theology, a license to sin or a substitute for repentance. The cruder promises of the indulgence-sellers — that one could buy forgiveness for future sins, or spring a soul from purgatory by the mere ringing of a coin regardless of one's spiritual state — went beyond, and in some cases against, the church's authorized teaching. Tetzel was a salesman, and salesmen exaggerate. Defenders of the medieval church can rightly point out that the worst abuses were corruptions of the doctrine, not the doctrine itself, and that the church would later, at the Council of Trent, sharply reform the practice and condemn the trafficking in indulgences for gain.

And yet this defense, while true as far as it goes, does not finally exonerate the system — and Luther's deeper insight was precisely this. The abuse was not a random accident; it flowed naturally from the doctrine's own logic. Once it is granted that the church controls a treasury of merit, that the pope can dispense its grace to remit punishment, and that a charitable monetary gift can be the occasion of such remission, the slide toward commerce is not a perversion of the system but its predictable outworking. A doctrine that makes grace a quantity to be administered, and money an acceptable channel for obtaining it, will tend toward the sale of grace as surely as water runs downhill. Luther came to see that the problem was not merely that bad men had abused a good doctrine, but that the doctrine itself rested on a false foundation: the assumption that salvation operates through a transferable economy of merit, managed by the church. To strike at that assumption was to strike at the medieval system entire. And so the sale of salvation, beginning as a financial scandal, opened onto the deepest theological question of all — the question of how a sinner is made right with God. To find why that question burned so fiercely in Luther's own soul, we must turn next to the anxious religion of the late medieval conscience.
