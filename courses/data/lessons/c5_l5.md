The sale of indulgences was the spark, but a spark needs tinder, and the deepest tinder of the Reformation was not an institutional abuse but a *spiritual* one — a torment of the conscience that the late medieval system of salvation produced in its most sensitive souls. To understand why Martin Luther reacted to Tetzel not with mild disapproval but with the force of a man whose own soul was at stake, we must understand the theology of salvation he had been taught and the agony it had cost him. The late medieval church offered grace, but to a certain kind of earnest believer it offered no *assurance* — and the result was a religion shadowed by dread. This is the anxious conscience, and it is the problem to which Luther's whole recovered gospel would be the answer.

## The Theology Luther Was Taught

Luther was trained in a particular school of late medieval theology known as the *via moderna* — the "modern way" — which had grown out of the philosophical movement called nominalism, associated above all with the fourteenth-century thinker William of Ockham and transmitted to Luther's generation through theologians such as Gabriel Biel.[^1] We need not enter the technical thickets of nominalist philosophy, with its denial that universal concepts have any real existence apart from the particular things they name. What matters for our purposes is the *theology of salvation* this school taught, for it was this that shaped — and tormented — the young Luther.

The via moderna held that salvation operated within a framework of covenant. God, in his freedom, had established a pact (a *pactum*) with humanity, the terms of which could be summed up in a Latin slogan that Luther would have known well: *facienti quod in se est, Deus non denegat gratiam* — "to the one who does what is within him, God does not deny grace." The idea was this: although salvation ultimately depends on God's grace, the sinner must first *do what lies within him* — must do his utmost, love God as far as he is able, turn from sin with all his natural power — and to the one who thus does his best, God has bound himself by covenant to respond with the gift of saving grace. Human effort initiates; divine grace follows. It was not crude works-righteousness; grace remained necessary and central. But it placed upon the sinner a prior demand: *do your best*.

## The Torment of "Doing Your Best"

Here lay the trap that would catch and nearly destroy the young Luther. For the demand to "do what is within you" raised a question that admitted of no certain answer: *How do I know when I have done enough?* How can I be sure that I have truly done my best, loved God with all my strength, turned from sin with full sincerity? How can I know that my repentance is genuine and not secretly tainted by self-interest, that my love for God is real and not merely the fear of punishment? The standard was inward and immeasurable, and to a scrupulous and honest conscience it offered no floor to stand on, no point at which one could say, "Now I have done what is required; now I may rest."

Luther, as a young Augustinian monk, threw himself into this system with a desperate, almost frantic earnestness. He fasted, prayed, kept vigils, and disciplined his body; he confessed his sins with such exhausting thoroughness that his confessors grew weary of him, for he would return again and again, tormented by the fear that he had forgotten some sin or that his contrition was insufficient. And the harder he tried, the worse it became. For the more seriously he took the demand to do his best, the more clearly he saw that his best was never good enough — that even his repentance needed repenting of, that even his love for God was shot through with self-love and, at bottom, with resentment of a God who demanded what he could not deliver. The system designed to lead him to grace led him instead to despair. He could find no assurance, because the theology he had been taught offered none: do your best, and *perhaps* God will give grace — but you can never be certain you have done your best, and so you can never be certain of grace.

## A Confessor's Counsel

Luther did not endure his torment entirely alone, and one figure who stood by him in it deserves mention, for he planted seeds that would later bear fruit. Johann von Staupitz was the vicar-general of Luther's Augustinian order and Luther's own superior and confessor — the man who heard, with growing concern and patience, the endless confessions of the tormented young monk. Staupitz watched Luther wear himself out in the attempt to find peace through his own efforts, and he saw that the via moderna's relentless inward focus — examine yourself, do your best, search your motives — was driving his charge toward despair rather than toward God.

Staupitz's counsel pointed in a different direction. He urged Luther to stop obsessing over his own worthiness and his own performance, and to look instead *outside himself* — to the wounds of Christ, to the love of God revealed in the cross, to election grounded in God's mercy rather than in human striving. Look away from yourself and to Christ, was the burden of his advice. Staupitz was no Protestant, and he never followed Luther out of the Roman church; but his pastoral wisdom turned Luther's gaze, however haltingly at first, from his own efforts toward the grace of God in Christ. Luther never forgot it, and later said he had received the beginnings of the gospel from Staupitz. The seed of the great discovery — that the sinner's hope lies not in himself but in Christ received by faith — was, in part, planted by a faithful confessor who saw a soul drowning and pointed it toward the only One who could save.

## A Religion Shadowed by Dread

Luther's anguish was an extreme case, but it was not an isolated one; it dramatized a tension running through much of late medieval religion. For all the genuine comfort the sacramental system offered — and it offered much — it also generated, in many souls, a pervasive *anxiety* about salvation. The God of late medieval piety was very often the God of *judgment*: Christ depicted not as the gentle Savior but as the stern judge of the Last Day, seated in majesty to divide the saved from the damned. The fear of death, of judgment, of the cleansing torments of purgatory, and of the everlasting fire of hell pressed heavily on the medieval imagination, expressed in the grim art of the age — the danse macabre, the dance of death that led king and peasant alike to the grave, the vivid paintings of the damned tumbling into hell's mouth.

Within this world, the question that haunted the earnest believer was the question of *assurance*: Am I saved? Have I done enough? Is God for me or against me? And the system, for all its machinery of grace, gave no firm answer. One could confess, do penance, receive the sacraments, purchase indulgences — and still not *know*, still lie awake in dread, still face death uncertain of one's eternal fate. This was the spiritual condition into which the indulgence trade fell, and it explains the indulgence's terrible appeal: to a conscience desperate for some assurance, some tangible token that the debt was paid, the indulgence certificate offered a fragile comfort. It also explains Luther's fury, for he saw the indulgence as exploiting exactly this anxiety — selling a false and flimsy peace to souls whose true need it could never meet.

## The Question That Demanded an Answer

We can now see the late medieval situation whole, and feel the pressure building toward an explosion. The institutional church was in crisis, its authority cracked by schism and worldliness. Its economy of grace had drifted into the scandal of the indulgence trade. And beneath these visible problems lay an invisible one, deeper and more personal: a theology of salvation that, for the most earnest souls, produced not peace but terror — that set before the sinner an unattainable standard and then offered no certainty that he had met it. The burning question of the age, for a man like Luther, was intensely personal: *How can I find a gracious God? How can a sinner be sure that he is accepted, forgiven, righteous before a holy God?*

This is the question to which the whole Reformation would be, at its core, an answer. Everything that follows — the recovery of justification by faith, the doctrine of grace alone, the assurance of salvation, the very shape of Reformed theology — flows from Luther's discovery of a different answer to this question than the one his teachers had given. Where the via moderna said, "Do your best, and God may grant grace," Luther would come to read in Scripture a wholly different message: that the righteousness God requires, God himself *gives*, freely, as a gift received by faith alone, not earned by doing one's best — so that the sinner's standing rests not on his own uncertain efforts but on the finished work of Christ, and assurance becomes possible at last. That discovery, the "tower experience" that turned a tormented monk into a reformer, belongs to a later unit, where we will trace Luther's own story. For now, we have seen the problem in its depth: the anxious conscience of late medieval religion, crying out for a gracious God and finding none. The tinder was dry; the spark was about to fall.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Honesty requires us to guard against overstating this picture, for it is easy, in a Protestant telling, to paint all of late medieval religion as nothing but anxiety and dread, and that would be false. Several qualifications are in order. First, the via moderna was not the *only* theology of the late medieval church; it was one school among several. The older Thomist and Scotist traditions framed the matter differently, and there was a strong Augustinian current — represented by theologians such as Gregory of Rimini — that placed far greater weight on the priority and necessity of grace and would have found the "do your best" formula dangerously optimistic about human ability. Luther's torment arose from a particular strand of late medieval theology, not from a monolithic medieval consensus.

Second, and more importantly, countless medieval Christians evidently found in their faith genuine comfort, joy, and assurance — resting in the love of Christ, the intercession of the saints, the consolations of the sacraments, and the hope of heaven. The anxious conscience was real, but it was the experience of a particular kind of sensitive and scrupulous soul, not the universal condition of every believer. Many lived and died in serene faith. Third, defenders of the medieval system rightly insist that the "do what is in you" theology always involved grace and was not the crude Pelagianism (salvation by sheer human effort) that its critics sometimes alleged; God's enabling grace was assumed throughout. These qualifications matter, and a fair account must grant them. Yet they do not dissolve the central point. For a conscience as earnest and as honest as Luther's — and he was far from alone — the late medieval theology of salvation did fail to provide assurance, and did produce real torment, precisely because it rested the sinner's hope, in part, on the sinner's own uncertain performance. That failure was not imaginary, and the answer Luther would find to it would shake the world. But before we reach Luther's answer, we must meet those who had glimpsed it before him — the forerunners who, a century and more early, had already begun to question the system. We turn first to the morning star of the Reformation, John Wycliffe.
