The Reformation did not arrive without warning. More than a century before Luther, voices had already risen within the medieval church to challenge its corruptions, exalt the authority of Scripture, and anticipate — sometimes with startling precision — the very themes the Reformers would later sound. These were the forerunners, and the first and greatest of them was an English theologian whom later Protestants would honor with a luminous title: the "Morning Star of the Reformation." His name was John Wycliffe, and to understand the Reformation as the recovery this course takes it to be, we must see that its central convictions did not spring from nowhere in 1517 but had been glimpsed, generations earlier, by men who came before the dawn.

## The Oxford Reformer

John Wycliffe was born around 1330 and became the leading philosopher and theologian at Oxford, the foremost intellect of the English church in his generation. He lived through the very crises we have traced — the Avignon papacy and the opening of the Great Schism — and through a period of intense English resentment against the wealth, worldliness, and foreign exactions of the church. England chafed under papal taxation and the spectacle of a papacy seen as captive to England's enemy, France. Out of this context, and out of his own deepening study of Scripture, Wycliffe developed a body of teaching that grew steadily more radical and more threatening to the established order.

Wycliffe began as a respected academic and royal servant, useful to the English crown in its disputes with the papacy over money and authority. But his criticisms deepened from matters of church wealth and politics into the foundations of medieval theology itself. By the end of his life he had attacked some of the most central doctrines and institutions of the medieval church, and had set forth principles that would not be heard again with such force until the sixteenth century. He was condemned, harassed, and driven from Oxford, but he was protected by powerful patrons and died in his bed in 1384 — though, as we shall see, the church would not let his bones rest.

## Scripture Above the Church

The heart of Wycliffe's challenge was his exaltation of *Scripture* as the supreme authority — a conviction that anticipated, in substance, the Reformation's principle of *sola scriptura*. Wycliffe taught that the Bible is the supreme law of God, the standard by which all human authority, including that of pope and church, must be judged. Where the medieval system placed Scripture, tradition, and the teaching authority of the church together as coordinate authorities, Wycliffe insisted that Scripture stands *above* them all, and that any teaching or practice not grounded in Scripture stands condemned. The Word of God, not the word of the church, was for Wycliffe the final court of appeal.

From this conviction flowed his most famous and consequential act: the conviction that Scripture, being the supreme authority for all believers, must be made available to all believers in their own tongue. The medieval church had largely restricted the Bible to Latin, the language of the clergy and the learned, and viewed lay access to vernacular Scripture with deep suspicion. Wycliffe and his followers defied this, producing the first complete translation of the Bible into English — the "Wycliffite Bible" — so that ordinary English men and women might read, or hear read, the Word of God for themselves.[^1] This was a revolutionary act, embodying a revolutionary principle: that the Scriptures belong not to the clergy alone but to the whole people of God. It would be echoed, with world-changing effect, when Luther gave the Germans their Bible and Tyndale the English theirs.

## Attacking the Medieval System

Wycliffe did not stop at the authority of Scripture. Armed with that authority, he turned his criticism against the medieval system at its most sensitive points. He attacked the wealth and worldliness of the clergy with fierce indignation, calling the church back to apostolic poverty and denouncing the corruption he saw at every level. He criticized the papacy itself, questioning the biblical basis for its claims to supreme authority and, in his sharpest moments, identifying the worldly, grasping papacy of his day with the very enemies of Christ. He condemned the abuses of monasticism, the trade in indulgences and pardons, and much of the apparatus of medieval popular religion that he found without warrant in Scripture.

Most dangerously of all, Wycliffe attacked the doctrine of *transubstantiation* — the teaching, central to the Mass, that the bread and wine are changed into the substance of Christ's body and blood. This was the most perilous ground a medieval theologian could tread, for it struck at the very heart of the sacramental system and the priesthood's unique power. Wycliffe denied the medieval understanding, arguing on both philosophical and scriptural grounds against the change of substance. He also developed a doctrine of the *church* that anticipated Reformed thought: he taught that the true church is the company of those whom God has predestined to salvation — the elect — rather than simply the visible Roman institution with all its members good and bad. The true church, in other words, is invisible and defined by God's election, not coextensive with the external hierarchy. Here, in the fourteenth century, are the seeds of doctrines — the authority of Scripture, the priesthood of the people, predestination, the invisible church of the elect — that would flower in the Reformation.

## The Lollards

Wycliffe's ideas did not die with him; they were carried on by a movement of followers known as the *Lollards*. These were preachers and lay people, drawn from various ranks of English society, who embraced Wycliffe's teachings: the supremacy of Scripture, the reading of the English Bible, a personal and biblical faith, and a sharp criticism of clerical wealth, the cult of images and relics, pilgrimages, and the doctrine of transubstantiation. The Lollards emphasized the Bible in the vernacular and the direct relationship of the believer to God's Word, and they spread their convictions through itinerant preaching and the circulation of English Scriptures and tracts.

The established church responded with persecution. Lollardy was branded heresy, and in 1401 England enacted a fearful statute, *De heretico comburendo* — "concerning the burning of heretics" — which for the first time authorized the burning of obstinate heretics at the stake on English soil. Under this and later measures, Lollards were hunted, tried, forced to recant, and in some cases burned. Driven underground, the movement was suppressed as an open force, but it was never entirely extinguished. A substratum of Lollard conviction — Bible-reading, anticlerical, suspicious of the Mass and the trappings of medieval religion — persisted quietly in pockets of England for more than a century, and when the Reformation reached England in the sixteenth century, it would find in this surviving Lollardy a native soil already prepared to receive it.

## The Bones at Constance

The church's hostility to Wycliffe did not end with his death or even with the suppression of the Lollards. So dangerous were his ideas judged to be that the Council of Constance — the same great council that healed the Great Schism and burned Jan Hus — formally condemned Wycliffe as a heretic in 1415, more than thirty years after he had died peacefully in his bed. The council ordered that his remains be exhumed and burned, and his ashes scattered — a sentence carried out in 1428, when his bones were dug up, burned, and cast into a nearby river. A chronicler of the age observed that the river carried his ashes into the sea, and thence, as it were, to all the world — an image later Protestants would cherish, for Wycliffe's influence had indeed spread far beyond England, reaching Bohemia, where it would shape the man we meet in the next lesson, Jan Hus.

## The Spark Carried East

Wycliffe's influence might have remained confined to England, smoldering quietly among the Lollards, but for a thread of dynastic history that carried his writings far across Europe. In 1382, Richard II of England married Anne of Bohemia, and the marriage opened a channel of exchange between England and the Bohemian lands of central Europe. Bohemian scholars came to study at Oxford, where Wycliffe's ideas were alive and contested, and they carried his writings home to the University of Prague. There, in a kingdom with its own currents of reforming discontent, Wycliffe's books found eager readers.

Among those readers was a young Czech priest and university master named Jan Hus, who absorbed Wycliffe's exaltation of Scripture, his criticism of clerical corruption, and key elements of his theology, and began to preach them with passion in the Czech tongue to the people of Prague. The Englishman's fire, all but stamped out in his homeland, leapt across a continent and blazed up anew in Bohemia. The river that bore Wycliffe's ashes to the sea had, in a sense, already carried his ideas to Prague — where they would provoke a confrontation even more dramatic than Wycliffe's own, and end not in a quiet death and a posthumous burning, but in living flames. To Jan Hus, and to the price he paid, we turn next.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The title "Morning Star of the Reformation" is a Protestant honor, conferred in hindsight, and intellectual honesty requires that we examine it with care, for it can mislead as well as illuminate. Wycliffe was not, in fact, a sixteenth-century Protestant born too early. He was a medieval scholastic philosopher, with his own complex and idiosyncratic system — including an extreme philosophical realism quite foreign to the later Reformers — and his thought cannot simply be assimilated to theirs. Some of his positions were genuinely radical and socially destabilizing in ways the magisterial Reformers would have rejected: his theory of "dominion by grace," which held that legitimate authority and property belong only to those in a state of grace, had revolutionary implications, and his name became entangled, fairly or not, with the social upheaval of the Peasants' Revolt of 1381, which damaged his cause and frightened the authorities. The line from Wycliffe to Luther is real but indirect, and the Reformers largely rediscovered their central convictions through their own reading of Scripture and the Fathers, not by sitting at Wycliffe's feet.

So the "morning star" image should be held with nuance. Wycliffe did not cause the Reformation, and he was not its prototype in any simple sense; the continuity can be overstated, and the retrospective Protestant claiming of him as one of their own flattens the genuine differences. And yet — when all the qualifications are granted — the anticipations remain genuinely striking. Here, a century and a half before the Reformation, was a major theologian exalting Scripture above the church, translating the Bible for the people, attacking transubstantiation and clerical corruption, and teaching that the true church is the company of the elect. These were not nothing; they were real glimpses, in the late medieval gloom, of the dawn that was coming. Wycliffe came too early — before the printing press, before the political conditions that would protect a reformer, before the hour had struck — and so his movement was crushed rather than triumphant. But he had seen something true, and he had not been alone in seeing it. Far to the east, in Bohemia, his writings had kindled a fire in another reformer, one who would pay for his convictions with his life. To Jan Hus we turn next.
