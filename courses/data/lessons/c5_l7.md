The fire that Wycliffe kindled in England, all but stamped out at home, leapt across Europe to Bohemia, where it blazed up in a man who would pay for his convictions not with a posthumous burning of his bones but with living flames. Jan Hus was a Czech priest, preacher, and university master whose career ran a full century before Luther's, and whose story prefigures the Reformation with eerie precision: a reformer exalting Scripture and attacking the corruption of the church, summoned by the authorities under a promise of safety, betrayed, and burned — and a popular movement that, against all odds, survived his death and won a measure of the very freedom the Reformation would later claim. To understand the Reformation as the recovery this course takes it to be, we must understand Hus, the forerunner who became a martyr.

## The Preacher of Prague

Jan Hus was born around 1372 and rose to become a master and eventually rector of the University of Prague, and the most influential preacher in the Bohemian capital. His pulpit was the Bethlehem Chapel, a church founded specifically for preaching in the Czech language to ordinary people — and from it Hus thundered against the corruption of the clergy, the worldliness of the church, and the abuses of his day, in words the common people could understand. He was, by all accounts, a preacher of immense power and moral earnestness, and he commanded a devoted following among the people of Prague and the Czech nation.

Hus had absorbed the writings of Wycliffe, which had reached Prague through the scholarly exchange opened by the marriage of the English and Bohemian royal houses. From Wycliffe he took the exaltation of Scripture as the supreme authority, the conviction that the true church is the company of those whom God has predestined — with Christ, not the pope, as its true head — and a fierce criticism of clerical wealth and corruption. When, in 1412, an indulgence campaign came to Prague to raise money for a papal war, Hus denounced it openly, condemning the sale of forgiveness for money — a stand that placed him in direct conflict with the pope and that foreshadows, with startling exactness, Luther's protest of 1517. For this and his other teachings, Hus was excommunicated, and the city of Prague was placed under interdict on his account.

## A Crucial Difference

Here we must mark an important precision, for Hus was not simply a Czech Luther born early, and to flatten him into one would be to misread him. On several points Hus was significantly more *moderate* and more *Catholic* than the later Reformers — and notably more moderate than Wycliffe himself. Most importantly, Hus did *not* follow Wycliffe in rejecting the doctrine of transubstantiation; he continued to affirm the real, substantial presence of Christ in the Eucharist along orthodox medieval lines. His quarrel was not, at its core, a thoroughgoing dogmatic revolution but a campaign for the reform of a corrupt church, the authority of Scripture, and the moral renewal of the clergy.

The one liturgical innovation that became the banner of his movement was the demand for *communion in both kinds* — that the laity should receive not only the bread but also the cup, the wine, in the Lord's Supper, rather than the medieval practice of reserving the cup to the priest alone. This practice, communion *sub utraque specie* ("under both kinds"), gave the Hussite movement its enduring name: the *Utraquists*, and their symbol the chalice. It was a modest reform by later Protestant standards, but it carried a profound principle — that the laity should not be spiritually second-class, kept from the full sacrament — and it became the rallying emblem of a nation. The point to hold is this: Hus was a reformer working largely within the categories of medieval Catholicism, not a proto-Protestant in the full sense. His radicalism lay less in his doctrine than in his courage, his appeal to Scripture against the church, and the price he was willing to pay.

## Constance and the Broken Promise

The conflict came to its climax at the great Council of Constance, the same council we have already met as the body that healed the Great Schism and condemned the bones of Wycliffe. In 1414 Hus was summoned to Constance to answer for his views, and — crucially — he was granted a *safe-conduct* by the Holy Roman Emperor Sigismund, a solemn imperial guarantee of his safety. Trusting this promise, Hus made the journey, believing he would be given a fair hearing in which he might defend his teaching from Scripture.

The promise was not honored. Soon after his arrival, Hus was arrested and imprisoned. The council, which regarded him as a dangerous heretic in the line of Wycliffe, had no intention of debating him as an equal; it demanded that he recant. The justification offered for breaking the safe-conduct was a principle that reveals much about the age: that faith need not be kept with a heretic — that a promise made to one outside the church's protection was not binding. Hus, brought before the council, refused to recant doctrines he had not been shown to be false from Scripture; he would not condemn as error what he believed to be true and biblical merely on the council's command. For this he was condemned. On July 6, 1415, Jan Hus was stripped of his priestly office and burned at the stake, reportedly singing and praying as the flames rose. He died a martyr's death, and his courage in the fire made an impression that no condemnation could erase.[^1]

## The Bohemian Revolt

What followed set Hus apart from every earlier reformer, including Wycliffe, and gave the Reformation a crucial precedent. The burning of Hus did not crush his movement; it ignited it. The Czech nation, outraged at the judicial murder of their hero under a broken imperial promise, rose in revolt. When the authorities attempted to suppress Hussite belief by force, the Bohemians took up arms, and there followed the Hussite Wars — a remarkable series of conflicts in which the Hussites, fighting under brilliant commanders and with innovative tactics, repeatedly defeated the crusading armies sent against them by the emperor and the pope.

Against all expectation, the Hussites *won* — not total victory, but survival and a measure of toleration. By the agreements known as the Compactata, a substantial part of the Bohemian church secured the right to its distinctive practices, above all communion in both kinds, within a framework of nominal reconciliation with Rome. The movement divided into a more moderate Utraquist wing and the more radical Taborites, and from its later streams descended the Bohemian Brethren (the *Unitas Fratrum*), a body of earnest, Bible-centered Christians who survived as a distinct community right up to the eve of the Reformation — and beyond. Here, then, was something unprecedented: a reforming movement, sprung from one martyr's death, that had defied pope and emperor, carved out space for its convictions, and *endured* for a century. The Reformation, when it came, would not be the first time a people had successfully resisted Rome in the name of reform. Bohemia had shown it could be done.

## The Goose and the Swan

The connection between Hus and Luther is direct, and the men of the sixteenth century felt it keenly. At the Leipzig Debate of 1519, Luther's Catholic opponent, Johann Eck, sought to discredit him by maneuvering him into agreeing with positions that had been condemned at Constance — to brand Luther a Hussite, and therefore a convicted heretic. Luther, pressed, made the startling admission that among the articles for which Hus had been burned were teachings that were, in fact, genuinely Christian and biblical — that the council had condemned and burned a faithful man. It was a dangerous thing to say, for it implied that a general council of the church had erred, and erred murderously. Luther would later go further, declaring in effect that "we are all Hussites without having realized it."

There is also a legend, cherished by later Protestants, that at his death Hus prophesied his vindication. His name, *Hus*, means "goose" in Czech, and he is said to have declared that they might burn the goose now, but in a hundred years a swan would arise whom they would not be able to silence. Whether or not Hus ever spoke these words — and the prophecy is almost certainly a later embellishment — the symbolism was irresistible: a hundred years after 1415 brought 1517, and Luther was the swan. The legend captured a real truth about the relationship: Hus had sounded, a century early, themes the Reformation would take up in triumph, and his martyrdom stood as both a warning of what the church would do to reformers and a promise that their cause might yet prevail.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Fairness requires the same caution here that we applied to Wycliffe, perhaps more so. Hus was not a Protestant, and the continuity between him and the Reformation, while real, can be overstated. His theology remained largely within medieval Catholic categories; he affirmed transubstantiation and much of the sacramental system; his central demands — moral reform, the authority of Scripture, the cup for the laity — fell well short of the doctrinal revolution Luther would launch. Moreover, the Hussite movement was as much a matter of *Czech national identity* as of theology: it fused religious reform with Bohemian resistance to German and papal domination, and its remarkable military success owed as much to nationalism and to the genius of its commanders as to its doctrine. The "swan prophecy" is legend, and the line from Hus to Luther, though genuine, runs through a tangle of contingencies rather than a straight road.

Yet when all this is granted, the significance of Hus remains profound, and the Reformed tradition is right to honor him. He stood on the authority of Scripture against pope and council; he denounced the sale of indulgences a hundred years before Luther; he refused to recant what he had not been shown to be unbiblical; and he sealed his witness with his blood. And his movement proved, as nothing before had proved, that a reformation could survive the wrath of Rome. When Luther stood at Worms a century later and refused to recant unless convinced by Scripture, he stood in a line that ran back through the flames at Constance. The goose had been burned; the swan was coming. But before the swan could sing, the church needed not only martyrs and forerunners but new tools — and those tools were being forged, in these same generations, by a movement of scholars who insisted on returning to the original sources of the faith. To the Renaissance humanists we turn next.
