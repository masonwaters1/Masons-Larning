The Reformation needed more than martyrs and forerunners; it needed *tools*. It needed the recovered text of Scripture in its original languages, the scholarly methods to read it accurately, and a generation of minds trained to question received tradition by going back to the sources. These tools were not forged by the Reformers themselves; they were the gift of a movement that had been gathering force for a century before Luther — the Renaissance, and within it the scholarly revolution we call humanism. To understand how the Reformation became possible, we must understand how the humanists taught Europe to return *ad fontes* — to the sources — and how that return, applied to the Bible, would prove explosive.

## What Renaissance Humanism Was

First we must clear away a modern confusion. "Humanism" today often means a secular, even anti-religious outlook that makes man rather than God the measure of all things. Renaissance humanism was nothing of the sort. The word comes from the *studia humanitatis* — the "humanities," the study of grammar, rhetoric, poetry, history, and moral philosophy through the great texts of classical antiquity. A "humanist," in this period, was simply a scholar of these disciplines, a lover and student of the ancient languages and their literature. Most Renaissance humanists were sincere Christians; many were clergy. Humanism was a program of *learning*, not a creed of unbelief.

What defined the movement was its passion for the recovery of the classical past — the literature, philosophy, history, and languages of ancient Greece and Rome — and its conviction that the learning and culture of the present could be renewed by returning to those ancient sources. The humanists looked back at the long medieval centuries and saw, as they thought, a decline: a coarsening of Latin, a loss of the classical languages, a burial of the original texts under layers of clumsy commentary and barbarous scholastic jargon. Their remedy was to go *back* — back to the original texts, in the original languages, read with fresh eyes. This impulse, this longing to recover and renew by returning to the source, is the key to everything that follows.

## Ad Fontes

The humanist program found its battle cry in two Latin words: *ad fontes* — "to the sources," or "to the springs." The conviction was that to understand and renew any tradition, one must go back behind the accumulated commentaries, translations, and corruptions to the original sources in their original languages, and drink from the spring rather than the muddied stream downriver. Do not study Aristotle through medieval Latin paraphrases and the glosses of generations of schoolmen; learn Greek and read Aristotle himself. Do not rest content with received texts riddled with copyists' errors; recover the most accurate manuscripts and establish what the author actually wrote.

This demanded the revival of the ancient languages — not only classical Latin but, crucially, *Greek*, which had been largely lost to the medieval West, and increasingly *Hebrew* as well. It demanded the development of new scholarly skills: textual criticism, the comparison of manuscripts to reconstruct the original; philology, the precise study of language and its history; the careful editing of ancient texts. The humanists became, in effect, the first modern critical scholars, and they turned their new methods loose on the entire inheritance of the past. At first this was applied to classical literature. But it was only a matter of time before the same methods were turned upon the most important book of all.

## The Power of the New Scholarship

The explosive potential of humanist scholarship was demonstrated dramatically by the Italian scholar Lorenzo Valla, and his example is worth dwelling on because it reveals the stakes. The medieval papacy had long justified its claims to temporal power, in part, by appeal to a document called the *Donation of Constantine* — supposedly a decree of the emperor Constantine in the fourth century, granting the popes vast authority and dominion over the Western empire. For centuries it had been accepted as genuine and used to buttress papal claims.

Valla, applying the new philological methods, subjected the document to critical analysis — and proved it a *forgery*. The Latin of the supposed fourth-century decree, he showed, was full of words, phrases, and anachronisms that could not have existed in Constantine's time; the document had been fabricated centuries later. With the tools of humanist scholarship, a single scholar had exposed one of the foundations of papal temporal authority as a fraud. The lesson was not lost on anyone: the new learning was not a harmless antiquarian hobby. Applied to the church's own claims and documents, *ad fontes* could be a weapon of devastating power, capable of dissolving the historical and textual foundations on which church authority rested. Valla also turned his critical eye on the New Testament, comparing the Greek text to the Latin Vulgate and noting discrepancies — work that pointed directly toward the great biblical scholarship to come.

## Turning to the Bible

The most momentous application of the humanist method was, inevitably, to the Bible itself. The medieval church read and taught Scripture in Latin, through the Vulgate — the venerable translation made largely by Jerome a thousand years before, which had become the official and almost untouchable text of the Western church. But the original Old Testament was written in Hebrew and the New Testament in Greek, and the humanist principle demanded that scholars go behind the Latin translation to these originals. To read the Bible *ad fontes* meant recovering and studying the Hebrew and Greek texts, and comparing them to the Vulgate the church had used for a millennium.

This was revolutionary for two reasons. First, it revealed that the Vulgate, for all its authority, contained *errors* and questionable renderings — places where the Latin diverged from the original, sometimes on points where doctrines and practices had been built upon the Latin wording. If the official text could be shown to mistranslate the Word of God, then teachings resting on that mistranslation stood exposed. Second, and more broadly, the recovery of the original Scriptures put the very Word of God, in its authentic form, into the hands of scholars who could then test the teaching and practice of the church directly against it. The principle *ad fontes*, applied to the Bible, pointed straight toward the principle *sola scriptura*: back to the sources meant, ultimately, back to Scripture as the standard by which all else must be judged. The humanists were not, for the most part, trying to start a Reformation. But they were placing in the church's path a loaded weapon, and it would not stay unfired.

## The Gift of the Press

All of this was multiplied beyond measure by a technology that arrived at precisely the right moment: the printing press, developed by Johannes Gutenberg around the middle of the fifteenth century. Before the press, texts were copied by hand, slowly, expensively, and with the steady accumulation of errors; a scholar's critical edition might exist in a handful of manuscripts and reach only a few. The press changed everything. Now accurate, critically edited texts could be produced in hundreds and thousands of identical copies, distributed across Europe, and placed in the hands of scholars, clergy, and eventually ordinary readers.

The marriage of humanist scholarship and the printing press was extraordinarily powerful. The press could spread the recovered classical texts, the new grammars and dictionaries of Greek and Hebrew, and — most consequentially — printed editions of the Bible in its original languages and in vernacular translations. It meant that when a reformer made an argument from the sources, that argument could reach a continent in months. It meant that the recovered Greek New Testament could become the common possession of educated Europe. The humanists had forged the tools; the press would distribute them; and the Reformers would wield them. Without the convergence of the new scholarship and the new technology, the Reformation as it actually unfolded is scarcely imaginable.

## Northern Humanism and the Reform of the Church

It helps to distinguish two broad streams within the humanist movement, for they flowed in somewhat different directions. The humanism that arose first, in Italy, was deeply absorbed in the recovery of classical *pagan* antiquity — the literature, art, philosophy, and rhetoric of Greece and Rome — and while many Italian humanists were sincere Christians, the movement there had a strongly secular and aesthetic cast, fascinated above all by the glories of the ancient world for their own sake. As humanism spread north of the Alps, however, into Germany, the Low Countries, France, and England, it took on a distinctly different and more religious character.

This *Northern*, or *Christian*, humanism applied the methods of the new scholarship not chiefly to the recovery of pagan antiquity but to the renewal of the church and the Christian life. Its great aim was to return *ad fontes* in a specifically Christian sense — back to the sources of the faith: the Scriptures in their original languages and the writings of the early Church Fathers, behind the dense thickets of medieval scholasticism. The Northern humanists dreamed of a reformed and purified Christianity, recovered from its sources and freed from corruption, superstition, and the dry hairsplitting of the schoolmen — a return to what they called the simple "philosophy of Christ." They edited the Fathers, studied the Greek New Testament, and called for a religion of heartfelt piety and moral earnestness drawn from the gospel itself. This reforming, source-seeking Christian humanism is the soil from which the Reformation most directly grew, and its supreme representative was a Dutch scholar whose Greek New Testament would change the world. To him we turn in the next lesson.

## The Relationship to the Reformation

How, then, did humanism relate to the Reformation? The honest answer is: as *preparation*, not as *identity*. Humanism gave the Reformation its indispensable tools — the original languages, the critical recovery of the biblical text, the method of *ad fontes* — and a generation of scholars trained to appeal to the sources against received tradition. There is real truth in the old saying that humanism "laid the egg that the Reformation hatched." The Reformers were, almost to a man, products of the humanist revival; they knew their Greek and Hebrew, they prized the recovered sources, they turned the humanist method upon the church with revolutionary effect.

But humanism and Reformation were not the same thing, and we must not collapse them. Humanism was, at its core, a program of *scholarly and moral* reform — the renewal of learning, the purification of texts, the call to a simpler and more ethical Christianity drawn from the sources. It did not, of itself, entail the doctrinal revolution of the Reformation — the recovery of justification by faith, the doctrine of grace, the willingness to break the unity of the church. Many of the greatest humanists, as we shall see in the case of Erasmus, embraced the call to reform learning and morals while recoiling utterly from Luther's theology and his schism. The humanist method could lead toward the Reformation, but it could also feed the Catholic Reformation, or remain a purely scholarly enterprise. The relationship is one of vital preparation: humanism made the Reformation possible, supplied its tools, and trained its leaders — but the spark that turned the new scholarship into a revolution of doctrine and a rupture of the church came from elsewhere. To see both the debt and the gulf, we turn next to the supreme humanist of the age, the man who gave the Reformation its Greek New Testament and then refused to follow where it led: Erasmus of Rotterdam.
