If the Renaissance humanists forged the tools of the Reformation, one man above all placed the sharpest of those tools in the Reformers' hands — and then watched in dismay as they used it to do what he had never intended. Desiderius Erasmus of Rotterdam was the most famous scholar in Europe, the prince of the Christian humanists, the embodiment of *ad fontes* applied to the Bible and the renewal of the church. His Greek New Testament gave Luther and Tyndale the text from which they would translate the Scriptures into the languages of the people. And yet Erasmus refused to follow Luther into schism, broke with him decisively over the deepest question of all, and died within the Roman church he had so brilliantly criticized. To understand both the Reformation's debt to humanism and the gulf that separated them, we must understand Erasmus — the man who, it was said, laid the egg that Luther hatched.

## The Prince of the Humanists

Erasmus, born around 1466, rose from obscure and difficult beginnings to become the most celebrated intellect of his age — a scholar whose learning, wit, and elegant Latin made him the acknowledged leader of the European republic of letters. His program was what he called the *philosophia Christi*, the "philosophy of Christ": a vision of Christianity stripped of its accumulated externals — the empty ceremonies, the superstitions, the dry hairsplitting of scholastic theology — and returned to the simple, heartfelt, ethical religion of the gospel and the early Fathers. He longed for a church renewed by learning and genuine piety, in which ordinary Christians would read the Scriptures and live the teachings of Christ.

To this end Erasmus wielded the two great weapons of the humanist: scholarship and satire. As a scholar, he edited the writings of the Church Fathers and labored over the text of Scripture. As a satirist, he was without equal. In works like *In Praise of Folly* and the *Colloquies*, he lampooned with devastating wit the corruption of the clergy, the hypocrisy and superstition of much monastic and popular religion, the worldliness of the higher church, and the arid pedantry of the theologians. His satire was enormously popular and did much to discredit the abuses of the late medieval church in the eyes of educated Europe. Erasmus prepared minds for reform; he made the corruption of the church a subject of laughter and contempt across the continent.

## The Greek New Testament

Erasmus's single most consequential achievement was a work of scholarship that changed history. In 1516 he published the first printed edition of the *Greek* New Testament — the *Novum Instrumentum* — presenting the New Testament in its original Greek, accompanied by his own fresh Latin translation and a body of scholarly annotations.[^1] For the first time, the New Testament in its original language was available in print to scholars across Europe, who could now read the apostolic writings in the words the apostles had actually used, and compare them directly to the Latin Vulgate that the church had relied upon for a thousand years.

The implications were profound, and some were immediately incendiary. Erasmus's Greek text and notes exposed places where the Vulgate had mistranslated or obscured the original — and some of these touched matters of doctrine and practice. To take the most famous example: where Jesus begins his preaching, the Vulgate read *paenitentiam agite*, "do penance," seeming to point toward the sacrament of penance with its priestly absolution and acts of satisfaction. But the Greek word, Erasmus showed, was *metanoeite* — "repent," or "be penitent," "change your mind" — an inward turning of the heart, not the performance of the sacramental system. A pillar of medieval practice, it appeared, rested partly on a translation the original did not support. Multiply such instances, and the effect was to drive scholars back to the Greek as the standard against which the church's Latin tradition must be measured. Erasmus's New Testament was the textual foundation on which Luther would build his German Bible and Tyndale his English one. The recovered Word, in the original tongue and in print, was now loose in the world.

## The Egg and the Bird

This is the origin of the famous saying that "Erasmus laid the egg that Luther hatched." Erasmus had supplied the Reformation with its indispensable scholarly tools — the Greek New Testament, the philological method, the recovered sources — and had softened the ground with his relentless satire of ecclesiastical abuse. When Luther's revolt erupted, contemporaries saw at once how much the monk of Wittenberg owed to the scholar of Rotterdam. Erasmus, the story goes, ruefully accepted the charge but added a pointed qualification: he had laid a hen's egg, he said, but Luther had hatched a very different bird — a fighting cock, a creature of battle and division quite unlike the gentle reform Erasmus had envisioned.

The quip captures a genuine truth. Erasmus and Luther shared a starting point — the recovery of Scripture, the critique of abuse, the call to a more authentic Christianity — but they diverged utterly on where it led. Erasmus wanted reform *within* the church, gradual, scholarly, peaceful, preserving the unity of Christendom and working through education and persuasion. He recoiled from Luther's vehemence, his dogmatic certainty, his willingness to shatter the church's unity, and the violence and division his movement unleashed. For several years Erasmus tried to stay above the conflict, refusing to condemn Luther outright while distancing himself from his methods, valuing peace and unity above all. But the age of confessional war would not permit neutrality forever, and eventually Erasmus was forced to choose his ground.

## The Clash Over the Will

When the break finally came, it came over the deepest question of all — the question that lay at the very heart of the Reformation. In 1524, under mounting pressure to declare himself against Luther, Erasmus wrote a treatise titled *On the Freedom of the Will (De libero arbitrio)*. In it he defended, with characteristic moderation, the traditional view that the human will retains a genuine freedom and capacity to cooperate with God's grace in salvation — that the sinner is not merely passive but plays a real, if grace-assisted, part in turning to God. It was the humane, the reasonable, the characteristically humanist position: surely, Erasmus argued, human striving and choice must count for something, and surely God would not command what we are utterly unable to perform.

Luther's response, published in 1525, was *On the Bondage of the Will (De servo arbitrio)* — a thunderous, uncompromising assault on Erasmus's whole position, and a work Luther regarded as among the best things he ever wrote. The fallen will, Luther insisted, is not free but *bound* — enslaved to sin, incapable of turning to God by its own power; salvation is entirely the work of God's sovereign grace, not at all the achievement of human cooperation. To make salvation depend even partly on the free human will, Luther charged, was to rob God of his glory and to leave the sinner without hope, for the sinner cannot save himself. We will examine this doctrine of the bondage of the will in its own right in a later lesson, for it stands at the foundation of the whole Reformed understanding of grace. Here the point is what the clash *meant*: it marked the definitive parting of the ways between humanism and the Reformation. On the question of the will, the genteel reform of Erasmus and the radical grace of Luther proved to be not variations on a theme but two different religions of salvation. The egg and the bird had finally flown apart.

## The Tragedy of the Middle

Erasmus spent his last years in an unhappy middle position, distrusted by both sides of a divided Christendom. To the Protestants, he was a coward and a half-reformer who had seen the truth, supplied its weapons, and then lacked the courage to follow it — who clung to a corrupt church rather than break with it. To the hardening Catholics of the Counter-Reformation, he was a dangerous figure whose scholarship and satire had prepared the way for Luther, the man whose egg had hatched the heresy; some of his works would later be placed on the Index of Forbidden Books. He remained, to the end, within the Roman church, but he never embraced the militant Counter-Reformation, and he died in 1536, in a sense homeless in the Christendom his work had helped to divide.

There is a real pathos in Erasmus's story, and also a real question that the rest of this course will, in its way, keep asking. Erasmus represented a *third way* that the age of confessional conflict crushed: reform without revolution, renewal without schism, scholarship and moderation and the patient improvement of the church from within. He believed the unity of the church was a precious thing not lightly to be broken, and that the legitimate grievances might be met without tearing Christendom apart. The Reformation, and the bloody century that followed, can be read as the vindication of Luther's conviction that the corruption ran too deep for gentle remedies — or as the vindication of Erasmus's fear that breaking the church would loose horrors worse than the disease. We need not settle that question here. But Erasmus stands as the great representative of the road not taken, and his fate is a reminder that the Reformation's gains, which this course celebrates, came at a cost that thoughtful and godly people at the time genuinely mourned.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The Reformed tradition has tended to judge Erasmus harshly — as the man who would not go far enough, who saw the abuses but missed the gospel, who provided the tools of reformation but refused its substance, and whose defense of the free will placed him, in the end, on the wrong side of the deepest question. There is force in this judgment: from the standpoint of the doctrine of grace, Erasmus's position on the will was not a minor disagreement but a fundamental error, and his unwillingness to break with a church teaching what the Reformers regarded as a false gospel can look like a failure of nerve or of insight.

But fairness requires that we hear the other side, and it is not weak. Erasmus's longing for the unity of the church, his horror at schism and at the violence it would unleash, his preference for reform through learning and persuasion rather than rupture and war — these were not contemptible, and the catastrophe of the religious wars gives them a certain melancholy vindication. One can argue that Erasmus saw something the Reformers, in their righteous zeal, did not: that the cure of division might prove worse than the disease of corruption, and that a church reformed at the price of a shattered and bleeding Christendom was a tragic bargain. Whether the Reformation was worth its cost is, as we saw in this course's very first lesson, exactly the question on which recovery, revolution, and tragedy divide — and Erasmus is the age's most eloquent witness for the prosecution. The course will continue to honor the recovery while refusing to pretend the cost was nothing; and in that refusal, it keeps faith with the genuine wisdom in Erasmus's grief. With the tools forged and the egg laid, all the elements were now in place. In the next lesson we draw them together and survey a Europe poised, at last, on the very eve of the storm.
