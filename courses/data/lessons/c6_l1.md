This course opens not with origins, texts, or history, but with a collision in the present — because the collision is the stake that makes everything else matter. Each lesson in this first unit sets a specific claim of classical or modern Islamic law, stated in the tradition's own authoritative voice, against a specific feature of the American constitutional order. We begin with the deepest of these collisions, the one that frames all the others: the question of *sovereignty* — of who, in the final analysis, holds the right to make law. The Islamist doctrine of *hakimiyya* answers: God alone. The American Constitution answers: "We the People." These are not two policies in tension. They are two rival accounts of where ultimate political authority resides, and they cannot both be foundational at once.

Before proceeding, a word on method that governs this entire course. The argument here is about a *doctrine* and its *theorists* — about texts, claims, and the states that enact them — never about the inner beliefs or character of the world's many millions of Muslims, the overwhelming majority of whom live under, accept, and participate in man-made legal systems. The distinction between critiquing an idea and disparaging a people is absolute, and it will be maintained throughout. With that established, we turn to the idea.

## What Hakimiyya Claims

*Hakimiyya* (from the Arabic root *h-k-m*, "to rule, to judge") is the doctrine of God's exclusive sovereignty — the claim that the right to rule and, crucially, the right to *legislate* belong to God alone. In its developed modern form, the doctrine holds that God is the sole *Lawgiver* (*al-Hakim*), that his revealed law is the only legitimate law, and that for human beings to arrogate to themselves the authority to make law — to declare what shall be permitted and forbidden — is to usurp a prerogative that belongs to God and to him only.

The doctrine draws on Qur'anic language that its proponents read as decisive. Verses such as "the decision (*hukm*) is for none but God" (a phrase appearing in several forms, as at 12:40 and 6:57) are taken to establish that legislative authority is a divine monopoly. Especially load-bearing is a sequence in the fifth sura declaring that "whoever does not judge by what God has revealed" is among the unbelievers, the wrongdoers, the rebellious (5:44, 5:45, 5:47). Read through the lens of hakimiyya, these verses do not merely commend God's law; they delegitimize any rival source of law and attach the gravest possible verdict — unbelief — to the act of governing by humanly devised legislation.

## Mawdudi and Qutb

While the raw materials are ancient, hakimiyya as a developed *political* doctrine is largely a twentieth-century construction, the work of two enormously influential Islamist thinkers. The first was Abul A'la Mawdudi, the Indian-Pakistani founder of the Jamaat-e-Islami, who systematized the claim that sovereignty (in his vocabulary, a kind of divine kingship) belongs exclusively to God, and that the proper Islamic order is one in which no human legislature may contradict or supplement the divine law. Mawdudi cast the choice as one between the sovereignty of God and the sovereignty of man, and he regarded the modern doctrine of popular sovereignty as a direct rival to God's rightful rule.

The second and more incendiary figure was the Egyptian Sayyid Qutb, the foremost ideologue of the radical wing of the Muslim Brotherhood, whose short book *Milestones* (*Ma'alim fi al-Tariq*, 1964) became the seminal text of modern revolutionary Islamism.[^1] Qutb took hakimiyya to its sharpest conclusion. Any society in which sovereignty is exercised by human beings rather than submitted wholly to God's law, he argued, has reverted to *jahiliyya* — the "ignorance" of the pre-Islamic pagan age — regardless of whether it calls itself Muslim. To legislate apart from God's revealed law was, for Qutb, not a mere political error but a usurpation of God's defining attribute, and thus a form of unbelief. On this reading, the entire framework of human self-government stands condemned not as suboptimal but as illegitimate at the root.

## The Collision with "We the People"

Set this doctrine beside the opening words of the United States Constitution — "We the People of the United States... do ordain and establish this Constitution" — and the collision is immediate and total. The American constitutional order rests on the principle of *popular sovereignty*: the conviction that legitimate political authority arises from the consent of the governed, that the people are the source of the government's just powers, and that law is made by representatives accountable to the people. "Government by consent" is not one feature of the American order among many; it is its founding premise, the answer it gives to the most basic question of politics — who may make law.

Hakimiyya, read straight, denies that premise as a category. If the right to legislate belongs to God alone, then a people cannot be the source of binding law; popular legislation is not merely unwise but illegitimate in principle, an encroachment on a divine monopoly. This is what makes the clash so fundamental. It is not a disagreement about a particular statute, or about the proper balance between religious values and public policy — disagreements that any pluralist society can absorb. It is a disagreement about *who is entitled to make law at all*. One order locates ultimate legislative authority in the people; the other locates it in God's revealed law and treats the people's claim to it as usurpation. There is no obvious middle ground between "the people are sovereign" and "the people may not legislate," and that is precisely why this clash is placed first: every later collision in this unit — over apostasy, blasphemy, the status of non-Muslims — is downstream of this one disagreement about the source of law.

## What the Classical Tradition Actually Practiced

It sharpens the analysis to see how the premodern Islamic world actually handled the making of law, because the gap between the hakimiyya *theory* and classical *practice* is itself revealing. In classical thought, a crucial distinction was drawn — one often blurred in modern Islamist rhetoric — between *sharia* and *fiqh*. *Sharia* refers to God's law in itself, the divine ideal; *fiqh* refers to the vast body of *human juristic interpretation and elaboration* of that law, produced by generations of scholars who acknowledged that they were reasoning, disagreeing, and erring. The very existence of four distinct Sunni schools of law, differing on countless questions, was an open admission that the *application* of God's law required extensive human judgment. The divine law was held to be perfect; the human understanding of it was known to be plural and fallible.

Beyond *fiqh*, classical Islamic governance recognized an even more frankly human domain of law-making called *siyasa shariyya* — "governance in accordance with the sharia." Rulers across the centuries issued ordinances, regulations, and administrative laws (the Ottoman *qanun* is the great example) covering the enormous range of matters the revealed texts did not directly address: taxation, public order, commerce, military organization, and much more. Classical jurists generally accepted this as legitimate and necessary, recognizing that a functioning state simply must legislate in the spaces revelation leaves open. In practice, then, premodern Islamic polities were never governed by divine law alone with no human legislation; they combined a revealed framework with a large and acknowledged sphere of human-made law.

This matters because it shows that the strong hakimiyya claim — that human legislation *as such* is usurpation and unbelief — is not even an accurate description of how the classical tradition itself operated, let alone a unanimous doctrine. Mawdudi and Qutb did not simply restate the classical view; they radicalized it, collapsing the old distinctions and treating the modern legislative state as a totalizing rival to God in a way the classical jurists, who legislated freely within their framework, did not. The doctrine examined in this lesson is real and consequential — but it is a modern construction standing in tension with the tradition's own long practice, which is part of why so many Muslim scholars reject it.

## The Counter-Case: A Modern Doctrine, Not a Consensus

Intellectual honesty requires stating, with full force, the strongest case against treating hakimiyya as *the* Islamic position — because in important respects it is not. Several considerations sharply qualify the picture just drawn.

First, hakimiyya in its developed political form is a *modern* doctrine, not the settled consensus of fifteen centuries of Islamic thought. The systematic theory that man-made legislation as such constitutes unbelief is largely the construction of Mawdudi and Qutb in the twentieth century, responding to colonialism, secular nationalism, and the collapse of the caliphate. Many Muslim scholars regard this politicized reading as an *innovation* — a narrowing and radicalizing of older material — and reject it outright. Classical Islamic governance, for all its theory of the sharia, in practice always involved a substantial domain of human-made administrative law (*siyasa*), recognizing that rulers must legislate in the vast areas the revealed texts do not directly address.

Second, there is a developed body of Islamic political thought that holds God's sovereignty and meaningful popular self-government to be *compatible*, by distinguishing the levels at which each operates. On this view, affirming that God is the ultimate moral and legal authority need not mean that humans may not legislate at all; it can mean that human legislation operates within a framework of higher principles — much as a constitutional order places some matters beyond the reach of ordinary majorities. Proponents of "Islamic democracy" appeal to the Qur'anic principle of *shura* (consultation) and to the community's long-recognized role in choosing and constraining its rulers, arguing that the people may indeed govern themselves under God rather than in defiance of him. Whatever one makes of these arguments, they show that the hakimiyya reading is contested *from within* the tradition, not merely from outside it.

Third, and decisively for the doctrine/people distinction: the overwhelming majority of the world's Muslims live in states with constitutions, legislatures, and elections, and participate in them without evident sense that they are committing unbelief. The doctrine this lesson examines is the doctrine of particular theorists and movements and the states that have embraced it — not a description of how most Muslims actually understand their political obligations.

## What Survives

When the qualifications are fully granted, what remains is narrower than the maximalist version but still serious, and it is the form this course will defend. The claim is not that "Islam rejects democracy" — a sweeping assertion that founders against Muslim democracies and Muslim democrats. The claim is that *there exists within the Islamic tradition a powerful and textually grounded doctrine of divine legislative sovereignty, articulated most sharply by Mawdudi and Qutb but drawing on classical materials, which when read straight denies the legitimacy of popular sovereignty as such* — and that this doctrine, where it is embraced, collides at the foundations with the constitutional premise of government by consent. That this reading is contested within Islam does not dissolve the collision; it locates it. The fault line runs not between Islam and the West in some monolithic sense, but between two answers to the question of who may make law — answers that, where the hakimiyya answer prevails, cannot be reconciled with the American order's first premise.

This sets the pattern for the unit. In each lesson, a doctrine stated in the tradition's authoritative voice meets a specific constitutional guarantee, and we ask, precisely and fairly, whether they can both stand. Having begun with the deepest collision — over the source of law itself — we turn next to one of its sharpest consequences: what the classical law prescribes for the person who, having been a Muslim, chooses to leave. That is the question of apostasy, and the guarantee it meets is the liberty of conscience.
