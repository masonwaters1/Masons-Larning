This course opens not with the origins of Islam, nor with its texts, but with a collision in the present — the place where a strand of Islamic political thought meets the foundational premise of the American constitutional order and finds it impossible to accept. We begin here, with the question of *sovereignty*, because it is the deepest of all the incompatibilities this course will examine, and because it frames every clash that follows. The American order rests on a single revolutionary idea: that legitimate political authority arises from the consent of the governed — "We the People." A powerful current of modern Islamic political theology rests on the opposite premise: that sovereignty and the right to make law belong to God alone, and that any human assembly which claims that right has usurped what is God's. These two convictions cannot both be foundational to the same political order. Understanding why is the work of this lesson.

A word at the outset about what this course claims and what it does not. The argument here concerns *doctrine* — a body of political-theological ideas, their texts, and the movements that hold them — not the beliefs of the world's roughly 1.8 billion Muslims, the overwhelming majority of whom live peaceably under constitutions of every kind and have never embraced the integralist thesis we are about to examine. The doctrine of divine sovereignty in its hard political form is a particular and contested position with identifiable authors. It is also a serious and influential one, and it deserves to be understood accurately, on its own terms and in its own words.

## What Hakimiyya Claims

The Arabic term at the center of this lesson is *hakimiyya*, usually rendered as "sovereignty" or "the right to rule and to legislate." In its strong, modern, political form, the doctrine of hakimiyya holds that this right belongs to God alone — that the authority to command, to forbid, and above all to *legislate* is an exclusively divine prerogative. On this view, God is not merely the ultimate moral authority in some abstract sense; he is the sole lawgiver, and his revealed law (the sharia) is the only legitimate law. Any human legislature that presumes to enact laws of its own — to declare, by its own authority, what shall be permitted and forbidden — is therefore claiming a right that belongs to God, and is in that act usurping divine sovereignty.

It is important to feel the force of this claim, because in a Western frame it can sound like ordinary piety. Many religious traditions affirm that God is sovereign over all things; few draw from that affirmation the conclusion that human legislation is, as such, illegitimate. The hard doctrine of hakimiyya draws exactly that conclusion. It treats the act of human lawmaking — the very thing a parliament or a congress exists to do — not as a neutral administrative function but as a potential act of rebellion against God, a claim to an authority that is his alone. This is what makes it not a policy disagreement but a clash at the level of first principles.

## Mawdudi and the Sovereignty of God

The systematic development of hakimiyya as a modern political doctrine is associated above all with two twentieth-century thinkers, the first of whom is the Indo-Pakistani scholar and ideologue Abul A'la Mawdudi. Mawdudi built his political theology around the absolute sovereignty of God, arguing that the central confession of Islam — that there is no god but God — carries a directly political meaning: it denies that any authority, including the authority of the people or the state, may stand alongside or above God's. To attribute sovereignty to the people, Mawdudi argued, is to set up a rival to God, a kind of idolatry in the political sphere.

From this premise Mawdudi derived a vision of the Islamic state as a "theo-democracy" in which God, not the people, is sovereign, and in which human rulers and assemblies function not as originators of law but as God's agents, administering a law they did not make and may not contradict. The people might choose their administrators; they might deliberate about how to apply the divine law to new circumstances; but they could never *legislate* in the full sense — never claim the authority to determine, by their own will, what is ultimately right and wrong, lawful and forbidden. That authority was God's, exercised through his revealed law. Mawdudi's formulation gave the sovereignty of God a sharp, modern, anti-democratic edge it had not previously carried in quite this form, and it set the terms for what followed.

## Qutb, Jahiliyya, and the Radical Turn

The doctrine reached its most radical and influential expression in the work of the Egyptian writer Sayyid Qutb, whose short book *Milestones* became, more than any other text, the seminary of modern revolutionary Islamism. Qutb took Mawdudi's concept of divine sovereignty and fused it with a second, explosive idea: *jahiliyya*.[^1]

*Jahiliyya* traditionally referred to the "age of ignorance" — the pagan condition of Arabia before the coming of Islam. Qutb radicalized the term. Any society, he argued, that does not submit wholly to the sovereignty of God — that locates the authority to legislate in human hands, whether in a king, a parliament, or "the people" — is not truly Islamic at all but has reverted to *jahiliyya*, to a new paganism, however much it may call itself Muslim. On this reckoning, nearly every government in the modern world, including the nominally Muslim governments of his own day, stood condemned as *jahili* — as regimes in rebellion against God's sole right to rule. Qutb's logic was uncompromising: to legislate is to claim hakimiyya; to claim hakimiyya is to usurp what belongs to God alone; and a society organized on that usurpation is not a flawed Islamic society but a pagan one to be opposed. Read straight, this is not a critique of particular laws but a rejection of the very category of human legislation, and therefore of popular sovereignty as such.

## "We the People"

Set against this stands the founding premise of the American constitutional order, announced in the first three words of the Constitution: "We the People." The American order locates ultimate political authority in the people — in their consent, expressed through representative institutions that make law by the people's delegated authority. This is the doctrine of *popular sovereignty*: the conviction that legitimate government derives its just powers from the consent of the governed, that law is the product of the people's own deliberation and choice, and that no law binds except as it issues, ultimately, from this consenting source. The Declaration of Independence stated the principle; the Constitution embodied it; and the whole edifice of American self-government rests upon it.

The collision with hard hakimiyya is now exact and total. The American order says the people may make law; hakimiyya says only God may make law. The American order treats popular legislation as the very fountain of legitimate authority; hakimiyya treats it as a usurpation of divine authority. This is not a dispute about *which* laws should be passed — a dispute every political order contains and manages. It is a dispute about *who may make law at all*, and on that question the two systems give opposite and irreconcilable answers. One cannot simultaneously hold that ultimate legislative authority belongs to "We the People" and that it belongs to God alone. The premises exclude each other. This is why sovereignty is placed first: every other clash in this course — over apostasy, blasphemy, the status of non-Muslims, the reach of the sacred law — is, at bottom, a downstream consequence of this single disagreement about where the authority to make law resides.

## The Counter-Case: How Much of Islam Is This?

Intellectual honesty requires immediately pressing a hard question against the thesis just stated, the question this course returns to at the end of every unit: *how much of Islam does this doctrine actually represent?* For the strong, anti-democratic doctrine of hakimiyya is not the timeless and universal teaching of Islam. It is, to a significant degree, a *twentieth-century construction*, the work of particular modern thinkers — Mawdudi and Qutb above all — responding to the specific traumas of colonialism, secular nationalism, and the collapse of the caliphate. The very prominence of *hakimiyya* as a political slogan is a modern development, and serious scholars argue that Qutb and Mawdudi gave the concept a political sharpness it did not classically possess.

The classical Islamic tradition, in fact, had a more complex and flexible relationship between divine law and human governance than the hard hakimiyya doctrine allows. Alongside the sharia, classical Islamic statecraft recognized a wide domain of *siyasa* — the legitimate administrative and discretionary authority of the ruler to govern, regulate, and address matters the sacred law left open. Pre-modern Muslim societies were not, in practice, governed by a simple equation of all law with revelation; rulers issued administrative regulations, customary law operated, and jurists accommodated a great deal of human governance without regarding it as rebellion against God. The notion that *any* human legislation is *ipso facto* a usurpation of divine sovereignty would have struck many classical jurists as foreign.

Still more important is the contemporary reality. The overwhelming majority of the world's Muslims live, and by every evidence are content to live, under constitutions that vest authority in the people and the state — in Indonesia, the world's largest Muslim-majority country and a functioning democracy; in Turkey, Senegal, Bangladesh, and many others. A vast body of modern Muslim political thought explicitly argues that the sovereignty of God, rightly understood, is *compatible* with democratic self-government — that affirming God as the ultimate moral authority need not entail denying the people's authority to legislate within the bounds of justice, and that consultation (*shura*) and consent have deep roots in the tradition. These thinkers regard the hard hakimiyya doctrine not as the authentic voice of Islam but as a modern distortion of it.

## Holding the Claim at Its Defensible Width

What, then, survives — and what must be set aside? Two overstatements must be refused. We should not say that "Islam rejects democracy," for that flattens a vast and internally diverse tradition and is contradicted by hundreds of millions of Muslims who practice democratic self-government and by the scholars who defend it from within the faith. Nor should we say that hakimiyya is the secret true belief of Muslims generally; it is the explicit doctrine of a particular, identifiable, modern current, and most Muslims do not hold it.

What does survive is narrower, and it is enough to make the lesson's point. *Islam contains a textual and intellectual tradition — given its most systematic modern form by Mawdudi and Qutb, and resting on the genuine theological materials of divine sovereignty — from which a thoroughgoing rejection of popular sovereignty can be, and has been, derived; and the movements that draw this conclusion can cite real sources and a coherent logic, not a distortion that mainstream institutions have found easy to refute on purely textual grounds.* This is the defensible claim: not that Islam *is* anti-democratic, but that it contains an authoritative current that *theorizes* the incompatibility of human legislation with divine sovereignty, and that this current is intellectually serious, textually grounded, and politically consequential. Where it is held and acted upon, it collides at the foundations with the premise of "We the People." That collision — real, principled, and irreducible to any quarrel over particular policies — is the frame within which every remaining lesson of this unit unfolds. We turn next to its first and sharpest application: the fate, under the classical law, of the one who would leave the faith.
