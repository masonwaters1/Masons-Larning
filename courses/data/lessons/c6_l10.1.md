The previous lesson closed Unit 1 with its most important corrective: classical fiqh is not what 1.8 billion people believe, and sliding from "the law books contain this ruling" to "this is what Muslims want" is the unit's cardinal error. But that corrective raises an empirical question that deserves its own answer rather than an assumption in either direction: what do Muslims in Western countries actually believe, where their attitudes touch the points of clash this unit has mapped? There is survey data on this — a lot of it — and this appendix examines the major exhibits with the same discipline applied everywhere else in the course. No genre of evidence is more abused than the opinion poll: the hostile reading cherry-picks the worst numbers and presents contested methodology as settled fact; the defensive reading dismisses every unwelcome finding as Islamophobic pseudo-science while embracing the favorable ones from the same instruments. The task here is to state what the surveys show, what their critics say, and what survives both.

## The Six-Country Study

The largest rigorous European dataset comes from the Six Country Immigrant Integration Comparative Survey, roughly nine thousand interviews conducted across Germany, France, the Netherlands, Belgium, Austria, and Sweden among respondents of Turkish and Moroccan background, analyzed by the sociologist Ruud Koopmans of the WZB Berlin Social Science Center and published in 2013.[^1] Its headline findings were stark. About two-thirds of the Muslims interviewed said that religious rules were more important to them than the laws of the country in which they live. Roughly three-quarters held that there is only one legitimate interpretation of the Qur'an, binding on all believers. Koopmans measured "fundamentalism" as the combination of three attitudes — return to eternal unchangeable rules, a single binding interpretation, and the priority of religious over secular law — and found consistent agreement with all three among a large share of Muslim respondents, reported in the study's presentations at between roughly a third and forty-four percent. The comparison group of native European Christians answered very differently: about thirteen percent put religious rules above national law. And the study found the fundamentalist cluster strongly correlated with out-group hostility: nearly sixty percent of Muslim respondents said they would not want homosexuals as friends, forty-five percent said Jews cannot be trusted, and a similar share believed the West is out to destroy Islam.

## The British and French Exhibits

The most detailed British data comes from the ICM survey commissioned by Channel 4 in 2015–16 — over a thousand face-to-face, in-home interviews with British Muslims, with a national control sample. Its findings ran in two directions at once, and both must be stated. On belonging, British Muslims scored higher than the general population: eighty-six percent felt a strong sense of belonging to Britain (against eighty-three percent nationally), and eighty-eight percent said Britain is a good place to be a Muslim. On liberal values, a chasm opened. Fifty-two percent said homosexuality should be illegal, against five percent of the general public; only eighteen percent said it should be legal. Forty-seven percent disagreed that a homosexual person should be allowed to teach in a school. Twenty-three percent supported the introduction of sharia in areas of Britain; thirty-nine percent agreed that wives should always obey their husbands; and around four in ten endorsed antisemitic conspiracy statements about Jewish power in business or media. On violence, the overwhelming majority condemned it, but four percent expressed some sympathy for those who take part in suicide bombing, and eighteen percent sympathized to some degree with violence against those who mock the Prophet.

The French data is more recent and, on its face, more dramatic, because it captures a trend over time. A major Ifop study published in late 2025, surveying about a thousand self-identified Muslims drawn from a national sample of more than fourteen thousand, found that forty-four percent of French Muslims overall — and fifty-seven percent of those aged fifteen to twenty-four — consider the rules of Islam more important than the laws of the Republic, in domains such as marriage, inheritance, and ritual practice. The youth figure represents a steep generational rise: comparable polling in the late 1990s put the equivalent number around thirty-six percent. The same study reported that forty-six percent of respondents said Islamic law should be applied in non-Muslim countries (fifty-nine percent among the under-25s), that thirty-eight percent endorsed all or part of a battery of positions Ifop classified as "Islamist" (double the 1998 figure), and that markers of strict observance among the young — mosque attendance, Ramadan fasting, veiling among young women — have risen sharply over three decades. The study's own director described it as contradicting the assumption of gradual secularization: in France, the youngest generation is measurably more, not less, committed to the priority of religious law than its parents.

## The American Counter-Exhibit

Any honest presentation must now turn the telescope around, because the best American data tells a substantially different story, and it comes from the most reputable instrument in the field. The Pew Research Center's 2017 survey of U.S. Muslims — the third in a series begun in 2007 — found a population overwhelmingly integrated in identity and rapidly liberalizing in attitude. Ninety-two percent said they were proud to be American, and about nine in ten were proud to be both American and Muslim. Sixty-four percent said there is more than one true way to interpret the teachings of Islam — the inverse of the European single-interpretation finding. More than three-quarters said violence against civilians can never be justified for a religious or political cause, a higher rate of categorical rejection than the American general public itself. And on the very issue where the British numbers were most illiberal, the American trend line bent the other way: fifty-two percent of U.S. Muslims in 2017 said homosexuality should be accepted by society, nearly double the twenty-seven percent of 2007, and a higher acceptance rate than American white evangelical Protestants. Whatever explains the transatlantic difference — selective high-skill immigration, the American model of religious integration, the absence of ghettoized housing patterns, generational composition — the American data refutes, on its own, any claim that Muslim populations as such cannot or do not converge toward liberal norms. Convergence has happened, is measured, and is accelerating, in the United States.

## The Methodology Wars

Each of the European exhibits is contested, and the criticisms are part of the record. The ICM poll sampled only in areas at least twenty percent Muslim — a budget-driven necessity for face-to-face interviewing of a low-incidence population, but one that skewed the sample toward poorer, more conservative, more segregated communities, as the Muslim Council of Britain and others objected; ICM and independent pollsters replied that about half of British Muslims live in such areas and that the survey remained the best available, while conceding the compromise. The 2025 Ifop study was attacked by French Muslim bodies on the grounds that the word "sharia," headlined everywhere, never appeared in the questionnaire — the instrument asked about "Muslim law" and specific domains — and that some findings strain plausibility against physical facts like mosque capacity. The WZB study drew academic criticism over whether its three-item battery measured "fundamentalism" or merely conventional piety, and whether Turkish and Moroccan communities can stand in for European Muslims generally. And a caution from this course's own earlier lesson applies across the board: respondents who affirm the priority of "religious rules" or "Muslim law" are frequently thinking of prayer, fasting, halal food, and family ethics, not stoning and blasphemy statutes; the survey question rarely distinguishes the devotional from the political meaning, and the slide between them is where most abuse of these numbers occurs.

## The Honest Counterpoint

So what survives? The dismissive reading — that the European findings are all methodological artifact — does not survive, because the same signal appears across different instruments, countries, decades, and survey houses: large minorities of Western European Muslims, and in the French data a majority of the young, affirm the priority of religious law over national law; majorities or near-majorities in the British data hold illiberal positions on homosexuality that place them far from the host society; and the French trend runs generationally upward, against the secularization assumption. Those are real, replicated, documented findings, and they land precisely on the clash points this unit has mapped — law above the constitution, and the liberties of Lesson 6. But the hostile reading does not survive either, and its refutation comes from the same data: the same British respondents report deeper belonging to Britain than the general public; only one percent of them want fully separate sharia enclaves; violence is overwhelmingly condemned everywhere; the American Muslim population has liberalized faster than American evangelicals; and every headline number sits atop genuine methodological dispute. The exact claim, stated with the whole record in view, is this: survey evidence documents substantial and persistent attitudinal gaps between Western European Muslim populations and their host societies on exactly the doctrinal points where classical Islam and the liberal state conflict, gaps that in France are widening by generation — and the same evidence documents strong civic belonging, near-universal rejection of violence, sharp liberalization in America, and enough methodological contestation to forbid treating any single number as settled. Both halves are the data. A course that quoted only one half would forfeit the credibility this one is built on.
