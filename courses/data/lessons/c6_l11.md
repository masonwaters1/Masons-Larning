Unit 1 examined the content of classical Islamic law; this unit examines something prior and more fundamental — the evidentiary foundation on which the entire Islamic account of its own origins rests. The question sounds academic and is anything but: everything Islam claims about itself runs through a historical narrative — a man in Mecca receiving revelation, a community in Medina, a preserved text, a remembered practice — and that narrative carries the full weight of the law, the theology, and the exemplar doctrine built upon it. This unit asks whether the documentary record can bear that weight. It begins with the single most startling fact in the study of Islamic origins, one that most believers and most Westerners alike have never confronted: the profound thinness of the evidence from the period itself. For the founding decades of one of the largest movements in human history, the contemporary documentary record is very close to empty — a black hole at the center of the tradition, around which everything else orbits.

## The Shape of the Problem

State the problem in its simplest form: Muhammad is traditionally dated from 570 to 632. The earliest surviving biography of him was written by Ibn Ishaq around 130 to 150 years after his death — and even that work does not survive; we possess it only through the edited recension of Ibn Hisham, who died in 833, two full centuries removed, and who tells us candidly that he omitted from his source material things "which would distress certain people."[^1] The great narrative histories are later still: al-Waqidi died in 823, Ibn Sa'd in 845, al-Tabari in 923. The canonical hadith collections, the foundation of law and of the Prophet's biography in detail, were compiled by Bukhari (d. 870) and Muslim (d. 875) — roughly two and a half centuries after the events they report, working entirely from chains of oral transmission. To feel the scale of this: it is as if the earliest surviving life of George Washington had been written in the 1930s, edited by a man writing in the 1980s who admits to removing embarrassing material, with the detailed record of Washington's sayings first sorted and certified in the twenty-first century from word-of-mouth chains. No serious historian would treat such a corpus as straightforward history. For Islamic origins, this corpus is not merely the best evidence; for the life of Muhammad in any detail, it is effectively the only evidence.

## What the Seventh Century Actually Left

The natural response is to ask what survives from the seventh century itself — and the answer is the heart of this lesson. From the traditional lifetime of Muhammad: nothing. No document, no inscription, no coin, no text names him during the years he is said to have lived. The famous letters the tradition says he sent to the emperors of Byzantium and Persia survive only as later literary reports and purported relics of no established authenticity. The first datable appearances of the name come decades after his death and from outside the community: a Syriac chronicle fragment from the 630s-640s referring to an Arab prophet, the Armenian chronicle attributed to Sebeos in the 660s describing a merchant-preacher among the Ishmaelites, scattered Christian notices of the conquests. From within the Arab empire, the earliest hard evidence is numismatic and epigraphic, and it arrives strikingly late: the coins of the first decades of the conquest carry Byzantine and Sasanian imagery, and it is only under the caliph Abd al-Malik in the 690s — sixty years after Muhammad's death — that the name Muhammad appears on coinage and, monumentally, in the mosaic inscriptions of the Dome of the Rock, built 691-692. Those inscriptions are the earliest securely dated, physically surviving Islamic theological statements in existence — and it is worth noting what they are: not neutral records but polemical proclamations against Christian doctrine, insisting that God has no son. Between the traditional death date and the first substantial physical attestation stretches roughly two generations of near-silence, filled only retrospectively, by literature written under empires with theological and political stakes in how the story was told.

## The Rules of Evidence

Why does the gap matter so much? Because of what historians know happens to oral tradition, and to origin-narratives, in exactly such conditions. Two generations is ample time for legend to form; the comparative study of oral transmission shows stories reshaped within decades, not centuries, and reshaped most powerfully around founding figures, whose lives attract the community's later needs the way a magnet attracts filings. And the literature that eventually filled the Islamic gap was not composed by disinterested archivists. It was composed under the Umayyad and Abbasid caliphates — states whose legitimacy, factional quarrels, legal debates, and dynastic propaganda all ran through claims about what the Prophet said and did. Every party in every early Islamic dispute — Umayyad and Abbasid, Sunni and Shia and Kharijite, one law school against another — had reasons to remember the origins in its own favor, and the surviving literature bears the marks of all of them. This is why the founding insight of modern Islamic-origins scholarship, stated by scholars from Ignaz Goldziher onward and radicalized in the twentieth century, is that the traditional literature is primarily evidence for what eighth- and ninth-century Muslims believed, needed, and fought about — and only secondarily, if carefully sifted, evidence for the seventh century it describes. The sources are a mirror of the community that wrote them before they are a window onto the man they describe.

## What This Does and Does Not Establish

The discipline of precision matters here as much as anywhere in this course, because the black hole is routinely overstated into a claim it cannot support. The documentary gap does not establish that Muhammad never existed; the mythicist thesis is a fringe position, and rightly so — the external notices, the trajectory of the conquests, the Qur'an itself as an early text, and the sheer improbability of the alternative make the existence of an Arabian prophet-founder the overwhelmingly best explanation of the evidence. Nor does the gap establish that everything in the tradition is false; late sources can preserve early material, and some demonstrably do. What the gap establishes is narrower and, for this course's purposes, more important: that the detailed biography — the specific episodes, the dated campaigns, the recorded conversations, the marriages and rulings and sayings on which law and exemplarity rest — cannot be independently verified and comes to us exclusively through a literature written by later parties with stakes in its content. The confident traditional narrative, in which the community simply remembered everything accurately across two centuries of empire-building and civil war, asks the historian to believe in a transmission process unlike any other known to history. The honest position is not "we know it was otherwise" but "we cannot know it was so" — and for a tradition whose law and exemplar doctrine require that it was so, in detail, that uncertainty is not a small concession. It is a structural problem, and the next three lessons trace it into the biography, the text, and the hadith in turn.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The counterpoints are genuine and set the boundaries of the claim. First, the comparative defense: the documentary record for most ancient figures is thin, and the gap between Jesus and the Gospels (forty to seventy years) or between Socrates and our sources is routinely bridged by historians using critical method — lateness alone does not void a literature, it demands sifting, and Islamic sources can be sifted like any other. Second, the seventh-century record is not quite empty of Islam even if it is nearly empty of biography: the external chronicle notices arrive impressively early, the documentary papyri of the conquest administration begin in the 640s, and recent radiocarbon datings of early Qur'an manuscripts — Birmingham, Sana'a — suggest a text substantially earlier than the revisionists of the 1970s supposed, a point the text lesson will weigh in full. Third, the tradition was not a rumor mill but a self-conscious transmission culture: the isnad system, whatever its flaws, represents an attempt at source-criticism without parallel in ancient literature, and the tradition's own scholars rejected hundreds of thousands of reports as fabricated — evidence of a corpus under discipline as well as under pressure. Fourth, oral cultures can transmit some kinds of material — poetry, formulae, a scripture publicly recited — with remarkable fidelity, and the Qur'an's transmission may belong to that category even if the biographical anecdote does not. All of this is right, and it is why the strongest revisionist claims have retreated, as the unit's closing lesson will document. What survives the counterpoints is the exact claim: for the founding period itself the record is nearly silent; the detailed biography rests wholly on literature one and a half to three centuries removed, shaped by the needs of later empires and factions; and therefore the tradition's confident, detailed account of its own origins is a matter of faith in that literature, not of independently establishable history. Everything this course examines — the law of Unit 1, the biography of Unit 4, the doctrines still to come — stands on that foundation, and the reader should know exactly how much weight it can bear before anything else is placed upon it.
