The previous lesson established the gap: the earliest surviving life of Muhammad stands a century and a half to two centuries from its subject. This lesson examines what fills the gap — the sira literature itself — and asks the question that modern scholarship has pressed with increasing force: what kind of book is it? The traditional answer treats the sira as biography in the ordinary sense, a record of remembered events, perhaps embellished at the edges but sound at the core. The critical answer, developed across a century of scholarship, is that the sira is better understood as hagiography and theology in narrative form — a literature whose episodes were generated, in significant part, not by memory of events but by the needs of the later community: to explain obscure verses of the Qur'an, to glorify the founder in the idiom of ancient holy men, to settle legal and political arguments by projecting them backward into the founding age. The distinction is not academic. If the sira is memory, Unit 4's biography is history. If the sira is theology, it is doctrine — which, as the counter-scholarship lessons have argued, changes the register of every claim built on it, the tradition's and the critic's alike.

## Exegesis Wearing the Mask of Memory

The most powerful single argument for the critical reading concerns the relationship between the sira and the Qur'an, and it runs opposite to the direction a believer would expect.[^1] The Qur'an is a strangely allusive book: it refers constantly to events without narrating them — a battle here, a domestic crisis there, a change of prayer-direction, an expedition, an accusation — addressing an audience presumed already to know the story. The sira supplies the stories. On the traditional view, this fit demonstrates the sira's reliability: the biography preserves the events the scripture alludes to. But scholars from the mid-twentieth century onward — most incisively John Wansbrough and his students — noticed that the dependence often runs the other way. Large tracts of the sira read as narrative exegesis: stories built after the fact to give obscure verses a setting, a cause, an occasion. The telltale signs recur throughout. Multiple, mutually incompatible stories survive to explain the same verse — the classic mark of exegetical invention, since a real remembered event would anchor one account, not five rivals. Episodes are constructed transparently around a verse's vocabulary, the narrative supplying characters and incidents that exist only to make the words land. And the genre the tradition itself called "occasions of revelation" (asbab al-nuzul) institutionalized the procedure: for verse after verse, later scholars recorded competing traditions about the incident that prompted it, disagreeing about the most basic facts. Where the biography exists to explain the scripture, the biography is not an independent witness to the scripture's world; it is commentary in costume. This does not condemn every episode — some allusions may preserve real settings — but it dissolves the assumption that the sira's fit with the Qur'an certifies its memory. The fit is, in large part, the whole reason the stories were composed.

## The Founder in the Idiom of Legend

A second line of argument examines the sira's literary texture, and finds it saturated with the conventions of late-antique hagiography — the standard narrative kit by which the seventh and eighth centuries wrote the lives of holy men. The annunciations and portents before birth; the light shining from the father's brow; the miraculous protection of the orphan; the recognition of the future prophet by a Christian monk who reads the seal of prophethood between his shoulders; the opening of the breast and washing of the heart by angels; the night journey to heaven — these are not the incidental details of the sira but its formative episodes, and each has abundant parallels in the saints' lives, infancy gospels, and prophet-legends of the surrounding cultures. The biography also bears the marks of composition in an age of empire and sect rather than memory of a merchant town: anachronisms in its legal and political assumptions, battle narratives swollen with the topoi of conquest literature, numbers that inflate as the tradition ages, and — as the previous lesson noted — an editor, Ibn Hisham, who openly tells us he cut material his contemporaries would find distressing. Add al-Waqidi, whom even classical Muslim hadith critics accused of fabrication wholesale, and the picture sharpens: the sira is a literature doing what its genre always did — presenting the community's founder in the forms sanctity required — and the historian who strips the legendary layer away is left not with a lean, reliable core but with the honest admission that the layers go all the way down further than anyone can confidently peel.

## History Written by the Winners of Later Wars

The third argument concerns motive. The sira was composed and canonized under the Abbasid caliphate, by scholars living amid the aftermath of civil wars, dynastic revolution, and fierce sectarian and legal conflict — and the biography repeatedly takes sides in the quarrels of its writers' present. The portrayal of the Prophet's companions tracks later Sunni-Shia polemic: the same figures are heroes in one transmission stream and villains in another, depending on the transmitter's loyalties. Stories exalt or diminish Abbas, ancestor of the ruling dynasty, with suspicious convenience. Legal anecdotes have the Prophet ruling on questions that only arose generations later, in exactly the terms of later juristic debate. Even the chronology — the neat scheme of Meccan suffering and Medinan triumph, the dated raids and ordered campaigns — appears, on close inspection, to have been systematized late, with early authorities disagreeing about the sequence and dating of major events to a degree that suggests the framework was imposed rather than remembered. None of this required conspiratorial forgers; it required only what every tradition-community does across two centuries of high-stakes conflict — remembering usefully — performed at scale, under empires, with the founder's authority as the ultimate prize. The result is a biography that functions, verifiably, as a weapon in eighth- and ninth-century arguments, which is precisely what makes its testimony about the seventh century so difficult to isolate.

## The Stakes of the Reclassification

Grasp what follows if the critical reading is even substantially right, because the consequences run through this entire course. The sira is the skeleton of Islamic self-understanding: the chronology of revelation, the meaning of Qur'anic allusions, the context of the law's verses, the exemplary episodes of Unit 4 — all of it is sira-dependent. Reclassify the sira from memory to theological narrative and the tradition does not lose a book; it loses its independent access to its own founding, retaining instead a portrait painted by the great-great-grandchildren of the founding generation in the colors their own battles required. Note carefully what this does to the critical case as well — the discipline this course has insisted on since Unit 4. The critic who narrates the massacre, the marriages, and the assassinations as established seventh-century fact is trusting the same hagiographers he has just impeached; the honest critical claim is always about the portrait the tradition canonized, not the events behind it. The reclassification is symmetrical: it forbids the confident pious narrative and the confident polemical one alike, and leaves standing only claims about what the literature says and what the tradition made normative — which, as this course has argued throughout, is where the real force of the critique lay all along.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The defenses deserve their full weight. First, hagiographical form does not entail fictional content: ancient lives of genuinely historical figures — emperors, bishops, rabbis — wear the same legendary dress, and historians extract sound material from them by criteria of embarrassment, multiple attestation, and dissimilarity; applied to the sira, such criteria arguably certify a real core, since the tradition preserved much that embarrasses it (defeats, rebukes, the Satanic Verses episode, the poisoning) that no hagiographer would invent. Second, the exegetical argument can be overrun: that some stories were built around verses does not show all were, and the Qur'an's allusiveness itself presupposes an audience that knew an underlying story — the scripture is evidence that events of some kind stood behind it. Third, scholars such as Harald Motzki have argued, through painstaking isnad-cum-matn analysis, that portions of the tradition can be traced back to within living memory of the events — much earlier than the wholesale-fabrication thesis allows — and the radical Wansbroughian datings of the literature have not survived manuscript discoveries, as the unit's closing lesson will detail. Fourth, the tradition's internal criticism was real: Muslim scholars themselves distrusted al-Waqidi, graded transmitters, and built the most elaborate authentication apparatus in the ancient world — imperfect, but not credulous. All of this stands, and it is why the sober critical position is not "the sira is fiction" but "the sira is a theological literature within which some early material survives, without any reliable method for isolating it episode by episode." That residue — uncertainty, not fabrication — is precisely calibrated, and it is enough: a tradition whose law and exemplar doctrine require the detailed biography to be true cannot rest them on a literature of this kind, and a critic who wants his case to survive must build it, as this course has, on what the tradition canonized rather than on what the seventh century can be proven to have done. From the biography we turn to the book itself: the text of the Qur'an and the question of its preservation.
