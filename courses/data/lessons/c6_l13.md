The biography, the last two lessons argued, cannot be verified. The tradition's reply has always had a trump card ready: whatever the fate of the stories, the Book stands. The Qur'an, on the orthodox account, is the exception to everything this unit has said — revealed to Muhammad, memorized and written by his companions, collected under Abu Bakr, fixed in a single authoritative recension under the caliph Uthman within twenty years of the Prophet's death, and transmitted thereafter without the alteration of a letter: the one perfectly preserved scripture, its textual integrity a standing miracle and a standing argument. This lesson examines that claim with the same instruments applied to everything else. The finding, stated in advance with the precision the course requires: the Qur'an is indeed, by the standards of ancient texts, remarkably early and remarkably stable — far more so than the revisionists of the 1970s believed — and the claim of perfect, letter-for-letter preservation without variant or history is nonetheless false on the evidence, including evidence the Islamic tradition itself preserves. Both halves matter, and each disciplines the other.

## What the Tradition Itself Records

The first witnesses against the perfect-preservation claim are the Islamic sources themselves, which record a textual history far messier than the modern apologetic slogan.[^1] The tradition reports that portions of the revelation were lost outright — companions recalling verses once recited that appear in no Qur'an, including a stoning verse whose absence from the text became a standing puzzle for the jurists who nonetheless applied its rule; Aisha is even reported saying that the sheet on which certain verses were written was eaten by a domestic animal after the Prophet's death. It reports abrogation as a formal category: verses whose wording, or whose ruling, or both, were divinely withdrawn — a doctrine that concedes on its face that the revelation as delivered and the text as it stands are not identical. It reports that the companions held differing codices: the collections of Ibn Mas'ud and Ubayy ibn Ka'b, among the most authoritative early reciters, differed from the Uthmanic text in wording and even in contents — Ibn Mas'ud reportedly excluding suras the canon includes — and that Ibn Mas'ud resisted the destruction of his codex when the order came. And it reports the central act itself: Uthman, confronted by armies quarreling over divergent recitations, commissioned a standard text and ordered all other copies burned. The tradition presents this as pious consolidation. A historian notes what it concedes: within a generation of the Prophet's death, the community possessed materially different Qur'ans, and uniformity was achieved not by flawless transmission but by state selection and the fire. Whatever one concludes, the doctrine of a text identical in every letter from revelation to today is contradicted by the tradition's own memory of its formation.

## What the Manuscripts Show

The second body of evidence is physical, and it cuts in both directions — which is exactly why it must be stated carefully. The great discovery is the Sana'a palimpsest, a Qur'an manuscript found in the roof of the Great Mosque of Sana'a in 1972, whose parchment carries two layers: a standard Uthmanic-type text written over an earlier, erased text that ultraviolet imaging has recovered. The lower text is the only known surviving witness to a non-Uthmanic Qur'an — and it differs from the canonical text in hundreds of places: variant wordings, added and omitted words and phrases, differences in verse order and sura arrangement, largely paralleling the variants the tradition attributes to the companion codices. Radiocarbon dating places the parchment in the first Islamic decades. Here, then, is material proof of what the literary tradition remembered: the early community had variant Qur'ans, and the standard text is the survivor of a selection, not the sole descendant of a single flawless stream. At the same time — and this is the half the polemicist omits — the manuscripts have devastated the radical revisionist thesis. Wansbrough had argued the Qur'an crystallized late, perhaps two centuries after Muhammad, out of a long communal process; the Sana'a folios, the Birmingham folios radiocarbon-dated to within or near the Prophet's lifetime, and the growing corpus of first-century fragments show a recognizably complete Qur'anic text circulating within decades of the traditional dates, with the Uthmanic consonantal skeleton dominant early and its variants — real as they are — overwhelmingly at the level of wording rather than doctrine. The manuscripts refute the perfect-preservation slogan and the late-invention thesis together. The text is early, substantially stable, and humanly transmitted — with a history, like every book on earth.

## The Readings and the Skeleton

A third layer of the problem is structural, built into the script itself. The early Arabic in which the Uthmanic text was written was a defective script: a consonantal skeleton (rasm) lacking most of the dots that distinguish one consonant from another and all of the marks that indicate short vowels. A single written word could support multiple readings; the skeleton fixed far less than the recitation required. The tradition managed this openly: variant recitations proliferated until the scholar Ibn Mujahid, in the tenth century — three centuries after Uthman — canonized seven acceptable reading-systems, later expanded to ten, which differ among themselves in thousands of places: mostly vocalization and minor wording, occasionally meaning. These canonical readings coexist today; the Qur'an of North Africa (the reading of Warsh) differs in wording from the Qur'an of Cairo and Mecca (the reading of Hafs, standardized globally largely through the 1924 Egyptian edition). The differences are doctrinally trivial — that should be said plainly — but their existence is fatal to the popular claim, repeated in countless apologetic settings, that every Qur'an on earth is identical to every other letter for letter. The tradition's own science of readings (qira'at) is the documentation of the variance; the modern slogan is contradicted by the mushafs one can buy in two different airports.

## The Outer Edge: Luxenberg

At the far edge of textual criticism stands the thesis published pseudonymously by "Christoph Luxenberg" in 2000: that beneath obscure passages of the Qur'an lies a substrate of Syro-Aramaic, the literary language of the region's Christians, and that reading difficult words as Syriac transforms their meaning — most famously rendering the houris of paradise as "white grapes," an emendation the houris lesson of Unit 4 has already met. The thesis matters to this unit for what its reception teaches. Mainstream scholarship — including scholars with no apologetic stake — has rejected Luxenberg's method as philologically undisciplined: emendations applied ad hoc, Syriac forced where Arabic reads adequately, results driven by the desired conclusion. It is a fringe position and should be cited as one. But the sober core it sensationalized is mainstream and important: the Qur'an emerged in a milieu saturated with Syriac Christianity, Jewish tradition, and Arabian religion; its vocabulary includes loanwords and its narratives engage the surrounding traditions; and some of its obscurities remain genuinely unsolved, as the classical commentators themselves — recording multiple guesses at cryptic words and the unexplained letters that open certain suras — candidly admitted. The text can be early and stable and still be, in places, imperfectly understood — even from within.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Assemble the defense at full strength. By any comparative standard, the Qur'an's textual pedigree is extraordinary: a consonantal text substantially fixed within decades of the founder's death, physical witnesses reaching back near his lifetime, variance overwhelmingly marginal to meaning — a record the New Testament, with its thousands of manuscripts and hundreds of thousands of variants across far longer transmission, cannot match for early uniformity, as textual critics of both corpora acknowledge. The companion-codex variants and the Sana'a lower text are real but modest: no lost doctrines, no alternative Islam, no smoking gun — differences of wording, order, and inclusion at the margins. The qira'at are a documented, bounded, tradition-managed phenomenon, not chaos. And the Uthmanic standardization, viewed without hostility, was an act of responsible textual stewardship that succeeded brilliantly: the skeleton it fixed is the skeleton the manuscripts show. All of this is true, and the critic who denies it discredits himself. What survives it is the exact claim, and the asymmetry that gives it force. The Qur'an is an early, stable, humanly transmitted text with a real history — losses its own tradition records, variant codices its own tradition remembers and its earliest manuscript confirms, a defective script resolved by later canonization, and standing obscurities. For a historian, that is a superb ancient text. But the tradition's claim is not that the Qur'an is a superb ancient text; the claim is perfect preservation — a text without loss, without variance, without history, letter-identical from the eternal tablet to the printed page — because the doctrine of the Qur'an as God's uncreated, unmediated speech requires exactly that. Judged against the ordinary standards of ancient books, the Qur'an passes with distinction. Judged against the standard its own theology sets for it, it fails — not catastrophically, but demonstrably, from the tradition's own records outward. It is the gap between those two standards, not any sensational discovery, that constitutes the text problem. From the Book we turn to the other half of the canon: the hadith, where the reliability crisis is not a gap but a chasm.
