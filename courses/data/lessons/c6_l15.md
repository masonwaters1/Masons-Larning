This unit has argued four things: the founding period is a documentary near-void; the biography that fills it is theological narrative; the Qur'an, though early and stable, has a human textual history its doctrine denies; and the hadith corpus, bearing the weight of law and sunna, grew backward out of later conflicts under a filter that certifies pedigrees rather than events. Now the discipline that governs this course: the strongest answers get the floor, and only what survives them may be carried forward. Here that discipline has a special edge, because the counter-scholarship in this unit is not merely the tradition defending itself. It is the story of what happened inside secular academia over the last fifty years — a story in which the radical revisionist school that once seemed poised to dissolve Islamic origins altogether was itself tested, corrected, and substantially beaten back by evidence. Any honest presentation of the source-critical case must include the retreat of its own vanguard. This lesson tells that story, and then states, with the exactness the course demands, the residue that survived it — which is uncertainty, not fabrication, and which turns out to be all the argument ever needed.

## The Radical Wave

The high-water mark of revisionism came in the 1970s.[^1] John Wansbrough, applying biblical form-criticism to Islamic texts, argued that the Qur'an itself was not the fixed early book of tradition but a late crystallization — canonized perhaps two centuries after Muhammad, assembled from communal prophetic logia in a sectarian milieu — and that the entire Islamic origins narrative was "salvation history," a literary back-projection with no recoverable event behind it. His students Patricia Crone and Michael Cook, in the deliberately provocative Hagarism (1977), went further: setting the Islamic sources aside entirely and reconstructing from external testimony alone, they proposed that "Islam" began as an Arab messianic movement entangled with Judaism, centered originally not on Mecca, its prophet and scripture and sanctuary acquiring their classical shape only later, under the empire. Others pressed the archaeology of the sanctuary, the orientation of early mosques, and the silence of the material record. For a moment it seemed possible that secular scholarship would conclude that almost nothing of the traditional account — book, biography, city, chronology — survived critical scrutiny. If that school had held the field, this course's task would be easy and its tone very different.

## The Correction

It did not hold the field, and the reasons are instructive because they are evidential, not apologetic. The decisive blows fell on the late-Qur'an thesis. The Sana'a palimpsest, radiocarbon-dated to the first Islamic decades, presented a Qur'an — indeed two textual layers of Qur'an — centuries before Wansbrough's proposed crystallization; the Birmingham folios and the accumulating corpus of first-century fragments confirmed it; and the philological work of scholars such as Fred Donner showed the Qur'an's internal profile (its concerns, its silences, its vocabulary) fitting the early seventh century far better than the imperial age that supposedly produced it. Wansbrough's dating is today held by almost no one. Hagarism fared no better as a positive thesis: its external-sources-only method proved as selective as the method it replaced, its Jewish-messianic reconstruction found no following, and its own authors abandoned it — Crone stating plainly in later years that the book's thesis was wrong, while defending its methodological provocation. Meanwhile, on the hadith front, Harald Motzki's isnad-cum-matn analyses demonstrated that Schacht's instruments, wielded more finely, could authenticate select traditions back to the first Islamic century, breaking the assumption that nothing early was recoverable. By the 2000s the center of the field had settled into what is sometimes called critical moderation: an Arabian prophet, an early Qur'an substantially as transmitted, a conquest movement issuing from his community — established; the detailed narrative superstructure — unverifiable.

It is worth seeing what that moderate scholarship looks like when it builds rather than demolishes, because its positive reconstructions are themselves instructive. Fred Donner, perhaps the most influential of the moderates, argues from the Qur'an's own vocabulary that the movement began not as a sharply bounded "Islam" but as a community of "Believers" — monotheist, apocalyptic, and initially open enough that pious Jews and Christians may have belonged to it — which only hardened into a distinct confession, with Muhammad's name on its coins and its polemic on the Dome of the Rock, two generations later under Abd al-Malik. Whether or not Donner's reconstruction is right in detail, notice its shape: even the field's constructive center now holds that classical Islam's self-definition crystallized decades after the founder, under the empire — a conclusion gentler than Wansbrough's but still fatal to the tradition's picture of a faith delivered complete. The apologist's telling of this story ends with revisionism routed and tradition vindicated. That telling is false, and seeing why is the whole point of this lesson.

## What the Correction Did Not Restore

Distinguish, with care, what the counter-revolution established from what it left exactly where Goldziher and Schacht put it. The manuscripts vindicated the Qur'an's earliness — against Wansbrough. They did not vindicate perfect preservation; the same Sana'a parchment that refuted the late dating is itself the physical proof of the variant codices, and the readings, the losses, and the abrogation doctrine stand in the tradition's own records untouched. Motzki vindicated the recoverability of some early traditions — against the claim that nothing pre-common-link is reachable. He did not certify the canon; a handful of painstaking case-studies cannot underwrite thousands of legal and biographical reports, and Motzki himself insisted his method authenticated early circulation, not prophetic origin. The collapse of Hagarism removed a rival reconstruction; it did not supply the missing seventh-century documentation for the traditional one — the black hole is as empty after Crone's retraction as before her book. And nothing in the correction touched Goldziher at all: the corpus still mirrors the later conflicts, the isnads still grow backward in the legal material, the sira is still exegesis and hagiography by genre and demonstration. The revisionists lost their alternative story. The tradition did not thereby regain its own. What the field converged on is precisely the position this unit has argued lesson by lesson: skeleton established, flesh unverifiable — and it is on the flesh, the detailed sunna and biography, that the law and the exemplar doctrine stand.

## The Apologetic Overreach and the Polemical One

The residue disciplines both camps, and the course states both disciplines with equal force. The apologetic overreach runs: revisionism failed, therefore the tradition is reliable — treating the defeat of the strongest attack as certification of the strongest claim. It is a non sequitur twice over: the moderate critical position that defeated revisionism itself denies the tradition's detailed reliability, and the manuscript discoveries that armed the moderates simultaneously falsified the perfect-preservation doctrine. The believer who cites Sana'a against Wansbrough has cited a variant Qur'an into evidence. The polemical overreach runs the other way: the sources are late and tendentious, therefore the massacre happened, the child-marriage happened, the assassinations happened, and worse — asserting as fact what the impeached sources report, the incoherence this course has policed since Unit 4. Both overreaches die in this lesson. What lives is the register the course has enforced throughout: the tradition's canon says what it says, grades it as it grades it, and builds on it what it builds — normative facts, verifiable today — while the seventh century behind the canon remains, in detail, beyond proof and beyond disproof alike.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The tradition's best remaining reply, granted in full, is this: uncertainty is not a weapon that cuts one way. If the sources cannot establish the massacre, they cannot establish it did not happen; if the detailed sunna is unverifiable, it is also unfalsifiable; a faith may reasonably rest on a transmission its community trusts, as every historical religion's must; the criterion of embarrassment, the early Qur'anic anchor, and the sheer coherence of the tradition's self-account are genuine evidences, not nothing; and the believer's epistemic position — trusting a tradition's testimony about its founder — differs in degree, not kind, from the Christian's or the Jew's. All of this is fair, and the course does not begrudge it. But the reply meets the asymmetry that has structured every counter-scholarship lesson so far, and meets it at the foundation. The critic's case, run through the normativity register, needs only the canon's existence: that the tradition certified these texts, holds this man exemplary, and derives this law — facts no uncertainty can touch. The tradition's case needs the canon's accuracy: the exemplar doctrine is empty unless the life is known; the sunna cannot bind unless the reports are true; the law's divine warrant runs through a chain of custody this unit has shown cannot be verified at the required scale. Uncertainty, distributed evenly over the evidence, therefore falls unevenly on the parties — it is survivable for the critic, who asserted nothing about the seventh century, and structural for the tradition, which asserted everything about it. That asymmetry, not any revisionist reconstruction, is the durable finding of a hundred and thirty years of scholarship, and it is the foundation on which the rest of this course now stands: Unit 3 takes up why a tradition so constructed is so difficult to reform from within — the authority problem that the sources' status makes permanent.
