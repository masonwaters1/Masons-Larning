The apostasy law of the previous lesson punishes the one who leaves the faith; the blasphemy law of this one punishes the one who insults it. The two are close cousins, both treating belief and speech about religion as matters the state may police with force, and both colliding with a core liberty of the constitutional order. Here the liberty at stake is the freedom of speech — and specifically the freedom, which the First Amendment protects with unusual strength, to criticize, mock, satirize, and even revile religion. Classical Islamic law and its modern statutory revivals treat the reviling of the Prophet Muhammad as a grave crime, in its severest form a capital one. The collision is sharp, and its edge is not merely historical: it has been written in blood within living memory.

## The Classical Law of Insulting the Prophet

In classical Islamic jurisprudence, the offense of *sabb al-nabi* — reviling or insulting the Prophet — was treated with extreme gravity, distinct from and in some respects more severe than ordinary apostasy. The classical schools broadly held that a Muslim who insults the Prophet thereby apostatizes and is liable to death, and that even a non-Muslim *dhimmi* who reviles the Prophet may forfeit the protection of his covenant and his life. The fourteenth-century jurist Ibn Taymiyya devoted an entire treatise to the subject — *al-Sarim al-Maslul ʿala Shatim al-Rasul* ("The Drawn Sword Against the One Who Insults the Messenger") — arguing for the death penalty for the offense and, in his stringent view, holding that for the Muslim offender repentance might not even avert the sentence.[^1]

The standard manuals reflect this. *Reliance of the Traveller*, the certified Shafi'i text we have already met, lists among the acts that entail leaving Islam — and thus expose one to the apostasy penalty — reviling God or His Messenger.[^2] The classical consensus, in short, was that insulting the Prophet is not protected speech to be answered with counter-speech, but a punishable crime, and in its gravest classical form a capital one. The premise beneath the ruling is the inverse of the liberal premise: that certain religious speech is so injurious that the law must suppress it by force, rather than leaving offensive expression to be met by argument, rebuttal, or simple indifference.

## The Modern Statutory Revival

This is not merely a medieval relic. Several modern states have revived the classical logic in statutory form. The most prominent example is Pakistan, whose penal code criminalizes defaming the Prophet — section 295-C prescribing the death penalty for the offense — and whose blasphemy laws have been invoked in a large number of cases, often against religious minorities and frequently accompanied by mob violence quite apart from any formal trial. Other states maintain blasphemy statutes of varying severity. The classical doctrine, in these jurisdictions, has been translated into the machinery of the modern state, with the result that words deemed insulting to the Prophet can carry the gravest legal consequences.

The collision has also repeatedly broken out beyond any courtroom, in acts of violence presented by their perpetrators as the enforcement of this very principle. The 1989 fatwa issued by Ayatollah Khomeini calling for the death of the novelist Salman Rushdie over his novel *The Satanic Verses*; the 2015 massacre at the offices of the French satirical magazine *Charlie Hebdo*, which had published caricatures of the Prophet; and a series of related killings and attempted killings across the West — these are the live edge of the collision, the points at which the classical principle that insulting the Prophet merits death has been asserted, by some, against writers and artists exercising what the liberal order regards as a fundamental freedom.

## The Collision with Free Speech

The American constitutional order takes nearly the opposite view of religious insult. The First Amendment's free-speech guarantee protects, with particular vigor, exactly the kind of expression that blasphemy law forbids: harsh criticism of religion, mockery, satire, caricature, and even deliberate offense. Under settled constitutional doctrine, there is no exception to free speech for blasphemy; the state may not punish a person for insulting any prophet, deity, or sacred figure, however crude or offensive the insult. The governing assumption is that the answer to offensive speech is more speech — rebuttal, protest, ridicule in return, or simply turning away — never the force of the criminal law, and certainly never violence.

Between these two positions there is no compromise to be found. One order treats the reviling of the Prophet as among the gravest of crimes; the other treats the freedom to revile any sacred figure as among the most protected of liberties. The clash is not about whether insulting religion is *rude* — reasonable people everywhere may find it so — but about whether it may be *criminalized*, and in the severest versions, punished with death. On that question the classical law and its statutory revivals stand on one side, and the constitutional guarantee of free expression stands flatly on the other.

## The Counter-Case

Fairness requires several genuine qualifications, and they matter. The first is historical perspective: blasphemy was a crime in *Christian* Europe and colonial America as well, and the robust modern free-speech protection of religious insult is a relatively recent achievement even in the West, won against the West's own long tradition of blasphemy law. The contrast, then, is not between an intrinsically censorious Islam and an intrinsically free Christendom, but between classical religious law in general — which the West largely abandoned — and a modern liberal settlement that much of the world, Muslim and otherwise, has not adopted. To present free speech as the natural human default rather than a particular and hard-won development would be to misread history.

The second qualification is internal to the Islamic tradition. A number of Muslim scholars argue that the Qur'an itself counsels *patience and forbearance* in the face of mockery rather than violence — pointing to verses instructing the believer, when he hears God's revelations ridiculed, to withdraw from the company until the subject is changed (as at 4:140), and to respond to the ignorant with peace. On this reading, the severe classical penalties for *sabb al-nabi* reflect later juristic development and the political conditions of empire more than the Qur'an's own prescribed response to insult, and reformist scholars accordingly reject blasphemy executions as without firm Qur'anic warrant.

The third is the now-familiar doctrine/people distinction. The overwhelming majority of Muslims do not participate in or endorse the killing of those who insult the Prophet, and many actively condemn it. Blasphemy *laws* enjoy significant popular support in some societies, but support for a statute is not the same as support for vigilante murder, and the two should not be conflated. The subject of this lesson is the classical doctrine and its statutory and violent revivals — not the disposition of Muslims as a body.

## Statute, Mob, and the International Dimension

To weigh this collision fairly, it helps to distinguish the several different forms in which the blasphemy principle operates today, because they are not equally grave and conflating them obscures the analysis. There is, first, *formal statute* — laws like Pakistan's that criminalize defaming the Prophet, prosecuted (however unevenly) through courts. There is, second, *extrajudicial violence* — the mob that kills on a rumor of blasphemy, or the lone attacker who acts on the classical principle without any legal process at all; this is frequently the more lethal form, and it operates precisely where formal law is weak or where individuals appoint themselves its enforcers. And there is, third, the *transnational* assertion of the principle against people in countries whose law does not recognize it at all — the fatwa against Rushdie reaching into Britain, the attack on *Charlie Hebdo* in France — by which the classical doctrine is pressed across borders, against writers and artists who are subject to no Islamic jurisdiction.

These forms shade into one another, but the distinctions matter. A blasphemy statute, however objectionable to the free-speech principle, at least operates through law; the mob and the cross-border assassin operate against it entirely. And the chilling effect ranges across all three: the knowledge that depicting or criticizing the Prophet may bring prosecution in one country, lynching in another, or assassination in a third produces a powerful self-censorship in publishers, artists, and even scholars far beyond the reach of any formal blasphemy code — a quiet narrowing of expression that the violence is, in part, designed to achieve.

There is also an international-institutional dimension worth noting. For years, a bloc of states pressed at the United Nations for resolutions against the "defamation of religions" — an effort to give the blasphemy principle a foothold in international human-rights language. Western states and free-speech advocates resisted, arguing that human-rights law protects *persons*, not *ideas or beliefs*, from criticism, and that a right against the "defamation" of a religion would license exactly the suppression of dissent that the free-speech principle exists to prevent. The eventual shift in that debate — away from protecting religions and toward protecting individuals from incitement and discrimination — marked a reaffirmation, at the international level, of the very distinction this collision turns on: between shielding people from harm, which liberal orders accept, and shielding doctrines from insult, which they refuse.

## What Survives

When the qualifications are credited, the surviving claim is precise. It is not that "Islam is uniquely censorious" — blasphemy law was once general, and the West's own free-speech settlement is recent and particular. It is that *the classical Islamic law of insulting the Prophet established severe penalties, up to death, anchored in the major jurists and manuals; that this doctrine has been revived in the statutes of modern states such as Pakistan and asserted in lethal violence against writers and artists in the West; and that in both forms it collides directly with the free-speech principle that protects even offensive religious expression.* That the Qur'an may counsel forbearance, and that reformers contest the classical penalties, is significant — but it again concedes that the classical penalties are the thing being contested. The drawn sword of Ibn Taymiyya's title is not a modern invention; it is a classical doctrine with modern enforcers.

The three collisions we have now examined — over the source of law, the freedom to leave the faith, and the freedom to insult it — all concern the Muslim's relationship to the Islamic order. The next two turn outward, to the status the classical law assigns to those who are *not* Muslims: first the subordinated tax and legal disabilities of the non-Muslim living under Islamic rule, and then the sacred ground from which the non-Muslim is barred entirely.
