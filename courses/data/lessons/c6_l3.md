From the one who leaves the faith we turn to the one who insults it. If apostasy law bars the exit, blasphemy law guards the speech of those who remain outside or within — and it collides with what may be the most distinctively American of all liberties: the freedom to say offensive, irreverent, even blasphemous things about religion without fear of state punishment. In the classical Islamic legal tradition, insulting the Prophet Muhammad is a grave crime, and the classical consensus holds it a capital one. In the modern world, that classical rule lives on in the blasphemy statutes of several states and in episodes of lethal violence that have reached into the heart of the West. This lesson examines the classical law of blasphemy, its modern statutory revival, and the constitutional principle it cannot be reconciled with — and it weighs, as the course requires, the contesting voices within Islam itself.

The object of study is again the *law and the doctrine*, not the inner life of Muslims, the vast majority of whom have never harmed anyone over an insult to their faith and many of whom oppose blasphemy laws as vigorously as any Western liberal. What is under the microscope is a body of classical jurisprudence and the statutes and incidents that flow from it.

## The Classical Law of Insulting the Prophet

The classical crime is *sabb al-rasul* — reviling or insulting the Messenger of God — and the classical jurists treated it with extraordinary severity. Across the Sunni schools, the dominant classical position held that a person who insults or reviles the Prophet has committed a capital offense. For a non-Muslim living under Muslim rule, such an insult was held to break the protection (the *dhimma*) under which he lived; for a Muslim, it was frequently treated as a form of apostasy or as an independent capital crime. The penalty, in the classical consensus, was death — and in the Maliki, Hanbali, and Shafi'i formulations, the offense was one for which repentance did not avert the worldly punishment: the insulter of the Prophet was to be executed even if he recanted. Mark what that means against the previous lesson. The apostate, in the classical law, was given the chance to return; the blasphemer, in most schools, was not. Insulting the Prophet was thus treated as the graver crime — harsher than abandoning the religion altogether — a severity the jurists defended on the ground that apostasy wrongs God, who may forgive, while the insult wrongs the Prophet's personal honor, which no repentance before a human court can repair.

The most influential classical treatment is the massive treatise of the fourteenth-century jurist Ibn Taymiyya, *al-Sarim al-Maslul 'ala Shatim al-Rasul* — "The Drawn Sword Against the One Who Insults the Messenger."[^1] In it Ibn Taymiyya assembled the textual and juristic case that one who insults the Prophet is to be executed, and his work became a touchstone for the doctrine. The classical manuals concur: *Reliance of the Traveller*, the certified Shafi'i manual we met in the last lesson, lists among the acts that entail leaving Islam — and thus the capital consequences of apostasy — speaking words that revile or belittle God, the Prophet, or the religion. The classical law, in short, did not treat insult to the Prophet as a matter of manners or even of serious offense to be answered with rebuke; it treated it as a crime against the foundation of the social and religious order, punishable by death.

## The Textual Roots and the Counter-Current

The Qur'anic basis for a *worldly penalty* is, as with apostasy, more limited than the severity of the classical ruling might suggest — and this is the seam the reformists will press. The Qur'an certainly records that the Prophet was mocked and reviled, and it condemns the mockers; it warns of divine punishment for those who insult God and his Messenger. But its direct counsel to the Prophet and the believers in the face of mockery is frequently one of *patience and disengagement*: to turn away from those who ridicule the faith, to bear insult with forbearance, to leave the matter to God. Verses instruct the believers, when they hear God's signs mocked, simply to withdraw from the company of the mockers until they turn to another subject. The Qur'an nowhere plainly commands the execution of the blasphemer.

The classical death penalty, then, was built primarily on certain *hadith* — including reports of individuals who mocked or insulted the Prophet during his lifetime being killed — and on juristic reasoning, rather than on an explicit Qur'anic command. The jurists wove these reports into a settled doctrine. But the gap between the Qur'an's counsel of patience and the *fiqh*'s sentence of death is real, and it is precisely the ground on which modern reformers stand when they argue that the classical penalty is a historical construction rather than a divine mandate. We will return to that argument; first we must see the doctrine's collision with the constitutional order, and its disturbing modern afterlife.

## The Clash With the American Order

The First Amendment protects, with very few exceptions, the freedom of speech — and American constitutional law has been emphatic that this protection extends to speech that is offensive, irreverent, provocative, and blasphemous. The Supreme Court has held that the government may not punish the expression of an idea simply because society finds it offensive or disagreeable, and it has specifically struck down the notion that the state may suppress "sacrilegious" speech. In the American order, religions and their founders may be criticized, satirized, ridiculed, and denounced; the offense this causes to believers, however deep and sincere, is not a ground for legal punishment. The remedy for speech one hates is more speech, not suppression, and certainly not violence.

The classical law of *sabb al-rasul* stands at the opposite pole. Where the First Amendment protects even the most offensive religious criticism as a fundamental right, the classical law criminalizes it as a capital offense. The two cannot be reconciled, because they answer the same question — may the state punish irreverent speech about the sacred? — with a flat yes and a flat no. And this is not an abstract opposition. The collision has become bloody and concrete in living memory.

## The Live Edge: Rushdie, Charlie Hebdo, Paty

Three episodes mark the live edge of this clash, and they brought the classical doctrine of blasphemy into direct, violent contact with the Western order of free expression. In February 1989, Ayatollah Khomeini, the supreme leader of Iran, issued a fatwa calling for the death of the novelist Salman Rushdie over his novel The Satanic Verses, held to insult the Prophet — a ruling that sent Rushdie into hiding under British protection for a decade, and that struck those around the book when it could not reach the author: the novel's Japanese translator, Hitoshi Igarashi, was stabbed to death at his university in 1991; its Italian translator was stabbed and its Norwegian publisher shot and gravely wounded. The fatwa was never rescinded, and thirty-three years later it found its arm: in August 2022, Rushdie was stabbed repeatedly on a lecture stage in New York State by a young man born a decade after the fatwa was issued, and lost an eye. In January 2015, gunmen entered the Paris offices of the satirical magazine Charlie Hebdo and murdered twelve people, cartoonists and staff, announcing that they had avenged the Prophet against the magazine's cartoons. And in October 2020, Samuel Paty, a French middle-school teacher, showed cartoons of Muhammad in a civics lesson on freedom of expression; an online campaign against him followed, and days later an eighteen-year-old attacker beheaded him on the street near his school. In each case, the logic invoked by the perpetrators and their defenders was the logic of the classical blasphemy law: that to insult the Prophet is a crime deserving death, and that the sentence may be carried out by those with the will to do it.

These episodes are the reason the classical blasphemy doctrine cannot be filed away as a historical curiosity. Its core principle — that insulting the Prophet is a capital matter — has been asserted, in the modern West, as a justification for murdering writers, artists, and a schoolteacher, and cartoonists and critics in Europe live under standing police protection now, years after their offending drawings. The clash between this principle and the free-speech order is therefore not merely doctrinal but has been written in blood, and it represents the silencing of the critic as a righteous act — the seventh-century root of which we will examine when we come to the killing of the poets who mocked the Prophet during his lifetime.

## The Statutory Revival

Beyond acts of private violence, the classical doctrine survives as codified law, and the codification should be stated with statute numbers and names, because the specifics are the argument. Blasphemy carries a potential death sentence today in a set of states including Pakistan, Iran, Afghanistan, Saudi Arabia, Mauritania, Brunei, and the sharia-implementing states of northern Nigeria. The most consequential is Pakistan. Section 295-C of the Pakistan Penal Code, added in 1986 under General Zia's Islamization program, provides that whoever "by words, either spoken or written... defiles the sacred name of the Holy Prophet" shall be punished with death — a mandatory capital sentence, after a court ruling struck the life-imprisonment alternative, for the precise classical offense of sabb al-rasul.

The state has never actually hanged anyone under 295-C; the accusation does the killing before the courts can. Asia Bibi, a Christian farm laborer, was accused in June 2009 after a quarrel with fellow workers over drinking water from a shared well — the Muslim women refused to drink after her and words followed. She was convicted in 2010 and spent roughly eight years on death row before Pakistan's Supreme Court acquitted her in 2018, a verdict that set off days of national rioting demanding her execution; she had to be flown out of the country to Canada. The two most prominent politicians who defended her were both assassinated within months of each other: Salman Taseer, the governor of Punjab, was shot twenty-seven times in January 2011 by Mumtaz Qadri, a member of his own security detail, for calling 295-C a "black law"; Shahbaz Bhatti, the federal minorities minister and the cabinet's only Christian, was gunned down that March. What followed Taseer's murder is the detail that measures the doctrine's living force: Qadri was showered with rose petals by lawyers at his court appearances, his funeral after his 2016 execution drew crowds estimated in the hundreds of thousands, and a shrine was built over his grave outside Islamabad, now a site of pilgrimage. And the mob does not wait for trials: in April 2017 Mashal Khan, a journalism student at Abdul Wali Khan University in Mardan, was stripped, beaten, and shot to death by a crowd of his fellow students inside his own campus over a blasphemy accusation that the subsequent investigation found to be baseless, apparently seeded by a dispute with the university administration. Lynchings on the same pattern recur — accused men killed in police custody by crowds, a Sri Lankan factory manager beaten to death and burned in Sialkot in 2021 — and researchers count dozens killed by mobs over accusations since the law took its current form. Here the classical doctrine is not a juristic memory but living statute and living mob, each amplifying the other: the law announces that the offense deserves death, and the crowd carries out the sentence the courts are too slow to deliver.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The course's discipline requires stating the strongest case against its own thesis, and on blasphemy that case has real force. First, the reformist argument applies here as it did to apostasy: the death penalty for blasphemy rests on *hadith* and juristic construction, not on an explicit Qur'anic command, and the Qur'an's own counsel in the face of mockery is largely one of patience — so many modern Muslim scholars argue that the classical penalty is a historically conditioned ruling that should not bind today. Second, blasphemy laws are not unique to Islam; they existed across Christendom for centuries, lingered on the books of Western states into the modern era, and the impulse to protect the sacred from insult is a broadly human one rather than a peculiarly Islamic pathology. Third, the geography must not be overdrawn: the capital statutes are the law of a specific set of states, and a long list of Muslim-majority countries — from Senegal to Bosnia to most of Central Asia — maintain no such laws at all, so the leap from "this is enforced in some countries" to a claim about Islam-as-such everywhere is exactly the overstatement this course refuses. And fourth — the point that most complicates any civilizational framing — the machinery mostly grinds up the people inside it. The loudest victims of blasphemy enforcement are overwhelmingly Muslims themselves: reformers and skeptics like the lecturer Junaid Hafeez, sentenced to death in 2019 after years in solitary confinement on a student-seeded accusation; minorities within Islam, above all the Ahmadis, whom Pakistani law since 1984 criminalizes for merely "posing as" Muslims and who supply a wildly disproportionate share of the accused; and Muslims like Taseer who simply objected out loud. The majority of those charged under Pakistan's blasphemy laws are Muslims. Blasphemy law is less a sword pointed outward at the West than a machine of internal terror, policing the tradition's own dissenters — which, if anything, strengthens rather than weakens the case for criticizing it openly from the places where such criticism is still legal, since the people it silences cannot.

These points are true, and they must be granted. But they do not dissolve the core claim, which is narrower and harder to move: *the classical Islamic legal tradition treats insulting the Prophet as a capital offense; this ruling is recorded in the authoritative manuals and given its fullest defense by major classical jurists; it survives in the blasphemy statutes of modern states and in the logic invoked by those who have murdered writers and artists in the West; and it collides absolutely with the free-speech principle that the offensive and the sacrilegious are nonetheless protected.* That a serious reformist tradition contests the ruling, that other civilizations had blasphemy laws too, and that many Muslims abhor the violence are all real and important — they locate the doctrine within a contested and changing tradition rather than presenting it as the fixed will of all Muslims. What they do not do is make the classical doctrine, or its living edge, go away. From the speech that insults the faith, we turn next to the price the classical law sets on simply existing within it unconverted: the tax and the subordinate status of the non-Muslim.
