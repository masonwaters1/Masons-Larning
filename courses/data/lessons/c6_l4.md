We have examined the price the classical law sets on leaving Islam and on insulting it. We turn now to the price it sets on simply existing within its domain *unconverted* — the legal status, the tax, and the disabilities that the classical tradition assigns to the non-Muslim who lives under Muslim rule. This is the system of *dhimmitude*, built around the *jizya*, the tax levied on non-Muslim "protected peoples." It is, in the judgment of this course, one of the cleanest exhibits in the whole syllabus, because the relevant text states the principle of subordination so plainly, and because the classical exegesis of that text leaves little room for a benign reading. It is also a place where the counter-scholarship is unusually strong, and where intellectual honesty requires real care. This lesson sets out the doctrine, draws the constitutional contrast, and then weighs, more fully than in any lesson so far, the case for the defense.

The object is the *classical legal framework* and the texts that ground it — not the conduct or beliefs of Muslims today, almost none of whom live under or advocate a functioning dhimmi system, which has effectively vanished from the modern world. What is under examination is what the classical law *prescribed*, and what its own most authoritative interpreters said it meant.

## The Qur'anic Foundation

The textual foundation is a single, much-discussed verse — the twenty-ninth verse of the ninth chapter (sura al-Tawba). It commands the believers to fight those who have been given the Scripture — that is, Jews and Christians, the "People of the Book" — who do not believe in God and the Last Day as they should, *until they pay the jizya out of hand, in a state of submission* — in the Arabic, *wa-hum saghirun*, "while they are humbled" or "small."[^1]

Everything turns on that final phrase. The verse does not merely authorize a tax; it sets the *terms* under which the People of the Book may be permitted to live unconverted under Muslim rule: they are to pay the *jizya*, and they are to do so *wa-hum saghirun* — in a condition of lowliness or subjection. The question of how to read *saghirun* — whether it denotes merely the payment of a tax by a subordinate political community, or whether it requires a deliberate, ritualized humiliation of the payer — has been debated, but the dominant classical reading is not the gentle one. The great medieval exegete Ibn Kathir, in his widely used commentary, reads *saghirun* as requiring that the *jizya* be paid in a manner that is *disgraced and humbled* — that the non-Muslim be made to feel his subordinate status in the very act of payment. On this authoritative reading, the verse frames the tax not as a neutral fiscal arrangement but as *ritualized subordination*: the price of existing unconverted is to accept, and to enact, one's own lowliness.

## The Pact of Umar and the Disabilities

If the verse establishes the principle, it is the body of classical law built upon it that supplies the concrete content of dhimmitude — and the central document is the so-called *Pact of Umar*, a set of conditions traditionally attributed to the second caliph and elaborated by later jurists. The Pact, and the classical *fiqh* of the *dhimma* that grew around it, codified the disabilities under which the "protected" non-Muslim was to live in exchange for the *jizya* and the security of his life and property.

The classical disabilities were extensive. Non-Muslims were typically forbidden to build new houses of worship or to repair old ones beyond certain limits; forbidden to display their religion publicly — to ring church bells, to process with crosses, to recite their scriptures within earshot of Muslims; required in various formulations to wear distinguishing dress or markers that set them apart; forbidden to ride horses or bear arms; required to yield the way to Muslims and to show deference; barred from positions of authority over Muslims; and subjected to disabilities in court, where in the classical law the testimony of a non-Muslim against a Muslim was not accepted. The *dhimmi*, in the classical scheme, was tolerated — permitted to live, to keep his faith, to manage his communal affairs — but tolerated precisely as a *subordinate*, an inhabitant of a lower legal caste whose toleration was conditional on his acceptance of that lower place. *Reliance of the Traveller* devotes a section to these rules of the non-Muslim subject population, setting out the terms of the *dhimma* and the conduct required of those who live under it.

## The Clash With the American Order

The collision with the American constitutional order requires little elaboration, for it is almost diagrammatic. The American order rests on the *equal protection of the laws* and the *free exercise of religion*: every person, regardless of creed, stands equal before the law, bears the same rights and the same burdens, and may practice — or not practice — any religion freely and publicly, without civil disability attached to that choice. There is no religious test, no second-class citizenship, no tax levied on the unconverted as the price of toleration, no requirement that members of a disfavored faith mark themselves, defer to members of the favored faith, or accept a subordinate standing in court or in public life.

The classical dhimmi system is the systematic negation of each of these principles. A religion-based poll tax levied on non-believers as the price of their continued existence unconverted is a frontal collision with equal protection. A body of law that assigns civil disabilities — in worship, dress, public religious expression, testimony, and office — on the basis of religious identity is the construction of exactly the religious caste system the American order forbids. And the classical framing of the *jizya* as paid *wa-hum saghirun*, in a state of enacted humiliation, collides not only with the letter of equal protection but with the deeper civic premise it expresses: that the unconverted are not merely tolerated guests of a favored majority but equal citizens by right. This is, as the syllabus says, one of the cleanest exhibits in the course, because the subordination is not inferred from hostile sources but stated in the tradition's own verse and spelled out in its own law.

## The Strongest Case for the Defense

And yet this is also the lesson where the counter-scholarship is strongest, and where the discipline of the course is most tested. The case for the defense is serious and must be stated at its full strength, not waved away. It runs along several lines.

First, *comparative context*. The dhimmi system must be judged against the alternatives available in the pre-modern world, not against the standard of a modern liberal democracy that did not yet exist anywhere. And by that comparison, the historian Mark Cohen and others have argued, the medieval Islamic treatment of religious minorities — for all its built-in subordination — frequently afforded Jews and Christians *more* security, autonomy, and continuity than Latin Christendom afforded its own religious minorities in the same centuries, when Jews faced expulsion, forced conversion, ghettoization, and massacre, and heretics faced the stake. The dhimmi lived as a subordinate, but he lived; his community endured, governed its own affairs, and often prospered. Measured against its actual historical peers rather than against an anachronistic ideal, the system can be presented as comparatively tolerant.

Second, *variability of enforcement*. The full apparatus of disabilities described in the legal manuals was an *ideal type* that was unevenly applied across the vast sweep of Islamic history and geography. In many times and places the harsher restrictions were ignored, non-Muslims rose to high office, and the *jizya* functioned as little more than a tax. The gap between the manual and the lived reality — a theme of this entire course — was often wide. Some have also argued that the *jizya* can be understood as a tax paid *in lieu of military service* and the alms-tax (*zakat*) required of Muslims, rather than purely as a badge of humiliation. Third, and decisively for the present, *the system is defunct*: no modern state operates a formal dhimmi order, the disabilities have been abolished across the Muslim world under modern constitutions and citizenship law, and the framework survives as classical doctrine and historical memory rather than as living law — with the chilling exception of its brief, deliberate revival by the Islamic State, which reimposed the *jizya* on Christians in the territory it held as a conscious restoration of the classical rule.

## Holding the Line

How, then, should the honest student weigh the exhibit and its defense? The defense genuinely qualifies the indictment, and the qualifications must be kept. It is false and unfair to portray the dhimmi system as uniquely or maximally cruel by the standards of its own age; in important respects it was, comparatively, a tolerant order, and it is no longer enforced anywhere as a system. To leave those facts out would be to distort the historical record and to slide from the critique of a legal doctrine toward a caricature.

But the qualifications do not overturn the core claim, which survives them intact: *the classical Islamic legal tradition prescribes, on the authority of its own scripture and the reading of its own most respected exegetes, a system in which non-Muslims may live under Muslim rule only as a subordinate legal caste, paying a religion-based tax "in a state of submission" and bearing civil disabilities assigned by faith; and this system, whatever its comparative mildness in the medieval setting, is the systematic opposite of the equal citizenship on which the American order rests.* That the dhimmi order was comparatively tolerant for its time, variably enforced, and is now largely defunct locates it accurately in history; it does not make the doctrine compatible with equal protection, and it does not erase the fact that the doctrine's logic was deliberately revived in living memory. The comparison that condemns the system is not with medieval Christendom, which was often worse, but with the principle of equal citizenship the modern constitutional order holds out — a principle the classical dhimmi framework was built to deny. From the tax on the unconverted, we turn next to a place from which the unconverted are barred altogether.
