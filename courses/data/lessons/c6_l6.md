This lesson takes up a doctrine where the gulf between classical Islamic law and the contemporary American constitutional order is at its widest and most stark — the legal treatment of homosexual acts. The American settlement, fixed by the Supreme Court's decisions in *Lawrence v. Texas* (2003) and *Obergefell v. Hodges* (2015), has decriminalized same-sex conduct and extended to it the protection of marriage. The classical Islamic legal consensus runs in precisely the opposite direction, treating same-sex intercourse as a grave crime, with death among the penalties prescribed in the major schools — a penalty that a handful of modern states still impose. As always in this course, the discipline is precision, for the claim is devastating when stated exactly and worthless when overstated.

As throughout, the subject is *a body of law and the states that enforce it*, not the character or worth of any human being. Many Muslims are gay; many Muslims reject these rulings entirely; many interpret them in ways we will examine. What is under the microscope is the classical legal doctrine, its textual basis, and what it does and does not establish — not a judgment about people.

## The Classical Doctrine

The classical position of Islamic jurisprudence (*fiqh*) on same-sex intercourse between men (in the technical vocabulary, *liwat*) is clear in its condemnation and was the subject of broad consensus across the schools: the act was held to be a grave sin and a serious crime. The disagreement among the jurists was not over whether it was criminal but over the *penalty*. Here the four Sunni schools diverged. Several major authorities held that the act was punishable by death, differing among themselves on the method; others classified it under or alongside the law of unlawful intercourse (*zina*), applying its penalties; and still others assigned a discretionary punishment (*ta'zir*) at the judge's discretion. The death penalty was thus not universal, but it was the position of weighty authorities within the tradition, and it is this — the presence of capital punishment within the mainstream classical consensus — that constitutes the exhibit.

One feature of the classical law must be stated precisely, because it cuts against overstatement: where the act was assimilated to *zina*, conviction required the same extraordinary evidentiary standard — four adult male witnesses to the act itself — which made conviction, in classical practice, exceedingly rare. The law on the books was severe; its actual application, under the strict evidentiary rules, was hedged about with obstacles. Precision requires holding both facts together: a grave penalty in the doctrine, and a high bar to its imposition in classical procedure.

## The Textual Basis

The condemnation rests on identified texts. The Qur'an returns repeatedly to the story of the people of Lot (*Lut*), who are presented as having been destroyed for their conduct, and from whose name the Arabic term for the act derives.[^1] The narrative is treated in the tradition as establishing the gravity of the act and the divine judgment upon it. Beyond the Qur'an, the juristic tradition drew on hadith reports — sayings attributed to the Prophet — including reports prescribing death for the parties, though the authenticity and strength of particular reports were debated among the scholars themselves. On the foundation of the Lot narrative and these reports, the jurists built the consensus that the act was a serious crime, and the major schools' positions on penalty followed.

The point of stating the textual basis precisely is twofold. It shows that the classical position is genuinely *grounded* in the foundational sources as the tradition read them — this is not a marginal or invented doctrine. And it shows the locus of contemporary reinterpretation, for the reformist arguments we will examine fasten precisely on how these texts should be read.

## Live State Enforcement

What lifts this from a question of historical jurisprudence to a live clash is that a number of states today criminalize same-sex acts on an Islamic-law basis, and a small number prescribe death. Among states applying capital or near-capital penalties grounded in *sharia*, observers have identified Iran, Saudi Arabia, Yemen, Mauritania, the northern states of Nigeria operating under *sharia* criminal codes, and Brunei, which introduced stoning for the offense in its penal code in 2019. Many additional Muslim-majority states criminalize the conduct with imprisonment or corporal punishment without prescribing death. This is the exhibit in its most concrete form: a classical religious penalty, still on the statute books and, in some places, still carried out.

The clash with the American order is total and admits of no compromise. The American constitutional settlement does not merely *tolerate* same-sex conduct; it affirmatively *protects* it as a matter of constitutional liberty and extends to same-sex couples the equal protection of the marriage laws. The classical *sharia* position criminalizes the same conduct, in its severest form capitally. Between a legal order that protects a thing as a constitutional right and a legal order that punishes it as a capital crime, there is no overlap. This is not a difference of emphasis or culture; it is a direct contradiction at the level of law.

## The Modern Turn

A point of precision deserves its own notice, because it cuts against a comfortable assumption. One might suppose that the severity belongs to a distant past and that modern states have softened it. In several important cases the opposite is true: the modern state, with its bureaucratic capacity and its codified penal law, has in some places made the penalty *more* systematic and enforceable than classical practice ever was. The classical *hadd*, hedged by the near-impossible four-witness standard, was rarely applied; the historical record shows few executions under the strict evidentiary rule. Modern codification can dispense with those classical restraints, replacing the evidentiary near-impossibility with statutory offenses and modern methods of proof.

The Iranian penal code after the 1979 revolution, and Brunei's introduction of stoning into its penal code in 2019, are instances in which a modern state has codified the capital penalty as enforceable statutory law rather than leaving it as a classical ideal restrained by procedure. This matters for the course's theme, because the clash with the constitutional order is not a museum piece. It is the product of contemporary states choosing, through modern legislative machinery, to enact the classical penalty in a form more administrable than the tradition itself typically allowed. The exhibit is not merely that such a penalty once existed in the books of medieval jurists; it is that modern legislatures have deliberately written it into living law.

## The Discipline of Precision

Here the course's methodological rule must be applied with care, for this is exactly the kind of claim that is ruined by overstatement and made unanswerable by exactness. The *false* version — that "Islam mandates killing all gay people," or that every Muslim or every Muslim-majority state endorses execution — is untrue and hands an opponent an easy and deserved rebuttal. Most Muslim-majority states do *not* impose the death penalty; the schools differed on the penalty; the classical evidentiary standard made conviction rare; and vast numbers of Muslims reject the capital position outright. To state the claim that way is to lose the argument.

The *accurate* version is narrower and therefore far stronger. It is this: classical Islamic jurisprudence reached a consensus that same-sex intercourse is a grave crime; the death penalty was the position of major authorities within that consensus; and a small number of modern states still impose capital or severe penalties on this basis today — in direct and total contradiction to the post-*Obergefell* American settlement that protects the same conduct as a constitutional right. Every clause of that sentence is documented; none of it can be waved away; and it does not depend on any claim about Muslims as people. That is the discipline: the exact claim is unanswerable precisely because it claims no more than the sources and the statute books establish.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Several genuine counterpoints must be weighed. First, there is a real and growing body of *reformist and minority Muslim scholarship* that contests the classical position — questioning the authenticity of the relevant hadith, arguing that the Lot narrative concerns coercion, exploitation, and the violation of hospitality rather than consensual same-sex love, distinguishing orientation from acts, and pressing for reinterpretation. Affirming Muslim voices and communities exist; they are a minority within the tradition, but they are real, and intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that the classical consensus is contested from within, not only from without.

Second, the *comparative* point. Classical Christianity and Judaism likewise condemned same-sex acts, and Western legal systems criminalized them — through sodomy laws — until strikingly recently; the United States itself did not decriminalize nationwide until 2003. The doctrinal *condemnation*, in other words, is not unique to Islam but was shared across the Abrahamic traditions and embedded in Western law within living memory. What is distinctive in the present is twofold: the *capital* penalty preserved in major classical schools, and, above all, the *live state enforcement* today, at the very moment the West has moved to decriminalization and protection. The clash is sharp not because Islam alone ever condemned the conduct, but because Islamic law alone, among the major traditions, still executes for it in some jurisdictions while the West extends it constitutional protection.

Third, *diversity and the gap between law and practice*. The states that impose death are a minority of Muslim-majority states; many criminalize without capital penalty; some have decriminalized; attitudes and enforcement vary enormously across societies and over time. Much modern enforcement proceeds through discretionary state law rather than the strict classical *hadd*, and actual executions, even where the penalty exists in law, are relatively few. None of this dissolves the clash — a religious penalty that puts people to death for consensual conduct is a profound matter even if rarely applied — but precision requires that the doctrine not be flattened into a uniform practice it does not have. The exact claim stands: the classical consensus criminalized the act with death among its penalties, a handful of states still enforce this, and the contradiction with the American constitutional order is absolute. We turn next to a domain where the clash is less about criminal penalty than about the structure of legal equality itself: the treatment of women in classical jurisprudence.
