We turn now from a question of criminal penalty to a question of legal structure — the treatment of women in classical Islamic jurisprudence. Where the previous lessons examined specific prohibitions, this one examines a set of *asymmetries* built into the classical law: differences in legal testimony, inheritance, guardianship, marriage, and dress that distinguish the legal standing of women from that of men. These are documented features of the *fiqh*, grounded in identified texts, and several are enforced by states today — and they stand in direct tension with the American constitutional principle of equal protection regardless of sex. The discipline of precision is especially vital here, because no subject is more easily distorted, in either direction, than this one.

The caution must be stated at the outset and held throughout: the subject is *classical legal doctrine and its state enforcement*, not Muslim women as people. Many Muslim women freely choose to observe practices we will examine and find dignity and meaning in them; many reject the classical rulings; many live lives wholly unlike the picture the law might suggest. To examine the law is not to pronounce on the women, and the course explicitly rejects the condescending assumption that Muslim women are a uniform class of the oppressed. What is under the microscope is a body of jurisprudence and the states that codify it.

## Veiling and the Logic of Covering

The requirement of covering rests on Qur'anic texts that instruct believing women to draw their head-coverings over themselves and to dress modestly, and that speak of an outer garment by which women may be known and not molested.[^1] From these texts the jurists developed the law of *awra* — that which must be covered — and debated its extent: whether the face and hands must be covered (the position underlying the *niqab*) or whether the covering of the hair and body, leaving the face visible, suffices (the more common position underlying the headscarf, *hijab*). The juristic literature is detailed and the schools differ, but the principle of a religiously mandated covering for women, more extensive than that required of men, is a settled feature of the classical law.

Underlying much of this law is a concept that the jurists invoke explicitly: *fitna*. The word denotes temptation, disorder, and the chaos that threatens the social order — and in the juristic discussion of women, it names the idea that the visible presence and beauty of women constitute a potential source of sexual temptation and therefore of social disorder. On this logic, the covering of women, and in stronger forms the segregation of the sexes and the restriction of women's public presence, are framed as measures to contain *fitna* — to protect the social order from a danger located in the female presence itself. It is important to state this concept precisely and from the sources, because it is the *rationale* that the tradition itself supplies, and it reveals that the law of covering is not merely about modesty in the abstract but rests on a particular theory of women, sexuality, and social order.

## The Asymmetries of the Law

Beyond dress, classical *fiqh* contains a set of specific, well-documented asymmetries in the legal standing of men and women. In the law of *testimony*, the Qur'an, in the context of recording debts, directs that two women witnesses stand in the place of one man, so that a woman's testimony counts, in certain matters, as half a man's. In the law of *inheritance*, the Qur'an assigns to a daughter half the share of a son, with a structured system of asymmetric shares throughout. In the law of *guardianship* (*wilaya*), classical doctrine holds that a woman requires a male guardian (*wali*) for her marriage contract, and the broader principle of male authority (*qiwama*) over women, drawn from a much-discussed verse, establishes a structure of male responsibility and authority within the family. In the law of *marriage and divorce*, a man may contract plural marriage — up to four wives — while a woman may not; and the husband possesses a unilateral power of repudiation (*talaq*), while the wife's access to divorce is, in classical doctrine, more restricted and typically conditional.

These are not slanders or generalizations; they are specific provisions of the classical law, each grounded in identified texts and elaborated in the juristic tradition. Stated precisely, they constitute a legal structure in which the standing of women differs systematically from that of men across the central domains of law — evidence, property, family authority, and marriage. That is the exhibit, and its strength lies in its specificity.

## State Enforcement and the Clash

Several of these provisions are enforced by states today. Various Muslim-majority jurisdictions apply asymmetric inheritance and testimony rules in their codified law; family-law regimes in many states preserve male guardianship requirements, asymmetric divorce, and the permission of polygamy; and a number of states have at times *legally mandated* covering — Iran enforces compulsory hijab by law, and Afghanistan under Taliban rule has imposed severe restrictions on women's dress, movement, education, and public life. Saudi Arabia long enforced an extensive male-guardianship system, significant parts of which have been the subject of recent reform. The picture varies enormously from state to state, but the enforcement of classical gender asymmetries through modern law is a live reality, not a historical memory.

The clash with the American constitutional order lies at the level of the foundational principle. The American settlement, through the Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee of equal protection and the body of sex-discrimination jurisprudence built upon it, holds that the law may not assign rights and standing on the basis of sex. The classical *fiqh* provisions — half-weight testimony, half-share inheritance, male guardianship, asymmetric divorce, mandated covering — assign legal standing precisely on the basis of sex. The conflict is therefore structural: it is not about any single rule but about whether sex may be a basis for differential legal treatment at all. The American order says it may not; the classical law, in these domains, says it may and must.

## The Discipline of Precision

Here, more than anywhere, exactness is everything, because the subject invites distortion from both sides. The overstated claim — that "Islam oppresses all women," that Muslim women are uniformly subjugated, that all veiling is coercion — is false, condescending, and self-defeating; it erases the agency of the millions of women who choose these practices freely, and it collapses an enormous diversity of law and life into a slogan. To argue that way is to lose, and to deserve to lose.

The accurate claim is narrower and unanswerable: classical Islamic jurisprudence contains specific, textually grounded asymmetries in the legal standing of women — in testimony, inheritance, guardianship, marriage, and dress — and a number of states enforce these asymmetries in codified law today, in structural tension with the constitutional principle of equal protection regardless of sex. That claim is about *law*, it is documented in every particular, and it stands entirely apart from any judgment about Muslim women as people or about whether a given woman experiences a given practice as oppressive or as meaningful. The clash is between two legal principles — sex-based differentiation and sex-blind equality — and stating it at that level is both exact and devastating.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The genuine counterpoints are substantial. The first and most important is *agency*. Vast numbers of Muslim women freely choose to veil, regard it as an expression of devotion, dignity, identity, or modesty, and resent the Western assumption that the covering is imposed or that it marks them as oppressed. The law that *mandates* covering in Iran or Afghanistan is one thing; the woman who chooses the headscarf in Chicago or Cairo is another, and to conflate them is both inaccurate and an erasure of her choice. The course's examination of the law makes no claim about her, and honesty requires saying so plainly.

The second is the *reformist tradition* within Islam. Islamic feminists and modernist scholars reinterpret the relevant texts — contextualizing the testimony rule to the specific commercial conditions of its revelation, reading the authority verse in light of mutual obligation, arguing that the inheritance asymmetry was paired with male financial responsibility, contesting the *fitna* framework. Many Muslim-majority states have substantially reformed family law: restricting or regulating polygamy, raising the age of marriage, expanding women's access to divorce, curtailing guardianship. The classical consensus is contested vigorously from within. The third is the *comparative* point: pre-modern Western law and classical Christianity and Judaism likewise embedded deep gender asymmetries — coverture, the denial of property and the vote to women, ecclesiastical restrictions — many of which persisted into the twentieth century. Patriarchy in law is not unique to Islam, and the West's own escape from it is recent. What is distinctive is the *live state enforcement* of specific religiously grounded asymmetries today, at the level of codified law, in tension with a constitutional equality the West has only lately achieved. The exact claim survives every counterpoint: the asymmetries are real, textually grounded, and enforced by states, and they conflict with sex-blind equality — while the women who live within or against these laws retain an agency that no examination of the law should be permitted to erase. We turn next to a doctrine that bears directly on the question of civic belonging itself: the principle of loyalty and disavowal.
