We have talked about the heretic's situation — the consensus they face, the burden they bear, the marks that separate the prophet from the crank. Now we turn to the heretic themselves and ask a more intimate question: what kind of person does this? What sort of mind and temperament is required to look at the massed conviction of everyone around them and conclude, and then declare, that everyone is wrong? It is a rare and peculiar thing to do. Most of us, most of the time, go along — and, as we will see, the pressure to go along is far stronger than we like to admit. The dissenter is the person in whom something resists that pressure. Understanding what that something is, and what it costs, tells us a great deal about how new truths ever break through at all.

## The Crushing Weight of Conformity

To appreciate the dissenter, you must first appreciate the force they are resisting, and here psychology has delivered one of its most sobering findings. In the 1950s, Solomon Asch ran a now-famous series of experiments. He seated a subject in a group and showed everyone a simple task: match the length of a line to one of three others. The answer was obvious. But the other people in the room were confederates instructed to give the same wrong answer aloud, one after another, before the real subject's turn. The question was whether the subject would trust the plain evidence of their own eyes, or cave to the unanimous group.[^1]

The results were dismaying. A large proportion of subjects, confronted with a group unanimously asserting something they could see was false, denied the evidence of their own eyes and conformed — giving the wrong answer to match the crowd. Many did this repeatedly. Afterward, some said they had genuinely come to doubt their own perception; others admitted they saw the truth but could not bear to be the lone voice against everyone else. The experiment revealed how enormous, how nearly irresistible, the pull toward conformity is — strong enough to make ordinary people deny what is in front of their faces rather than stand alone. Against this backdrop, the dissenter is revealed as a genuine anomaly: the rare person in whom the pull toward agreement fails to override the evidence, who will say the true thing while everyone else says the false one. Asch also found something hopeful and important — that the presence of even a *single* ally, one other person willing to break with the group, made dissent vastly easier. The truly rare figure is the one who dissents *entirely alone*, with no ally at all.

## The Temperament of the Dissenter

What kind of person can do this? Research on nonconformity points to a recognizable cluster of traits. High *openness to experience* — a hunger for the new, the unusual, the untried — inclines a person to entertain ideas the conventional mind rejects. A low *need for social approval*, an unusual indifference to being liked or accepted, frees a person to hold positions that invite disapproval. A high tolerance for *conflict and social discomfort* lets them endure the hostility that dissent provokes without folding. Add a streak of *independence* verging on stubbornness, a *self-confidence* strong enough to trust one's own judgment against the crowd, and often an *obsessive focus* that fastens onto a problem and will not let go, and you have the raw psychological material of the heretic.

Notice that several of these are not, in the ordinary sense, comfortable or even likable traits. The willingness to be disliked shades into a certain prickliness; the independence shades into disagreeableness; the self-confidence shades into arrogance; the obsessive focus shades into monomania. The dissenter is frequently not an easy person. The very qualities that allow someone to stand against the consensus — to not care what others think, to trust their own vision over authority, to persist in the face of universal opposition — are qualities that also make for difficult colleagues, stubborn friends, and abrasive personalities. This is not incidental. The social machinery of conformity is powerful precisely because most people are wired to seek approval and avoid conflict; the person who escapes that machinery has, almost by definition, an unusual and somewhat awkward relationship to the approval of others. The price of not being moved by the crowd is often not being warmed by it either.

## The Same Profile, Two Fates

Here we must confront the disturbing fact at the center of this course, because it appears again at the level of psychology: the temperament that produces the visionary is the *same* temperament that produces the crank and the fanatic. Independence of mind, indifference to disapproval, unshakable self-confidence, obsessive focus, the willingness to defy all authority — this identical psychological profile can belong to a Galileo or to a conspiracy theorist, to a Semmelweis or to a deluded zealot certain that he alone has seen the truth. The traits themselves are *neutral*. They determine whether a person is *capable* of dissent; they say nothing whatever about whether the dissent is *correct*.

This is why the psychology of the dissenter, fascinating as it is, cannot by itself tell us whom to believe. Knowing that someone has the temperament of a heretic — that they are independent, confident, indifferent to disapproval, obsessively focused — tells us they are the *sort* of person who defies consensus, but it does not tell us whether this particular defiance is prophecy or delusion. The crank and the prophet are, temperamentally, close cousins; they differ not in personality but in whether their vision happens to be true and in the epistemic discipline they bring to testing it, which was the subject of the last lesson. The trait makes the dissent possible; only the evidence makes it right. We admire the courage of the temperament while reserving judgment on the content of the claim — and we remember that the same courage animates the fool.

## The Cost

Whatever the fate of their ideas, dissenters pay, and the cost is often crushing. Socially, they are ostracized — shunned, ridiculed, cast out of the community whose agreement they refused. Professionally, they are frequently destroyed: careers ended, positions lost, reputations ruined, work suppressed. We will meet the physician Ignaz Semmelweis, driven from his profession and into an asylum for insisting that doctors were killing mothers with unwashed hands; the physicist Ludwig Boltzmann, worn down by the hostility of colleagues who denied the reality of atoms, who took his own life not long before he was proven right; the many heretics whose vindication, when it came at all, came only after their deaths. The price of standing where the dissenter stands is real, and it is frequently paid in full — in loneliness, in ruin, and sometimes in despair or madness under the sheer strain of being the one who is right, or believes they are, while all the world says otherwise.

Why, then, does anyone do it? The motives are as varied as the people. Some are driven by an overpowering commitment to truth — the simple inability to un-see what they have seen, to pretend agreement they do not feel, to say the false thing to buy peace. Some are driven by temperament alone, by a bloody-minded refusal to be told what to think. Some are moved by a sense of mission, even a messianic conviction that they have been given a truth to defend. And some — this must be said — are driven by less noble things: by ego, by grievance, by the intoxicating desire to be special, to be the lone genius against the blind herd. This last motive is worth naming honestly, because it is exactly the motive that most often produces the crank, who wants the *identity* of the persecuted visionary without the underlying truth that would earn it. The desire to be a heretic is not the same as being right, and it can be a powerful and corrupting drive in its own.

## Why Society Needs Them Anyway

Set the individual aside and consider the dissenter's role in the whole society, and something remarkable comes into view. From the point of view of a community, dissenters function like a *portfolio of bets*. Most of them are wrong — that we have established repeatedly. But a society that produced no dissenters at all, that achieved perfect conformity, would also produce no corrections to its errors, no challenges to its false consensuses, no source of the rare true heresy that overturns a mistaken orthodoxy and moves knowledge forward. The occasional vindicated prophet is worth the many mistaken cranks, because there is no way to get the one without tolerating the many — and because we cannot tell in advance which is which. The temperament that is individually costly and usually mistaken is, at the level of the society, *indispensable*.

This is one of the deepest arguments for freedom of thought and speech, and it goes back at least to John Stuart Mill, who argued that even false dissent serves a vital function — forcing the holders of true beliefs to defend and understand them, keeping live convictions from decaying into dead dogma, and preserving the conditions under which the rare true heresy can emerge and be heard. A society that silences its dissenters to be rid of the cranks will, in the same act, silence the prophets it cannot distinguish from them, and so cut itself off from its own capacity to correct its errors. The dissenter, difficult and usually wrong, is the price a society pays for the possibility of ever being told, by one of its own members, that it is mistaken. It is a price worth paying, and understanding why is central to everything this course will argue about the open society and the debt it owes to those who refuse to conform.

## The Necessary Caution

The caution here is subtle, because the dissenter's courage is genuinely admirable and it is easy to let that admiration do too much work. The willingness to stand alone against the crowd, to endure ridicule and ruin for a conviction, to pay the terrible price of being early — this is a real virtue, and the great vindicated heretics displayed it magnificently. But admiring the *courage* must never slide into endorsing the *conclusions*. Courage and correctness are entirely separate things. The crank who ruins his career defending a false theory shows exactly the same courage as the prophet who ruins his defending a true one; the fanatic who dies for a delusion dies as bravely as the martyr who dies for a truth. The temperament earns our respect for its contribution to the whole; the individual claim earns our assent only by meeting the evidence.

So we hold, once more, the double vision this course keeps demanding. We honor the dissenting temperament for the priceless service it renders society — the willingness of a few to think and say what the crowd will not, without which no error could ever be corrected. And we judge each dissenter's actual claims coldly, on the evidence, refusing to let the admirable courage of standing alone persuade us that the lonely stander is therefore right. The heretic's courage is a virtue; the heretic's correctness is a separate question; and confusing the two is the error that turns the honest study of dissent into the credulous worship of it. With the heretic's situation, discipline, and psychology now before us, we have nearly finished anatomizing the type, and can soon turn from the anatomy of heresy to the heretics themselves — beginning, in the next unit, with the men and women who broke what everyone knew about the physical world.
