At any given moment, in any given society, there is a range of ideas one can respectably hold and openly say — and, surrounding it, a vast territory of ideas considered too radical, too dangerous, or too absurd to be entertained by any serious person. The boundary between the two is invisible to those inside it; we simply experience some positions as reasonable and others as unthinkable, and mistake this feeling for a permanent fact about the world. It is not permanent. The boundary moves. Ideas that were once unthinkable become radical, then debatable, then acceptable, then obvious, then so taken for granted that their former unthinkability is forgotten. Understanding how that boundary shifts — and the heretic's role in shifting it — is the subject of this lesson.

## The Window of the Thinkable

The concept has a name: the *Overton window*, after the policy analyst Joseph Overton, who observed that at any time only a certain range of positions is considered politically and socially acceptable — a "window" of legitimate discourse.[^1] Ideas inside the window can be advocated by respectable people without penalty; ideas outside it are dismissed as radical, extreme, or unthinkable, and those who voice them pay a price in credibility or worse. Though Overton framed it in terms of public policy, the concept applies to every domain in which a society holds a consensus about what may be believed and said — science, morality, religion, art. There is always a window, and always a shadowed region beyond it.

The crucial insight is that this window is not fixed. It *moves* over time, sometimes slowly, sometimes with startling speed, and as it moves, ideas migrate across the boundary of the thinkable. A position that was once beyond the pale — unmentionable, disqualifying, the mark of a crank or a monster — can, over years or decades, drift into the window and become first a respectable minority view, then a mainstream one, then the settled consensus, and finally so obvious that people cannot imagine having thought otherwise. And the reverse also happens: ideas once comfortably inside the window drift out of it, becoming first controversial, then unacceptable, then unspeakable. The window is always in motion, and what feels eternally reasonable or eternally unthinkable is merely what happens to be inside or outside the window *now*.

## The Heretic at the Edge

Here is where the heretic enters, for the heretic is precisely the person who stands at the *edge* of the window, or beyond it, voicing what the consensus considers unthinkable. And by doing so — even when they are dismissed, even when they lose — heretics play a central role in *moving* the window. When someone voices a previously unthinkable idea, they make it, for the first time, *sayable*. The idea enters the discourse, if only as the thing everyone rejects. And once an idea can be spoken and argued against, it has already ceased to be truly unthinkable; it has become merely radical. The heretic who is mocked for an outrageous position has, in the very act of being mocked, dragged that position from the unthinkable into the debatable — and the debatable is halfway into the window.

This reveals a subtle and important dynamic: heretics shift the window not only when they succeed but sometimes even when they *fail*. The dissenter who stakes out an extreme position, though rejected, moves the boundary by making a slightly less extreme position seem moderate by comparison — the "radical flank" that, by occupying the far edge, makes the once-radical reformer in the middle look reasonable. Over time, through the repeated voicing of the unthinkable, through argument and event and generational change, positions that began far outside the window are pulled toward and into it. The heretic is the leading edge of this movement — the one willing to say first what the consensus is not yet ready to hear, and thereby to begin the long process by which the unthinkable becomes the obvious.

## The Window Moves Both Ways — Morally

Now we arrive at the fact that makes the Overton window so important, and so dangerous to misunderstand: *the window's movement is morally neutral*. It can shift toward truth and justice, or toward error and atrocity, and the mechanism is exactly the same in both directions. On the hopeful side, the great moral advances of history are stories of the window moving toward justice: the abolition of slavery was once literally unthinkable — slavery was simply how the world worked, a universal and unquestioned institution — and it moved, through the labor of heretics who first dared to call it evil, from the unthinkable, to the radical, to the debatable, to the settled conviction that it must end. The same arc describes the extension of rights and dignity to those once excluded from them: what was unthinkable became, in time, obvious.

But the window moves toward horror by the identical process. Atrocities that a society would once have found unthinkable can be made thinkable, step by step, as the window shifts — as the previously unspeakable is spoken, normalized, debated, and finally accepted, until a society finds itself doing what it once could not have imagined doing. The persecution and destruction of whole groups of people, the abandonment of hard-won protections, the embrace of cruelties once beyond the pale — these too move into the window through the voicing of the previously unthinkable and its gradual normalization. The very neutrality of the mechanism is the point: the process that lets moral progress happen is the same process that lets moral catastrophe happen. The window's movement tells you *that* the thinkable is changing; it tells you nothing about whether it is changing for better or worse.

## What This Should Teach Us

The existence of the Overton window carries two lessons that must be held together, and getting the balance right is everything. The first lesson is *humility about our own certainties*. What our society currently finds obvious and what it finds unthinkable are not eternal truths but the contents of a window that has moved before and will move again. Some of what we are certain of will look wrong to our descendants; some of what we find unthinkable, they will find obvious, and vice versa. We are inside a window we cannot fully see, mistaking its boundaries for the edges of reason itself, exactly as every previous generation did. This should make us humble — slower to assume that the current consensus is the final truth, and slower to dismiss the dissenter merely because they stand outside the present window.

But the second lesson is a warning against the opposite error: humility about the window's contingency must not collapse into *relativism*. The fact that the window moves, and that what is thinkable changes, does *not* mean that all positions are equal or that there is no truth of the matter beneath the shifting consensus. Abolition was not merely *newer* than slavery; it was *better*, truer, more just — the window moved toward something real, not just toward something different. That the boundary of the thinkable is contingent does not make the things on either side of it equally valid. There remain better and worse answers, truer and falser beliefs, more and less just arrangements, independent of where the window happens to sit. The window's movement is a fact about what a society will *entertain*; it is not the measure of what is *true* or *good*.

## The Familiar Frame

There is a resonance here worth naming, for readers of a certain cast of mind will recognize it. The Overton window is another instance of the invisible frame we mistake for reality itself — the assumptions so pervasive that we do not experience them as assumptions at all, but as the simple shape of the reasonable. Like the deepest inherited categories through which a culture thinks, the window is a body of water we swim in, unaware that it is water, unaware that its edges could be anywhere else. And like all such frames, it becomes visible only when someone crosses it — when a heretic voices the unthinkable and, in the shock of that crossing, reveals to everyone that there was a boundary there at all, and that it was not the edge of the world but merely the edge of what we had been willing to think.

This is why heretics are epistemically valuable even apart from whether they are right: they make the window visible. By standing at its edge or beyond it, they show the rest of us where the boundary is, and thereby remind us that it is a boundary and not a horizon — that there is a territory beyond the thinkable, and that some of what lies out there may be true. A society that never encountered its heretics would never see the edges of its own window, and would mistake the accidental limits of its imagination for the necessary limits of reason. The heretic, whatever else they are, is the one who shows us the wall we did not know was there.

## The Necessary Caution

And now the caution, which is the most important thing in the lesson, because the Overton window is one of the most easily abused concepts we will encounter. The fact that heretics shift the window, and that the window's past movements include great moral triumphs, does *not* mean that every window-pushing heretic is a moral pioneer, or that pushing the window is itself a virtue. The window moves toward atrocity as readily as toward justice, by the identical mechanism, and the person normalizing a monstrous idea uses precisely the same move — "I am merely saying the unthinkable, shifting the boundary, ahead of my time" — as the person advancing a just cause. Window-shifting is a *mechanism*, not a merit. Whether shifting the window in a given direction is good or evil depends entirely on whether the new position is actually *better* than the old — which is a separate question that the concept of the window cannot answer and must not be used to dodge.

This is the trap: to hear that great reforms began as unthinkable heresies, and conclude that one's own unthinkable position is therefore probably a great reform in the making. It is exactly what the moral pioneer and the moral vandal both believe about themselves. The concept of the Overton window is a powerful tool for understanding *how* the thinkable changes; it is worthless, and dangerous, as a tool for deciding *whether* a given change is good. For that, one must leave the mechanism behind and return to the substance — to the hard question of whether the new position is true and just, judged on its merits, which no amount of talk about shifting windows can settle. The window tells you the boundary is moving. It never tells you whether to cheer. With that firmly in hand, we turn to the final and darkest complication in the anatomy of heresy: the ways the label "heretic" itself can be forged and weaponized.

The abolition of slavery and the extension of rights are standard illustrations of the window moving toward justice; the concept's crucial and often-neglected feature is that the mechanism is morally neutral and moves toward atrocity by the same process, so that the window describes what a society will *entertain* without indicating whether a given shift is toward truth and good or away from them.
