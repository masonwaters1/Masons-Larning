"A specter is haunting Europe," begins the most consequential pamphlet ever printed, and the sentence has aged into something its author did not intend: a plain statement of fact about the world since 1848. No body of ideas in human history has moved more people, seized more states, killed more human beings, or survived more refutations than the system Karl Marx built in the reading room of the British Museum. At its twentieth-century height, regimes calling themselves Marxist governed more than a third of the human race; the most careful scholarly accounting places the death toll of those regimes at roughly one hundred million people — not war dead, but their own citizens, killed by execution, engineered famine, and the camps; and yet, a generation after the system's flagship collapsed in Moscow, Marx's categories are taught, his diagnosis quoted, and his banner carried in the universities of the very societies that won. That triple fact — the scale of the claim, the scale of the catastrophe, the persistence of the appeal — is why this course exists. Marxism is not a museum piece; it is the most successful failed idea in the history of thought, and understanding why it fails, why it kills, and why it returns is among the most practically urgent intellectual tasks a citizen of a free society can undertake.

## The Scale of the Claim

Begin with what Marxism actually claims, because its ambition is the first thing modern readers underestimate.[^1] Marx did not offer a policy program, a party platform, or even, primarily, an economics; he offered a total account of human history and a guarantee about its destination. History, on his theory, is not chaos or providence but law: a determinate sequence of stages — ancient slavery, feudalism, capitalism, socialism, communism — driven by a single engine, the struggle between economic classes, and headed toward a knowable end: the abolition of private property in the means of production, the dissolution of classes, and with them the end of exploitation, of the state, and ultimately of scarcity's tyranny over human life. This is not a modest hypothesis. It claims to explain everything social — law, religion, morality, art, and philosophy are all "superstructure," reflections of the economic base — and it claims the authority not of preference but of science: Engels, at Marx's graveside, declared that "just as Darwin discovered the law of development of organic nature, so Marx discovered the law of development of human history." The claim's practical corollary made it lethal in ways the course will document: if history's direction is known and its destination is paradise, then those who resist are not opponents to be persuaded but obstacles to be removed — reactionaries standing athwart a law of nature; and the men who act as history's instruments answer to a tribunal higher than any existing law or morality. Every Marxist regime's terror was justified in exactly these terms, from Lenin's decrees forward — which is why the course insists, against a common evasion, that the connection between the theory and the terror is a question to be examined, not an accusation to be waved away or assumed. Lesson by lesson, that examination is much of what this course is.

## The Scale of the Catastrophe

State the record now, in summary, so the course's stakes are never abstract; Unit 3 will spend thirteen lessons earning every number. The Soviet Union: Lenin's terror and civil-war famine; Stalin's collectivization war on the peasantry and the terror-famine that starved millions in Ukraine — the Holodomor; the Great Terror's quota-driven shootings; the Gulag's decades. China: the Great Leap Forward's famine, the largest in recorded human history — the serious scholarly estimates run from the tens of millions — followed by the Cultural Revolution's decade of organized savagery. Cambodia: the Khmer Rouge killed roughly a quarter of the national population in under four years — proportionally the most intense self-inflicted catastrophe of the century. North Korea's dynastic famine-state; Ethiopia's Red Terror and famine; the Vietnamese boat people; Cuba's firing squads and permanent exodus. The Black Book of Communism's much-debated total — on the order of one hundred million dead — will be examined with its methodological controversies in full (Unit 3's closing lessons), because this course does not launder numbers even in a good cause; but no serious accounting, including those of the Black Book's harshest critics, brings the toll below many tens of millions, and the course's point survives every adjustment: this is the largest man-made, peacetime death toll ever attached to an idea. Two disciplines govern how the course handles it. First, specificity: names, dates, decrees, and survivor testimony, not round numbers alone — the difference between knowing about the Holodomor and reading a requisition order. Second, the honest question stated in advance: were these crimes the perversion of Marxism or its application? The course will argue, with evidence, for a precise answer — that the terror was not required by Marx's texts in detail but was made probable by the theory's structure (the known destination, the class enemy, the science that cannot be wrong) and that the experiment's repetition, in every culture and climate, with the same result, is itself data. But the argument will be earned against the strongest replies, because the reader deserves the verdict of a trial, not a rally.

## The Persistence of the Appeal

The third fact is the strangest and, for the citizen, the most urgent: none of this has killed the idea. The Soviet collapse of 1989–91 was as close to a controlled experiment's verdict as history supplies — the flagship regime, after seven decades, with a continent's resources, abandoned by its own rulers as unworkable — and yet within a generation Marx's stock had recovered in the academy, "late capitalism" had become the educated cant for whatever displeases, and surveys of the young in the free world showed startling sympathy for socialism as a word, if not a memory. Why? The course's answer occupies its last two units and is previewed here in three theses. Because the appeal was never primarily economic: Marxism is, in structure, a religion — fall, chosen class, apocalypse, redemption — and religions are not refuted by famines (Unit 1's closing lesson, and Unit 5 throughout). Because its psychological engine — resentment of the successful, moralized as justice; the intoxication of a theory that explains everything and assigns the believer a heroic role — renews itself in every generation, particularly among intellectuals, for reasons Hayek, Aron, and in our own day Jordan Peterson have anatomized (Unit 5). And because the grievances it feeds on are perpetually real: inequality, precarity, elite failure, and the genuine cruelties of every actually existing market order supply the openings, and a critique of Marxism that answers only the theory, and never the grievances, loses the next generation on schedule — the sobering thesis of the course's final lesson. The free society's case must be made, not assumed; that is this course's other half.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The course's method is its sister seminars': the strongest opposing case, built into every lesson and answered rather than avoided — beginning with the case against the course itself. A skeptic may fairly ask: is this a seminar or an execution? Three commitments answer. First, Marx will be read, not just read about — the Manifesto, Capital's core arguments, the theory reconstructed at full strength before it is judged; the reader will encounter what is genuinely powerful in Marx (the analysis of capitalism's dynamism and instability, the moral force against real nineteenth-century horrors, predictions about globalization that landed) in a dedicated lesson, because a demolition of a strawman demolishes nothing. Second, distinctions will be kept that partisans of both sides blur: Marxism is not the welfare state, democratic socialism is not Leninism, and the course will say so explicitly (Unit 6), because a critique that flattens Sweden into Stalin forfeits the credibility this subject demands. Third, the critics themselves will be audited — Sowell, Hayek, Peterson, and the rest get their counterpoints too, because the course's authority rests on its willingness to apply the same standard everywhere. What the course will not do is pretend neutrality about the verdict: the evidence, it will argue, convicts — the theory fails on its own terms, wars with human nature, produced catastrophe in every implementation, and persists by psychological rather than intellectual power. But the conviction will be argued, sourced, and exposed to cross-examination at every step. The trial opens where every trial should: with the accused as a human being. The man from Trier — brilliant, furious, exiled, and strange — is next.
