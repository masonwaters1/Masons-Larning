Unit 1 ended by taking the lab coat off the religion; Unit 2 puts the coat back on and tests the science as science — and it begins at the exact point where everything rests, because Marxism's architecture has the unusual property of a single foundation stone. The labor theory of value is not one Marxist doctrine among many: the exploitation theorem (L5) is a corollary of it; the surplus-value engine of Capital (L7) runs on it; the "scientific" superiority over merely moral socialism is staked on it; and the claim that profit is unpaid labor — the claim that made millions of converts and justified every expropriation — is meaningless without it. This lesson conducts the full trial the earlier lessons promised: the theory stated at its strongest, in its classical pedigree; the revolution that dissolved it; the internal contradictions that its own volumes confessed; the empirical anomalies its patches could not absorb; and the modern rescue attempts, examined honestly, including the remarkable fact that the ablest Marxist philosophers of the last half-century have themselves abandoned the theory — a concession whose consequences for the system they have been less eager to total. The sister seminar in the Free-Market Tradition performed parts of this autopsy from the economics side; this lesson performs it from inside Marxism, because the verdict matters most when reached in the defendant's own courtroom.

## The Theory at Its Strongest

Fairness first: the labor theory was not a Marxist eccentricity but the inherited mainstream of classical economics, and Marx could justly claim to be radicalizing Adam Smith and David Ricardo rather than inventing.[^1] The intuition is ancient and genuinely powerful: things exchange in ratios; the ratios seem to need a common measure; and the obvious candidate is cost, whose ultimate substance is human toil — the deer that costs two hours hunts against the beaver that costs one at two-to-one, in Smith's primitive illustration, and Ricardo built the classical system on labor-quantities regulating exchange value. Marx tightened the inheritance into his own precise formulation: value is socially necessary labor time — the labor required at prevailing technique and average skill (so laziness creates no value; an hour wasted is not an hour embodied), with skilled labor counting as a multiple of simple labor, and with the crucial rider that the labor must be socially validated: goods no one wants embody no value however long they took. On this foundation the exploitation theorem rises with the elegance L5 displayed: if all value is congealed labor, and the laborer receives in wages less value than his labor deposits, the difference — surplus value — is extracted unpaid, and profit stands revealed as theft with paperwork. Feel the theory's strength before the trial: it explains exchange with one variable; it grounds a moral indictment in an apparently technical fact; and it carried the authority of the entire classical tradition. In 1867 a reasonable person could hold it. The question is what a reasonable person could hold after 1871, 1896, and 1977 — the three dates of the collapse.

## 1871: The Revolution That Dissolved the Question

The marginal revolution — Jevons in England, Menger in Vienna, Walras in Lausanne, independently and near-simultaneously — did not answer the classical question so much as dissolve it, and the sister course's lessons on the revolution supply the full story; here is the payload aimed at Marx. Value, the marginalists showed, is not a substance embedded in objects by production; it is a relation between valuing minds and scarce means, determined at the margin — by the usefulness of the next unit, not the toil behind the last one. The water-diamond paradox that had forced the classicals to split "use value" from "exchange value" (and then ground the latter in labor) dissolves instantly: water is life itself but abundant, so the marginal gallon is worth little; diamonds are frivolous but scarce, so the marginal stone is worth much — no labor substance required. Costs, on this analysis, do not determine value; they are determined by it: the vineyard's land and labor command high prices because buyers value the wine, not the reverse — the causal arrow of the entire classical system, reversed. And the reversal is fatal to the exploitation theorem specifically, because the theorem needs labor to be the source of value in order for profit to be labor unpaid; once value flows from consumers' marginal valuations, the wage is simply the price of one input among several — determined, like all prices, by (marginal) productivity and scarcity — and the "unpaid residue" is no longer theft's fingerprint but the compensation of the other inputs: capital's waiting (the worker is paid today; the product sells in a year — someone advances the interval, and the interval has a price) and enterprise's risk. Böhm-Bawerk's development of exactly this point — the time-structure of production as the honest explanation of the margin Marx called theft — is the sister course's set piece, and the reader has it; what this lesson adds is the historical observation: within a generation of 1871, the entire economics profession, across every school and country, had crossed the river, and no serious value theory since has been built on embodied labor. Marxism alone stayed on the far bank — for reasons L10 explained: foundations of faith are not vacated on new evidence.

## 1896: The Confession and the Close

The internal collapse the course has twice previewed now gets its full statement, because it is the rare case of a system convicted by its own posthumous testimony. Volume I's theory requires goods to exchange at labor values; Volume I also knows the fatal fact — competition equalizes profit rates across industries, while industries differ enormously in their ratios of labor to machinery, so if goods sold at labor values, labor-heavy industries would earn systematically higher profits, which they observably do not. Marx acknowledged the contradiction and promised the solution in Volume III. When Engels published it (1894), the solution was the "transformation": goods do not exchange at their values; they exchange at "prices of production" (cost plus average profit), deviating from labor values systematically — with the labor theory retained only as an aggregate claim (total value equals total price; total surplus value equals total profit) operating invisibly beneath the actual price system. Böhm-Bawerk's Karl Marx and the Close of His System (1896) drew the verdict that has never been overturned: a law of exchange that governs no actual exchange has been abandoned, not saved; the third volume "contradicts, and must contradict, the first"; the aggregate identities are unfalsifiable bookkeeping. The twentieth century's mathematical economists then finished the demolition from inside sympathy's own camp: Bortkiewicz (1906–07) proved Marx's transformation arithmetic internally inconsistent (inputs left untransformed); the corrections that fixed the math showed the aggregate identities cannot in general both hold; and the Sraffa-school reckoning culminated in Ian Steedman's Marx after Sraffa (1977), which demonstrated — with tools friendly to the classical tradition — that labor values are analytically redundant: everything the theory computes (prices, profits) can be computed from technology and wages directly, with the value magnitudes an idle wheel, and in perverse cases (joint production) the labor accounting yields negative values while profits stay positive, reducing "profit is surplus labor" to incoherence. Note the provenance, because it is the lesson's quiet centerpiece: 1896 was an Austrian; 1907 was a statistician correcting Marx's algebra; 1977 was a school of economists opposed to marginalism and sympathetic to the classical tradition — the foundation was condemned from every direction, including the defendant's own experts.

## The Patches, and the Concession

The theory's career since is a study in the escape-patterns L10 predicted, and the course examines the three main patches honestly. The "socially necessary" rider is the first: pressed by counterexamples (mud pies embody labor and no value; land and old masters embody no labor and vast value), defenders lean on the rider — labor counts only as the market validates it — which saves the theory by circularity: if "value-creating labor" is defined as whatever the market rewards, the theory no longer explains prices but repeats them, with utility smuggled in through the validation clause; a theory rescued by its own competitor is not rescued. The skilled-labor reduction is the second: skilled hours count as multiples of simple hours — but the multiple can only be read off relative wages, which are prices, so the value theory again presupposes the price system it claims to ground. The third is the honest one, and it changed the field: the Analytical Marxists of the 1970s–80s — the movement's ablest philosophical minds — conceded the theory outright. G.A. Cohen granted the labor theory of value false and attempted to rebuild the exploitation charge without it; John Roemer reconstructed "exploitation" as an artifact of unjust initial property distributions, explicitly dropping the surplus-labor mechanics. The course engages the reconstruction seriously because it is serious — and notes what the concession costs: exploitation-as-unjust-endowments is no longer a claim about the wage relation at all (a worker hired at market wages by a justly-acquired firm is, on Roemer's own terms, not exploited), the indictment of profit as such collapses into a contestable theory of distributive justice requiring independent defense against Nozick and the whole liberal tradition, and the word "exploitation" survives as branding on a different product. When the system's own philosophers concede the foundation and keep only the vocabulary, the trial's verdict writes itself.

## The Honest Counterpoint

What remains for the defense, stated at strength. Some economists note that empirical correlations between labor content and prices are surprisingly high across industries — true, and unsurprising, since labor is the largest cost share in most production; correlation with the largest input is not vindication of a causal monopoly, and the same data fit cost-of-production theories with no exploitation theorem attached. Others defend the theory as a "qualitative" insight — capitalism as a system organized around the purchase of human time — which the course happily grants as sociology while insisting it grounds no theorem: that employers buy time is common ground; that the purchase is theft required the quantitative machinery, and the machinery is gone. And the deepest defense simply relocates the claim: the moral conviction that workers deserve more, or that returns to mere ownership are suspect, can be held without the labor theory. Indeed it can — and that is precisely the course's conclusion: with the labor theory's collapse, Marxism's economic indictment of capitalism reverts to a moral assertion, standing in line with every other distributive-justice claim, obliged to argue against rivals on philosophical grounds, stripped of the scientific privilege that let it dismiss argument as ideology. The "science" that licensed expropriation was, at its foundation, a nineteenth-century value theory that died in the 1870s, was confessed against in the 1890s, and was buried by its own heirs in the 1970s. One pillar remains before the human-nature lessons: even granting socialism its aims, could a planned economy function at all? The answer was delivered in 1920, in twenty pages that called the century — the calculation problem, next.
