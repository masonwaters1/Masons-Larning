The previous lesson ended on a fair challenge: perhaps the incentive mechanism dooms only compulsory, national-scale collectivism — while small, voluntary communities of the committed, self-selected for idealism and free of the state's dead hand, can make "from each according to his ability" live. This is not a strawman; it is the strongest remaining form of the socialist wager, and history has tested it with unusual generosity: hundreds of intentional communities — Owenite, Fourierist, Icarian, anarchist, religious, and Zionist — planted across two centuries, mostly in the tolerant spaces of America and Palestine/Israel, by believers who staked their lives on the experiment. This lesson reads the results as the natural experiment they collectively constitute, and the findings are more interesting than either triumphalism: secular socialist communes died young, at rates that permit no excuse-making; religious communes lasted dramatically longer, for reasons that confirm this course's religion thesis from the other direction; and the greatest of all the experiments — the Israeli kibbutz, communal socialism's genuine success story — survived a heroic century by mechanisms economists have now mapped precisely, and then privatized itself, from within, by the votes of its own children. The communes' verdict is the incentive argument's completion: voluntary socialism can run — on sacred discipline, small scale, and a founding generation's fervor — and cannot reproduce itself; and what the exceptions share tells us exactly which features of human nature were doing the work all along.

## The Owenite and Fourierist Graveyard

Begin with the nineteenth century's great wave, because its failures were fast, well-documented, and diagnosed by the participants.[^1] Robert Owen — wealthy manufacturer, humane reformer, and the man for whom the word "socialism" was substantially coined — purchased the town of New Harmony, Indiana, in 1825 and invited the world to found the new moral order: property common, competition abolished, character to be re-formed by environment (Owen's explicit doctrine — man's character is made for him, not by him — is L15's engineering project in embryo). The community attracted a thousand settlers, ran through Owen's fortune, split into rival constitutions — seven in two years by the standard count — and dissolved by 1827; Owen's own son Robert Dale Owen, a believer who lived it, left the era's most quoted post-mortem: the experiment gathered "a heterogeneous collection of radicals, enthusiastic devotees to principle, honest latitudinarians, and lazy theorists, with a sprinkling of unprincipled sharpers thrown in" — the free-rider problem, in period costume: open doors plus common store selects for exactly the members a commune cannot survive. The Fourierist wave of the 1840s — some thirty-odd "phalanxes" inspired by Fourier's system, including the famous Brook Farm of the Transcendentalists — replayed the pattern at scale: average lifespan of the phalanxes, by the standard tabulations, on the order of two years; Brook Farm itself limped six, its intellectuals discovering (Hawthorne's rueful testimony survives in fiction and letters) that manure does not respect genius and that the enthusiastic dwindle when the novelty does. Cabet's Icarians, Owen's British successors, the anarchist colonies of the century's end — the graveyard filled on schedule, and the pattern held with remarkable consistency: secular socialist communities, founded on shared ideas rather than shared worship, collapsed within a handful of years, from the same internal causes their own records name — withheld effort, quarrels over distribution and governance, the exit of the able, the arrival of the idle. External pressure and undercapitalization (the standard defensive explanations) killed some; but the diagnostic fact cuts through: their religious contemporaries, facing identical markets, climates, and hostile neighbors, survived by decades and generations — which isolates the variable.

## The Religious Exception, and What It Proves

For the century's enduring communes were the godly ones: the Shakers (lasting two centuries, peaking at several thousand members), the Harmonists and Amana Inspirationists (each seven-plus decades of communal economy, both prosperous), Oneida (three decades of full communism under Noyes's perfectionist theology, ending — the era's best irony — by incorporating as the silverware company that still bears its name), and the Hutterites, running communal agriculture continuously since the sixteenth century, now hundreds of colonies strong. Modern research quantified the gap: Richard Sosis's comparative study of two hundred nineteenth-century American communes found religious communities dramatically outlasting secular ones — several-fold differences in survival at every duration, with the strictest (costliest) religious codes predicting the longest lives; among secular communes, demands and duration showed no such relationship. The mechanism, and its meaning for this course, deserve careful statement. Communal production's fatal disease is free-riding, and free-riding is constrained only by monitoring and sanction — which small religious communities possess in unmatched strength: an all-seeing God monitors what no steward can; costly ritual and sacrifice (plain dress, dietary codes, celibacy at the extreme) filter out low-commitment joiners and signal commitment continuously; sacred meaning reframes drudgery as worship (the previous cluster's Colossians 3:23, operationalized); and excommunication gives sanction teeth. Note what this finding does and does not say. It does not say religion suspends human nature — it says religion is the most powerful technology ever evolved for managing it: the Shaker barn was swept because God watched and the elders could expel. And it completes this course's religion thesis (L10) from the material side: communal economics runs only on sacred fuel — which is why Marxism, needing commune-scale solidarity at national scale, necessarily became (as Unit 1 argued it structurally was) a religion, with the Party as church, surveillance as omniscience, and the camps as excommunication; the secular commune's failure and the total state's terror are the same functional gap, filled by piety in one case and police in the other. The Hutterites add the final design detail: their colonies deliberately split at around 120–150 souls — below the threshold where everyone knows everyone, monitoring is ambient, and shirking is socially impossible; communal man, the longest-running experiment concludes, exists — at Dunbar's number, under God's eye, with the door open for leavers.

## The Kibbutz: Socialism's Best Case, and Its Own Verdict

The twentieth century then produced the experiment this lesson has been building toward — the one every honest anti-socialist must reckon with, because it worked. The Israeli kibbutzim were voluntary, democratic, and small; their members self-selected for idealism from Zionism's most fervent cadres; they practiced the real article — no wages, no private property to speak of, common dining, at the extreme communal child-rearing in children's houses — and they were, for two generations, a triumph: they drained the swamps and greened the desert, supplied the young state's elite (officers, pilots, and politicians far beyond their share of population — a few percent of Israelis, a large fraction of its fallen heroes), and proved, against the graveyard above, that secular communal socialism could function — with high productivity — for decades. The economist Ran Abramitzky, kibbutz-descended himself, has given the standard account of how: the kibbutz solved the free-rider and brain-drain problems with a precise institutional toolkit — small scale and lifelong cohabitation made monitoring ambient and social sanction devastating; powerful founding ideology (Labor Zionism functioning, exactly per the previous section, as a secular religion with its own liturgy, festivals, and heresies) reframed labor as national redemption; communal socialization raised children inside the norms; and mutual insurance in a dangerous frontier environment made equality genuinely valuable to everyone, including the able. And then the same account maps the unraveling, mechanism by mechanism, as the conditions decayed: ideological fervor could not be inherited at founding strength (the religion thesis again — second-generation faith is cooler); rising outside wages raised the price of staying for the talented, and the ablest young increasingly left (adverse selection operating exactly as theory predicts: exit for the skilled, entry pressure from those who gain by equality); prosperity weakened mutual insurance's value; scale and modernity diluted monitoring; and the state's 1980s financial crisis stripped the subsidies that had cushioned everything. From the 1990s the kibbutzim, by internal democratic vote — this is the verdict's authority: no commissar, no conquest, their own general assemblies — adopted "renewed kibbutz" models: differential salaries, privatized budgets and housing, the children's houses long since abolished by their own alumni mothers; by the 2010s roughly three-quarters of kibbutzim had formally privatized, and the remainder ran hybrid economies. The kibbutz story, told whole, is thus the opposite of a gotcha and better than one: it establishes the true boundary conditions of communal economics — voluntary, small, ideologically saturated, mutually insuring, exit-permitting communities of a self-selected founding generation can run socialism admirably; and every one of those conditions is (a) unreproducible at state scale, (b) unenforceable across generations, and (c) precisely what Marxist states abolished — the kibbutz worked because it was everything the Soviet Union was not, and it privatized because even everything was not enough once the founders' fire cooled.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The defenses, at strength. First: external causes — communes died of undercapitalization, hostile surroundings, and market pressure, not human nature; the kibbutz privatized amid a state debt crisis and a neoliberal turn. Partly true case by case, and the controls answer it: religious communes prospered in the same markets that killed their secular twins (Amana and the Harmonists were rich); the kibbutz decline's internal mechanisms — the brain drain, the fervor decay, the assemblies' own votes — are documented from within, by sympathetic scholars, in communities that were net recipients of external support; and Bradford's saints and Xiaogang's farmers (last lesson) faced no capital markets at all. Second: the scale objection inverted — "communes prove nothing about socialism, which is a state project with modern administration." The course agrees they differ, and the difference runs the wrong way for the objection: every mechanism that made the best communes work — ambient monitoring, social sanction, sacred meaning, self-selection, exit — is destroyed by anonymity and compulsion; the state does not scale the kibbutz's solutions, it scales New Harmony's problems, and must therefore substitute what it substituted (the police for the elders, the border guard for the open door). Third: the noble-experiment defense — even failing, the communes enriched their members' lives and pioneered practices (cooperatives, communal childcare debates) the wider society learned from. Granted without reservation; this lesson's tone toward the communards, kibbutznikim above all, is respect — they paid their own bills, coerced no one, and left data instead of graves, which is more than can be said for the subject of the next lesson. For the Marxist states, reading the same human material, drew the opposite conclusion from these failures: if man as he is cannot sustain communism, then man must be remade — and the largest project of human re-engineering ever attempted, the New Soviet Man, is next.
