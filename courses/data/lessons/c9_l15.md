The communes' verdict left Marxism one exit, and the doctrine took it in advance: if man as he is cannot sustain communism, remake man. This was never a fringe aspiration; it follows from the theory's core (L4): if consciousness is determined by social being, then changing the relations of production must change consciousness itself — selfishness, acquisitiveness, family-first loyalty, and religion are artifacts of class society, and the society that abolishes their base will grow, as naturally as the old soil grew the old man, a new human type. Trotsky wrote the project's prospectus in 1924 with the movement's full voice: under communism, "man will become immeasurably stronger, wiser and subtler; his body will become more harmonised, his movements more rhythmic, his voice more musical... The average human type will rise to the heights of an Aristotle, a Goethe, or a Marx. And above this ridge new peaks will rise." The Soviet state then spent seven decades and unlimited coercive resources on history's largest experiment in human re-engineering — schooling, ritual, art, family law, biology, and psychiatry all conscripted — and the results are among the most instructive data human nature has ever yielded: not the New Man, but a recognizable and pitiable deformation the Soviets' own sociologists ended by naming homo sovieticus; a biology so bent to the malleability doctrine that it starved the nation and killed its greatest geneticist in prison; a family policy the state itself had to reverse within fifteen years; and, in 1991, the instant, unassisted resurrection of everything seventy years of engineering had claimed to abolish. This lesson is the human-nature argument's crucible: the strongest possible attempt to falsify it, conducted by its enemies, at continental scale, with the safety off.

## The Machinery of the New Man

Take the instruments in order, because their comprehensiveness is the point.[^1] Formation began at the cradle and never stopped: the age-graded pipeline of Octobrists (seven-year-olds wearing the infant Lenin's star), Young Pioneers (the scarf, the oath — "I warmly love my Soviet Motherland" — the camps and palaces), and Komsomol, through which the party curated childhood itself; schooling and the arts were conscripted under doctrines that said the quiet part aloud — writers, in Stalin's phrase, were to be "engineers of human souls," and socialist realism's mandate was to depict not life as it was but life as it was becoming, the New Man displayed in advance so the old could imitate him. The cult of models supplied the templates: Stakhanov the miner, whose fourteen-fold norm-breaking shift (staged, with helpers and prepared equipment, as the archival record later confirmed) launched a national movement of "socialist emulation" — competition readmitted through the back door, wearing overalls; and, darkest of the icons, Pavlik Morozov, the thirteen-year-old canonized across every Soviet schoolroom for denouncing his own father to the police — statues, songs, and Pioneer shrines teaching two generations of children that the New Man's first loyalty ran to the state against the family (the historical Pavlik, later research showed, was largely a fabrication assembled from a squalid village murder — which sharpens rather than softens the lesson: the state needed a child who betrayed his father badly enough to invent one). Family law itself was, in the twenties, a laboratory: the early Bolshevik program — divorce on demand by postcard, abortion legalized, marriage deflated toward registration, Alexandra Kollontai theorizing the family's withering and love's socialization, children raised increasingly by the collective — pursued the Manifesto's "abolition of the family" (L6) in earnest; and then, in the thirties, the state ran headlong into its results — plummeting birthrates, armies of abandoned children (the besprizornye roaming in hundreds of thousands), social chaos registered in the regime's own alarmed reports — and reversed itself wholesale: the 1936 and 1944 laws restored restricted divorce, banned most abortion, decorated "Mother Heroines," and re-enthroned the family the doctrine had marked for dissolution. Mark the evidential weight of that reversal, because it is this lesson's cleanest finding: the most ideologically committed regime in history, unconstrained by elections or churches, attempted the family's supersession, measured the outcome, and retreated — human nature adjudicating the experiment through the one statistic no state can propagandize, the birthrate.

## Lysenko: Biology Bent to the Doctrine

The malleability doctrine's most catastrophic enforcement came in, of all places, agronomy — because genetics, as a science, asserts limits: organisms carry natures, inherited, not infinitely reworkable by environment; and a state whose anthropology required infinite reworkability found the science itself ideologically intolerable. Trofim Lysenko — a barely credentialed agronomist with a gift for promising the impossible on Bolshevik schedules — rose by offering biology as the doctrine wanted it: acquired characteristics inherited, wheat "educated" by cold into new heredities, species transformed by conditions; "vernalization" and cluster-planting would remake harvests as the Plan remade man. Mendelian genetics was denounced as bourgeois, idealist, fascist "metaphysics"; and in 1948, at the infamous VASKhNIL session, Lysenko announced — to a standing ovation once he revealed it — that the Central Committee had approved his report: genetics was, from that day, effectively outlawed in the Soviet Union — laboratories closed, chromosomes purged from textbooks, thousands of biologists dismissed, recanted, or arrested. The project's martyr measures its cost: Nikolai Vavilov — the world-renowned botanist who had assembled, in expeditions across five continents, the world's greatest seed bank precisely to breed hunger out of existence — was arrested in 1940 after years of Lysenko's ascendancy, sentenced as a saboteur, and died of starvation in a Saratov prison in 1943: the century's great scientist of food, starved by the state he had tried to feed, while his colleagues in besieged Leningrad famously died at their desks guarding his seed collection rather than eat it. The agricultural damage of Lysenkoism ran for decades and crossed borders (Mao's China imported the doctrines into the Great Leap's agronomy, with consequences Unit 3 counts); Soviet biology lost a generation and its world standing; and the episode stands as the reductio of the entire New Man premise: a regime that begins by declaring human nature infinitely malleable must end by legislating what wheat may inherit — and when reality dissents, it is the geneticists, not the doctrine, who are liquidated. The same logic's psychiatric branch closed the circle on dissent itself: if the system is scientifically perfect, opposition is not argument but pathology — and Soviet psychiatry duly supplied the diagnosis ("sluggish schizophrenia," detectable, its school taught, in reform-mindedness and stubborn truth-telling), committing sane dissidents to hospital-prisons in numbers that made the World Psychiatric Association's condemnation one of the Cold War's clarifying documents. The New Man project, followed to its ends, meant: children informing on parents, biology criminalized, and sanity redefined as agreement.

## Homo Sovieticus: What the Furnace Actually Forged

Seventy years being sufficient for the experiment, read the results — and the most damning readings are internal. The system did not produce Trotsky's Goethe-average; it produced, in the mordant term coined by the exiled Soviet philosopher Alexander Zinoviev and adopted, post-collapse, by Russia's own leading sociologists, homo sovieticus: a survival-adapted type formed not by the doctrine's ideals but by its incentives — doublethink as fluency (public professions and private beliefs held separately, switched without friction); initiative suppressed (initiative was dangerous; the system punished the conspicuous); responsibility exported upward ("they decide"); work decoupled from effort in the immortal folk summary this course has waited to quote — "we pretend to work, and they pretend to pay us"; envy and leveling institutionalized (the neighbor's extra cow reported, not emulated); and trust — the sociologists' consistent finding — corroded to the family threshold, everything beyond it presumed predatory. Yuri Levada's post-Soviet survey program, designed to watch the type die out with freedom, produced instead the project's final irony: the traits persisted and reproduced in the post-Soviet generation — the engineering had worked, in the only direction such engineering works: not upward to the New Man but downward to the adapted one, and the adaptation outlived its cause. Meanwhile everything the project had claimed to abolish returned the moment the pressure lifted, with a speed that constitutes the experiment's control condition: markets erupted from every kiosk and courtyard in 1991–92 (the previous lesson's ineradicable exchange, nationwide); religion surged from the catacombs — church attendance, baptisms, the Orthodox revival, seventy years of scientific atheism evaporating in five; nationalism, buried under "Soviet man" for decades, returned armed; and the family, the acquisitive instinct, the status hunt — all present, all intact, as if the tomb had been empty the whole time. The furnace had deformed the metal; it had not transmuted it.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Three defenses deserve their hearing. First: every society engineers its citizens — American schools drill pledges, markets manufacture desires, all education inculcates; the Soviet project differed in degree, not kind. The course answers with the distinction the evidence enforces: formation that works with the grain of human nature — cultivating dispositions (honesty, industry, patriotism) that small-scale life already rewards, in societies with exit, dissent, and competing formators (family, church, press) — differs in kind from a monopoly project to delete constitutive features of the species (family priority, self-interest, faith, truth-telling) on pain of denunciation; the proof of kind-difference is the outcome-difference: liberal formation's failures produce hypocrites and rebels; the Soviet project's "success" produced homo sovieticus and its failures filled camps. Second: the project had real achievements — mass literacy at astonishing speed, formidable scientific cadres, social mobility for workers' children; honesty concedes them (the literacy campaign was genuine and vast) while noting their shape: the achievements were precisely the ones compatible with human nature (people readily learn to read — this curriculum's Bible-literacy lessons show ordinary motivation suffices) and they served the regime's power; the moment achievement required the New Man's actual traits — initiative, truthfulness, care for the common — the pipeline delivered Lysenko's yes-men and the pretend-work economy. Third, the deepest: human plasticity is real — cultures vary enormously, and the blank-slate strawman cuts both ways; perhaps the Soviets merely engineered badly. The course's reply closes the unit's argument: plasticity is real and bounded, and the boundaries are exactly what the experiment mapped — seventy years, total control, and unlimited coercion moved behavior massively (that is plasticity) while the underlying dispositions — kin loyalty, exchange, status-seeking, faith — bent, hid, and snapped back intact (those are the bounds); the experiment did not fail from insufficient rigor, it succeeded in demonstrating the constraint, at a price Unit 3 is about to count. Man, the twentieth century's largest laboratory concluded, has a nature; systems that build with its grain harness it; systems that declare war on it get deformation, terror, and — the moment the guards leave — restoration. Why the doctrine could never see this — the epistemology that made Marxism unfalsifiable to its holders — is Popper's question, and the next lesson.
