Unit 2 has established that the evidence runs one way — and everyone knows the evidence has settled nothing: the arguments of this unit are a century old, their conclusions are checkable, and the dispute is as alive as ever. The last lesson explained part of the puzzle (an immunized theory cannot lose an argument); this lesson supplies the deeper part, from the thinker this course, at its patron's request, gives the largest single role in Unit 4: Thomas Sowell's A Conflict of Visions (1987), the book Sowell himself has called his own favorite, and the deepest frame in the entire anti-Marxist arsenal — precisely because it is not, on its face, anti-Marxist at all. Sowell's claim is that beneath ideologies, arguments, and evidence lie visions: pre-analytic senses of how the world works and what man is, absorbed before argument begins and governing which arguments feel plausible, which evidence feels relevant, and which conclusions feel decent. Two great rival visions, he argues, have organized Western social thought for two centuries — the constrained and the unconstrained — and once the reader can see them, the whole political landscape reorganizes: why the same people cluster on seemingly unrelated issues; why debates between camps feel like dialogues of the deaf; why Marxism recruits the way it does; and why everything this unit has documented — the incentives, the communes, the New Soviet Man, the unfalsifiability — belongs to a single, ancient, still-running argument about human nature. This lesson states both visions at full strength, places Marx with the precision Sowell himself insisted on (he is not the simple case), and closes with the frame's implications and its honest audit.

## The Constrained Vision: Trade-offs for the Fallen

The constrained vision begins from a judgment about the human material: man's nature is given, not chosen — limited in knowledge, limited in benevolence, permanently self-interested beyond the circle of the near — and no social arrangement will change it.[^1] Adam Smith is Sowell's cornerstone exhibit, and deliberately via the Moral Sentiments rather than the Wealth of Nations: Smith's famous thought experiment — a man of humanity in Europe, learning that China has been swallowed by an earthquake, will express sorrow, reflect on commerce, and then sleep "with the most profound security"; but let him face losing his own little finger tomorrow, and he will not sleep at all — establishes the datum (our feelings are incorrigibly local) from which the constrained vision's whole method follows: since men cannot be made to love strangers as themselves, wise institutions do not require them to; they economize on virtue, arranging incentives so that ordinary self-love produces public benefit — the butcher's dinner, the invisible hand, Federalist 51's ambition counteracting ambition (the reader of this curriculum's American course has already met the vision's constitutional masterpiece, and its Calvinist genealogy). Knowledge is constrained no less than virtue: no mind or council can hold what a civilization knows (Hayek's dispersed knowledge, L12's calculation argument — the constrained vision's twentieth-century economics), and therefore the vision trusts systemic processes over brilliant deciders: markets, prices, traditions, families, common law — evolved arrangements that mobilize millions of minds and encode the trial-and-error of generations, none of them designed, all of them smarter than any designer. From these premises flow the vision's signature habits: there are no solutions, only trade-offs (every good purchased at the cost of some other good; the question is never "what will end the evil?" but "at what price, compared to what?"); prudence outranks sincerity (results are judged, not intentions); processes are judged rather than outcomes (a fair race with unequal finishes versus a fixed one with equal); and the reformer's burden of proof is heavy, since existing arrangements, however defective, embody survival information the critic cannot see — Burke's contract of the generations, Chesterton's fence. The vision's founding theological ancestor is unmistakable, and this course's charter names it: the constrained vision is original sin, secularized — man crooked timber, systems built for sinners — which is why its lineage (Smith, Burke, the Federalist, Hayek, and Sowell himself) keeps converging with the Augustinian anthropology the American course traced into the Constitution's gears.

## The Unconstrained Vision: Solutions for the Perfectible

The rival vision begins from the opposite judgment, and Sowell's exhibit is William Godwin, whose Enquiry Concerning Political Justice (1793) states it with a purity Marx never matched: man is perfectible — not currently perfect, but without fixed limits; reason can progressively replace self-love; the feelings Smith called incorrigible are artifacts of bad institutions, and "men are not born selfish; they are made so." Social evils, therefore, are not the price of human nature but the products of arrangements — property, privilege, superstition, bad education — and what arrangements made, arrangements can unmake: there exist solutions, not merely trade-offs, and settling for trade-offs is complicity. Knowledge, in this vision, is not hopelessly dispersed but concentratable: the enlightened — those who have seen through the conventions — can know what the mass, still conditioned, cannot, and are therefore entitled, indeed obliged, to act as surrogate decision-makers for the not-yet-rational (Condorcet's legislators of progress, Rousseau's Legislator, the vanguard's family tree); sincerity and intention are the coin of judgment (the well-meaning failure ranks above the self-interested success); the past is a record of crimes and errors rather than a reservoir of tested wisdom; and the future is open — the vision's characteristic emotional register is not prudence but hope, its characteristic political form the crusade. Sowell's insistence on fairness governs the portrait: the unconstrained vision is not stupidity or villainy — it has attracted men of the highest gifts and motives, and its occasional crusades have been gloriously right (the counterpoint returns to abolition); it is a coherent reading of the human situation, argued by serious minds for two centuries. It is also, this course's whole record argues, wrong about the load-bearing facts — and the wrongness is not incidental to Marxism but constitutive, as the placement now shows.

## Marx on the Map — and Why the Argument Never Ends

Sowell's placement of Marx is the book's most sophisticated move, and this course adopts it precisely because it forbids the lazy version. Marx is a hybrid: constrained about the past and present — historical materialism explains each era's morality, politics, and possibilities as products of its productive stage, so that slavery, feudal lords, and even capitalists are not wicked choosers but personifications of their epoch (Capital's phrase, L4), and utopian blueprints are mocked precisely for ignoring historical constraints — but unconstrained about the future: the constraints themselves are historical, scheduled to dissolve; after the revolution, scarcity yields to abundance, the division of labor's "enslaving subordination" ends, the state administers itself away, and man, no longer deformed by class society, becomes the being of Trotsky's prospectus (L15). The hybrid structure explains what pure classifications cannot: Marxism's simultaneous hard-headedness and utopianism — merciless "realism" about every existing society, boundless credulity about the one that does not exist; constraints acknowledged everywhere except at the destination, which is exactly where acknowledgment would have mattered. And the visions framework now pays its full fee, in three findings this unit deposits. First, it unifies Unit 2: the incentive evidence, the communes' verdict, the New Soviet Man's failure, and the calculation problem are not four separate defeats but one — each is reality voting for the constrained premises (fixed motivational grain, dispersed knowledge, trade-offs) against the unconstrained ones on which the entire Marxist project rests; refute the anthropology and the system has nowhere to stand. Second, it explains the argument's immortality, which evidence-based accounts cannot: visions are pre-analytic — they filter what counts as evidence (to the unconstrained eye, every failure of the crusade proves only sabotage or insufficient zeal; every trade-off argument sounds like apologetics for suffering), so the dispute survives any finding because the findings are read through the dispute; the reader now holds, beside Popper's supply-side account of immunity (L16), Sowell's demand-side account of why immunity finds buyers. Third, it forecasts the sociology Unit 5 will document: a vision in which the enlightened few see through what conditions the many is structurally flattering to intellectuals — it assigns them the starring role — and Sowell's sequel, The Vision of the Anointed, maps the resulting pattern in modern dress: crusades launched on the anointed's superior insight; catastrophic feedback ignored (the pattern of the age: policy fails, the anointed's response is never revision but escalation and the indictment of critics' motives); "verbal virtuosity" replacing evidence; and the benighted — the ordinary, the traditional, the religious — cast as obstacles to be managed rather than fellow citizens to be persuaded. The reader will recognize the vanguard party's family features, because they are family.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The frame's audit, in three entries. First, dichotomies simplify, and Sowell says so himself: the visions are poles of a continuum, not boxes — hybrids abound (Marx the designated exhibit; Mill famously inconsistent; most working politicians incoherent mixtures), and the taxonomy's value is orientation, not sorting every thinker without remainder; used as a bludgeon ("you're just unconstrained"), it becomes the ad hominem this course forbids. Second, the awkward glories: some of history's most righteous causes were unconstrained crusades — abolition, the subject of this curriculum's proudest lessons, was precisely a refusal of trade-off reasoning ("gradual emancipation balances interests") by evangelical zealots who insisted a solution existed and burned the cost-benefit analyses; the constrained vision's own record includes prudent men counseling patience with monstrosities. The course's answer, with Sowell: the constrained vision does not forbid moral absolutes — it holds them where knowledge suffices and processes can bear them (slavery's evil required no social engineering to identify, and abolition dismantled a coercion rather than designing a utopia) — and the base rates still govern: for every abolition in the unconstrained ledger there stand the guillotine, the Five-Year Plans, and the Great Leap; crusades are occasionally right and systematically dangerous, and a wise polity prices them accordingly. Third, the reflexive question the previous lesson armed: is the visions framework itself falsifiable, or one more total explanation? The honest answer: it is a descriptive taxonomy, not a predictive theory — it risks little and therefore proves little; its warrant is usefulness (does it organize the record? — this unit is the demonstration), and its dangers are the seductions of all master keys, which the reader should hold as loosely as the course holds everything. What cannot be held loosely is the record itself — and before the course descends into that record's darkest unit, one debt of honesty remains: the full, ungrudging accounting of what Marx got right, so that the verdict, when it comes, convicts the real defendant.
