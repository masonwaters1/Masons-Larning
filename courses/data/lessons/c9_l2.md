Ideas have biographies, and the biography of Marxism begins with a man whose life contradicted his theory at nearly every point: the prophet of the proletariat who never entered a factory; the theorist of economic determinism whose own career was determined by everything but economics — by ideas, feuds, exile, and the unearned income of a manufacturer; the scourge of the bourgeoisie who lived, aspired, and grieved as a bourgeois paterfamilias down to the family crest on his notepaper. This lesson tells the life whole — the genuine brilliance and the genuine suffering with the sponging, the feuds, and the cruelty — not to refute the system by the man (a fallacy the lesson's close will name and reject) but because the system cannot be understood without the man: Marxism's fury, its certainty, its contempt for rivals, and its strange bourgeois-apocalyptic imagination are all legible in the biography, and the reader who knows the life will recognize its fingerprints on every doctrine the coming lessons examine.

## The Making of a Radical

Karl Marx was born in 1818 in Trier, an ancient Rhineland town, into a family of rabbis on both sides — a lineage of learned men stretching back generations — broken by his father's conversion.[^1] Heinrich Marx, a cultivated Enlightenment lawyer, had himself and eventually his children baptized as Protestants because Prussian law barred Jews from the professions: the future author of "religion is the opium of the people" entered religion's rolls as the child of a career decision, an origin some biographers read into his lifelong rage at every establishment (and into the ugly streak of anti-Jewish invective in his own writing, which this course will not launder — his essay "On the Jewish Question" and his letters' slurs, including against fellow socialists, are part of the record). The young Marx was formidable and knew it: at the university in Bonn he drank, dueled (carrying a scar), and ran up debts; transferred by his exasperated father to Berlin, he found his fate — Hegel's philosophy, then the reigning system of the German mind, and the circle of radical "Young Hegelians" who were turning the master's dialectic against throne and altar. His doctorate (on ancient atomist philosophy) was earned by mail from Jena; an academic career was foreclosed when his patron was purged; and so Marx became what he remained — a journalist of genius and a conspirator of ideas — editing the Rheinische Zeitung with such effect that the Prussian censors killed the paper, and departing for Paris in 1843 with his new wife: Jenny von Westphalen, a baron's daughter, four years his senior, the belle of Trier, who had waited seven years for him and would spend the rest of her life discovering what she had married — genius, devotion of a kind, and ruin.

## Exile and the Partnership

Paris made Marx a communist and gave him Engels. Friedrich Engels — two years younger, heir to textile mills in Barmen and Manchester, self-taught, generous, and gifted — had written a study of Manchester's working poor (The Condition of the Working Class in England, drawn from the factories his own family profited by) that showed Marx the empirical world his philosophy needed; the two fused into the most consequential intellectual partnership in history, Engels supplying, for forty years, research, ghost-writing, English polish, and — the detail on which everything material rested — money. Expelled from Paris, then Brussels (where the Manifesto was written for a tiny league of émigré tailors and printers on the eve of 1848), Marx threw himself into the revolutions of that year as editor in Cologne, was tried and acquitted, expelled again, and washed up in 1849 in London, where he would live stateless for the rest of his life. The London decades are the legend's core, and the legend is substantially true: the family in two squalid rooms in Soho; the pawned coats and the dunning creditors; Marx hiding upstairs from the bailiffs; three children dead — the boy Edgar in his father's arms, the infant Franziska whose coffin was bought with a borrowed pound; Jenny aged and broken; and Marx, through it all, at desk G-7 in the British Museum's round reading room, day upon day for decades, building the system. The suffering was real, and so was its cause: Marx was constitutionally incapable of finance — inheritances and windfalls (several arrived, substantial ones) evaporated in months; the family kept a maid and, when flush, moved to houses beyond their means, gave a daughter's ball, and maintained the appearances of the class he was plotting to destroy — while Engels, who spent twenty years he loathed managing the Manchester mill precisely to fund his friend, sent the equivalent of a gentleman's income. The theorist of capitalism was, in strict fact, capitalism's dependent: every page of Capital was subsidized by the profits of the Ermen & Engels cotton works — an irony Marx never publicly acknowledged and the course records without glee, as the first specimen of a pattern (the doctrine exempting the doctrinaire) that Unit 5 will meet at scale.

## The Man in Full

Honesty requires the portrait's dark quarter, and the sources are ample — police spies, correspondence, and the memoirs of allies and enemies alike. Marx was a devoted, playful father to the children who lived — "Moor" on all fours giving rides, the Sunday picnics on Hampstead Heath are as attested as the squalor — and he was, outside the family circle, among the most vicious controversialists of his century: rivals were not answered but annihilated, in polemics larded with racial abuse (the letters about Lassalle, mixing anti-Jewish and anti-Black slurs, are unprintable in most modern collections' footnotes and are printed in this course's), and the socialist movement's history from 1848 forward is littered with the friendships he burned and the organizations he split rather than share. In 1851 the family maid, Helene Demuth — Jenny's lifelong servant, unpaid for years — bore a son, Frederick; Engels assumed the paternity publicly, and on the evidence of Louisa Freyberger's account of Engels's deathbed statement, most biographers conclude the boy was Marx's, farmed out to foster parents and kept from the family — the prophet of the exploited, on this reading, exploiting the one proletarian in his own house and letting his friend carry the shame. (The course notes, per its method, that a minority of scholars contest the deathbed evidence; the balance of scholarship accepts it.) Jenny died in 1881, his eldest daughter fourteen months later; Marx followed in March 1883, stateless, in his armchair, and eleven mourners stood at Highgate to hear Engels declare that history would remember the name forever. On that, at least, the prophecy held.

## What the Life Proves — and Doesn't

Close with the methodological honesty the lesson promised. The ad hominem temptation here is enormous and must be resisted in form: Marxism is not false because Marx sponged, raged, or fathered and abandoned Freddy Demuth — theories answer to argument and evidence, and Unit 2 will convict the theory on its merits or not at all. But biography is not irrelevant either, in three precise ways the course will use. It explains the system's temperament: the totality, the fury, the annihilating certainty toward dissent — the doctrine institutionalized its author's controversial style, and the party culture that later shot deviationists learned its manners in the master's correspondence. It supplies a test the theory fails: historical materialism claims consciousness is determined by class position — yet its own founders were a lawyer's son living on mill profits and a mill owner, theorizing a class they observed from across a considerable distance; the theory cannot account for its theorists, a small crack that Unit 2's Popper lesson will widen. And it establishes the pattern of exemption — the rules for thee — that recurs from the Soho household to every nomenklatura dacha in Unit 3: a doctrine of radical equality whose bearers, beginning with the first, lived by other arrangements. The man now yields to the system. Its deepest root is not economic at all but philosophical — a method inherited from the most influential thinker in Germany and turned upside down — and Hegel's shadow is the next lesson.
