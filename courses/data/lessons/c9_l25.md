The famine had discredited Mao inside his own party — the pragmatists Liu Shaoqi and Deng Xiaoping spent 1962–65 quietly restoring private plots, markets, and sanity — and Mao's counterattack became the twentieth century's strangest great atrocity: a ruler making revolution against his own state, licensing the nation's teenagers to destroy its institutions, its inheritance, and his rivals in one operation. The Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution (1966–1976) killed fewer than the Leap — the careful reconstructions run between one and two million dead, with tens of millions persecuted — but it belongs at this unit's center for what it uniquely demonstrates: the New Man project (L15) in its Chinese maximum, where remaking consciousness meant criminalizing the entire past ("Destroy the Four Olds"); the political religion (L10) at full liturgical flower — the little red book as scripture, the loyalty dance as ritual, and, in the era's perfect self-parody, a mango worshipped on altars; and the war of the generations that Unit 5's Peterson lessons will return to — because the Cultural Revolution's signature crime, the one survivors name first, was children set against parents and students against teachers, the young weaponized to annihilate the sources of their own formation. This lesson follows the sequence — the launch, Red August, the factional wars, the rustication, and the massacres of the "cleansing" phase that the Red Guard imagery conceals — with the unit's discipline of names, numbers, and testimony.

## The Launch and Red August

The machinery started with documents and a poster.[^1] The May 16, 1966 circular declared the party, government, army, and culture infiltrated by "representatives of the bourgeoisie" — enemies now defined as being inside the church, at every level, requiring the masses' direct action; days later a Beijing University instructor's wall poster denouncing the university leadership was broadcast nationwide at Mao's order, and in August Mao supplied his own: "Bombard the Headquarters," the chairman inciting the population against his own party-state. The instrument was the young: Red Guards — middle-school and university students, organized in weeks, sanctified in person when Mao, in a series of mass rallies from August 18, reviewed millions of them in Tiananmen Square, donning their armband; the police were ordered to stand aside (the public security minister explaining that if people were beaten to death, "it is none of our business"). What followed in the capital is documented in the party's own later accounting as Red August: in Beijing alone, official figures record 1,772 people beaten or tortured to death in late August and September 1966 — teachers first. The emblematic first was Bian Zhongyun, deputy principal of the elite girls' school attended by the leadership's own daughters, beaten to death by her students with nailed clubs on August 5, 1966, after hours of "struggle" — the first of a wave, as struggle sessions (the victim bent double in the "jet-plane" position, placarded, spat upon, made to confess absurdities before crowds) became the era's sacrament and the teacher-victim its characteristic offering. The Four Olds campaign ran alongside: old ideas, culture, customs, habits — in practice the physical destruction of the Chinese inheritance: temples smashed and monks paraded, private homes raided by the hundred thousand for books, art, and genealogies (burned in courtyard bonfires), churches and mosques gutted, and, in the desecration that stands for all, Red Guards traveling to Qufu to dynamite and dig through the temple and cemetery of Confucius — corpses of the sage's descendants exhumed and hung from trees, the civilization's founding grave violated by its children on doctrinal instruction. The suicides began at once and never stopped: Lao She, among modern China's most beloved writers, drowned himself in a Beijing lake the morning after a struggle session in August 1966; the universities and conservatories counted their dead by floors jumped from — the persecution suicide becoming so routine that surviving it was itself charged as "alienation from the people."

## Devouring the Headquarters — and the Countryside

The mob phase served the palace purpose. Liu Shaoqi — President of the People's Republic, the man who had said "three parts natural disaster, seven parts man-made" (L24) — was struggled, beaten, denied medication for his pneumonia and diabetes, and died alone on a concrete floor in Kaifeng in 1969, cremated under a false name, his family informed years later; Peng Dehuai, the marshal of Lushan, was dragged back to Beijing, beaten in hundreds of sessions (ribs broken, lungs injured), and died of his captivity in 1974; Deng Xiaoping was exiled to a tractor factory, and his son Pufan, persecuted at Beida, went out a fourth-floor window — pushed or driven, the record leaves both open — and lives paralyzed to this day. By 1967 the Red Guard factions were fighting each other with seized army weapons (the Wuhan incident brought regions to the edge of civil war; Chongqing's factions dueled with artillery and tanks, and the city preserves the era's only Red Guard cemetery), and Mao, having used the flood, turned it off: the army restored order, and the instrument was discarded into the countryside — the "Down to the Countryside" movement rusticating some seventeen million urban young people to the villages for years of their lives, the "lost generation" (China's current leadership cohort among them) whose education ended where their usefulness had. And then came the phase the imagery conceals, statistically the deadliest: the Cleansing of the Class Ranks and allied campaigns (1968–71), when the new Revolutionary Committees — army-backed, quota-minded — conducted organized killing that dwarfed the mobs'. The scholarly reconstructions from county gazetteers (Walder and Su's landmark work) locate most of the Cultural Revolution's dead here, in rural mass killings by authorities and militia: Guangxi province the worst — on the order of one hundred to one hundred fifty thousand killed, with the provincial archives themselves documenting, in the campaign's frenzy, cases of politically ritualized cannibalism of "class enemies" (the investigation by the writer Zheng Yi, later corroborated from official materials, which this course cites with the care its horror demands); the Inner Mongolia purge fabricating a separatist party and torturing hundreds of thousands of Mongols, with deaths in the tens of thousands; Dao County, Hunan, where over nine thousand "class enemies" and their children were massacred in two months of 1967 by militia — victims bound and pushed into limestone pits, the county's own later investigation counting the families exterminated entire. The whole decade's toll, in the serious ranges: roughly 1.1 to 1.6 million dead by the gazetteer-based reconstructions (with estimates running higher toward two to three million on broader assumptions), some 22 to 30 million persecuted — and a cultural loss beyond arithmetic: a decade of closed universities, burned libraries, and a generation taught that the highest act available to a Chinese person was the destruction of a Chinese inheritance.

## The Religion at Maximum

For this course's argument, the Cultural Revolution is the political-religion thesis (L10) at its liturgical peak, and the inventory reads like comparative religion. Scripture: Quotations from Chairman Mao — the little red book — printed in the billions, carried on the body, brandished in unison, cited as verdict in struggle sessions; study of the works as daily office. Icon and cult: the badge industry (billions of Mao badges), the morning "asking for instructions" and evening "reporting back" before the portrait, the loyalty dance performed in workplaces and train stations — congregational choreography of devotion; and the mango: in August 1968 Mao regifted a crate of mangoes to worker teams, and the fruit became relics — mangoes sealed in formaldehyde on altars, wax replicas touring provinces under guard, workers filing past in reverence, at least one man executed for blasphemy against a mango — the sacred object cult reinventing itself, within a generation of "scientific atheism," down to the reliquary. Inquisition and confession: the struggle session as auto-da-fé (L10's frame), self-criticism as the confessional, biography itself the site of sin (class origin as original sin, inherited — the "bloodline theory" couplet of Red August: "If the father's a hero, the son's a great fellow; if the father's a reactionary, the son's a rotten egg"). And iconoclasm as first commandment: the Four Olds campaign was the unconstrained vision (L17) applied to memory itself — the past not as inheritance to be sifted but as contamination to be burned — and its harvest is the standing exhibit for Unit 5's argument about what the war on tradition produces: not liberation but a void, into which poured the cult, the terror, and, when the god died in 1976, the most complete disillusionment a modern society has recorded (the Lin Biao affair of 1971 — the anointed heir dying in a Mongolian crash while fleeing an alleged coup — had already cracked the faith for millions of the faithful, memoir after memoir marking it as the night belief ended).

## The Honest Counterpoint

Three replies, weighed. First, the New Left reading — that the Cultural Revolution contained a genuine anti-bureaucratic, radically democratic impulse (mass criticism of privileged officialdom; some Western theorists, Badiou most persistently, still defend it as politics' great experiment). The record answers: the "mass democracy" was licensed from the throne, aimed where the throne pointed, and terminated by the army the moment it exceeded the throne's uses; its victims included workers and peasants by the hundred thousand (the Cleansing's dead were overwhelmingly rural nobodies, not privileged cadres); and a movement whose signature institutions were the struggle session and the bloodline couplet was an inquisition wearing democracy's slogans — the course notes, for Unit 5, that its Western admirers admired it precisely from a distance at which the teachers' funerals were invisible. Second, the chaos defense — this was madness and power struggle, not Marxism. The course's standing answer applies with a Chinese accent: the categories were the doctrine's (class enemies, the Latsis question asked of teenagers about their parents), the project was the doctrine's (consciousness remade by destroying the old — L15's engineering, radicalized), the anathema mechanics were the founder's (L9), and the power struggle was conducted in, and only made possible by, a system the doctrine had built without any legitimate mechanism for succession or opposition; madness of this shape is what the blueprint looks like when a founder-god fights his own church. Third, the regime's own verdict is banked as partial honesty: the 1981 Resolution declared the Cultural Revolution "responsible for the most severe setback and the heaviest losses suffered by the Party, the state, and the people since the founding of the People's Republic," its responsibility Mao's — an admission real as far as it goes, and its limits are the present tense: the archives are closed, the Dao County and Guangxi investigations were shelved after brief openings, public commemoration is censored, and the portrait under which the verdict was passed still hangs over Tiananmen. The doctrine's next demonstration required no such ambiguities, because its executors kept better records of a purer experiment: Cambodia, Year Zero, next.
