The unit's tour now reaches the case closest to Western hearts — the one regime in this catalogue that still sells posters. Cuba matters to this course not because its tolls rival Mao's (they do not; proportion is part of honesty) but because it is the laboratory of the romance: the test of what happens when the record of a Leninist state — firing squads, block surveillance, labor camps for homosexuals and priests, an exodus of a tenth of the nation across shark water — is filtered through ninety miles of distance, tropical aesthetics, and the Western intellectual's hunger for a socialism with better music. Cuba is where the course's documentary method meets its strongest headwind, because here the apologetics are not fringe: the literacy campaign and the health statistics appear in respectable textbooks as achievements to balance against "authoritarianism," the embargo stands ready as the universal solvent of responsibility, and the man who ran the revolution's execution wall became, in death, the best-selling face on the world's t-shirts. This lesson does what the unit does everywhere else — the chronology of the Leninization, the record with names and ranges, the celebrated claims audited against the regime's own baseline, and the counterpoint (the embargo above all) at genuine strength — and adds the theme Cuba uniquely teaches, which Unit 5 will inherit: the political economy of glamour, and the double standard that made one Caribbean police state the age's romantic exception.

## The Leninization: 1959–1961

Begin with the honesty the course owes the revolution's origins: Batista's Cuba was a corrupt, repressive, mob-entangled dictatorship, born of a coup against a constitutional order, and the revolution of 1959 arrived on a genuine wave of national hope — including the hope, explicitly promised by Fidel Castro throughout the insurgency, of restored elections under the 1940 constitution.[^1] The promise's fate timestamps the regime. Elections were deferred, then mocked — "Elections? What for?" became the rally cry (¿Elecciones para qué?) — and were never held: sixty-plus years without one contested national election, the longest such run in the hemisphere's history. The machinery arrived on the Leninist schedule the reader now knows by heart: revolutionary tribunals and the execution wall at La Cabaña fortress, where Che Guevara presided as prosecutor-commandant through 1959's first months — several hundred shot in that opening season by the documented counts, with Guevara's own doctrine on the record in his 1964 UN address: "Fusilamientos, sí, hemos fusilado, fusilamos y seguiremos fusilando" — "Executions? Yes, we have executed, we are executing, and we will continue to execute" — and, privately to an associate, the admission that judicial proof was a bourgeois nicety the revolution did not require. The press was gone within two years — every independent newspaper, radio, and station closed or seized by mid-1960; the universities purged; the unions captured. And in September 1960 came the institution that made Cuba a total-surveillance pioneer: the Committees for the Defense of the Revolution — the CDRs, a watch committee on every block, in time enrolling millions, tasked in the founding speech with knowing "who lives on every block, what they do... what they believe" — the inminban (L27) of the Caribbean, and the reason Cuban dissent has always been prosecuted with witnesses from next door. By the Bay of Pigs (April 1961), when the regime rounded up tens of thousands of suspects in days — stadiums and theaters as holding pens — the architecture was complete: party-state, security organs, category enemies, no exit. What the architecture then did fills the record: the Cuba Archive's documented cases — name by name, its method conservative — count some 5,600 executions and over 1,100 extrajudicial killings across the regime's decades (broader estimates run higher, into five figures); political prisoners in the tens of thousands at the peaks, including the plantados — the "planted ones" who refused re-education, of whom Armando Valladares served twenty-two years for declining to put a pro-regime sign on his desk, emerging to write Against All Hope, the Cuban Gulag's testimony; the UMAP camps (1965–68), where homosexuals, Jehovah's Witnesses, seminarians, and other "deviants" were interned for forced agricultural labor under the gate-slogan "Work Will Make Men of You" — a sentence whose European cousin the reader will recognize; the Ochoa show trial of 1989 (the regime shooting its most decorated general on cocaine charges most historians read as politics); and the exodus — the record's summary statistic: some one and a half to two million Cubans, well over a tenth of the nation, fled — the rafts, the Mariel boatlift (the regime salting the 1980 exodus with emptied prisons and asylums), the balseros of the 1990s with their uncounted drowned, and the case the course names because the regime denies it: the 13 de Marzo tugboat, July 13, 1994 — seventy-two Cubans fleeing on an old tug, rammed and swamped by state boats with water cannon seven miles off Havana; thirty-seven dead, ten of them children, no one ever charged. People, this course has said before, vote with their feet; in Cuba they voted with inner tubes, against the current, for sixty years.

## The Claims, Audited

Now the achievements, at their strongest and then against the baseline. Literacy: the 1961 campaign was real — brigades of young volunteers, genuine gains, a legitimate logistical feat. The baseline: pre-revolutionary Cuba already stood among Latin America's most literate nations (censuses in the mid-70s-percent range, near the region's top), so the campaign finished a climb well underway across the hemisphere (peers like Costa Rica and Chile reached comparable literacy without dictatorship); and the audit's second entry is the one the textbooks omit — what the new readers were permitted to read: a literacy campaign conducted alongside the closure of every independent publication is teaching the population to read the state, and the course scores it accordingly. Health: Cuban infant-mortality and doctor-ratio statistics are the apologetic's crown jewels, and the audit has three entries. The numbers themselves are partly administered — demographers have documented anomalies (late-fetal/early-neonatal reclassification patterns and reported pressure on physicians, alongside an exceptionally high abortion rate screening difficult pregnancies) consistent with statistical management of the headline figure. The baseline again: Cuba in 1958 already ranked among Latin America's best in physicians per capita and infant mortality — the revolution inherited, not created, a regional health leader — and its relative standing against the Costa Ricas and Chiles has, on honest series, roughly held rather than transformed. And the system's texture: the showcase hospitals serve tourists, foreigners, and the nomenklatura for hard currency while ordinary pharmacies stand empty (the two-tier structure documented by the dissident and diaspora medical literature), and the celebrated medical missions abroad function, per UN special rapporteurs' findings, under conditions meeting the legal markers of forced labor — passports confiscated, the state pocketing the great majority of the doctors' wages, families held as return insurance: the doctors are not aid; they are the regime's largest export commodity. The general baseline completes the audit: 1958 Cuba, for all Batista's rot, stood near the top of Latin America on income per capita, urbanization, and consumption diffusion (among the region's leaders in telephones, televisions, and automobiles per capita); six decades later it trails the hemisphere on nearly every material measure, with rationing continuous since 1962 — the achievement claims, run against the honest baseline and the regional controls, describe a country that fell down the tables while its peers climbed.

## The Romance and the Double Standard

Cuba's distinct contribution to this course is the demonstration that glamour is a political technology. The pilgrimage literature — Sartre's "Hurricane over Sugar," the celebrity delegations, the enduring genre of the returned enthusiast — established the tropes (the vitality, the music, the leader's marathon charisma) that let the CDRs and La Cabaña recede into "complexity"; Che's afterlife perfected it: the commandant of the execution wall, whose Congo and Bolivia expeditions ended in fiasco and whose own early diaries record racial condescension his admirers never quote, became — via one photograph — the global brand of rebellion, worn by millions who could not name one thing he administered. And the asymmetry completes the exhibit: the same Western opinion that (rightly) made Pinochet's Chile a byword — its toll, per the truth commissions, some 3,000 killed and tens of thousands tortured, crimes this curriculum condemns without reserve — feted for decades a neighboring regime with a documented record of the same order or worse, plus totalitarian architecture Chile never built; the difference was never the corpses, it was the direction of the gun and the quality of the poster, and Unit 5 will take the psychology of that difference as a primary text. The romance, the course notes for the ledger, was also load-bearing at home: tourism and diaspora remittances — capitalist dollars — have been the system's life support since the Soviet subsidy died, the regime sustained by the very exiles it created and the foreign currencies it forbids its citizens to earn freely.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The defenses, strongest first. The embargo: the U.S. embargo (1962–present) is real, costly, and — this course's tradition being what it is — a policy many free-market economists themselves criticize; the regime's losses from it are genuine, and the course books them. What the embargo cannot carry: Cuba has traded throughout with essentially the entire world except the United States — Canada, Europe, Latin America, the Soviet bloc and then China — so "blockade" is propaganda vocabulary for a one-country boycott; the economy's structural failures track the system's (the Special Period collapse of the 1990s was caused by the withdrawal of Soviet subsidies running at several billion dollars a year — the dependency itself the diagnosis); and the embargo explains no executions, no CDRs, no UMAP, no tugboat — political prisoners are not a trade phenomenon, and a regime that answers every internal critique with a foreign tariff has conceded the internal case. Second, the Batista/imperialism context: conceded above and bounded as everywhere in this unit — the old regime's crimes explain the revolution's welcome, not the new regime's design; the 1940-constitution road was promised, available, and closed from within. Third, the achievements: audited above at full strength; what survives the audit (real vaccination logistics, real disaster-response capacity, the 1961 brigades' genuine idealism) is banked honestly and weighed against the price no peer paid. Fourth, popularity and longevity: the regime's endurance is cited as consent; the course applies its standard test — consent is measured by contested elections and open doors, and the regime has risked neither in sixty years; where the door cracked (Camarioca, Mariel, the rafts, the 2022 exodus of a quarter-million in a year), the measurement arrived. The romance's Latin sequel — the ideology's career through a continent, and its forgotten catastrophes across Africa and Asia — is the next lesson's ledger.
