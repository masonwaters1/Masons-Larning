The catalogue's famous rooms — Russia, China, Cambodia — can create a false impression: that the doctrine's lethality was a property of a few great tyrannies rather than of the export itself. This lesson corrects the frame with the forgotten ledger: the Marxist-Leninist regimes and movements of the Third World — Ethiopia above all, with Angola, Mozambique, South Yemen, Afghanistan, and Peru's Shining Path in the survey — where the same institutions, copied from the same manuals, produced the same categories of dead in countries the Western memory never mourns. The forgotten fronts matter to the course's argument for three reasons the lesson will establish. First, replication: by the 1970s the doctrine was installed across a dozen new states with carbon-copy architecture — vanguard party, security organ, plan, category enemies — and the outcomes replicated with it, the closest thing to experimental repetition history provides. Second, the victims: the doctrine sold itself to the postcolonial world as the shortcut to development, and its bill was paid by the poorest people on earth — the peasant societies least able to survive requisition and villagization; the famine returned wherever the model did. Third, the asymmetry of mourning, which Unit 5 will inherit as evidence: the Ethiopian Red Terror is unknown to educated Westerners who can discuss Chile's stadium in detail — and the selective memory is itself a datum about how the ideology's account has been kept.

## Ethiopia: The Red Terror and the Engineered Famine

The century's last classical Marxist-Leninist terror state rose in Africa's oldest polity.[^1] The Derg — the military committee that deposed Haile Selassie in 1974 and strangled him in his palace the following year — consolidated under Mengistu Haile Mariam into an explicitly Marxist-Leninist regime (Soviet-armed, Cuban-assisted, complete by 1984 with a vanguard Workers' Party and Lenin's statue in Addis Ababa), and in 1976–78 conducted the Qey Shibir — the Red Terror, the name borrowed consciously from 1918 (L19): a campaign against rival leftists and "counter-revolutionaries" in which urban youth were the primary harvest. The documented practices carry the unit's signature plus one refinement of its own: neighborhood kebele committees with kill authority; bodies of teenagers displayed in the streets under placards ("the Red Terror is unleashed against..."), families forbidden to mourn; and the bullet fee — parents required to reimburse the state for the cartridge before receiving a child's body, a receipt system whose paperwork survived to convict. The urban toll of the Terror runs, in the standard reconstructions, from thirty to fifty thousand and beyond (Amnesty's contemporaneous estimates; the later trials' documentation), with tens of thousands more tortured in the kebele cellars. Then the countryside: the regime collectivized on the classical model — state farms, marketing monopolies with forced grain quotas at confiscatory prices, villagization (millions moved into planned villages) — and when drought struck a countryside already squeezed, war finished what policy began: the famine of 1983–85, the one the world knows from Live Aid, killed on the order of four hundred thousand to a million Ethiopians, and the scholarly verdict on it (de Waal's above all) is the unit's verdict in African dress: the famine was in large part created by government — its epicenters tracked the counterinsurgency zones of Tigray and Wollo, where the army's scorched-earth campaigns, market closures, and movement controls converted shortage into mass death; food and aid were wielded as counterinsurgency instruments; and the regime's flagship response, the forced resettlement of some six hundred thousand northerners to the south (trucks and Antonovs, families separated, malaria lowlands), killed by the tens of thousands itself — Médecins Sans Frontières, expelled for saying so, put resettlement's dead in the range of the famine's worst districts. The Live Aid irony is part of the record this course keeps: Western generosity, mediated through a totalitarian state, partly financed the instruments of the catastrophe it meant to relieve — the lesson being not against charity but about what regimes of this type do to every flow that passes through them. Mengistu, deposed in 1991, was convicted of genocide in absentia by an Ethiopian court in 2007 — and lives today in Harare, sheltered first by fellow ex-Marxist Robert Mugabe and left undisturbed by his successors: the author of the century's last classical terror-famine has never faced a day of his sentence.

## The Survey: Aden to the Andes

The replication record, compressed but named. Angola and Mozambique: the Portuguese collapse of 1975 delivered both to Marxist-Leninist liberation parties (MPLA, FRELIMO), which built the standard kit — one-party states, secret police (the DISA's camps; Mozambique's re-education camps holding tens of thousands), forced villagization and state farms that broke peasant agriculture — and both fell into superpower-fueled civil wars (Cuban divisions and Soviet arms on one side; South African and American clients on the other) whose combined tolls ran to roughly half a million to a million dead in each country across the era, famine included. The course flags the attribution honestly and holds the flag through the counterpoint: civil-war dead belong to a shared ledger of the Cold War's clients and cannot be booked wholesale to the doctrine — what the course books to the doctrine are the regimes' own policy components (the camps, the villagization famines, the economic collapse pattern identical from Luanda to Maputo) while noting that their opponents' atrocities (RENAMO's especially) fill their own page of hell. South Yemen — the Arab world's only Marxist-Leninist state — contributed the pattern's chamber-piece: in January 1986, the ruling party's factions settled a Politburo dispute by machine-gunning each other in session and then fighting a twelve-day civil war that killed on the order of ten thousand party members and civilians — the anathema mechanism (L9, L22) running at maximum speed in a state the size of a city's politics. Afghanistan: the 1978 Saur coup installed the PDPA, whose year of classical terror — the Pul-e-Charkhi prison executions, the village massacres, the death lists (the regime's own prisoner rolls, recovered decades later, name thousands; the standard reconstructions put the pre-invasion terror's dead at 27,000 from one prison alone and some 50,000–100,000 overall) — ignited the national revolt that drew the Soviet invasion of 1979, and the ensuing decade killed roughly a million to a million and a half Afghans and exiled five million: booked, per the course's attribution discipline, as a war ledger opened by the doctrine's coup and terror, prosecuted with the doctrine's methods (the KhAD's torture archipelago documented), and shared with every power that fed the fire. And Peru closes the survey with the doctrine's pure non-state form: Sendero Luminoso — the Shining Path, built by the philosophy professor Abimael Guzmán ("President Gonzalo," whose cult ran to "Gonzalo Thought") as the hemisphere's most orthodox Maoist project — waged a people's war from 1980 whose signature was the massacre of the very peasants it claimed (Lucanamarca, 1983: sixty-nine villagers, elders to infants, killed with machetes and axes on Guzmán's acknowledged orders, "to teach a lesson"); Peru's Truth and Reconciliation Commission counted roughly 69,000 dead in the conflict and assigned the majority share — unique among Latin American commissions — to the insurgency itself, with the state's dirty-war crimes (booked and condemned) the second column. A professor's reading group become a death cult: the Paris-circle pattern (L26), Andean edition, and the survey's closing exhibit that the doctrine's lethality never required state power — only belief and organization.

## The Pattern Findings

Three findings from the replication record, entered for the unit's summation. First, the export was institutional, not atmospheric: these regimes did not converge on terror independently — they were built, with Soviet, Cuban, East German, and Chinese advisers, from the same blueprints (vanguard party, plan, political police on the Stasi/KGB template — the training relationships documented country by country), and the convergence of outcomes across Amharic, Portuguese-African, Arab, Pashtun, and Quechua societies eliminates culture as the explanation the way Korea's partition (L27) eliminates it for race: the common variable is the operating system. Second, the poorest paid most: the doctrine marketed itself to the postcolonial world precisely as the development shortcut — and delivered, in every agrarian society that applied its rural program, the same sequence (marketing monopolies, forced villages, broken peasantries, famine vulnerability) documented from the Volga (L20) to Tigray; the cruelest fact in this lesson is that the pitch worked best on those it would cost most. Third, the mourning asymmetry: set the Ethiopian Red Terror's tens of thousands of murdered students — bodies placarded in the streets, bullet fees receipted — beside any comparably sized European or Latin American atrocity of the same decade and compare the footprint in Western memory, curricula, and culture; the difference is not information (the records exist, the trials happened) but attention, and Unit 5 will ask directly why the victims of this ideology, and these victims most of all, have never had their proportionate mourning. The unit's arithmetic — what all the rooms sum to, and how honestly the summing can be done — is the next lesson.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Three entries, at strength. First, the Cold War attribution problem, conceded structurally: nearly every case above was also a proxy war, and the anticommunist side's ledger is real, large, and named in this course without euphemism — Indonesia 1965–66, where the army and its militias massacred on the order of half a million alleged communists (among the century's great slaughters, Western-abetted and long Western-excused); Operation Condor's murder network; Guatemala's highland genocide against Mayan communities under anticommunist doctrine. These are booked, condemned, and counted — the accounting lesson next addresses both ledgers explicitly — and the course's claim survives the booking: the anticommunist atrocities were crimes of regimes and clients that no text of liberal economics licenses or requires, while the communist cases' machinery (category enemies, kill quotas, engineered famine) executed the doctrine's own written categories — the "does the doctrine license it" test, which Unit 6 will formalize, separates crimes committed against a creed's teaching from crimes committed as its implementation. Second, inheritance: these were poor, colonially wrecked, often war-born states that might have failed under any system — conceded as baseline, answered by controls: their non-Leninist peers (Botswana beside the Marxist south; Kenya beside Ethiopia; post-1978 China beside itself) failed less, or reformed out of failure, and the specific signatures (the camps, the villagization famines, the party massacres) appear only where the blueprint does. Third, drought and nature in Ethiopia: real, and apportioned per de Waal — nature made the shortfall, counterinsurgency and policy made the famine, and the epicenters' geography (war zones, not driest zones) is the apportionment's evidence. The rooms now toured, the unit turns to the sum: the Black Book, the hundred-million figure, and what honest accounting can and cannot claim — next.
