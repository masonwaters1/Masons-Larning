Marxism's economics came from Ricardo and its politics from the French revolutionary tradition, but its skeleton — the thing that makes it a total system rather than a bundle of grievances — came from a Berlin philosopher who died when Marx was thirteen and whose shadow Marx never escaped. G.W.F. Hegel is the most influential thinker most educated people have never read, and this lesson supplies exactly as much of him as Marxism requires: the dialectic, the idea of history as a rational process moving through conflict toward a destination, and the intoxicating doctrine that what is real and what is rational are converging. Then it watches the two operations that created Marxism: Feuerbach's inversion, which turned Hegel's God-driven history into a story about man, and Marx's own famous claim to have found Hegel standing on his head and set him right side up — history driven not by ideas but by material production. Understanding this pedigree is not academic tidiness; it explains the three features of Marxism that made it unlike every other radicalism and, the course will argue, uniquely dangerous: its certainty (history has a direction), its totalism (everything is connected and explained), and its license (conflict is not a tragedy but the very mechanism of progress).

## Hegel in One Lesson

Reduce Hegel to what the story needs and three doctrines remain.[^1] First, history is rational: not a chronicle of accidents but the unfolding of Spirit (Geist) — mind, reason, ultimately something like God-coming-to-self-awareness — through time; "the history of the world," Hegel taught in Berlin, "is none other than the progress of the consciousness of freedom," moving from the Oriental world where one was free, through the classical world where some were, toward the modern world where all are. Whatever happens of world-historical consequence is, in the famous and endlessly abused formula, rational: "What is rational is actual and what is actual is rational." Second, the engine of this progress is contradiction — the pattern later summarized (in a schema Hegel himself never used as a slogan) as thesis, antithesis, synthesis: every form of life, thought, and society generates its own internal opposition, the conflict between them is fought out, and the resolution preserves what was true in both at a higher level (Hegel's untranslatable Aufhebung — simultaneously to cancel, preserve, and lift up). Conflict, in this vision, is not the failure of history but its method: peace is stagnation, and the tension tearing an age apart is the next age being born. Third, the individual is an instrument: world-historical progress works through passions, wars, and great men who serve purposes they do not understand — the "cunning of reason" uses Caesar's ambition to build what reason required — and the individual's protests against the process have no standing against the verdict of history: die Weltgeschichte ist das Weltgericht, world history is the world's court of judgment. Mark each doctrine as it passes, because each will reappear wearing Marxist clothes: the direction of history, the fertility of conflict, the individual as material, and the court of history before which losers have no appeal.

## The Inversion: Feuerbach, Then Marx

Hegel died in 1831 a Prussian establishment figure, and his heirs split over what the system implied: the Right Hegelians read "the actual is rational" as a blessing on throne and altar; the Young Hegelians — Marx's Berlin circle — read the dialectic as dynamite: if history moves by negating existing forms, then the existing Prussian state and the Christian religion were not termini but targets. The decisive blow came from Ludwig Feuerbach, whose Essence of Christianity (1841) electrified the generation ("we were all Feuerbachians," Engels remembered) with a single inversion: theology is anthropology — God did not make man in his image; man makes God in his, projecting humanity's own best attributes (love, wisdom, power) onto an imaginary being and then impoverishing himself before his own creation. Religion, on this account, is alienation: man's essence, exported to heaven and worshipped from below. Marx absorbed the inversion and immediately radicalized it in two moves that define him. First, the diagnosis goes deeper: religion is not the disease but the symptom — "the sigh of the oppressed creature, the heart of a heartless world... the opium of the people" (note, against the common misquotation, the register of anesthetic pity as much as contempt) — and criticizing heaven is idle until one criticizes the earthly misery that makes heaven necessary. Second, therefore, the task changes: "The philosophers have only interpreted the world, in various ways; the point is to change it" — the eleventh thesis on Feuerbach, later carved on Marx's tomb, and the sentence in which philosophy announces its intention to become power. Applied to Hegel himself, the same inversion yields historical materialism: Hegel was right that history is a law-governed dialectical process moving through conflict toward freedom — he had merely identified the wrong subject. Not Spirit but production; not ideas generating social forms, but the material mode of production generating ideas. "With him," Marx wrote of the dialectic, "it is standing on its head. It must be turned right side up again" — and turned upside down, Hegel's rational theodicy of Spirit became a rational theodicy of matter: class struggle as the engine, communism as the destination, and every Hegelian guarantee transferred intact to the new owner.

## What Survived the Inversion

Here is the lesson's payload, because what Marx kept from Hegel matters more than what he changed — the inversion flipped the driver and preserved the vehicle, and the vehicle is where the danger lives. The certainty survived: history still has a knowable direction and a guaranteed destination; Marx's socialism therefore called itself scientific and dismissed rivals as utopian — not wrong about values but ignorant of the law, a difference in kind that turned every disagreement into a diagnosis. The totalism survived: as in Hegel everything expresses Spirit's stage, so in Marx everything expresses the mode of production — law, religion, family, art, morality are superstructure, which means no domain of life is neutral ground and no appeal (to justice, to rights, to conscience) escapes reduction to class interest; the system is armored in advance against every objection, a feature Popper's lesson will indict as its scientific death and Unit 5 will identify as its psychological charm. The fertility of conflict survived, sharpened: where Hegel's contradictions resolved in synthesis, Marx's classes resolve in expropriation — struggle is the mechanism of progress, violence its midwife ("force is the midwife of every old society pregnant with a new one"), and the moral vocabulary of restraint belongs to the superstructure of the class being negated. And the court of history survived, with its terrible corollary: individuals are material, losers have no appeal, and — the addition that armed the century's executioners — the party that knows history's verdict may execute the sentence early. When Lenin's tribunals and Mao's campaigns justified themselves as history's instruments against classes objectively condemned, they were not betraying the system's logic; they were speaking fluent Hegel with a materialist accent — a genealogy the course asserts here and will document, decree by decree, in Unit 3.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The counter-case deserves its full turn. Scholars of Hegel protest, rightly, that the man himself was no proto-totalitarian: his mature politics defended constitutional monarchy, law, and civil society; the "actual is rational" formula, read in context, is closer to "the real tendencies of history are intelligible" than to "whatever wins is right"; and Popper's famous prosecution of Hegel as a totalitarian ancestor (The Open Society) is widely judged, even by Popper's admirers, a caricature of the texts. Scholars of Marx add that he was among Hegelianism's critics as well as heirs — the theory of ideology is a genuine insight (ideas do serve interests; this course itself deploys the point against Marxists in Unit 5, and fairness notes the blade's two edges) — and that determinism in Marx is contested: the later Engels hedged ("the economic element is not the only determining one"), and humanist Marxists have built entire schools on the early, alienation-centered writings against the iron-law reading. All granted, and the core stands: whatever Hegel meant, what Marxism took from him — guaranteed direction, total explanation, progressive conflict, history as supreme court — is exactly the equipment that distinguishes it from every merely reformist radicalism, and each element reappears, cited and operational, in the decrees of the regimes this course will examine. A movement that believes it knows the end of the story behaves differently, in power, from one that does not; the twentieth century is the demonstration. The vehicle now built, the course examines its engine: the theory of history itself — base, superstructure, class struggle, and the stages — next.
