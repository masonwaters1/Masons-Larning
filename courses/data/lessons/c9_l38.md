The exceptions now testify in person, and the first is the weightiest scholarly witness the subject possesses: Leszek Kolakowski, the Polish philosopher who was Communist Poland's most brilliant young Marxist — a party ideologue trained to teach the doctrine at its highest level — and who ended as the author of Main Currents of Marxism, the three-volume history that remains, by common consent across the spectrum, the definitive scholarly account of the doctrine from its ancient roots to its breakdown, and whose opening and closing verdicts frame this entire course: Marxism examined as "the greatest fantasy of our century" — a philosophical dream of salvation whose Stalinist realization was not its betrayal but one of its legitimate offspring. Kolakowski matters to the witness list for three reasons this lesson develops: the insider's authority (like Djilas at L31 and Sowell at L35, he knew the system from inside its priesthood — the revisionist's road out of Warsaw runs through every station of disillusion this course has mapped); the scholarly consummation of the course's two structural theses — his trilogy is the thousand-page version of L10 (Marxism as eschatology, traced to its actual religious sources) and of L31 (Stalinism as heir, argued by the man the party had trained to know); and the polemical masterpiece of the Cold War — "My Correct Views on Everything," his reply to E.P. Thompson — in which the century's argument between socialist hope and historical evidence was settled, on points, in public, with a wit this lesson will let the reader taste.

## Warsaw: The Priest and the Jester

The biography is the argument's warrant.[^1] Born in 1927, Kolakowski passed his boyhood under the Nazi occupation (schools closed, education clandestine, the war's Poland supplying a lifelong education in what ideologies do when armed) and emerged into the new Communist order a believer: he joined the party young, rose fast, and became the regime's philosophical prodigy — a professor at Warsaw trained partly in Moscow, deployed in the early 1950s as a hammer of "bourgeois" and Catholic philosophy: the genuine article, a stellar mind inside the church of L10, teaching its theology. The unraveling followed the classic revisionist chronology: the 1953–55 thaw's first doubts; 1956 — Khrushchev's Secret Speech and the Polish October — detonating the faith's Stalinist floor; and Kolakowski emerging as the intellectual voice of "revisionism": the attempt, sincere and doomed, to save a humanist, democratic Marxism from the system built in its name. The essays of that period made him famous and marked him: "What Is Socialism?" (1956), a mock-catalogue of what socialism is not ("a state whose neighbors curse geography... a state that knows better than its citizens what makes them happy...") that the censors suppressed and Poland memorized; and "The Priest and the Jester" (1959), his self-defining distinction — the priest guards the absolute; the jester questions everything the priest guards — with philosophy's honor assigned to the jester. The system answered as systems of that type must (L9's anathema, L16's immunity): reprimands, then the 1966 expulsion from the party for a tenth-anniversary lecture on 1956's unkept promises, then March 1968 — the regime's "anti-Zionist" purge — stripping his chair and driving him into exile, whence the road led through Montreal and Berkeley to All Souls College, Oxford, his base for forty years. The exile then performed the labor that gives this course its scholarly backstop: Main Currents of Marxism — written in Polish in the 1970s, published 1976–78 — three volumes, The Founders, The Golden Age, The Breakdown: the whole doctrine, from its deepest sources to its Parisian afterlife, reconstructed with a fairness that makes Sowell's look brisk, by the one man who had taught it from inside at its highest level and read everything.

## Main Currents: Eschatology and the Legitimate Heir

The trilogy's two theses are this course's two structural claims, delivered with the field's fullest documentation. The first is the deep genealogy: Main Currents famously opens not with Hegel but with Plotinus — because Kolakowski traces Marxism's root idea (the self-alienated whole returning to itself; history as the drama of a lost unity recovered) through Neoplatonism, Eriugena, and the mystics into German idealism and thence to Marx: the doctrine's engine, he demonstrates at scholarly length, is a soteriology — a salvation story of contingency overcome and man restored — wearing the century's scientific costume; his one-line verdicts distilled it for the general reader: Marxism as "a doctrine of blind confidence that a paradise of universal satisfaction is awaiting us just round the corner," whose influence owed nothing to scientific merit and everything to its prophetic, fantastical content — "the greatest fantasy of our century... an ideological organization of humanity's worst impulses in the language of its best aspirations" (the reader will recognize L10, here with a thousand pages of sourcing under it). The second thesis is the succession question settled: writing as the ex-insider whom no one could accuse of ignorance of the texts, Kolakowski concluded that Leninism-Stalinism was not a Russian perversion of an innocent doctrine but "one possible interpretation — and a well-founded one" of Marx's own: the doctrine's utopian terminus (perfect unity, conflicts abolished) joined to its contempt for "formal" freedoms and its silence on institutions (L8) made the totalitarian realization not inevitable but legitimate — among the heirs, the Soviet state had a valid claim to the estate, which is L31's verdict rendered by the estate's own former lawyer. The third volume then performed the autopsy on the Western afterlife: the Frankfurt School, existential Marxism, and the New Left examined and, in the book's most quoted judgments, dismissed as the doctrine's decadence — a scholasticism of "auxiliary hypotheses" (L16's degeneration, diagnosed independently) whose function was to keep the fantasy alive after its predictions died; the volume's mature verdict, delivered before 1989 confirmed it: as an explanatory system, Marxism was dead, while as a fantasy-generating framework it would survive precisely because it had never depended on being true.

## "My Correct Views on Everything"

The polemic belongs in the portrait because it is the course's L31 conducted as a duel. In 1973 the English Marxist historian E.P. Thompson published a hundred-page "Open Letter to Leszek Kolakowski" — more in sorrow than anger, reproaching the exile for deserting the socialist "we," pleading the ideal's innocence of the regimes' crimes, and offering, as such letters do, the unpaid moral drafts of a better socialism to come. Kolakowski's 1974 reply, "My Correct Views on Everything," is the most devastating twenty pages the argument ever produced, and its method is this course's: precision about who paid. To Thompson's easy "we" and unearned securities: "You say... 'we' should do this and that. I do not know who is 'we'" — and the passage this lesson quotes at length because nothing summarizes Unit 3's stakes better: Thompson had written that he and Kolakowski shared, after all, the same socialist "problematic"; Kolakowski replied by inventory — that for Thompson's British "we," socialism's costs were a matter of seminar disappointments, while "your text is on many points a scarcely credible monument of a mind that has slept through the history it discusses"... and, to the plea that the idea stands innocent of the practice: the observation that a doctrine which has produced the same result in every incarnation, and whose advocates each time explain that this too was not the real thing, has forfeited the right to the excuse — "there are no signs," he wrote of the promissory better socialism, that the word stands for anything except the sum of its failures plus a hope exempted from evidence. The essay's serious core (its wit should not conceal it) is the challenge L31 formalized: state in advance what would count; absent that, the "idea" is an unfalsifiable consolation — and Thompson's own tradition's best minds conceded the field's verdict over the following decade. Kolakowski's late credo completes the portrait and speaks directly to this curriculum's own coalition: "How to Be a Conservative-Liberal-Socialist" (1978) — the ironic manifesto affirming, at once, the conservative's knowledge that human evils cannot be designed away, the liberal's defense of freedom and suspicion of utopia, and the socialist's refusal to let the profit motive be exempt from moral judgment — ending that such an International "will never exist" because its members cannot supply certainty; the jester's politics, and a mirror this course's fusionists (L18's tension) should study.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The audit. First, the trilogy's contested readings: Marxist and sympathetic scholars have pressed real objections — that the eschatology frame front-loads the verdict (a genetic argument, they claim, convicting the doctrine by its ancestors — the course's answer stands at L10: the classification rests on structural criteria, and Kolakowski supplies the structures at length); that the young-Marx and Gramsci treatments are thin or tendentious in places; that the Breakdown volume's dismissals of the Frankfurt School read more like sentencing than analysis. The course grades honestly: the exposition volumes' authority has survived hostile review for fifty years (the standard scholarly judgment, granted even by opponents, is that no comparable history exists), while the third volume's temperature is part polemic — read it as a great scholar's verdict, not a neutral survey, and check its targets against their own texts. Second, the succession thesis overstated? Critics note Kolakowski's "well-founded interpretation" is not "the only interpretation" — he himself insisted Marx contained democratic strands (the course banked them at L8/L31) — and the fair statement of his claim, which this course adopts, is disjunctive: Marxism's heirs include both the social-democratic and the totalitarian lines, and the totalitarian line is a legitimate child, not a changeling; the defense that needed him to say "inevitable" in order to refute him is answering a claim he did not make. Third, the pessimism: the late Kolakowski's religious turn (his profound writings on God, evil, and the Devil — "the Devil is part of our experience... an active force in history" — and on modernity's endless trial) has been read as conservatism's melancholy exceeding its evidence; the course notes the reading and the counter-reading — that a man who taught inside the fantasy, and buried it in three volumes, had earned his sobriety about the next one. The witness stands: the doctrine's own trained theologian, testifying that the church he served was, from the foundations, a temple to a fantasy — and that the fantasy's power was never a mistake about facts but a hunger facts cannot feed. The next witness knew the hunger from the other side of the class line, and told the truth about the faithful while remaining, to his death, a man of the left: Orwell, next.
