The witness list now calls a man the prosecution does not own — deliberately. George Orwell was a socialist from his late twenties to his deathbed: a democratic socialist by explicit, repeated, final profession, who wanted the mines nationalized and the class system dismantled, and whom this course will not conscript into a tradition he refused. He is here because the course promised (L31) full credit to the left's own truth-tellers, and because Orwell is their captain: the man who saw Communist totalitarianism early and from inside the left's own war; who paid the full professional price the anointed's economy (L37) charges for defection — his dispatches spiked, his classic rejected by his own publisher, his fables declined by the age's best editors as inconvenient to an ally; and who then converted what he had seen into the two books that did more than any archive to teach the ordinary reader what Units 2 and 3 of this course document — Animal Farm and Nineteen Eighty-Four, written before the archives opened and confirmed by them in detail, down to the airbrushed commissar (L22) and the slogan on the arithmetic (2+2=5 was a real Soviet plan-fulfillment slogan before it was Winston Smith's torment). Orwell's testimony has a special epistemic weight this lesson develops: he is the control case for bias — the witness whose priors ran the other way, whose indictment of the Communists cost his own side's approval and served no interest of his class, career, or creed; when such a witness and the NKVD's own files agree, the finding is as settled as history gets.

## Spain: The Thing He Saw

Everything turns on Barcelona, so the road there can be brief.[^1] Eric Blair — Eton scholarship boy, then five years an imperial policeman in Burma (the guilt of which he spent a decade expiating), then the deliberate descents of Down and Out in Paris and London and The Road to Wigan Pier (whose second half, notoriously, flays the English socialist movement's cranks and snobs while affirming socialism — the one-standard habit already in place) — went to Spain in December 1936 to fight fascism, and by accident of paperwork joined the militia of the POUM, a small anti-Stalinist Marxist party, rather than the Communist-run International Brigades. The accident made him a witness. He served months in the Aragon trenches, took a sniper's bullet through the throat (missing the carotid by millimeters — the wound ended his soldiering), and returned to a Barcelona transformed: in May 1937 the Communists — executing Moscow's line and the NKVD's local apparatus — turned their guns on their own coalition, suppressing the anarchists and the POUM in street fighting, then outlawing the POUM as "Trotskyist-fascist" traitors: Orwell's comrades were jailed and disappeared; the party's leader, Andrés Nin, was abducted by Soviet operatives, tortured (he refused to sign the script), and murdered — while the Communist press worldwide, in perfect unison, reported that Spain's revolutionaries were Franco's agents. Orwell and his wife escaped Spain with the police hunting them, sleeping in ruins with warrants out — hunted, as he never tired of specifying, not by fascists but by Communists, in a republic he had bled for. What Spain taught him he stated with the precision that makes him this course's charter witness on epistemology: atrocities he could weigh; what shook him was the lying — "I saw great battles reported where there had been no fighting, and complete silence where hundreds of men had been killed... I saw newspapers in London retailing these lies and eager intellectuals building emotional superstructures over events that had never happened. I saw, in fact, history being written not in terms of what happened but of what ought to have happened according to various 'party lines.'" And the fear it planted — "the very concept of objective truth is fading out of the world" — became the seed of everything after: the totalitarian innovation was not cruelty (old as man) but the claim of control over the past and over reality's arbiter; if the Leader says of such and such an event, it never happened — well, it never happened; if he says two and two are five... The reader has met the machinery this insight predicts: the vanishing commissar (L22), the memory-holed famine (L21, L24), the renamed march (L27). Homage to Catalonia (1938) told the story straight — and completed the education: his own publisher, Gollancz of the Left Book Club, refused it unread (the club's line was pro-Communist); the New Statesman spiked his Spain dispatches as inconvenient; the book sold a few hundred copies while the intellectuals (L37) canceled him for spoiling the front. He had discovered the anointed's price schedule in person, and paid it without changing his answer.

## The Fables: Animal Farm and Nineteen Eighty-Four

The two books that followed are Units 1–3 of this course, compressed for the permanent shelf. Animal Farm (completed 1944, published 1945) is the Soviet arc as fable — the revolution's promise, the pigs' emergence as the new class (Djilas's book, L31, eight years early, in farm animals), the rewritten commandments (the doctrine's elastic scripture, L16), Boxer the faithful worker carted to the knacker with the pigs toasting his memory (the Stakhanovite's arc, L15), and the ending this course's L20-and-after made documentary: the creatures looking from pig to man and man to pig, "and already it was impossible to say which was which." Its publication history is the fellow-traveling record (L37) in miniature: written when the USSR was the ally, it was rejected by Gollancz, by Cape (after a Ministry of Information official — later identified as a Soviet agent — advised against), and by T.S. Eliot at Faber, whose letter survives as the era's self-portrait: the fable's viewpoint, Eliot wrote, was "not convincing," and after all "what was needed... was not more communism but more public-spirited pigs." Four publishers refused the truth as inopportune; the book then sold in the millions and entered every language the Soviet empire policed — smuggled, per the samizdat records, as contraband scripture. Nineteen Eighty-Four (1949, written by a dying man on Jura) is the deeper work for this course because it is not satire but anatomy — the totalitarian system's epistemology, taken to its limit and explained from inside: the Party's control of the past ("Who controls the past controls the future: who controls the present controls the past") as the memory holes' principle; Newspeak as the language-control project (L16's immunizing vocabulary given a grammar — the aim, as the appendix states, to make heresy literally unthinkable); doublethink as the trained capacity to hold contradictions — homo sovieticus (L15), specified before Levada measured him; the Two Minutes Hate and the rewritten enemy (the Party line reversing mid-sentence, the crowd's rage re-aimed without breaking rhythm — the Nazi-Soviet pact's choreography, which Orwell had watched the faithful perform); and O'Brien's catechism, the book's black heart and this course's L8-through-L22 stated as doctrine: the Party seeks power entirely for its own sake — "The object of persecution is persecution. The object of torture is torture. The object of power is power" — and the future, if you want a picture of it, is "a boot stamping on a human face — for ever." The book's standing in this course is evidentiary: written before the archives, from published sources, a bullet wound, and one betrayed war, it specified the system's mechanisms — the quota terror's confessions, the vaporized unpersons, the arithmetic of the slogans — that the archives (Units 3's documents) then confirmed item by item; among the century's acts of political understanding, only Bakunin's 1873 prophecy (L8) rivals it for verified reach.

## The Honest Counterpoint

Three entries, the first being the frame itself. Orwell was not ours: he remained a democratic socialist explicitly (The Lion and the Unicorn's nationalization program; his statement, near the end, that every serious line he had written since 1936 was "against totalitarianism and for democratic Socialism, as I understand it"), he distrusted businessmen nearly as much as commissars, and this course honors the position by not laundering it — his testimony convicts Marxism-Leninism and the fellow-traveling intelligentsia, not the democratic left, and the distinction he embodied is precisely the one Unit 6 will draw; conscripting Orwell posthumously is a violation of his own one-standard rule, and the reader should discount any polemicist, on any side, who performs it. Second, the List: in 1949, dying, Orwell gave a friend at the Foreign Office's anti-Communist information department a notebook list of writers and public figures he judged unreliable as propagandists for the democratic cause — crypto-Communists and fellow-travelers, with occasional cruel and sometimes prejudiced annotations. Defenders note its actual character (advice on whom not to hire for counter-propaganda, from a man who had watched the NKVD operate inside his own trench line — not a denunciation for punishment, and nothing happened to anyone on it); critics answer that the informer's posture sits ill on the century's great anti-informer, and the annotations' streaks (his catty notations on Jews and homosexuals among them) are indefensible on his own standards. The course reports both and grades by its one rule: the act was small, human, and defensible in context; the annotations fail the Orwell test, and he would have printed the failure. Third, the prophecy discipline: Nineteen Eighty-Four is routinely misread as a forecast of Western surveillance technology and scored against CCTV counts; the book's subject is the totalitarian claim on truth, not the camera — its confirmations belong to Pyongyang's genealogy, not to advertising cookies — though the reader is licensed to apply its language-corruption chapters ("Politics and the English Language" is their essay form: the defense of the indefensible via euphemism — pacification, transfer of population, elimination of unreliable elements) to every polity including his own, which is exactly what Orwell built them for. The witness stands, unowned and unimpeached: the socialist who told the truth at his own side's expense, whose fables outlived the archives they anticipated. The next witness went further in and came out lower: the Soviet camp system's graduate, whose three volumes did to the Gulag what no army could — Solzhenitsyn, next.
