Strip away the economics, the politics, and the polemics, and Marxism's load-bearing wall is a single theory: historical materialism — the claim that history is a law-governed process driven by the development of material production, that every society's politics, law, religion, and ideas are determined by its economic base, and that the sequence of social systems from ancient slavery to communism unfolds with the necessity of a natural process. This is the doctrine Engels placed beside Darwin at the graveside; it is what made Marxism "scientific" socialism against every merely moral rival; it is the license by which the future was declared known; and it is, this lesson will argue, both the most genuinely fruitful idea Marx produced and the site of his system's deepest intellectual failure. The lesson proceeds in the course's trial method: first the theory reconstructed at full strength — stronger than most of its modern defenders bother to state it — then the audit: what it explains, what it predicted, what happened, and the strange fact that its own existence is among the best evidence against it.

## The Doctrine, at Full Strength

The canonical statement is a single paragraph Marx wrote in 1859, in the preface to A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy, and it deserves to be walked through clause by clause.[^1] "In the social production of their existence, men inevitably enter into definite relations, which are independent of their will" — the foundation: before humans philosophize, worship, or legislate, they must eat, and the way a society organizes eating (and clothing, housing, producing generally) is not chosen but inherited, a structure each generation is born into. These "relations of production" — who owns the tools and land, who works for whom: master and slave, lord and serf, capitalist and worker — correspond to a given stage of the "material forces of production," the technology and technique available: the hand-mill, in Marx's famous epigram elsewhere, gives you society with the feudal lord; the steam-mill, society with the industrial capitalist. Together the forces and relations constitute "the economic structure of society, the real foundation, on which arises a legal and political superstructure and to which correspond definite forms of social consciousness." There is the architecture in one sentence: base determines superstructure — the economy is the foundation; law, politics, religion, philosophy, morality, and art are the building erected on it, shaped to its load. Hence the theory's most famous inversion: "It is not the consciousness of men that determines their existence, but their social existence that determines their consciousness." Ideas do not drive history; they are its exhaust. What drives history is a mechanism: the forces of production grow (technology advances), until they collide with the existing relations of production, which "turn into their fetters" — the old ownership structure strangles the new productive possibilities — "then begins an era of social revolution," the fettering class is overthrown by the rising class the new forces have created, and a new structure is born carrying, in time, its own gravediggers. Run the mechanism backward and forward and you get the famous stages: primitive communism, the ancient slave economies, feudalism, capitalism — each progressive in its day, each doomed by its own success — and ahead, after capitalism's contradictions mature, socialism and finally communism, when class itself dissolves and, in the theory's most audacious clause, the state — being merely the instrument by which one class holds down another — "withers away," and human "prehistory" ends. Two features give the doctrine its power and must be felt before the audit. It is genuinely explanatory in ambition: nothing social is accidental; everything from Gothic cathedrals to Victorian marriage law finds its place in one causal order. And it is predictive in form: this is why Marx's socialism condemned rivals as "utopian" — they merely wished for a better world; he claimed to have discovered where the actual one was necessarily going, and a man who knows the future does not negotiate with the past.

## What the Theory Genuinely Sees

The audit begins with honest credit, because the theory's fruitfulness is real and the course's verdict means nothing if it starts by denying the obvious. Before Marx, history was overwhelmingly written as the deeds of kings, generals, and ideas; after him, no serious historian anywhere writes without asking the materialist questions — who owns what, who works for whom, whose interest does this institution serve, what changed in production before this changed in politics? The entire disciplines of economic and social history are, in method, Marx's grandchildren, including in their most anti-Marxist practitioners. The kernel insights hold: economic structures do shape political possibilities (no industrial democracy without industry; the course's own free-market tradition argues versions of this constantly); ideas do often serve interests (the doctrine of ideology — that a class's moral certainties tend suspiciously to license its advantages — is a permanent addition to the world's critical equipment, and this course will wield it against Marxist intellectuals themselves in Unit 5, which is only fair, since it cuts every direction); and technological change does destabilize social orders — the stirrup, the printing press, the steam engine, and the microchip have each redrawn the map of power in ways their societies' official ideas caught up with afterward. Even the stages contain a real observation: there is a rough directionality in economic history — forager, agrarian, commercial, industrial — that any theory must accommodate. If historical materialism claimed only this much — economics matters greatly, ideas have material contexts, technology drives change — it would be true, valuable, and banal. The system requires vastly more: not that the base matters but that it determines; not that history has directions but that it has a direction, with a known terminus. On those claims, the audit turns.

## The Predictions, Graded

A theory that calls itself science invites the scientist's question: what did it predict, and what happened? Marx's theory generated real predictions — specific, central, and repeated — and the honest ledger is devastating. It predicted revolution in the most advanced capitalist countries, where the contradictions were ripest: England, Germany, America. Every Marxist revolution that ever succeeded occurred in a backward, agrarian, or war-shattered society — Russia, China, Vietnam, Cuba, Cambodia — and none in an advanced one; the theory's own founders would have read the twentieth century's map as a refutation, and Lenin's tortured doctrine of imperialism was invented precisely to explain the anomaly away. It predicted the progressive immiseration of the working class — wages driven toward subsistence, misery accumulating with capital. What happened is the largest fact of modern economic history: real wages in capitalist countries rose, decade upon decade, until the proletarian of 1850 would not recognize his great-grandson's life — a refutation so total that later Marxists quietly rewrote "immiseration" as relative (inequality) or psychological (alienation), redefinitions the Popper lesson will examine as the system's characteristic escape. It predicted the polarization of society into two classes, the middle vanishing into the proletariat; the middle class instead became, in every advanced economy, the largest class in history. It predicted the falling rate of profit and capitalism's terminal crisis; capitalism has produced crises on schedule and terminal collapse never, each "final" crisis (1873, 1929, 2008) followed by adaptation the theory said was impossible. Where the theory scored — and the course records the hits with the misses — was in capitalism's dynamism and globalization (next lesson's Manifesto reading) and in the reality of business cycles. But its central, load-bearing forecasts — where revolution comes, what happens to workers, where the class structure goes, how the system ends — failed in the exact places the science staked its name. A physics with this record would have no defenders. Historical materialism kept millions, which is itself a datum the course banks for Unit 5.

## The Deeper Failures

Beneath the failed predictions lie three structural problems the counterpoint below will let the defenders answer. The reflexivity problem, planted in the biography lesson: if consciousness is determined by class position, the theory cannot explain its own discoverers — a Rhineland lawyer's son and a Manchester mill owner theorizing the proletariat's viewpoint — nor the awkward pattern, constant from 1848 to the present, that Marxism's carriers have been overwhelmingly bourgeois intellectuals while actual workers, when free to vote, have chosen reformist parties and pickup trucks; the standard rescue (renegade intellectuals can adopt the rising class's standpoint) concedes that consciousness is not, after all, determined — and with that concession the doctrine's central inversion quietly dies. The direction problem: Weber's famous study of the Protestant ethic argued the causal arrow can run from ideas to economics — religious conviction shaping economic behavior and helping birth capitalism itself — and a century of scholarship on everything from Islam's commercial law to Confucian development states has established what common sense suspected: base and superstructure interact, and a theory reduced to "the economy is important and everything influences everything" has surrendered its scientific pretensions while keeping the laboratory coat. And the agency problem, which is where theory touched blood: a doctrine in which individuals are "personifications of economic categories" (Marx's own phrase for how Capital would treat them) and history's verdict is already in has no principled ground for moral restraint toward the categories marked for liquidation — the kulak, the class enemy, the bourgeois element are not neighbors who might be wronged but fetters scheduled for removal; Unit 3 will document, in the regimes' own decrees, how fluently this grammar translated into quotas.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The defense, at strength. Sophisticated Marxists reply, first, that determinism is a caricature: Engels himself, late in life, protested that he and Marx claimed the economic element was determining only "in the last instance," amid reciprocal interactions — and a generation of analytical Marxists (G.A. Cohen's celebrated reconstruction foremost) rebuilt the theory as functional explanation, rigorous and modest, in which superstructures persist because they stabilize the base, a formulation that survives many objections above. The course's answer: the softened theory is respectable and no longer Marxism in the operative sense — "in the last instance" determinism licenses no predictions, grounds no science of revolution, and would never have filled a single square with believers; the choice is between a strong theory that is false and a weak one that is true and toothless, and the movement's entire history ran on the strong one. Second, defenders note that prediction is an unfair standard for social science generally — true enough as a caution, except that Marxism claimed precisely the predictive authority its rivals disclaimed; it is judged by the standard it chose. Third, the hits: globalization, concentration, commodification of culture, crisis-proneness — real, acknowledged in the Manifesto lesson, and explicable without the system (Schumpeter and the Austrians derived the dynamism from entrepreneurship, the crises from credit, with no stages and no terminus). What survives the audit, then, is the toolkit without the prophecy: ask always who owns, who benefits, what production makes possible — questions this course itself will keep asking — while the science of history's direction, the guaranteed destination, and the classes read as categories rather than persons go to the archive of failed certainties. But the archive is not where they went; they went into pamphlets, parties, and eventually decrees, because the theory's grip was never really its evidence. Its grip was moral — the story of innocence robbed and redemption coming — and the moral engine, alienation and exploitation, is the next lesson.
