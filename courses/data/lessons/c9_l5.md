The theory of history supplied Marxism's skeleton; this lesson examines its heart — because no one ever joined a revolution for a theory of stages. What Marxism offered, beneath the science, was a moral indictment of extraordinary power: that the modern economy, whatever it pays, steals from the worker something more important than money — his activity, his creativity, his relation to his fellows, his very humanity (the doctrine of alienation); and that even the wage bargain itself, apparently free and fair, is theft dressed as contract — the systematic extraction of unpaid labor (the doctrine of exploitation). These two doctrines are Marxism's recruiting sergeants. They are also, intellectually, its most instructive components: alienation, because it reveals the romantic religion beneath the science; exploitation, because it stands or falls with a value theory the course's sister seminar has already watched fall. The lesson reconstructs both at full strength — including the chapters of Capital that remain genuinely harrowing reading — and then conducts the audit, asking the question that decides everything: were these real diagnoses with the wrong prescription, and what actually cured the conditions they described?

## The Four Alienations

The doctrine of alienation comes from the young Marx — the Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844, unpublished until 1932, whose appearance split Marxism into rival churches (the counterpoint returns to this).[^1] Its framework is Feuerbach's projection-analysis (L3) transferred from heaven to the factory: as religious man exports his essence to an imaginary God and then kneels to it, so economic man under capitalism pours his life into products and processes that confront him as alien powers. Marx anatomizes the estrangement into four dimensions, and they deserve careful statement because their descriptive force is real. First, alienation from the product: the worker makes what he does not own, design, or often even understand; the more he produces, the mightier the world of objects standing over him — "the worker becomes all the poorer the more wealth he produces... the devaluation of the world of men is in direct proportion to the increasing value of the world of things." Second, alienation from the activity of labor itself: work, which ought to be the free expression of human powers, becomes forced, external, a mere means to stay alive — "the worker... does not feel content but unhappy, does not develop freely his physical and mental energy but mortifies his body and ruins his mind"; he is at home only outside his work, and in his work feels outside himself, so that life begins where labor ends, and the human functions shrink to the animal ones — eating, drinking, procreating. Third, alienation from "species-being" (Gattungswesen): humanity's distinguishing essence, for the young Marx, is free, conscious, creative production — man makes his world as an artist makes, "in accordance with the laws of beauty" — and wage labor inverts the essence, turning the defining human activity into a commodity sold by the hour. Fourth, alienation from other men: where labor is sold and bought, the other person appears primarily as competitor, customer, or boss — the cash nexus, as the Manifesto will phrase it, dissolving the bonds between man and man. Feel the doctrine's reach before auditing it: this is not a complaint about wages, hours, or conditions, all of which could be reformed; it is a metaphysical indictment of the wage relation as such — no raise cures it, no regulation touches it, only the abolition of private property and the market itself, which is why the doctrine, wherever it is sincerely held, forecloses reform in advance and mandates revolution as a conceptual necessity. It is also — mark this for Unit 5 — a secularized doctrine of the Fall: an original human essence, lost in history, to be recovered at history's end, when labor becomes "life's prime want" and man returns to himself. The science of 1859 never mentions species-being; the religion of 1844 never left the system's bloodstream.

## Exploitation, and the Chapters That Burn

The mature Marx replaced the romantic vocabulary with an economic mechanism, and its logic must be stated fairly because millions found it irresistible. Labor-power — the capacity to work — is, under capitalism, a commodity, and like every commodity it sells at its cost of production: the subsistence of the worker. But labor-power has a unique property: it produces more value than it costs. Hire a man for a day at the cost of his keep — say, the value his labor creates in six hours — and he can be worked for twelve; the six unpaid hours are surplus value, the spring of all profit. Nothing illegal has occurred; every contract was honored; and precisely there lies the doctrine's genius — exploitation without cheating, theft built into the grammar of the wage bargain itself, so that the mildest employer participates in the extraction as fully as the cruelest, and the remedy can never be fairness within the system but only the system's end. Around this engine Marx built, in Capital's historical chapters, the indictment's empirical body — and the course insists the reader know that these chapters, drawn from the British factory inspectors' reports and parliamentary bluebooks, are among the most powerful pages of nineteenth-century prose: the seven-year-olds in the potteries and the match-works' phosphorus jaw; the milliner Mary Anne Walkley, twenty, dead after twenty-six hours' continuous work in a fashionable London shop, the case running through the papers under the title "Death from simple over-work"; the lace-runners, the chimney boys, the twenty-hour shifts at relay mills. Marx did not invent these horrors, and the course will not pretend he did; he weaponized documentation his enemies' own government had compiled — which is why the indictment landed, and why every honest account of capitalism's history, including this course's, owns the ledger's early pages. The moral question the audit must answer is not whether the horrors were real — they were — but what actually ended them, and whether the mechanism Marx built on them survives.

## The Audit

Take exploitation first, because its fate is quick and has already been sealed elsewhere in this curriculum. The surplus-value mechanism is not a moral intuition; it is a theorem, and its sole premise is the labor theory of value — value as congealed labor time, profit as the unpaid residue. That premise did not survive the 1870s: the marginal revolution dissolved it (value flows from utility at the margin; costs are foregone alternatives), and Böhm-Bawerk's dissection — treated at full length in the Free-Market Tradition course, and summarized in this course's Unit 2 — established what the economics profession has held ever since: the wage bargain spans time (workers paid now, output sold later), the employer's margin is, analytically, the price of time and risk-bearing, and the "unpaid hours" identification collapses once value is no longer a substance labor deposits in things. What remains of "exploitation" after the theorem falls is a family of real but different questions — bargaining power, monopsony, desperation contracts — which modern economics studies without the metaphysics and which reform, not revolution, addresses; the course will meet them honestly in its closing unit. Alienation's audit is subtler, because the doctrine describes something real — the reader who has hated a job knows it from inside, and a literature from the factory memoirs to Studs Terkel's interviews confirms that drudgery, meaninglessness, and the boss's clock are permanent features of much modern work. Three findings, though, close the case against the doctrine as system. First, the conditions Marx documented were cured — hours, safety, child labor, wages — and cured within capitalism: by the very reformism (unions, factory acts, the franchise, and above all productivity growth raising the price of labor) that the doctrine declared conceptually impossible; the Tenth-Hour agitators and evangelical factory reformers (Shaftesbury's story belongs to this course's sister volumes) did what the revolution never did anywhere. Second, the socialist experiments made it worse on the doctrine's own terms: the Soviet worker — forbidden to strike, bound to his enterprise, producing for a plan he never saw, under managers he never chose — was alienated by every one of the four measures beyond anything in Manchester, with the difference that no inspector's report could say so; if alienation indicts systems, the twentieth century's verdict is unambiguous and runs the wrong way. Third, the doctrine's metaphysical core — the lost essence, the labor that should be life's free artistic want — is not a finding but a romance: it presupposes an Eden of unalienated creation that no anthropology locates, measures human work against a standard drawn from the artist and the philosopher (the occupations, an unkind observer notes, of the doctrine's authors), and quietly redefines the ordinary human condition — that work is often toil, that necessity binds — as a crime demanding a cosmic remedy. Genesis, the course's sister volumes would observe, told the same truth without the promise of a political cure: by the sweat of your face you shall eat bread. The difference between a religion that locates paradise beyond history and one that schedules it is, Unit 3 will show, measured in bodies.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The defense deserves its strongest witnesses. The humanist-Marxist tradition — Fromm, the young Marcuse, the 1960s rediscovery of the Manuscripts — argues that alienation, not the failed economics, is the living Marx: a moral psychology of modern work whose confirmation is every engagement survey, every epidemic of burnout, every bullshit-jobs bestseller; and that dismissing it as romance ignores how much of it modern people recognize as their own condition. Granted in part: the description lives — the course concedes alienation as diagnosis while rejecting it as system, precisely because the diagnosis's real remedies (meaningful work where achievable, ownership broadly spread, the dignity agenda that runs from profit-sharing to the weekend) arrived through the market order's reforms and nowhere else. Defenders of the exploitation doctrine reply that stripping the labor theory leaves the power asymmetry standing — the worker who must sell today negotiates against the owner who can wait — and they are right that the asymmetry is real; the course's answer, developed later, is that this is an argument about competition and alternatives (tight labor markets, mobility, and growth are what actually shifted power toward workers, doubling nothing so much as the Austrian point that the worker's true protector is the rival employer), not a vindication of the surplus-value machine. And historians add a fairness point the course accepts: Marx wrote inside the rawest decades of industrialization, when the ledger's horrors were the visible present and the coming rise of wages was not — his error was to freeze 1848 into a law; his critics' recurring error is to forget that 1848 was real. What survives the whole audit is precisely what the recruiting sergeant cannot use: work can be brutal and often was; the brutality was documented by capitalism's own institutions and cured by its own reforms; the theory that promised the cure delivered, where tried, the disease in a purer form; and the moral energy of the indictment — real, and honorable in many who felt it — was mortgaged to a mechanism that was false and a metaphysic that was a religion in disguise. That religion's founding pamphlet — the most consequential ever printed, and a far stranger and more brilliant document than either its enemies or its wavers know — is the next lesson: the Manifesto, read closely.
