Every religion needs its scripture and every science its Principia, and Marxism's had to be both. Das Kapital — Volume I published in 1867 after two decades in the British Museum, Volumes II and III assembled by Engels from chaotic manuscripts after Marx's death — is the system's cathedral: vast, unfinished, astonishing in ambition, by turns unreadable and unforgettable, and far more often invoked than opened. Its purpose was to supply what the Manifesto had only asserted: scientific proof that capitalism is a historically specific system, built on exploitation, driven by laws of motion that must destroy it. This lesson does what the course's method requires — walks the cathedral's actual architecture, from the famous first chapter on the commodity to the apocalyptic final chapters on expropriation; identifies what is genuinely great in it (more than its enemies admit) and where the load-bearing columns stand; and then conducts the structural inspection, because the cathedral has a known engineering flaw at its foundation, confessed in its own third volume, that the course's sister seminar has already examined from the outside and this lesson now examines from within.

## The Commodity, and the Famous Fetish

Capital opens not with factories or tycoons but with a philosophical x-ray of the simplest economic object: "The wealth of those societies in which the capitalist mode of production prevails, presents itself as an immense accumulation of commodities... Our investigation must therefore begin with the analysis of a commodity."[^1] A commodity, Marx argues, is a double thing: a use-value (it satisfies some want) and an exchange-value (it trades against other things in definite ratios) — and the ratios, he insists with the classical economists behind him, must rest on some common substance, which can only be the socially necessary labor time congealed in each. The reader of this curriculum knows the fork in the road here — the marginal revolution four years after Capital's publication would locate value in valuing minds rather than embodied labor, and the course returns to the wreckage below — but first, honest credit to the chapter's celebrated climax: the section on commodity fetishism, the most influential twenty pages Marx ever wrote. In a market society, he observes, relations between people take on "the fantastic form of a relation between things": the labor of millions coordinates through prices, so that human social relations appear to participants as objective properties of objects — the price "belongs" to the coat the way weight does — and the market's outcomes confront their own makers as natural facts, like weather, rather than as social arrangements that could be otherwise. Whatever one concludes about the economics, the observation is genuinely profound, and its intellectual progeny is enormous — everything from the sociology of consumption to a century of cultural criticism descends from these pages. The course's marginal note, banked for later: the Austrian tradition looked at the same phenomenon — social coordination through prices appearing as objective facts no one designed — and called it not mystification but the marvel: the price system as the only possible way dispersed knowledge coordinates at scale. The same X-ray, developed as indictment by Marx and as vindication by Hayek, is one of intellectual history's great forking paths, and this course obviously travels the second road; but it insists the reader see that both roads start from a real and brilliantly stated observation.

## The Machine of Accumulation

From the commodity the book builds its engine in a sequence of famous steps. Money emerges from exchange; capital appears when money stops being a means (sell in order to buy: C-M-C) and becomes the end (buy in order to sell dearer: M-C-M′) — value in self-expanding motion, and the capitalist its "personification," character-masked, driven by the system whether kindly or cruel. The expansion's source is the previous lesson's mechanism: labor-power purchased at its value yields more value than it costs, and surplus value can be stretched two ways — absolutely, by lengthening the working day (the harrowing Chapter 10 the course has already honored), and relatively, by raising productivity so the worker's subsistence is produced in fewer hours, which is why capitalism, unlike every previous exploiting system, revolutionizes technique incessantly: the machine chapters analyze industrialization's logic — machinery raising productivity, deskilling labor, drawing in women and children, disciplining the workforce by the reserve army of the unemployed — with a power that made even hostile economists concede the description. The engine then runs to its destination in the "general law of capitalist accumulation": wealth accumulating at one pole, misery at the other; capital concentrating and centralizing into ever fewer hands; the reserve army swelling — until the famous penultimate chapter of Volume I sounds the trumpet in Hegelian brass: "The monopoly of capital becomes a fetter upon the mode of production... Centralisation of the means of production and socialisation of labour at last reach a point where they become incompatible with their capitalist integument. This integument is burst asunder. The knell of capitalist private property sounds. The expropriators are expropriated." The reader has already graded the forecast (L4): wages rose, the middle grew, the knell never rang; what this lesson adds is the placement — the failed predictions are not asides but the cathedral's spire, the point the whole structure was built to hold up. And one chapter more belongs to honest inventory: the closing section on "primitive accumulation," Marx's history of how capitalism's starting capital was actually gathered — enclosure driving peasants from the land, colonial plunder, the slave trade ("capital comes dripping from head to foot, from every pore, with blood and dirt") — pages of real historical force that this curriculum's own honest ledgers (the slavery lessons of its American volume; the plantation economics of its market volume) partially corroborate: early capital formation was frequently violent and state-assisted. The Marxist conclusion (therefore the system is constituted by theft) does not follow — the market order's defense never rested on the sanctity of every historical acquisition, and the course's tradition (from Smith's attacks on mercantile plunder onward) condemned the same crimes contemporaneously — but the chapter is strong medicine, and the reader should take it straight.

## The Confession in Volume III

Now the structural inspection, and it can be brief because the sister seminar performed the autopsy in full (the Free-Market Tradition's Böhm-Bawerk lessons; this course revisits the economics in Unit 2). Volume I's entire mechanism runs on the labor theory of value; Volume I also acknowledges what every economist knew — that actual market prices don't track labor content, since capital-intensive and labor-intensive industries earn the same rate of profit despite employing very different amounts of the sole value-creating input. Marx promised the resolution in a later volume. When Engels published Volume III in 1894, the resolution turned out to be the "transformation" of values into prices of production — individual prices deviate systematically from labor values; the labor theory holds only in aggregate — and Böhm-Bawerk's verdict within two years became the profession's permanent one: a law of exchange ratios that governs no actual exchange ratio has been abandoned, not defended; the third volume contradicts the first; the system is closed. Even sympathetic mathematical economists spent the twentieth century confirming the wound (Bortkiewicz's demonstration that Marx's own transformation arithmetic fails; Sraffa-school proofs that labor values are a redundant detour). The cathedral's foundation stone, in short, was removed by its own architect's posthumous volume — a fact known to every economist and almost no general reader, and the single most important thing this lesson can teach: when Capital is invoked as economic authority, the invoker is citing a building whose foundation was condemned, in public, in 1896, with the demolition never answered on the merits.

## The Honest Counterpoint

What, then, keeps the cathedral standing in cultural memory, and what do its defenders rightly claim? Three things, honestly weighed. First, the descriptive and historical chapters — the working day, machinery, primitive accumulation — remain powerful and partially durable: capitalism's early brutality was real (this curriculum documents it independently), the analysis of technological dynamism was ahead of its century, and the crisis-proneness Marx insisted on against the era's complacent equilibrium theorists was vindicated in 1873, 1929, and 2008, even though his mechanism for it was not (the Austrian and monetarist accounts in this curriculum explain the cycles without the value theory or the terminus). Second, commodity fetishism and the book's sociological imagination — the market as a human arrangement misperceived as nature — spawned a genuinely fertile tradition, and the course has conceded the observation while contesting the valuation. Third, defenders argue the book should be read as Verstehen rather than mechanics — Edmund Wilson called Capital the greatest work of irony ever written, a moral epic in scientific costume, and read so, its power is undeniable: the prose of the fetishism section, the working-day chapter's controlled fury, the expropriation trumpet are literature of the first rank. The course accepts the literary verdict completely — and observes that it concedes the case: a moral epic is exactly what Unit 1 has been arguing Marxism is, and epics are not refuted by Bortkiewicz, which is why the refutation never mattered to the faithful. The economics fell; the scripture stood; and the scripture's final promise — the revolution, the dictatorship, and the state that would wither into brotherhood — is the strangest and most consequential part of the whole edifice, because it is the part Marx left almost blank. What he wrote, what he refused to write, and who filled in the blank is the next lesson.

## Reading Capital Today

A practical coda, because this course wants readers who have actually met the primary texts. The book's publication history is itself instructive: Volume I appeared in 1867 to near-total silence — Engels resorted to writing pseudonymous reviews, attacking the book from various angles to provoke attention — and its first foreign translation was, of all things, Russian (1872), passed by the Tsarist censor on the reasoning that "few will read it and fewer still will understand it": the single worst call in the history of censorship, since Russia became the book's most consequential readership. Sales gathered slowly; by Marx's death the "Bible of the working class" (Engels's phrase) was a movement's totem more than its reading, and so it has remained — the book is vastly more owned than finished, in every century including ours. For the reader who wants the real thing rather than the reputation, the course's guidance: read Chapter 1, Section 4 (the fetishism of commodities) for the philosophical Marx at his most brilliant; Chapter 10 (the Working Day) for the empirical Marx at his most powerful — it stands alone, and no summary substitutes; Chapters 26 through 32 (primitive accumulation through the expropriation trumpet) for the historian and the prophet in full voice; and the first three chapters' value-theory apparatus only if prepared to hold it against the marginalist alternative, since reading it innocent of the 1870s is how each generation's undergraduates mistake a condemned foundation for bedrock. Skip, without guilt, the vast middle stretches of circulating capital and reproduction schemes that defeated even the faithful. Read this way, Capital delivers exactly what the inspection found: pages of genius, chapters of fire, and a foundation that its own third volume gave away — the full experience of the cathedral, nave, spire, and crack.
