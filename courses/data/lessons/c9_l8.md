Every doctrine of salvation must eventually describe the saved state, and here Marxism does the strangest thing in its entire architecture: it goes silent. The man who wrote thousands of pages diagnosing capitalism wrote scattered paragraphs — literally — on what would replace it, refusing on principle to compose "recipes for the cook-shops of the future." What the corpus does contain is a doctrine of transition of terrifying compactness: the proletariat seizes power; a "revolutionary dictatorship of the proletariat" holds and remakes society; production is socialized; classes dissolve; and then, in the most audacious prediction in political theory, the state itself — being nothing but an instrument of class rule — loses its function and "withers away," leaving the free association of producers, where "the government of persons is replaced by the administration of things." This lesson examines the transition doctrine, the blank where the destination should be, and the two most consequential facts about both: that the blank was filled, after Marx's death, by Lenin, with results that inverted the prophecy absolutely — the state, everywhere Marxism ruled, did not wither but metastasized into the most total states in human history; and that this outcome was predicted, in precise detail, in Marx's own lifetime, from inside his own movement, by the anarchist rival he destroyed his own International to defeat. Bakunin's prophecy is this lesson's centerpiece, because it demolishes in advance the most common defense of Marxism — that no one could have known.

## The Transition: Dictatorship and the Two Phases

Assemble the doctrine from its scattered texts.[^1] The Manifesto had ended in forcible overthrow; the Paris Commune of 1871 — the two-month workers' government of Paris, drowned in blood by the French army — gave Marx his one concrete model, and his pamphlet on it (The Civil War in France) drew the lesson Lenin would later canonize: the working class "cannot simply lay hold of the ready-made state machinery and wield it for its own purposes"; the existing state — army, police, bureaucracy — must be smashed, not inherited, and replaced by something like the Commune's arrangements: officials elected, revocable, paid workmen's wages. The fullest statement came four years later in a private document, the Critique of the Gotha Programme (1875), Marx's furious marginalia on a draft party platform, and its key sentence is the doctrine's constitutional text: "Between capitalist and communist society lies the period of the revolutionary transformation of the one into the other. Corresponding to this is also a political transition period in which the state can be nothing but the revolutionary dictatorship of the proletariat." The same document sketches the destination's two phases. In the lower phase (what the tradition came to call socialism), still "stamped with the birthmarks of the old society," distribution follows contribution — equal pay for equal labor, an advance on capitalism but still, Marx notes with characteristic honesty, a form of "bourgeois right," since unequal talents yield unequal rewards. Only in the higher phase — when the division of labor's "enslaving subordination" has vanished, when labor has become "life's prime want," when productive forces flow abundantly — can society inscribe the banner: "From each according to his ability, to each according to his needs!" And then the state's fate, stated most fully by Engels: the proletariat's seizure of the means of production is "the last independent act" of the state as state; with class antagonism gone, there is nothing left to repress; "the state is not 'abolished,' it withers away" (Anti-Dühring), interference in social relations becoming superfluous "in one domain after another," until governing men gives way to administering things. Feel the doctrine's audacity before auditing it: an unlimited dictatorship is prescribed as the road to the state's disappearance — absolute power as the cure for power — on the strength of a theoretical guarantee that power, once classless, has nothing to do and simply evaporates.

## The Blank, and Who Filled It

Now the silence. Beyond these paragraphs, Marx refused system: no constitution for the dictatorship (how chosen? checked how? by whom recallable, once the smashing is done?); no economics of the plan (how are millions of production decisions coordinated without prices? — the question Mises would detonate in 1920, and which the entire corpus never once seriously engages); no account of the higher phase's abundance (scarcity is to end — how?); no criterion for when the dictatorship, having absolute power, will judge itself finished. The refusal had a theory behind it — blueprints were "utopian," the future would be worked out by the struggle itself, and history's laws guaranteed the destination without requiring its architecture — and the course marks the structure of that move, because it recurs: the guarantee substitutes for the design; the certainty that it must work replaces any account of how.

Pause on the formula meant to fill the largest gap, because its emptiness is analytical, not rhetorical. "The government of persons is replaced by the administration of things" — the phrase (inherited from the Saint-Simonians) dissolves on contact: things do not administer themselves, and the things in question are the means of everyone's life. To "administer" steel is to decide which cities get bridges and which wait; to administer grain is to decide who eats how much and who grows it under what compulsion; to administer housing, print, and transport is to hold every lever that governing persons ever consisted of — which is why the formula, wherever implemented, produced not the end of politics but its totalization: when the state owns everything, every administrative act is an act of power over persons, and there is no employer, landlord, publisher, or grocer left to appeal to against it. The phrase promised the state's evaporation and described, without knowing it, the total state's job description — a confusion between abolishing power and consolidating it that the twentieth century clarified at scale. Into the blank, in the crisis of 1917, stepped Lenin — State and Revolution, written between revolutions, is a scriptural commentary on exactly the passages above, radicalizing the Commune texts (smash the state) while indefinitely extending the dictatorship — and then reality performed the inversion this course will document for thirteen lessons: the state that was to begin dying grew, within five years of October, a political police larger than the Tsar's by orders of magnitude; within fifteen, the total state — passports, plans, censorship, camps — beyond any despotism in history; and at no point, in any Marxist regime, on any continent, in a century, did the withering begin. The doctrine's defenders answer that this proves betrayal — Stalin was not the prophecy's fulfillment but its abandonment — and the course's reply is the lesson's hinge: a theory whose central political prediction (state power, concentrated absolutely, will dissolve itself) produced its exact opposite in every implementation is not betrayed by the outcome; it is refuted by it — and refuted in the precise way that matters most, because "give us absolute power and it will wither" is the kind of claim civilization cannot afford to test twice per century.

## Bakunin's Prophecy

The most devastating witness was contemporary, internal, and specific. Mikhail Bakunin — the anarchist titan who fought Marx for control of the First International until Marx expelled him (by packed congress, 1872, then moved the International's council to New York to die rather than let rivals inherit it — the founder's own political practice, noted for the next lesson) — published in 1873, in Statism and Anarchy, an analysis of what Marxist power would actually become, and the passages read today like leaked minutes from the future. The Marxists say the dictatorship will be the proletariat itself, Bakunin writes — but "the whole proletariat" cannot govern; there will be governors: "a small number of representatives... ex-workers, who, as soon as they become rulers or representatives of the people, cease to be workers and begin to look down upon the toiling people. From that time on they represent not the people but themselves and their own claims to govern the people." The regime will be rule by intelligentsia: "the reign of scientific intelligence, the most aristocratic, despotic, arrogant, and elitist of all regimes. There will be a new class, a new hierarchy of real and counterfeit scientists and scholars" — and the peasant majority, whom Marx's schema despised, "will be ruthlessly ruled," placed "under direct command of the state engineers, who will constitute the new privileged scientific-political class." To Marx's assurance that the state would be temporary, Bakunin answers with the sentence that outlived them both: "They maintain that only a dictatorship — their dictatorship, of course — can create the will of the people, while our answer to this is: No dictatorship can have any other aim but that of self-perpetuation... Liberty can only be created by liberty." New class; red bureaucracy; the intelligentsia enthroned; the peasants crushed; the dictatorship self-perpetuating — every clause was cashed, within fifty years, in the currencies of the Cheka, the nomenklatura, collectivization, and the Party that never withered; and the prophecy's provenance is its power: this is not hindsight, not a capitalist's slander, but the movement's own co-founder reading the doctrine's structure in 1873 and deriving the Gulag's org chart from it. Marx read the book — his sneering marginal notes survive — and answered the argument nowhere.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The defenses, at strength. First, textual: Marx's dictatorship, scholars rightly note, meant class rule, not one-man tyranny — Marx and Engels late in life allowed that in England or America the transition might come by ballot, and Engels's last writings embraced electoral struggle; the Leninist vanguard-party dictatorship is a genuine mutation, and the democratic-socialist tradition (Bernstein through the Second International's majority) descends from the same texts by the peaceful reading. Granted, and it matters — the course will keep the Leninist/democratic distinction sharp throughout (Unit 6 devotes a lesson to it). But the defense has limits the record enforces: the texts contain both readings (the Commune-smashing line and the ballot line), the founders never built the constitutional safeguards the peaceful reading needed, and where the doctrine was actually implemented as a system of power — every single time — the Bakunin dynamics, not the ballot dynamics, governed; a doctrine that reliably selects its violent reading in practice owns that pattern. Second, the "material conditions" defense: the withering presupposed abundance and advanced development, which backward Russia lacked — communism was never tried where the theory prescribed. The course's answer, developed fully in Unit 3's closing lesson: this explains the failures' location while conceding the theory's uselessness (a prophecy valid only under conditions that never obtain predicts nothing) and ignoring the prediction's structural flaw, which Bakunin identified independent of development levels — concentrated power self-perpetuates; the mechanism has no off switch, in Germany or Cambodia alike; public-choice economics would later formalize what the anarchist saw whole. Third, some defenders retreat to the regulative-ideal reading: the stateless end was a horizon, not a schedule. Perhaps — but a horizon that licenses unlimited present dictatorship is the most expensive metaphor in history, and the course declines to let the doctrine keep, as poetry, the promissory note it issued as science. The transition doctrine complete, one lesson remains before the unit's verdict on Marxism-as-religion: the founder himself, weighed — what the man's own practice, sacrifices, and exemptions legitimately prove about the system he built. Marx the man versus Marx the prophet is next.
