The biography lesson ended with a promise: that the course would neither refute the system by the man nor pretend the man irrelevant to the system, and would eventually state, with precision, what a founder's life can and cannot prove about his doctrine. This lesson keeps the promise, because the question is not academic housekeeping — it governs how every ideology in history should be judged, including the ones the reader favors. The materials are now all assembled: the life (L2), the system (L3–8), and the founder's own political practice, glimpsed in the Bakunin affair, which this lesson examines in full as the most probative evidence of all. The argument runs in three movements: the ad hominem fallacy stated and honored (what the man's sins cannot prove); the three legitimate biographical tests stated and applied (what they can); and then the synthesis the unit has been building toward — Isaiah Berlin's and others' recognition that the man is best understood not as a scientist at all but as something far older, which sets up the unit's closing lesson on Marxism as religion.

## What the Sins Cannot Prove

State the fallacy first, formally, because the course's credibility depends on refusing weapons it would deny its opponents.[^1] A doctrine's truth is independent of its author's character: Newton was a vindictive obsessive and the inverse-square law holds; Rousseau abandoned five children to the foundling hospital and his arguments must still be answered on their merits; and if Marx had been a saint, the labor theory of value would be exactly as false as Böhm-Bawerk showed it to be. So the standard indictment — the sponging, the rages, the racial slurs in the letters, Freddy Demuth farmed out and denied — proves, about the theory of history, nothing; and the course flags, for intellectual hygiene, the popular anti-Marx literature that leans on the man's squalor as if it were an argument: it is not, and audiences trained to accept it will accept the same move against every thinker they cherish. Honesty requires the counter-biography stated with equal fullness. Marx's poverty, whatever its financial mismanagement component, was substantially chosen: a man of his talents could have prospered in law, journalism, or officialdom (Prussia's price was silence, and he refused to pay it); he buried three children in that refusal's shadow, and the letters around Edgar's death are among the most broken a father ever wrote. The two people who knew him best and longest — Jenny, who lost her health and her children to his vocation, and Engels, who gave him twenty years of hated mill work — loved him totally and never recanted; devotion on that scale is data about a man. And the daughters' arc completes the tragedy without editorializing: Eleanor, his beloved "Tussy," who inherited the cause and the temperament, poisoned herself in 1898 over a worthless man; Laura and Paul Lafargue died in a considered suicide pact in 1911, their reasoning coolly materialist. The house of Marx paid for the mission in the oldest currencies, and the course's charge sheet against the system will never pretend otherwise.

## The Three Legitimate Tests

Now the biographical evidence that does bear, in three precise ways. First, the reflexivity test, planted in L2 and armed in L4: historical materialism asserts that consciousness is determined by class position — and cannot account for its own founders (a lawyer's son on mill dividends, a mill owner) or for the movement's permanent sociology (bourgeois intellectuals supplying the theory, workers declining it at the ballot box wherever free). This is not ad hominem; it is the theory tested against its nearest available data — its own history — and failing: the standard patch (intellectuals may defect to the rising class's standpoint) concedes that ideas are not class-determined after all, which quietly forfeits the doctrine's central inversion. A theory of everything that cannot explain itself has told us something about the theory, and the man's biography is simply Exhibit A. Second, the practice test, and here the Bakunin affair earns its full telling. The First International (1864–76) was the movement's founding institution, and Marx ran it from the General Council with consummate skill until a rival arose whose following, in Latin Europe, outgrew his own. What followed set the template: the 1872 Hague Congress, packed with mandates of contested provenance; Bakunin expelled on charges (a murky Russian financial affair via the sinister Nechaev) that served as the pretext for a factional execution; and then — the masterstroke that tells most — Marx and Engels moved the International's seat to New York, beyond their rivals' reach and everyone's relevance, where it duly expired. Confronted with the choice between sharing his creation and killing it, the founder killed it. Why does this bear on the doctrine where the maid does not? Because it is the doctrine in operation: the practice of the movement's founder, in the movement's founding institution, facing the movement's first pluralism — and the practice was: no rivals, no sharing, control or destruction; anathema as the answer to argument; the split as the standing solution. Every Marxist party in history has reproduced the pattern — the mother church's schisms, expulsions, and "renegade" literature are structural, not accidental — and the pattern was set not by Stalin's paranoia but by the founder's hands in 1872, before a single Marxist held state power. When Unit 3 reaches the regimes that shot deviationists, the reader will recognize the manners: they were learned at the founder's table. Third, the exemption test: the pattern by which the doctrine's demands bind others and excuse the doctrinaire. Marx preached the proletariat and never entered its company (his one recorded factory visit is a matter of biographical dispute); denounced exploitation while the household's own proletarian went years unpaid and, on the weight of evidence, bore the exploitation's oldest form; thundered against the bourgeoisie while duning Engels for funds to keep the daughters in bourgeois accomplishments, explicitly so they could marry well. Again, none of this touches the syllogisms — but it establishes, at the tradition's origin, the disposition Unit 3 will document at scale (the nomenklatura's dachas, the Party's special stores, the vanguard's permanent exemption from the equality it enforced) and Unit 5 will explain: a doctrine of radical leveling whose carriers, beginning with the first, understood it as applying downward. Three tests, three failures — and all three, note, are tests of the system through the man, not smears of the system by the man; the distinction is the lesson.

## The Prophet

The synthesis now, and it belongs to the man's greatest unsympathetic portraitist. Contemporaries reached for the same register unprompted. The Russian liberal Pavel Annenkov, watching Marx preside over a meeting in 1846, left the era's most quoted portrait: a figure "of the type... composed of energy, will and unshakable conviction," whose every word rang with the strange note of command — "he spoke only in the imperative"; Marx moved through rooms, Annenkov wrote, as "a democratic dictator," and the phrase has never been improved on. The Prussian police spy who infiltrated the Soho household in 1852 reported the same authority radiating from the chaos: amid the pawned furniture and the pipe smoke, the agent conceded, sat a man whose intellect made his visitors disciples on contact. These are hostile and neutral witnesses describing, without the vocabulary, what the movement's later history confirmed: the founder's relation to his followers was never that of a scientist to colleagues — it was that of a prophet to a remnant, and everyone in the room could feel it.

Isaiah Berlin's classic study — hostile to the doctrine, mesmerized by the figure — saw what the "science" framing conceals: Marx belongs not to the lineage of Newton and Darwin but to the lineage of the prophets. The fury at injustice that no data could calm; the certainty that history is a moral drama with a guaranteed vindication; the anathemas against deviation; the chosen agent (the proletariat as suffering servant, whose emancipation emancipates mankind); the apocalypse (revolution) and the kingdom (communism) — the structure, as Berlin and Edmund Wilson and Kolakowski all converged in seeing, is Hebrew prophecy secularized through German philosophy, delivered with the learning of a professor and the wrath of an Amos, by a man whose rabbinic ancestry the biography lesson recorded and whose household nickname, with no irony intended, was "the Moor" delivering judgment. The synthesis explains everything the science framing cannot: why refutation never mattered (prophecies are not falsified, they are betrayed and deferred); why the movement generated churches, schisms, heresies, inquisitions, and martyrs rather than research programs; why the founder's practice ran to anathema rather than debate; and why the man himself — who read no factory ledgers in his last decade but filled notebooks with algebra, Russian, and geology while the system stood unfinished — could not complete Capital: prophecy's authority would have been staked on economics' answers, and some part of the prophet, his best biographers suspect, preferred the trumpet unfalsifiable. The man, in the end, proves this much about the system: that it was built by a prophet in a scientist's century, and inherited the strengths and the pathologies of both costumes — science's prestige with prophecy's immunity to evidence.

## The Honest Counterpoint

The defenses of the man, at strength, and the course's answers. Marxists and fair-minded biographers reply, first, that the practice test overreaches: the First International was a snake pit in every direction — Bakunin's own methods (the Nechaev association was real and ugly, the secret Alliance within the International a genuine infiltration) were as ruthless as Marx's, and nineteenth-century radical politics knew no Marquess of Queensberry; judging Marx's factionalism by seminar-room norms is anachronism. Partly granted — Bakunin was no saint, and the course's use of him is as prophet, not exemplar — but the reply misses the test's point: the question is not whether Marx was uniquely ruthless but whether the doctrine, in its founder's hands, generated pluralism or anathema; the answer is anathema, and the pattern's perfect reproduction across a century of Marxist organizations, in every political culture, marks it as the doctrine's property, not the century's. Second, defenders note that the prophet framing can be flipped: all great moral traditions, including the ones this curriculum honors, run on prophetic fire; if Amos is admirable, why is Marx's fury a pathology? The course's answer is the distinction on which its final unit will close: the Hebrew prophets placed the kingdom beyond history and judgment in God's hands, which disciplined their politics (even kings were only rebuked, not liquidated, and the prophet himself stood under the law he proclaimed); Marx placed the kingdom inside history and its execution in the hands of men certain of the verdict — and a scheduled paradise with human executors is not a variant of prophecy but its most dangerous mutation, the one whose body count Unit 3 tallies. Third, the man's defenders make the honest human plea: he suffered, he loved, he never sold out, and he believed every word — sincerity attested by the buried children. The course accepts it all, and answers with the oldest lesson in its curriculum: sincerity is not a defense; it is an amplifier. The most dangerous men are rarely the cynics. The unit now closes where all its threads have pointed — the fall, the chosen class, the apocalypse, the paradise, the scripture, the schisms, the prophet: Marxism examined, at last, as what it structurally is. A religion for the godless — next.
